### Argument
- No need to restate the issues, just make a heading with the issue in it and get right into that shit
- Appellants submitted that all required elements of promissory estoppel are met, we respectfully disagree
- Accept that promissory estoppel requires considering the interactions between the parties first
- Wish to clarify that the test requires a promise or assurance made by words or conduct which was ***intended to affect*** the legal relations between them
- Stated on p. 770 of *Combes* and p. 258 of *High Trees* , and para 63 of the trial decision from *N.M.*
- Primary argument is that the trial judge correctly found that the conduct of the College did not amount to an implied promise to refrain from enforcing s. 3.02, and there was insufficient evidence of intent to affect legal relations
- Second, assuming that an omission alone meets the requirement of an implied promise to continue that omission, promissory estoppel cannot be established on the grounds that there was no evidence of knowledge that the promise would be acted on
### Primary Argument
- Accept that promissory estoppel can be established by an explicit or implicit promise
- However, the College did not make even an implied promise or assurance to Mr. Instructor either by words or conduct, as the common law suggests that a mere omission is insufficient to suggest a promise to continue that omission into the future
- This is most clearly stated by Lord Denning in his assessment of the consideration issue in *Combes v Combes*
- "There was, however, clearly no promise by the wife, express or implied, to forbear from applying to the court. All that happened was that she did, in fact, forbear..." (pp. 770-71)
- *Combes* can be distinguished from the present case, as the doctrine of promissory estoppel was being used as a sword in that case, rather than as a shield
- However, the circumstances of the alleged promise here are quite similar, as the wife never indicated she was refraining from exercising her legal rights, despite having every right and opportunity to do so, up until the moment the case went to court and she again decided to exercise her right
- Appellants submit that because the conduct of the College induced Mr. Instructor to make an assumption about their legal relations, this is sufficient evidence of an implied promise based on information from *N. M.*
- Respondent respectfully disagrees with this interpretation, as the text not only is referring to inducing an assumption of a legal relationship between the parties (which is already established in this case), but also explicitly states that the promisor must be aware of this assumption
- While Mr. Instructor certainly discussed the prevalence of AI use at the College with his fellow instructors, there is no evidence he ever took this information to the College directly, and certainly never raised the issue of s. 3.02 with the College administrators
- As the trial judge rightly points out, Mr. Instructor never took any action to confirm his assumption regarding the existence of the alleged promise
### Secondary Argument
- The appellants also submit that if there was an implied promise from the College not to enforce s. 3.02, this is sufficient to conclude that the College knew that the promise would be acted upon
- Respectfully, the respondent disagrees with this conclusion
- While we accept that Mr. Instructor was clearly acting in a manner that suggests that he believed s. 3.02 would never be enforced, recall that he never communicated this belief to the College itself
- If we accept the argument that all that is required to prove an implied promise is conduct shown to induce a belief in existence of said promise, then the possibility remains that the actor allegedly making this promise would be unaware of it
- Not only does this appear to be a violation of the core principles of promissory estoppel described in *High Trees*, but also means that without evidence that the College was aware of the existence of this promise, they could not have known that it was being acted on
