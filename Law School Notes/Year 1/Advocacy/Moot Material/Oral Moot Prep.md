### Intro
- Restate the elements of promissory estoppel, referencing the appellants argument if needed (call them my friend)
	- One party, expressly or by conduct, makes a promise **intended** to affect the parties' legal relations (p. 2 para 9)
		- Depending on how the appellants word the elements, may need to clarify the intentionality part
		- Quote for high trees is "I prefer to apply the principle that the promise, intended to be binding, intended to be acted on and in fact acted on, is binding so far as its terms properly apply" p. 259
		- Quote for combe is "The principle, as I understand it, is that where one party has, by his words or conduct, made to the other a promise or assurance which was intended to affect the legal relations between them and to be acted on accordingly" p. 770
	- Known that the promise would be acted upon
	- Promise was actually acted on
- The trial judge correctly found the first element was not met, conduct of the College didn't amount to an implied promise
- Reason for this is because at common law, omission alone is not evidence of an implied promise (p. 3 para 11)
- Even if this is implied promise, means no evidence to suggest intent to affect legal relations (p. 3 para 10)
- Also raises the question of whether respondent knew they made an implied promise
- If they did not, could not have known the promise would be acted upon
### Existence of Implied Promise
- Lord Denning suggests omission isn't implied promise in *Combe* (Page 3, Para 12)
- Facts are that a woman omitted to apply to divorce court to seek money from her ex-husband for several years
- Court said could be an implied promise in exchange for ex-husband's promise to give her an allowance, didn't work

“There was, however, clearly no promise by the wife, express or implied, to forbear from applying to the court. All that happened was that she did, in fact, forbear..."

- Indicates you need more than simple forbearance
- Otherwise every instance of someone refraining from exercising their legal rights in a contract could trigger this doctrine
- Also would not account for cases where one party wants to exercise a right, but cannot do so for reasons unknown to the other party
- Finally, no clear standard for how long before an omission becomes an implied promise
- Appellants submit that the doctrine applies where one party induces other to make an assumption about legal relations (p. 4 para 13)
- Could be the "something more" required
- We disagree and submit that this is a misinterpretation of case law
	- May need to change this depending on how they argue
- Main evidence allegedly supporting this argument comes from *N. M.*
- Case only deals with explicit promises, issue here was if one party was induced into believing promise was legally binding (p. 4 para 14)
- Agreement between College and Mr. Instructor was certainly binding, this is not an issue here
	- This part will probably be a hotspot for questions
- Inducement may be relevant for if an existing promise was intended to be binding, but not for if there was an implied promise
	- Requirement for inducement is that promisor knows the promisee is acting on the inducement (p. 5 para 15)
	- Can save this for later, or bring it up here depending on context
- Overall, case law doesn't support appellant argument, it works against it
- Transition into next topic by talking about how one party could accidentally induce the other party into believing a promise exists

### Summary
- Basis for implied promise in this case is a mere omission
- Case law indicates that this is not an implied promise
- Facts also do not indicate that the College intended to make a promise, or to affect legal relations
- Possible the College was completely unaware Mr. Instructor interpreted their actions as a promise
- Would be unreasonable to bind them to a promise they never intended to make
- Should also require clearer communication between the parties to establish promissory estoppel
- For these reasons, trial judge was correct on this issue
