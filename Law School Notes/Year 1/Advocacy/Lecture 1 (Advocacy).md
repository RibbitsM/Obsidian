- Main goal of the moot is to develop communication skills
- Need to be able to create a good argument and communicate it through writing or orally
- Two components of the course, important part rn is the written portion
### Factums
- In BC, a factum is specifically a written argument for appeal
- Has a different meaning in different provinces
- Includes a statement of the facts, statements of the applicable law (in this case we know this ahead of time), and an application of the law to the facts supporting a conclusion
- This last part is by far the most important part of the document
- Can think about it like a road map for the court
- Main point of having a good factum is that it gives you a strong footing before you show up in person to perform your oral argument
- Strong oral argument can overcome a poor factum, but a good factum will really reinforce your argument
- There is a set number of features your factum should have:
	1. Title Page
	2. Table of Contents
	3. Statement of Facts
	4. Errors in Judgement/Issues on Appeal
	5. Argument
	6. Nature of Order Sought
- Title page format is found in the rules, so is the table of contents format
- Statement of facts is a little more complicated, for the appellant you start with the finding of the trial court, then quickly summarize the factual findings relevant to the issues
- Key word here is "relevant", you don't need to restate all the facts
- Ideally you should do this after you already know what your argument is going to be
- The trial judgement has some red herrings you need to weed out
- Make sure not to include anything resembling an argument in this section
- As the appellant, don't leave any facts out because they help the other side
- Seek to provide a balanced summary of the relevant facts, most people tend to include too many facts
- For the respondent, you don't really need to make any changes compared to appellant's statement of facts
- Start by saying that you agree with the appellant's version
- If you feel something was left out, add that in afterwards by clarifying their statement or adding on something new
- Don't necessarily need to do this if you're arguing over minor wording
- If it's not substantial, leave it alone
- Again, avoid argumentative or mocking language
- For appellants, you have to deal with errors in judgement, respondents have to deal with issues on appeal
- Appellant usually uses the issues as framed in the trial decision, respondent may reword them
- As for the nature of the order, the appellant wants to allow the appeal and the respondent wants it dismissed
### Argument
- Real meat of your factum is your argument, which will be developed in this section
- Appellants will want to focus on where and why the trial judge was incorrect
- Respondents want to explain that the judge properly applied the law
- These decisions are quite short, so you might have to expand on it
- You have a set number of cases to deal with, and you will want to use them appropriately
- Can simply cite the decisions or paraphrase them, or quote them directly
- Can also make policy arguments, not limited to just legal reasoning
- Not allowed to go find secondary sources or journal articles to support these arguments, but you're free to argue given what you have
- Try to include your personal voice in your writing, try to keep it formal but you should still be writing genuinely, not how you think the professor wants you to write
- Look at the facts and start thinking about what's relevant and which arguments may help you or hurt you
- Remember there's no "right" way to argue the case, make the arguments that you personally find the most persuasive
- Organize your writing, order your points in a manner you think makes sense
- This is one of the crucial features that separate the great arguments from the good ones
- Recall that this is an appeal, you can't challenge findings of fact
- Also, your factum is just the core of your argument, you can't introduce completely new points at trial, but you can flesh out your points a lot more
- Overall, you should try to be as succinct as possible
- Remember that this is a road map, you're just making the skeleton of your argument, and you'll fill it in with your oral advocacy
- ***Short sentences are almost always more effective than long ones***
- If you have a really long sentence with multiple pieces of information, try breaking it up into several small sentences
- Assume your reader is considering an issue for the first time, even though the volunteer judges are lawyers, they may not remember some of these topics
- This also extends to where your paragraphs begin and end, this is a great way to divide points
- Use numbering and lists, it's heavily encouraged
- Also use headings and subheadings!!!! they also help a lot to signpost your argument
- Can also use your headings to make a point, and then explain it in the paragraph
### Don'ts
- Don't make cheap shots, seems obvious but don't use any aggressive or insulting language about the arguments of the opposing party
- Don't overstate facts, it turns a strong point into a weak point, it gives the other side ammunition to use against you
- Avoid saying something is "well-known" or "obvious", always try to moderate your language
- Don't use language that is too casual, especially contractions like "doesn't" and "don't"
- You can do this during the oral part, but keep it out of your written statement
- On the other side of the coin, try to avoid using too much legal vocabulary, try to stick to plain language without resorting to lawyerly words
- Make your points count, don't waste time stating the obvious
- After every sentence and paragraph you write, check if it's something that the court actually needs to here
- Don't make the same point twice, try to fit everything into one section
- Don't need to restate your position a million times, focus on supporting it
