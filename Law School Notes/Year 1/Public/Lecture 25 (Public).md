## Recap
- *R v Sharpe* is a reading in case, could be construed as reading down
### [[Doucet-Boudreau v Nova Scotia]]
- Deals with the issue of when a non-declaratory remedy will be appropriate under s. 24(1)
- Helpful to think about this as a "capstone case" because it deals with a bunch of earlier issues like separation of powers, institutional competence, and vindication of rights
### [[Vancouver v Ward]]
- Offers another alternative to a declaratory remedy, two-part test
- First, claimant has to show a functional justification for ordering the remedies
- Second, government has to show countervailing factors to negate the claim
- Don't have enough cases to talk about interference with good governance, but for our purposes the main issue is the availability of a sufficient alternate remedy
- Helps you think about the constitutional interests the court will focus on, how are they required to respond once a violation is found?
### Mapping Remedies
- Already talked about when an exemption is justified under s. 24
- [[Little Sisters Book and Art Emporium v Canada]] is the case for determining whether to use s. 52 or s. 24
- Can divide s. 24 into declaratory and non-declaratory remedies
	- For declaratory remedies, we can look at *Little Sisters*
		- In that case, there were case-specific reasons for issuing a declaratory remedy
		- Should be able to analogize and distinguish based on these facts, general reasons are to defer, need for supervision, etc.
	- For non-declaratory remedies, can get a mandatory order ([[Canada v PHS Community Services|PHS]]), a structural injunction (*Doucet-Boudreau*), and more
		- Need to look at the facts of each case, why did court give that remedy there?
			- In *Doucet*, part of the reasoning was the long-term suffering of the claimants

## Review
### Constitutional Interpretation
- Focus is on what kinds of arguments the courts will make here
- In *Edwards v Canada*, the court came up with the "living tree" analogy, contrasted with the approach of looking at the specific terms of the constitution in historical context
### Features
- Branches of government and separation of powers is a key feature, and a major source of the reasoning for various arguments for deference
- Also talked about the rule of law, example for this is *Roncarelli v Duplessis*
	- Courts can interpret the exercise of statutory powers, cannot use these powers outside the bounds of how they were granted, need a source of law for the power
	- In this case, the exercise of discretion was beyond the purposes of the statute
- Example of common law constitutionalism, how does rule of law work pre-Charter?
- Common law cases also make up the Canadian constitution
- Next feature is federalism, difference between federal and provincial powers
- Not a major issue for this class, cases are there to show the existence of division of powers
	- Cases for this were from [[Antecedents of the Charter]], illustrate how Charter rights issues like discrimination were dealt with prior to the Charter itself
	- Decided mainly based on the division of powers, policy was not a concern
- Another feature is the implied bill of rights, from cases like [[Antecedents of the Charter#Switzman v Elbling|Switzman v Elbling]]
	- Again, most of these cases would be dealt with using the Charter today
	- Specifically, this case would be dealt with by s. 2(a)
		- One judge in this case mentioned that it's possible that no part of the government would be allowed to do this, not in the division of powers
	- In [[Antecedents of the Charter#Dupond v City of Montreal|Dupond]], the same suggestion of a lack of government power comes up again
		- There are some things the government can't do, this would later become the Charter
		- Helps illustrate the changes that the Charter created
- Next feature is parliamentary sovereignty and the Bill of Rights
	- No parliament can bind any future parliament, only exception may be the Bill of Rights
	- Need to include a disclaimer when enacting legislation that violates the Bill of Rights
		- Not a hard bar, just a form/procedural requirement
		- Suggests that parliament cannot bind future parliaments in substance, only form
		- Ties into the discussion on s. 33, which has similar manner and form requirements
	- Status of the Bill of Rights is also weird, not quite constitutional, not quite statutory
- So far, all of this information is mainly background on the constitution
- Next feature is separation of powers, but in the context of s. 96
	- This dictates the powers of superior courts, main case for this is [[The Separation of Powers#Re Residential Tenancies Act|Residential Tenancies]]
		- Also touches on the issue of how rules develop over time
		- One of the first examples we saw of a doctrinal test, this will set the stage for how courts will develop these tests in the future
			- In *Sobey's*, the court adapted the *Residential Tenancies* test and altered it
		- Also deals with what powers the legislature is allowed to take away
			- Cases for these are *McEvoy*, *Crevier*, and *Re Code of Civil Procedure*
		- Section 96 extends to the issue of rule of law
			- *Trial Lawyers Association* shows another aspect of the rule of law, which is the idea that courts must be accessible
- Final part of this section of the course was [[Judicial Independence]]
	- In these cases, the courts tried to define judicial independence, and how the courts will give effect to this principle
		- How do we deal with financial compensation?
			- Addressed in [[Judicial Independence#PEI Provincial Court Judges Reference|PEI Provincial Judges]], also had to develop a doctrinal test
		- *Supreme Court Act Reference* also touches on issues of constitutional interpretation
