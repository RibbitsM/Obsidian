### Recap
- Initial question for s. 7 is whether or not there is a sufficient connection between the state action and a violation of a protected s. 7 interest
- This is part of the first step of s. 7 which requires you to identify which interest is at risk and whether or not it has been infringed
- If it was infringed, then you need to ask if that infringement was done in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice
- Life interest can be engaged by the death penalty, liberty interest is engaged by incarceration, and the security of the person can be engaged by physical or psychological harm
- Liberty can also apply to fundamental life choices
- Looked at the three core principles of fundamental justice: arbitrariness, overbreadth, and gross disproportionality
- Can also recognize discrete PFJs, in [[R v Morgentaler]] we had the principle that a defence cannot be illusory
- *Malmo-Levine* set out the standards for recognizing a new PFJ
- Must be a legal principle that sets a sufficiently precise and manageable standard that is generally considered to be fundamental
- One issue raised by *Morgentaler* was whether or not the legislative scheme would be considered invalid if it didn't create delays
- If you look at the arguments from each justice, there is not a clear majority on this position
### [[Rodriguez v BC]]
- Issue was the ban on doctor-assisted suicide under s. 241(b) of the *Criminal Code*
- Claimant argued that this ban violated her liberty and security interests
- Former was because she is barred from making a fundamental personal choice
- Court did not accept this, saying that people do not have the right to kill themselves, even if they are terminally ill
- However, her security interests were engaged because she sought assisted suicide to avoid fully losing physical control of her body and ultimately choking to death or living on life support
- When it came to identifying the PFJs, the court did not find that the law was arbitrary because the violation was related to the state interest of protecting human life and the vulnerable
- Also specified that the sanctity of life is not a principle of fundamental justice
- Claimant argued that the inherent dignity of human life is a PFJ, but the court said no because that would mean any violation of security also violates that principle
### [[Canada v PHS Community Services]]
- Issue was with a safe injection site (Insite) in the DTES that did not get its exemption from the *Controlled Drugs and Substances Act* renewed
- Argued that this violated their liberty interests by making all of Insite's employees and clients able to be imprisoned for drug possession
- Court found that there was a sufficient causal connection between the decision to not renew and the harm caused, despite the fact that the patients of Insite chose to use drugs
- Pointed out that the trial judge found that addiction is an illness that strips its victims of the ability to choose freely
- Sometimes courts will make a distinction between challenging a law itself and challenging the application of the law
- In this case, the court found that the *CDSA* itself does not violate s. 7, mainly because it allows for exemptions, but this particular application may have violated s. 7
- Also engaged the life interest because of the life-saving care done by Insite, which also extends to the security of the person
- All three protected interests are at issue in this case
#### Arbitrariness
- Two of the core PFJs were raised in this case, arbitrariness and gross disproportionality
- Goal of the *CDSA* is to promote public health and safety, but by not renewing Insite's exemption, the government was actively hurting this interest
- Can say that arbitrariness only arises where the action undermines the intentions of the act, which is what happened in this case
- However, you can also label a law as arbitrary as long as there is no factual connection between the law and its objectives
- Not directly relevant in this case, because both forms of arbitrariness were met, but will become more contentious where only the latter form works
- Court also found that the violation was grossly disproportionate, because any potential benefits of revoking Insite's exemption were greatly outweighed by the downsides
- No clear upsides, and the consequence is revoking essential care to vulnerable people
- Court also found that because there was no rational connection, not saved under s. 1
- Seems like they took their findings from the arbitrariness analysis and applied that to s. 1
- This was also before [[Dore v Barreau du Quebec]] so we were using the *Oakes* test
### [[Canada AG v Bedford]]
- Issue was with the laws against prostitution in Canada, three different prohibitions
- s. 210 makes it an offence to keep or be in a bawdy‑house; s. 212(1)(_j_) prohibits living on the avails of prostitution; and, s. 213(1)(_c_) prohibits communicating in public for the purposes of prostitution.
- Combined, these laws did not outlaw prostitution, but did make it a much more dangerous profession to undertake
- Again, this raises the issue of choice as the Crown argues that there is no causal connection because the harm is caused by the choice of the prostitutes themselves
- State is not causing the harm creating from johns and pimps, but because of the situation most prostitutes find themselves in, they do not have a choice
- Similar to the reasons from *PHS* rejecting the same choice argument
- Prostitution is also not illegal, all these laws do is make it more dangerous
- The law should not make a legal activity more dangerous than it needs to be, it would be like if a law was passed banning bikers from wearing helmets
- In this case, the prostitution laws were preventing prostitutes from operating out of a fixed indoor location and hiring bodyguards/receptionists, which would make things much safer
- Also prevented working on the street, so the only legal option is making house visits which makes the prostitutes much more susceptible to violence
- This engages the life and security interests under s. 7, although the focus is mainly on the security of the person interest
#### PFJs
- Goals of the prostitution provisions is mainly to prevent nuisances to the general public
- This is clearly grossly disproportionate to the very real risk of homicide and bodily harm faced by prostitutes because of these provisions
- The living on avails provision especially is very overbroad because it's intended to prevent exploitation of prostitutes but applies to many innocent situations as well
- Defines overbreadth as the law only needing to be arbitrary in some circumstances
- Also contrary to its objectives because it leads to further exploitation
- The prohibition on communication was also held to be grossly disproportionate because it was also intended to merely avoid nuisances
- Generally, for a law to be arbitrary there has to be no connection between the law and the objectives, overbreadth means arbitrariness in some cases
- This case also raises a major policy issue, in both cases the subtext of the arguments by the Crown is that the court has overstepped into legislative matters
