### Recap
- Mostly just talking about s. 7 and breaking down each case by engaged interest and by the relevant PFJs
- Recall that *Michaud* was the case where the court had issues with imposing speed limiters on semi trucks, was a risk to life and security because it can prevent them from avoiding collisions
	- Deemed overbroad, but saved under s.1 because it overall helps public safety even if it's overbroad in some scenarios
	- Unusual case of a s. 7 violation being saved under s. 1
- General relationship between s. 7 and s. 1 is that a violation of s. 7 will be based on the impact on the claimant, while s. 1 deals with the broader public interest
- Theoretically possible for any s. 7 violation to be saved under s. 1, but very hard to prove
### [[R v Big M Drug Mart]]
- This is one of those unusual cases where a s. 1 analysis ends up being irrelevant because the objective of the legislation is to violate s. 2(a)
- Two kinds of categories of s. 2(a) claims
	- Claims of interference, where the state is interfering with the religious freedoms of the claimant
	- Claims of a breach of neutrality, where the state allegedly takes a position regarding religion
- Not a strict typology, just two general kinds of cases
- Issue was with the *Lord's Day Act* in Alberta which prevents most retail businesses from opening on Sundays to compel observance of the Christian Sabbath
- Court said that you don't need to prove effect, the purpose of the legislation was already unconstitutional because it compels observance of a religious practice
- This is also an obviously coercive piece of legislation because it imposes criminal penalties
- Harms in effect by preventing people from doing things they would otherwise be legally allowed to do, but the main issue is the symbolic effect
- Constant reminder of the force of the Christian majority, signals hostility to religious minorities
- The purpose of the act itself is enough to be a violation of s. 2(a)
### [[R v Edwards Books and Art]]
- Problem with purpose doesn't arise here, it's a secular piece of legislation
- Purpose of this act was only to create secular holidays for workers, could only harm in effect
- No intention to force citizens to comply with the observance of the Sabbath
- Court found a violation in effect, mainly against those who observe a day of rest on Saturday
- Exemptions exist for small businesses, but it still makes operations more costly for those who run large stores and wish to have a day of rest on Saturdays
- Gives a pretty clear competitive advantage to the Sunday observer majority
#### Non-Violation Effects
- Also need to consider what kinds of effects do and do not infringe s. 2(a)
- Effects that are trivial and insubstantial do not rise to this level, this is not the case here
- The competitive disadvantage faced by Saturday observers is not insubstantial
- Example of a trivial burden would be a general sales tax applying to religious goods
- Additionally, the mere fact that there is an overlap between a secular law and religious requirements does not in itself give rise to a s. 2(a) claim
- This would be a claim based on a mere coincidence, would not suffice
### Section 1
- In *Big M*, we know that the Act failed at the first stage because there was no substantial and compelling objective
- Raised an argument that although the original purpose was to compel sabbatical observance, the purpose has shifted over time
- Court rejected this argument as we need to look at Parliamentary intention at the time the Act was created, this will not shift over time
- Also raised concerns about how *stare decisis* would be undermined if we allowed this, would let the court overrule previously acceptable legislation due to a shifting purpose
- This will come back in freedom of expression
#### Edwards Books
- Court focused mainly on the exemption provisions for the statute at issue here
- Section 3(4) of the *Retail Business Holidays Act* allowed businesses with seven or less employees with less than 5000 sq ft to stay open if they close on Saturdays
- Talks about how this balances the interests between business owners and workers
- One of the concerns with this legislation is that repealing the Act would benefit those who are better situated at a cost to vulnerable individuals
- Allowing this legislation would be proportionate balancing
- Lets small and mid-size retailers to benefit without having to prove their religious affiliations
- Other option would be to require any retailers who want to open on Sundays to show an honest religious belief in a day of rest other than Sunday
- Overall, the court found that although there was interference with the rights of those who observe a day of rest on non-Sundays, the exemptions accommodate them
- Saved under s. 1
### [[Mouvement Laique Quebecois v Saguenay]]
- Issue in this case was that municipal meetings started with a prayer
- Claimant argued that allowing this violated religious neutrality
- Defendant claimed that removing this practice would just be taking a position in favour of those who are atheist or agnostic
- Court said that silence is not taking a side, to be benefitting atheists they would have to have a prayer before meetings talking about how there is no God
- Raises the issue of whether this can actually be considered truly neutral
- We can look at the effects of a law, is a seemingly neutral piece of legislation actually a violation of freedom of religion in effect?
- Good example would be a ban on wearing religious headgear in public office in Quebec
- Intention is to create a neutral environment free from religious influence, but in practice this only affects certain religious groups who wear religious symbols
- Gives a clear privilege to groups who do not have to wear religious symbols
- Btw all these Quebec laws do violate s. 2(a) they just spam s. 33 to avoid that lol
- Another main issue in this case was the establishment of a bylaw that codified the prayer and added a pause between the end of the prayer and the start of the meeting
- Purpose of the pause was to let people who were waiting outside for the prayer to end to come in and sit down
- Court said this was a problem, you're compelling someone to make a public declaration of their faith by choosing to abstain from the prayer
- Exemption in *Edwards Books* did not require this kind of declaration
- Claimant in this case felt a pressure and believed they would be stigmatized if they did not conform
### Summary
- Identify categories of s. 2(a) claims
- Distinguish between effects/purpose violation
- Identify s. 1 issues with s. 2(a)
- Identify kinds of coercion coming from violations of neutrality
