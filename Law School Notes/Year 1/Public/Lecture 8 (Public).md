### [[Vriend v Alberta]]
- At issue here was the Alberta *Individual Rights Protection* Act, which was explicitly written to not prevent discrimination based on sexual orientation
- Supposed to be a basic human rights legislation, but lacks this common protection
- This is a primary mechanism for affecting private actors through the Charter
- Is this a violation of the Charter? technically the legislature didn't do anything, they only committed an omission by not including that common protection
- Court points out that this would create a broad power for legislature to avoid the Charter by specifically omitting protections instead of being actively discriminatory
- Issue here was an under-inclusion, which is subtly different from a positive obligation
- Example of a positive obligation would be s. 23 of the Charter, which imposes an obligation for the legislature to provide minority language instruction for groups of a certain size
- This is different, the state has acted and extended some rights, but excluded certain rights
- One side issue in this case was that conservative advocates in Alberta called for the legislature to invoke s. 33 to uphold the *IRPA*, but it refrained from doing so
- Generally speaking, if the government isn't acting at all, it's a case of positive rights
- If they did act, but the action was insufficient, then that's under-inclusion
- In this case, the remedy was "reading in" the protection based on sexual orientation
### [[Application|Application of the Charter Test]]
- How do we determine circumstances where the Charter applies?
- Can look to see if the act was from a state actor, for example we know that municipalities are subject to the Charter
- If it's a clear case, for example it's a law or regulation or action of a government department, you can immediately determine that the Charter applies
- Failing this, look at the three less clear cases
	- Is it an entity exercising a government function or power of compulsion like a municipality?
	- If it's not, is the actor routinely or regularly controlled by the government?
		- Look at cases like [[Greater Vancouver Transportation Authority|GVTA]] or *Douglas College* to see cases where it applies, or *McKinney* and other university cases where it doesn't apply
		- Are the facts closer to the former cases or the latter?
	- Then, look to see if the entity is implementing a government program, example here is *Eldridge*
- General approach is to start with the most clear cases and move to the less clear ones
- Other thing we can look at is if there's litigation between private parties
- Final thing is to look for a case of government inaction, like in *Vriend*
- These are also fairly clear cut, especially the first case like in *Dolphin*
### [[The Override|Section 33 and the Override]]
- Recall that s. 33(1) allows legislatures to expressly declare that an act operates notwithstanding s. 2 or a section between 7 and 15 in the Charter
- In the *Ford v Quebec (AG)*, one issue was whether s. 33 operates retroactively
- Court pointed to the word "shall" in the section as meaning that it does not
- Generally, if you have to choose between a retroactive and prospective interpretation, you should interpret it in the favour of the person claiming the violation, in this case prospectively
- The override in the French language sign act here was implemented as part of an omnibus bill that provided a full override for all Quebec laws
- Main point of s. 33 is to allow legislatures to have the final word on whether the Charter applies
- Can think of this as supporting parliamentary sovereignty, but also allowing the legislatures to enforce their view where they interpret the Charter differently from the judiciary
- One potential issue with s. 33 is that it only requires restrictions of form, legislature don't have to justify their use of s. 33, they can just do it if they use the magic words
- The reason that s. 33 exists is because the Charter would not exist otherwise, the provinces only agreed on the condition that they could still override it if needed
- Technically speaking, there is nothing in s. 33 that explicitly removes judicial review, and there is no real evidence that this was intended
### [[Andrews v Law Society of British Columbia]]
- Section 15(1) of the Charter deals with equality rights, and states that all individuals are equal before and under the law and are entitled to equal protection and benefit
- Specifically prohibits discrimination on several grounds, but is not limited to those grounds
- Additionally, s. 15(2) states that s. 15(1) doesn't exclude any law, program, or activity with the goal of benefitting disadvantaged groups based on the above protected grounds
- Allows the legislature to make distinctions based on these grounds, but they are still subject to the requirements of equality rights
- In this decision, the issue was whether someone who isn't a Canadian citizen to become a lawyer in BC violates s. 15(1)
- Argument was that it was discriminatory based on citizenship status without considering the applicants legal experience and ability
- This is analogous to discriminating based on national identity, which is a protected ground
- Arguably, non-citizens are by definition one of the most disadvantaged groups in Canada
- One of the famous aspects of this case is how it mentions that not every distinction is discriminatory, and assessing whether that distinction is justified falls under s. 1
- Raises the issue of what the relationship between s. 15(1) and s. 1, as well as what it means for a distinction to be discriminatory or not
