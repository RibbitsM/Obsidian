- More s. 2(a) claims, specifically relating to administrative decisions
### [[SL v Commission scolaire des Chenes]]
- Sincere belief in this case was that the claimants needed to pass their beliefs on to their children
- Making this a clear two-part test, need to identify the beliefs first, and then look to find interference with those beliefs
- Found that there was no interference with the sincere belief here, the program they were trying to get their kids exempted from only explained multiple religions in neutral terms
- Claimants argued that this would "confuse" their children, but the court found that this is not enough to constitute an infringement of s. 2(a)
- Children experience cognitive dissonance regularly, it's part of realizing that life outside your household exists
- Purpose of the ERC program was to foster an understanding of multiple important religions
- Court saw this as a prime example of the government fulfilling its duty of neutrality
### [[B (R) v Children's Aid Society]]
- Majority in this case said there was a violation of s. 2(a), justified under s. 1
- Decision on how to raise your children is a significant religious interest
- State's wardship order interfered with this practice and belief
- However, the protection of children is also a pressing and substantial state objective, and ultimately passes the *Oakes* test
- Recall that s. 2(a) rights are limited by other rights, and in this case it is interfering with the child's right to life under s. 7
- Allowing the parents to exercise their rights in this way also violates the s. 2(a) rights of the child by not letting them grow old enough to choose a faith
### [[Loyola High School v Quebec]]
- Recall that the *Dore* framework is the administrative equivalent of the *Oakes* test, it applies to administrative decisions instead of the law itself
- Main goal of the courts here is to balance the statutory objectives against Charter values
- In this case, the court was asking whether the ERC program's benefits were balanced against the protections of s. 2(a)
- Whole purpose of the school was to transmit Catholic beliefs
- By requiring the school to teach Catholicism from a neutral perspective, this neutralizes that purpose and created a violation of s. 2(a)
- Legislative objective on the other hand was to promote toleration of other religions
- Can argue that there was no violation because they would follow the instructions for all other religions, just not Catholicism
- Court found that requiring a religious school to present its own religion neutrally is ignoring religious freedom
- However, requiring them to teach about other religions neutrally does not interfere
- This was the main point of disagreement between the majority and the dissent
- Argued that this does not infringe it because it does not suppress Catholic beliefs, it makes sense to teach Catholicism from a Catholic perspective, but not Islam
- Dissent argues there is still an infringement here because it will inevitably raise questions on the validity of other religions which the school can't answer
- Can't be depicting all religions as equal because clearly if you truly believed this, you wouldn't be Catholic
- Is requiring them to depict other religions as equal to Catholicism a violation of religious freedoms? The dissent seems to say yes
- However, it's still possible to give an objective description of another religion without implying that they are equally credible as your religion
- Final decision is that the court found a violation here not saved under s. 1
### Distinguishing
- Recall that this is the same program that we were dealing with in *SL*, so what's the difference?
- In *Loyola*, the infringement is requiring the school to teach Catholicism from a neutral perspective, while in *SL* it's allowing your children to learn about Catholicism in the same way
- Content of the infringement is different, at least in terms of the level of coercion
- Forcing a religious community to teach their belief from a neutral perspective is state coercion, but teaching religious children their belief from a neutral perspective is not
- The parents in *SL* were free to homeschool their children if they wished to do so
### [[Law Society of BC v Trinity Western]]
- Trinity Western University (TWU) wanted to open a law school governed by a binding religious code of conduct called the Covenant
- This code included multiple religious laws, including prohibitions on extramarital sex and homosexual marriages
- LSBC rejected this proposal for the law school because of the Covenant
- Court found that it was a sincerely held belief that learning law in an environment governed by this religious code of conduct was spiritually valuable
- The LSBC decision infringed this belief by denying TWU students this environment
- Court found that this infringement was relatively minor
- First, the creation of a law school was not really required, and the only thing that the LSBC rejected was a law school with this specific version of the Covenant
- Is this effectively the courts passing judgement on the value of religious beliefs?
- Can maybe see this as the court going against [[Syndicat Northwest v Amselem]]
- Also said that LSBC would not be able to advance its statutory objective by approving the school, because it's objective is ensuring access to the legal profession
- Because the Covenant would exclude LGBTQ students, accepting it would go against the statutory objectives of the LSBC
### Dissenting Opinion
- Dissent argues that if the purpose of the LSBC is really that broad, this decision also undermines that purpose by denying diversity to some extent
- These groups are allowed to determine their own community practices, and we should respect that
- Infringement here was a restriction on the students from accessing the optimal learning environment for them
- Dissent focused mainly on the argument that the public interest requires diversity, and would be benefitted by allowing TWU to use the Covenant
- Allows them to operate freely without imposing the state's beliefs
- Is this distinguishable from *Loyola*
