### Recap
- Courts don't want to finish s. 15(1) before moving to 15(2) for symbolic reasons
- We want to exclude ameliorative legislation from questions of discrimination, s. 15(2) isn't being used to justify discrimination
- In an exam, if the issue clearly falls under s. 15(2), likely don't need to go any further but if it's debatable whether the law applies, write both arguments
- Overall, in these kinds of cases you're looking for some key factual element of discrimination, the cases in *Vriend* and *Eldridge* are particularly clear
- Want clear discrimination against a whole group
### Section 7
- Guarantees life, liberty, and security of the person and right to not be deprived of these rights unless in accordance with the [[Lecture 18 (Crim)|principles of fundamental justice]]
- Initial question is whether or not the state has deprived the claimant of a s. 7 right
- Need a sufficient causal connection between state action and a violation of s. 7 interests, established in a case called [[Canada AG v Bedford]] we'll cover later on
- Once this has been established, there are two main steps to cover
- First, need to identify which interest under s. 7 is at issue, and whether or not there has been an infringement of that interest
- Second, need to determine whether or not that infringement was done in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice
- Multiple interests can be engaged at once under s. 7
#### Life
- Engaged in cases like the death penalty, the state can't take your life away
- However, the death penalty was abolished before the Charter was even drafted, and this would more likely be handled as a case of cruel and unusual punishment
- Does apply in cases where people in Canada are at risk of being extradited to face an offence punishable by death
- Can also apply where claimants have been deprived of life-saving care or other crucial elements of survival like food and shelter
- Mostly appears where a claimant is denied access to medical care for one reason or another
#### [[B (R) v Children's Aid Society|Liberty]]
- Mainly engaged in cases of incarceration and confinement, example is [[Re Section 94(2) of the MVA]]
- Doesn't mean just detention for criminal reasons, also extends to involuntary committal in a hospital or immigration detention
- Majority of liberty cases deal with the criminal law for this reason
- Also applies to interference with "fundamental personal choices" like decision on how to raise a child, these are restrictions on liberty
- Ability to choose where you live and how to live your life are also fundamental choices
- Does not apply to freedom of contract, initial drafters of the Charter did not want it to be used to fuel market economics like in the US
- Overall, two main categories are incarceration and fundamental personal choices
#### Security of the Person
- Can be either physical security of the person, or psychological security of the person
- Engaged in cases where the state authorizes corporeal punishment, violates physical security
- These cases are usually pretty easy to establish, psychological aspect is harder
- Test seems to be that the claimant needs to be subjected to stresses exceeding the ordinary pressures and stresses of life
- Example of this was not providing parents with legal aid when the state seeks an order to remove custody of their child
- Not only a stressful experience, but also places social stigma on the parent(s)
- Extent of the psychological stress is assessed objectively, how would a reasonable person be affected?
- Has to be more than the ordinary pressures, but does not have to reach the level of a psychological illness
### [[BC Motor Vehicles]]
- Court held that the principles of fundamental justice are not limited to merely procedural matters like the right to a fair trial
- Issue in this case was that the law created an absolute liability offence, violating the FPJ that we should not punish the innocent
- The core principles of fundamental justice are prohibitions on arbitrariness, overbreadth, and gross disproportionality
	- Arbitrariness has the same meaning as in other aspects of constitutional law, requires some connection between goal and the results
	- Overbreadth ties in a bit to minimal impairment, goal is to limit the law to only the intended extent
	- Gross disproportionality is different from proportionality under s. 1, just means that the law should not have a disproportionate impact on s. 7 interests
- Also discrete, individual principles that will be identified on a case-by-case basis like in this case
- For a s. 7 analysis, you'll start with the big three and then draw comparisons and distinctions with the cases where other PFJs have been identified
- This case also set out the structure of a s. 7 claim
- First, claimant needs to show that the state action affected one of their protected s. 7 interests
- Second, claimant needs to show that the effect of that violation also went against a principle of fundamental justice
- If both of these elements are met, then their s. 7 rights have been violated and the court may attempt to justify the violation under s. 1
### Malmo-Levine
- This was a liberty case, accused claimed that criminalizing weed was a violation of his liberty
- Smoking weed was essential to his preferred lifestyle
- Court found that this is insufficient, not enough to qualify as a basic choice that is fundamental to personal identity
- Established a three-part test for determining new PFJs
- Recall that these principles come from the basic tenets of the legal system, not the realm of public policy
- Has to be more than just a common law rule, needs to be a consensus on the fact that this principle is core to our societal ideals of justice
- First, is the proposed principle a legal principle?
	- Examples of this would be like the best interest of the child, has to be recognized by law at some point to qualify
- Second, is their societal consensus that this is a fundamental principle?
	- Objective element, need to show that the principle is generally accepted by reasonable members of society
- Third, does it create a sufficiently precise and manageable standard?
	- This is a practical consideration, principle cannot be so vague that it can't produce clear results in application
### [[R v Morgentaler]]
- Issue in this case was a law preventing women from getting abortions for non-therapeutic reasons, could defend against this by proving she did it for health reasons
- However, it turned out that the criteria for this were very vague and varied greatly depending on the whims of doctor's committees
- Starting with CJ Dickson's reasons, he held that this law removed decision-making power from women, put them at physical risk, and caused psychological stress
- Effect is a violation of the security of the person
- Also created way too many hoops to jump through for a hospital to even be allowed to perform therapeutic abortions
- Created massive delays in these kinds of procedures, endangering the lives of women
- Raised a couple different of PFJs that are violated here, main one is that someone subject to liability cannot be offered an illusory defence
- While it appeared that women could defend them from a criminal charge by arguing the abortion was medically necessary, this was not true in practice
- Two options were to either go through the delayed official process and risk your health, or to get one anyway and risk criminal conviction
- Other PFJ at issue was arbitrariness, because of the massive variations in the system
- When it came to the s. 1 analysis, the rational connection failed because the goal of the legislation was to protect women, but it was actively harming them
#### Dissenting Opinion
- Argued that the only way to consider this a protected interest is that the Charter allows a right to an abortion, which it does not
- Only mention of an abortion in Canadian law is its prohibition under the *Criminal Code*
- If the legislature wanted to protect this right, they should have done it explicitly
- Similar to the argument made in [[BC Motor Vehicles]] as it focuses in on the actual intent and express language of the Charter
- Certainly we can do a lot of interpretation of the Charter, but where are the limits on that?
- Also points to the fact that the only clear violation of a s. 7 principle was the psychological harm, which McIntyre argued is not enough here
- Mere imposition of stress and strain by the government doesn't on it's own qualify
- Also raises the issue of whether or not the law would still violate s. 7 if it didn't create unreasonable delays in the official process
- Couple arguments you can make, it could still be a security of the person issue by not allowing the choice of an abortion, or a liberty issue for the same reason
- Can either argue that the issues flow from the delays, or from the removal of choice
