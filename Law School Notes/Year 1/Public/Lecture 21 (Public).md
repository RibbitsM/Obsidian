### [[RJR MacDonald Inc v Canada]]
- Court in earlier cases has shown a significant amount of scepticism about commercial speech, but this case shows them giving it a decent value when it comes to protection under 2(b)
- One of their main points is the broad swathe of areas that commercial speech applies to
- Pretty much any communication product like a book has a commercial motive
- Also, bans on advertising can also remove valuable information from consumers
- Not all advertising is purely persuasive, it can also be informative by providing comparisons between different brands
- Thus, commercial speech should not be considered a lower form of speech
- But is this an appropriate approach? Let's look at the example of gambling advertising
- We can argue that gambling addicts are a vulnerable population, like in [[Irwin Toy v Quebec (s. 2(b))]]
- Not really a case for conflicting social science evidence though considering that the literature on this topic is pretty unanimous on how it's harmful
- Because there's a reasonable apprehension of harm like in [[R v Butler]], this may be enough to justify deference to the legislature in this case
- Could also potentially argue that you're balancing competing interests between the public and companies/organizations that provide gambling products
- Shows that the value of the speech is not the only relevant factor for s. 1 deference
- Main issue in this case was that the prohibition was a total ban on advertising, which courts are generally hesitant to find would be minimally impairing
### [[R v Guignard]]
- This was the case where a guy was upset with his insurance company and set up a sign complaining about the insurance company
- Municipal bylaw prevented advertising outside of designated districts, city ordered Guignard to take down the sign and he argued this was a violation of s. 2(b)
- He was not doing commercial expression, he was exercising his right to express himself
- Court found that "counter-advertising" is not commercial speech, it's something that private citizens can and should be allowed to do
- Ban limits freedom of expression by forcing these people to resort to more expensive or less effective methods
- Overall ratio is that posting lawn signs expressing your opinion is protected speech, and is not considered a form of commercial expression
### Mapping Exercise
- First thing you need to consider is whether the act or communication is protected under s. 2(b), this is the first step of s. 2(b)
- Need to apply the test from *Irwin Toy*, protects anything that seeks to convey meaning, all of these activities are *prima facie* protected
	- This is a very broad definition, pretty much any act, physical or not, can convey meaning
- However, in some cases speech will not be protected because of the manner or location of the speech
	- We know that violence is not a protected manner of speech, the court states this in *Irwin Toy* and reinforces this several times
	- Also, *Montreal v Quebec Inc* sets out the test for determining what locations freedom of expression can be used in
		- Depends on the historical and actual function of the place, and whether allowing expression in that location goes against the purposes of s. 2(b)
- Finally, we have a couple of niche cases where we know that freedom of expression will be protected or not
	- For example, *Ontario Criminal Lawyers Association* held that s. 2(b) applies to government disclosure of documents as long as it's necessary and something else
	- Also, *Guignard* held that counter-advertising through lawn signs is protected
	- Compelling speech is not a protected limit from *MacDonald*
- Second step of the test after finding whether something falls under the scope of s. 2(b) is finding whether there is a restriction on that speech
	- This will depend on a number of factors, first is whether the law actually regulates the content of the speech, or the results of that speech (*Irwin Toy*)
	- Can think about this as regulation in purpose or effect
		- Example of regulation in purpose would be hate speech in [[R v Keegstra]], or in effect like in *Montreal v Quebec Inc*
		- If it's regulation in purpose, you can go straight to s. 1, otherwise if it's in effects the claimant needs to show that their speech upholds the principles
			- These purposes are mainly facilitating democracy, promoting truth, and protecting the freedom of individuals to self-actualize
- Next step is moving to section 1, which is the meat of s. 2(b)
- Start by considering whether we should give deference to the legislature
- First factor you need to consider is the value of the speech, is it high or low value?
	- We know hate speech and obscenity is low value from *Keegstra* and [[R v Butler]], and that political speech is high value from [[Harper v Canada AG]], and commercial speech is somewhere in the middle from *MacDonald*
	- This is mostly based on whether the speech advances the purposes of s. 2(b)
- Otherwise, we can look at the other factors from *Irwin Toy*
	- Vulnerable group? Competing social science evidence?
- Can also consider the strength of the limitation, is it a total ban or is it more specific?
### [[Schachter v Canada]]
- First case regarding remedies under s. 52
- Can declare a law immediately invalid, or we can wait a bit and defer the invalidity
- Also have access to a number of other remedies, this case deals with "reading down"
- This basically means interpreting a provision in the way that avoids the constitutional issue
- We also have remedies called "reading in" and severance
- Reading in means where the courts find a gap in the law, they will infer a term to solve the issue, a great example of this is [[Vriend v Alberta]]
- Severance is where the court only removes the problematic aspect of a law
- Main rationale for this is assuming that the legislature would have passed the law in the form that the court gives it after the remedies are performed
	- However, you can argue that the courts can't actually know this for sure
- Court will usually say that where the change added by the court affects the overall scheme, then maybe we should not do this
- Too far outside the legislative purpose, we should not be changing the law in this way
- Better to remove the law entirely than affect the legislative scheme
