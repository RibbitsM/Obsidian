### [[R v Keegstra]]
- Difference between majority and dissent exists in all parts of the *Oakes* test aside from the substantial and compelling objective
- Also a debate over how much we should defer to the legislature
#### Minimal Impairment
- Majority points to the restrictive elements of the offence, must be in a public form, needs to meet the standard of "hatred", and you need *mens rea* to convict
- Dissent argues that the definition of "hatred" is too broad, which can create a chilling effect where people self-censor for fear of being punished for hate speech
- Most of the debate between these two viewpoints boils down to disagreement over how hate speech legislation will play out in process
- Dissent says that criminalization will actually only encourage these harms, will also create a chilling effect which limits freedom of speech
	- Also extends to political speech, people might be too hesitant to speak their views on certain social issues for fear of conviction
- Majority talks more about how limiting hate speech is necessary to allow everyone to express themselves freely, and thus actually enables s. 2(b)
- Defer to the legislature slightly, do not require the absolute minimally impairing option, as long as the choice was reasonable, the infringement can be justified
- Example of how much of this analysis depends on issues of deference
#### Proportionality
- Court basically makes a summary of all the positive and negative points of both arguments
- Again, the legislature is given more deference because it is low value speech, majority found that the value of the objective was more important overall
- Dissent pointed more to how the provision is counterproductive, so in many ways the objective is not really achieved the way it was intended to be
### Summary
- Hate speech is expressive, so it is protected under s. 2(b)
- However, it is low value speech so the legislature gets a significant amount of deference under s. 1
- Majority argues that because hate speech undermines the foundations of freedom of expression, then limits on it are generally more acceptable
- It discourages people from engaging in public debates, and we shouldn't expect the victims of hate speech to be the main defenders against it
### [[R v Butler]]
- Main harms of porn listed by the court are an encouragement of the dehumanization and denigration of marginalized groups, especially women
- Can also be harmed by unintentional exposure to this kind of materials
- Still a form of expression, so it's protected under s. 2(b) regardless of the harms it causes
- Court also assigns pretty low value to porn as a form of expression, the only things they mention is that it could be self-fulfillment and is economically motivated
- Physical arousal is one of the lowest forms of self-fulfillment, so not a lot of weight given
- Since it does little to advance the purposes of freedom of expression, we can largely discard these kinds of speech
- Porn is defined as depictions of sex that are either violent or degrading and dehumanizing under the *Criminal Code*, very narrow definition
- Can argue that this definition introduces a standardized view of sexuality that can potentially be oppressing alternate forms of sexuality, this is the issue in *Little Sisters*
### Little Sisters
- Issue in this case was that deliveries for a queer-owned bookstore kept getting turned away at the border for being obscene material
- Court pointed out that the issue here was a misapplication of the law, the border agents were not using the same definition of obscenity that the Court used
- Claimants argued that you can't treat homosexual and queer porn in the same way as straight porn, pointed out that degrading practices are more accepted in this context?
- Also argued that the impact is not the same because the straight porn industry is so steeped in gendered violence, not the same on the other end
### Deference
- Why do we just argue that these forms of speech are not protected under s. 2(b)
- We are already effectively arguing this because the courts always significantly defer to the legislature when it comes to s. 1
- A big reason for this is the defence of artistic merit, an expressive work can generally always be justified if there is some artistic reason for the depictions of violence and degradation
- In *Butler*, the court also deferred to the legislature at the rational connection stage, as they could not draw a causative line between porn and real harm
### Shifting Purposes
- Recall that the court cannot use different justifications for the same law, this would be overbroad and remove the purpose of precedent
- However, the purposes for *Butler* have definitely shifted over time, historically most arguments regarding freedom of speech and porn were related to morality and decency
- Court argued that this was not a shift of the purpose, the purpose was always to prevent harm and the only thing that happened was that people's perception of harm changed
### R v Sharpe
- Case dealt with possession of CP without intention or circulate or publish
- Possession is also a form of protected expression, because it furthers the aim of self-fulfillment even if no effect is made on other people
- Ties into the discussion of remedies for s. 2(b)
