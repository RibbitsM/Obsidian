### Recap
- Can think of [[Mouvement Laique Quebecois v Saguenay]] as an example of the state breach of religious neutrality
- Other examples are cases like *Zylberberg* where religious practices were optional in schools, but the claimants argued this was not actually optional due to social pressures
- *Freitag* is another case like *Saguenay* where an optional prayer was used in a public context
- Seems like the court has consistently held that these kinds of optional public expressions of faith do violate s. 2(a) because they compel a public statement of faith
### [[Syndicat Northwest v Amselem]]
- According to the SCC, religion usually requires worship and a belief in a higher power
- It's a freely and deeply held personal conviction or belief that is linked to one's personal identity
- Court uses a very subjective definition, and also emphasizes the importance of religious activities creating a "connection with the divine"
- One utility of this definition is that it does not require claimants to prove their beliefs are commonly held throughout their religion
- Can provide expert evidence if they wish, but this is not necessary
- Also means that the courts do not have to be the adjudicators of what is and is not faith
### Analysis
- However, this definition does raise the issue of how we prove the sincerity of the belief
- Court gives two main options for this, you can examine the testimony of the accused, and you can also examine consistency with their other beliefs or practices
- Main problem here is how do we use this to weed out s. 2(a) claims made in bad faith
- Court does point out that there is no automatic protection for conduct that harms others in some way, but it's not made clear if this is part of s. 2(a) or s. 1
- In this case, that analysis was pushed to s. 1
- Issue for this case was whether a group of Orthodox Jews were allowed to build succahs on their balconies
- These are huts that orthodox Jews live in for 9 days during the annual festival of Succot, and was a violation of their building code which prevented constructions on balconies
- Building offered to let them make a communal succah in the building garden instead
- Court found that this was significant interference with their faith as the festival has to practiced joyously, and the communal succah would create undue inconvenience
- Because this would undermine the joyous character, it's a non-trivial interference
### Balancing
- Court needed to balance the religious freedom of the claimants against the rights of the other condo owners
- Found that the interference with the condo owners was pretty minor, biggest concern was uniformity of the aesthetic, but the huts are only up for 9 days of the year
- This isn't a significant issue, and will likely not affect the property value at all
- There was also no designated appearance for the succahs so there was no bar against requiring them to make best efforts to make it aesthetically compliant
- Also no evidence of major safety issues, could be built in a way that follows the fire code
### [[Alberta v Hutterian Brethren of Wilson Colony]]
- Used the same test from *Amselem*, need a sincere religious belief with a more than trivial or insubstantial interference
- Religious practice at issue here was not having your photograph taken, it's believed that this violates the second commandment against idols
- Created complications when Alberta reformed its drivers licensing system to require mandatory photographs for all license holders
- Previously, there was an exception made for religious objectors
- Argument from the claimants was that this created a difficult choice between following their religious beliefs and continuing their rural lifestyle
- Court said that a regulatory scheme can be reasonable as long as the minimal impairment falls within a range of reasonable alternatives
- Giving the legislature deference to resolve complicated issues
- Majority argued that the purpose of the legislation was to reduce fraud within the drivers licensing system to protect the integrity of the licensing system
- Because of this, it was more important to prevent a relatively small number of people from getting religious exemptions than addressing the number of unlicensed people
- Goal was to make a universal photo databank, meaning every licensed individual must have their own photo in the system
- Therefore, the current system was already minimally impairing because any solution that still meets the purpose needs a photo for every licensed driver
- Also found a rational connection on these grounds
### Proportionality
- Court makes a point that a law will seriously infringe a freedom if it requires those affected to make a choice between their religious beliefs and following the law
- However, if the law only imposes an increase in costs, not a serious infringement
- As long as there still is a meaningful choice, it's not that serious
- Found that in this case, the only effect was increased costs because the Hutterites could just hire people to drive them around instead of getting licenses
- On the other hand, the universal photo requirement has a pressing objective behind it and would have the beneficial effect of limiting fraud
- Overall, court found that this limit was justified
- Main ratio of this case was the distinction between meaningful and non-meaningful choices
### Dissent
- Argued that this was actually a full impairment on religious practice, and the test should fail on both minimal impairment and the balancing exercise
