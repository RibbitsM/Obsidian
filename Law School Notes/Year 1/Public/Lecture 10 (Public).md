### Recap
- Recall that the current test for s. 15 under [[R v Sharma]] requires two things
	- Direct or in effect distinction based on enumerated/analogous grounds
	- Burden imposed or benefit denied that reinforces disadvantage
- Second stage has the function of asking whether a distinction is discriminatory or not
- In *Ontario v G*, the protected group was those with mental disabilities, clear case of how the law discriminates against those who are found NCRMD
- By denying these people the opportunity offered to ordinary prisoners to have their names removed from watchlists, the government perpetuated myths about mental illness
### [[M v H]]
- Issue in this case was whether one member of a long-term same-sex relationship could qualify as a spouse under the *Family Law Act*
- Currently, the requirements to be a "spouse" was to be in a heterosexual relationship, that met a certain length requirement, and the relationship was conjugal
- Court found this to be a clear distinction on the face of the law, same-sex relationships could meet all of these requirements except the first one
- Held that it exacerbated the existing vulnerabilities of people in same-sex relationships by depriving them of the same rights that other couples enjoyed
- Also pretty clearly arbitrary, it distinguishes entirely based on sexual orientation
- Sexual orientation is an analogous ground, established in *Egan*
- Overall this was a clear case of a direct discrimination s. 15 case
### [[Vriend v Alberta]]
- Second time we've addressed this case, first time was talking about omissions
- Recall that this case dealt with the issue of whether the exclusion of sexual orientation as a protected ground under the Alberta *Individual Rights Protection Act*
- Creates two main distinctions here, first is that between heterosexual and non-heterosexual people
- Seems pretty clear that one of those groups would benefit much more from protection against discrimination based on sexual orientation compared to the other group
- This is an example of adverse effects discrimination
- However, the *IRPA* also discriminated directly, by extending protections to other grounds such as race and religion and not sexual orientation
### [[Eldridge But Again]]
- Recall that the distinction in this case was related to the decision of hospitals to not provide sign language interpretation services
- This is an example of adverse effects discrimination, as the decision does not directly discriminate against any group by doing this
- However, the court pointed to the fact that not providing these services for free would require deaf people to pay to get the same degree of communication everyone else gets for free
- Physical disability is also an enumerated ground, so there is no difficulty there
- Not an ancillary service, it's essential to the provision of healthcare for deaf people
- Adverse effects discrimination is important because it covers the most insidious cases of discrimination, where it comes from a failure to consider marginalized groups
- Disabled people as a whole suffer from a pervasive stereotype that they are ill or unfit, rather than their disadvantages just being a result of societal neglect
- Also face economic and social barriers in pretty much every aspect of life
- The goal of this legislation was to make free healthcare accessible to all, and what is happening here is that deaf people are being arbitrarily excluded from this
- Decision emphasized that where state action seeks to provide benefits, the state must ensure that those benefits are given without discrimination
- Reinforcing that this is not acceptable, reflecting [[Fraser v Canada (AG)]]
### [[Corbiere v Canada (Minister of Indian and Northern Affairs)|Analogous Grounds]]
- Two main arguments here, one over how we should identify analogous grounds, and the other over whether an analogous ground should always be recognized
- Also a side issue over whether the system of grounds works with [[Metaphysics of Gender|intersectionality]]
- Both the majority and the dissent identified immutability and historical disadvantage as two main factors for determining analogous grounds
- Additionally, a ground can either be actually immutable or constructively immutable
- Latter means that the characteristic can only be changed a great cost to your identity
- Great example for this is religion, government won't ask you to change that
- In this case, the court recognized "aboriginality-residence" as an analogous ground, meaning whether an Indigenous person lives on reserve or not
- Court found that residence on reserve was very important for Indigenous people, especially because of the different rights given to residents and non-residents
- Constructively immutable because it's essential to identity
- Other three analogous grounds are sexual orientation, citizenship, and marital status
- In *Miron*, where marital status was recognized, the court discussed how marital status is connected to patterns of disadvantage
- Also essential to personal identity, and cannot be easily changed
- Individuals have limited but not exclusive control over their marital status
- Raises the issue of whether or not being unhoused counts as an analogous ground
