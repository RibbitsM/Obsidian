### [[Application|Application of the Charter]]
- According to s. 32 of the Charter, the document applies to Parliament, the legislatures, and all matters controlled by those bodies
- This means the Charter applies to legislation and regulations, the executive branch, and public servants in all government departments
- These are the cases where it's pretty easy to see if the Charter applies
- Example of a clear exclusion would be in [[Application#RWDSU v Dolphin Delivery Ltd|Dolphin Delivery]], it doesn't apply to purely private interactions
- Section 32 is designed to apply to public action, so when it comes to a common law case where the state is not involved, that cannot be considered state action
- This is by design, if the Charter was extended to all private acts it would place an unreasonable burden on the courts, as well as private actors
- No clear line where it would start and end, surely it can't apply to absolutely everything
### McKinney v University of Guelph
- However, what happens when we get into entities that are controlled by the government to some extent, but may not be part of the government proper?
- To determine if the Charter applies to an entity like this, we need a test
- In this case, the Ontario universities were funded by the government, but they had the freedom to see how those funds were distributed
- Additionally, academic freedom is an important principle guiding the operations of universities, and giving the government too much control over them would limit that freedom
- However, this doesn't apply to all post-secondary institutions, as we have seen that the Charter does apply to Douglas College
- There, the entire board was appointed by the government, and the government could unilaterally modify the bylaws of the university
- Court stated that this is a degree of control that surpasses that seen for other entities like UBC
- While UBC can have all its funding taken away by the government, the day-to-day operations are still separate from the government, same is not true for Douglas College
- Can also apply this logic to [[Greater Vancouver Transportation Authority]], where it was found that BC Transit and Translink were also under governmental control
- These bodies are created and managed by statute, and were significantly controlled by the govt
### Godbout v Longueuil (City)
- Held that the Charter applies to municipalities because those bodies exercise what are essentially governmental functions
- They are democratically elected, have their powers inherited from the provinces including some duties the province normally exercises, and can levy taxes
- Some of these delegated powers are exclusively given to municipalities, meaning it's something the province would normally do but the municipality does instead
- Basically indistinguishable from a province except for the geographic scope and Constitutional status, the GTA is bigger than PEI and has a larger government
### Powers of Compulsion and Implementation
- Some government appointed bodies like law societies and arbitrators have something called "powers of compulsion"
- In *Eldridge*, a hospital was subjected to Charter scrutiny although we know hospitals are not routinely controlled by the government are not government-like entities
- Court held that if a non-governmental body is effectively implementing a government program, the Charter applies to the implementation of that program
- Requires a close connection between a policy and the action
- In this case, the hospital had discretion over what services it could provide for free
- Issue was that deaf patients weren't being provided with sign language interpreters
- Connection is because the legislature delegated to the hospitals the ability to choose what services are free and which are not, something the govt normally would do
- Court found that the hospital had to exercise this power in a manner consistent with the Charter, meaning they had to provide sign language interpreters
### Dolphin Delivery
- Basically just serves as an example of where the Charter does and does not apply to the common law
- Issue in this case was whether or not an injunction based on a common law case has to follow the Charter
- Since this isn't any sort of state action, because no statute was involved, the Charter does not apply even though a state actor (the court) issued the injunction
- This case is pretty confusing because technically the majority held that the judicial branch isn't bound by the Charter, which is not true
- There are certainly situations where the courts are subject to Charter scrutiny, there was a case where a BC court issued an injunction on picketers protesting the court itself
- Deemed to be a violation of the Charter, despite the decision here
- Therefore, if a state actor is relying on the common law, it's considered a state action, but if no state actor is involved, it's perfectly fine
### Recap
- We should be able to identify a couple categories when it comes to Charter application
- First, can tell where it clearly applies
- Second, can apply the test of sufficient governmental control
- Third, can define when an entity is exercising a "governmental function"
- Finally, if the two prior tests fail, can tell when an entity is implementing a government program
