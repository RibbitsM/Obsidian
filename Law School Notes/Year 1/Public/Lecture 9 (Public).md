### Recap
- If someone is treated a certain way because they are a member of a certain group, that's a **clear** example of discrimination
- However, this is not the only way that s. 15 can apply
- Central point of debate was over the relationship between s. 15 and s. 1
- Some say no elements of justification are required in s. 15 as that's the job of s. 1
- Today we will set out the new s. 15(1) test, and how it has been interpreted in various cases
### Current Test
- Drawn from *Sharma*
- Two steps, a distinction based on enumerated or analogous grounds either on its face or impact, and imposes a burden or denies benefit in a way that perpetuates disadvantage
- Court holds that not every distinction is discriminatory, so the second step is used by the court to figure out where a discrimination arises
- Most people agree on this broad framework for the test, becomes more contentious when it comes to actually applying it
- In *Ontario v G*, court found that legislation made a discrimination between those found NCRMD and those who were found guilty
- Former group did not have the possibility of exemptions from reporting requirements or the possibility of being removed from a sex offender registry
- SCC said that this was a clear distinction on the basis of a disability, and is discriminatory because it perpetuates prejudice against people with mental disabilities
### [[Fraser v Canada (AG)]]
- Issue revolved around a system in the RCMP where employees could join a job-sharing program where an employee effectively worked part-time
- Employees taking a temporary leave would lose some pension benefits, but when returning to work they would be able to "buy back" those benefits
- However, if they returned under the job sharing program, you couldn't do this
- Reason this was challenged under s. 15 was because the vast majority of people affected by this program were women
- They would take temporary leave for maternity, and then not be able to rejoin full-time afterwards because they could not find childcare services
- Between 2014-2020, 100% of the employees on the job share program were women, and almost all of those women were job sharing for childcare reasons
- Based on this information, Ms. Fraser argued the law had a distinction in impact
- When it came to the second step, the court pointed out that pension structures were historically designed to prioritize long-term middle-class male employees
- This law was effectively replicating that pattern, exacerbating an existing pattern of discrimination against women
- Used for evidence supporting the second step of the test
- When it came to the s. 1 justification, the Court held that the Attorney General had not provided any sort of pressing and substantial objective for this rule
- This is the only other way a rule can fail the first part of the *Oakes* test, other than the goal of the law itself being to infringe a Charter right
### Dissents
- Justice Cote argued that the distinction in the program was based on family status, whether or not the person had childcare responsibilities or not
- This is not a protected ground, and thus is not covered under s. 15
- Justices Brown and Rowe argued that there was no discrimination because any distinction here was caused by societal problems, not the legislature
- This is not something that changing the law here will fix
- Also argued that employers can decide how to allocate compensation based on the amount of work done by their employees
- True distinction was between part-time and full-time employees, which seems to be a perfectly normal way to distinguish between two groups
- Key part of the dissent's argument is that the majority decision would incentivize the government to not pass ameliorative legislation
- This is because this decision is essentially criticizing the government for not going far enough, the job sharing program was created to help employees with child care needs
- Also said that the majority was focusing too much on the historical disadvantage part because there was clearly no distinction on the first part
- It's not discriminatory to adjust compensation for hours worked, that's perfectly justified
### [[R v Sharma]]
- Court ultimately found that Sharma could not prove that the law was drawing a distinction here
- Main issue was that she relied too much on historical evidence of the disadvantages faced by Indigenous people, and had no evidence showing the law creates a distinction here
- Main differences between this case and *Fraser* were that the court held that a restriction placed on an ameliorative program is not automatically a distinction here
- Also put a lot of weight on the wording of the first step being changed to require a stronger form of causation compared to the original version
- Recall that there are two kinds of relevant evidence for the first step, evidence of the conditions faced by the claimant group, or the effect of the law in practice
- Majority in *Fraser* said that both kinds of evidence are not always required
- Implies that information on the claimant group alone is enough to establish the first step
- Major motivation for the dissent here, they're arguing that the majority is inserting a causation requirement that wasn't there before
- Recall that discrimination can be assessed by looking at the presence of stereotypes and prejudices attached to the protected group
- This was a major motivator in *Ontario v G*
- Can also look at arbitrariness, does the law make a distinction based on the actual circumstances of the person subject to the law?
- Recall that the claimant does not have to prove arbitrariness, the Crown has to prove that there is no arbitrariness
- Previously, these stereotypes and prejudices were also not required for a decision
- However, this decision explicitly made this a key part of the second step
### Disagreement
- A major point of contention is how important the governmental objective is when it comes to assessing s. 15(1)
- Is this part of establishing a *prima facie* infringement, or is it only part of the justification?
- Leads into the larger issue of where the dividing line is between s. 15 and s. 1
- In a side case called *R v CP*, the Court held that a lack of immediate right to appeal in youth courts was not discriminatory
- Rationale for this was because the *YCJA* was designed to already be ameliorative and was actively balancing several protected interests
- Can argue that this group is very broad, and within that group there are individuals who belong to groups that are especially vulnerable to miscarriages of justice
- Another major criticism of this judgement was that it was basically bringing s. 1 into the s. 15 analysis by holding there was no discrimination due to a legislative goal
### Summary
- Should be able to state the s. 15(1) test, identify tensions within the Court about the test, and distinguish the cases where the test was applied
