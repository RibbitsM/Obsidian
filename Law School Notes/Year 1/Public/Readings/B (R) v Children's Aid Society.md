- Defendant was attempting to establish that it can consent to a blood transfusion on the part of a child under its care
- Child's parents were both Jehovah's Witnesses and argued that allowing the defendant to do this would violate their right to give their child medical treatment in accordance with their religion
- Among other things, claimed that this was a violation of their right to liberty under s. 7
### Analysis
- Court notes that it seems like the right to care for a child and make fundamental decisions on its behalf falls under liberty
- Most contemporary statutes dealing with children focus on the best interest of the child but overall avoid significant intervention
- Tolerated only when absolutely necessary, showing that the ability of a parent to make choices for their children is a protected interest
- Does not mean that parents have absolute control or ownership of their children
- Children also benefit from the Charter, mainly from the life and security of the person elements
- Children cannot assert these rights themselves, so we assume their parents will use their freedom of choice in a manner that respects these rights
- Parents are allowed to make choices that limit the liberty of their children, but the state can intervene in cases where parental conduct is too far out of the norm
- Where the state prioritizes the rights of a child over their parents, there is a limit being placed on the parent's liberty rights
### Application
- In this case, the Ontario *Child Welfare Act* deprived the claimants of their right to determine the medical treatment of their child
- This is an infringement of their right to liberty under s. 7
- However, this deprivation was done in accordance with the fundamental principles of justice
- Procedure for granting wardship under the *Act* allows notice, a hearing before a judge, and places a heavy onus on the applicant to prove the need for wardship
- As such, there is no violation of s. 7 in this case
