- For constitutional law, the "constitution" includes basically anything that creates the system for governance of a society
- Usually the structures, procedures, and powers of the government
- Also relates to relationships between groups and the government
### Canada's Constitution
- Five main components: 
1. Parliamentary democracy
	- Ensures that statutes are created by elected bodies
	- Bodies give rights to executive actors like the Cabinet
2. Federalism
	- Determines how authority is divided geographically
	- In Canada we have the federal government and the provinces/territories
	- Parliament does general laws, provinces do local stuff
3. Individual/group rights
	- Includes right to democratic governances, mobility, free speech, etc.
4. Aboriginal rights
	- Undefined vague rights given to Aboriginal peoples
5. Principle of constitutionalism
	- Requires state power to be limited by overarching rules restricting what statutes can and cannot do
- This last bit is part of the overall principle of the rule of law, meant to ensure that power is not used arbitrarily
### Sources of Constitutional Law
- The Canadian Constitution is not a single document, but many smaller ones
- Common law is the most important sources, and is where things like Aboriginal law and parliamentary privilege come from
- Also how most other forms of constitutional law are defined
- Constitutional convention is the usual practices of elected officials, which are not legally enforces but societal and voter pressure tend to keep them consistent
- For example, the practice of electing the executive from the winning political party is not an enforceable rule, but is basically always followed
- Some British rules and declarations are constitutional law, like the 1763 *Royal Proclamation* and the *BNA Act*, which is now the 1867 *Constitution Act*
- All provinces are bound by this act, though not all to the same extent
- For example, Manitoba has special rules for land claims, PEI is guaranteed a ferry link, and the prairie provinces initially had different jurisdiction over resource use
- This act was also designed for when Canada was a colony, so it's missing a lot and also lacks any mechanism for modifying it
### Separation of Powers
- Sets out how different entities within the government use state power
- Usually three branches of government: legislative, executive, and judicial
- Canada's version is less strict than other countries like the US, and has an especially strong connection between the legislative and executive branches
- Additionally, Canada gives the executive branch an unusual degree of power over appointing the judiciary branch members
- Canada is also a constitutional monarchy, meaning that the ruler of England is technically the head of the country
- However, they always act on the "advice" of the executive branch meaning the prime minister is the effective head of state
- There was one example where this didn't happen in 1926, which is called the "King-Byng Affair"
- The legislative branch is called Parliament, which is made out of the representative of the Crown (governor general), the House of Commons (338 seats), and the Senate (105 seats)
- Parliament passes federal laws, and keeps the state funded by passing bills that are granted royal assent from the governor general
- The executive branch is primarily made up of the Cabinet, which consists of legislative branch members chosen by the prime minister
- These individuals are expected to obey the laws of the legislature, and respond to its requests and questions
- However, accountability between the branches is therefore mainly determined by party lines depending on whether the ruling party has a majority or minority government
- The judiciary branch consists of all courts in Canada set up in a hierarchy with the SCC at the top, and are responsible for different jurisdictions
- Provinces also operate under the separation of powers and have their own provincial legislatures and Crown representatives (lieutenant governors)
### Evolution of Canada's Constitution
- After Confederation in 1867, Canada gained the ability to modify existing imperial statutes and was free from any future such statutes
- Additionally, the SCC was reinforced as the highest court by removing appeals to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council which would overrule the SCC
- The *Constitution Act* was replaced in 1982, marking a significant change which implemented the *Charter of RIghts and Freedoms* and created Aboriginal law as we know it today
- Also modified the idea of legislative supremacy, but failed to address some of the issues prompting the reform like Quebec's desire for independent authority
- Quebec tried to be recognized as a distinct society within Canada during the Meech Lake Accords in 1987, but was ultimately rejected
- More reforms were proposed in 1992 as part of the Charlottetown Accord, which was also rejected in a referendum
- Most of constitutional law involves arguing over the meaning of different sources
- Can also come from disagreements on what the function of constitutional law is
- Primarily interpreted and enforced by the courts, which are not directly accountable to the public
- After 1982, Canada's Constitution is able to be amended, as long as Parliament and two-thirds of provincial legislatures representing at least 50% of the population agree
- This is established by Part V of the Act, and also requires approval of all legislatures for certain kinds of amendments under s. 41
- Really hard to change the constitution this way, usually done through judicial interpretation
- Over time, some constitutional change is expected as the values intended by its initial creators become more outdated
### Role of Lawyers
- Lawyers are the ones who initially draft the elements of the constitution, and are also the ones who determine how it is applied in the courts
- Examining constitutional law requires looking at a lot of court decisions, but remember that courts are not the only important institutions
- Finally, another field that often runs into constitutional issues is administrative law
- Since it's so closely related to how the government functions, it pops up pretty often
