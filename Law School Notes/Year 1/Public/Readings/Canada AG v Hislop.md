- Claimants in this case argued that because a law had been declared null, they are entitled to the full benefits of the law as they always should have been
- Issue for the court is whether declaring a law null has retroactive effect, and allows remedies for those who were deprived under the old regime
- Pretty sure this case is dealing with same-sex couples who wanted pension arrears issued after the decision in [[M v H]]
- Judicial decisions are generally retroactive, but not always
- In cases where a law was declared null, court will usually prefer prospective remedies
- Position of the claimant is based on Blackstone's idea that judges only discover the law is already exists
- Because of this, they should be entitled to arrears because if the true law had always been applied, they would have gotten money
- Court can follow this reasoning, if you are declaring the law as it has existed, can get retroactive relief, but this doesn't apply when new law is being developed
- To determine if a prospective remedy is appropriate or not, need to look at the nature and effect of the legal change that happened due to the declaration
### Changes in the Law
- Clearest examples if this is where the SCC overrules or repudiates an old decision either explicitly or implicitly
- Pretty clear that in these cases, we can grant prospective remedies
- Can also happen where the courts are giving content to existing rights that are broad or undefined, this is effectively creating new law
- Overall, a substantial change in the law is necessary to justify purely prospective remedies, but is not always sufficient
- Factors to consider here can be reasonable reliance by governments, fairness of limiting a retroactive remedy, and the potential effects of a retroactive remedy
- Fully retroactive remedies can potentially be very disruptive, as the government tends to rely on the law as it exists at the time when defining budgets and social programs
- Doctrine of qualified immunity also means that the government is not liable when legislation is invalid as a result of a judicial shift in the law
- Previous cases have stated that without bad faith conduct from the government, courts will generally not award damages for harms caused by enacting unconstitutional laws
- Laws must be given full force and effect as long as they are not invalid
- If ignorance of the law is not an excuse for individuals, governments cannot be excused for being ignorant of the Constitution
- Legislature relies heavily on the existing legal rules, and we should not hold them responsible for relying on that seemingly correct information
- Need to balance this reliance with the need to let the law evolve over time
### Fully Retroactive Remedies
- In *Miron*, the appellant was injured in an accident where the driver was uninsured
- Tried to claim for benefits based on their unmarried partner's insurance policy
- Barred because the policy only covered married couples, court later determined that you can't discriminate on the basis of marital status due to s. 15
- Court gave the appellant retroactive benefit of their partner's insurance policy
- Would not have been appropriate here to give only a prospective remedy
- Government did not show a substantial change in the law, as certain benefits had already been extended to unmarried people by other laws
- The vehicle insurance regime was just behind the times, fine to give retroactive remedy here
- Even if there was a substantial change in the law, there were other relevant factors
	- First, the legislature had amended the statute to include unmarried partners since the accident happened, so no interference with legislative objectives
	- Second, court found that a retroactive remedy was the fairest option here
	- Third, distinguishing on the basis of marital status would have been unreasonable even at the time the law was made
- Need to do all this analysis on a case-by-case basis
- Other example is *Kingstreet Investments v New Brunswick*
- In that case, the court held that taxpayers could recover taxes they had to pay based on a regulation that was deemed *ultra vires*
- This is pretty much the only restitution possible when it comes to cases where the government unconstitutionally collects taxes
- However, things get murkier when we're dealing with benefits-granting legislation
- More options in these cases, an excluded group can be lumped into the existing scheme, a new scheme can be created, or other benefits can be removed to compensate
### Appropriate Remedy
- Jurisprudence on equality of sexual orientation has shown a significant legal shift, the court should be able to consider prospective remedies
- Also seems to lean towards restricting retroactive remedies
- The decision in *M v H* was a significant departure considering that the court had historically upheld these kinds of distinctions in cases like *Egan*
- Supreme Court has the final word on the law, so conflicting decisions at lower courts don't count when considering if the law has shifted
- Since the court effectively overruled themselves, we can consider this a substantial change
- Also need to look at the other relevant factors for limiting retroactive relief
#### Reasonable Reliance
- Exclusion of same-sex partners under the *CPP* was based on the jurisprudence on s. 15 set by *Egan* and other similar cases
- Government reasonably relied on this information when drafting the CPP scheme
- This is not to excuse the government for being slow to adopt Charter rights
- As long as the reliance is reasonable, will reduce the chance of retroactive relief being the appropriate remedy for the situation
#### Good Faith
- Government did not act in bad faith here, and actually they tried to pre-emptively correct deficiencies under the *CPP* after the decision in *M v H* was made
#### Fairness to Litigants
- Rationale here is that the understanding we have of same-sex equality was the law back in 1985, which is pretty clearly not true
- The law evolves over time, and this is a great example of that
- Also, granting a prospective remedy in this case does not deprive the plaintiffs of the meaning behind their pursuit of the case
- This decision has had a significant impact, prompting sweeping legislative reforms across the country and a new legal respect for same-sex couples
#### Role of Parliament
- Claim in this case is effectively a claim for compensatory damages based on the underinclusiveness of the old *CPP* regime
- Imposing liability on the government in this situation without bad faith or unreasonable reliance is incorrect
- Retroactive remedy here would mess with the legislature too much
- Overall, retroactive relief is unavailable here
