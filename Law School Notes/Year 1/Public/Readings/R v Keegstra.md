- One of the primary justifications for free expression is the idea that freedom is crucial for promoting the free flow of ideas to facilitate democracy
- Means that within the protections for free speech, the most important kind of speech we should be protecting is political speech
- Idea is that we should protect the marketplace of ideas to make sure only the most valuable and most true succeed and reach into the democratic process
- However, there is not guarantee that true ideas will always succeed in this arena, this cannot be the only justification for free speech
- Don't need a utilitarian reason to protect it, the idea of free expression and the ability of the individual to form their own beliefs is valuable on its own
- But this approach is too broad, cannot be firmly enforced under the Charter
- True idea seems to be somewhere in the middle, or all of them at once
- Because of this complexity, we should not assign a single way to justify a limit on the freedom of speech, it will vary from case to case
- Depending on the facts, we can change what rationale for free speech we look at
## Section 2(b)
- Case dealt with a high school teacher from Alberta charged with hate propaganda for teaching his students that Jews were evil and faked the Holocaust to gain sympathy
- Section 319(2) of the criminal code criminalizes publicly communicating statements that wilfully promote hatred against any identifiable group
- This offence originally emerged from the *Universal Declaration of Human Rights*, Canada made a committee to study hate propaganda in Canada
- Main concern was how to limit the spread of hate propaganda without infringing on s. 2(b)
- First step of the [[Irwin Toy v Quebec (s. 2(b))]] test is met because these communications are clearly intended to convey a meaning, and do convey that meaning
- For the second step, s. 319(2) clearly limits the content of communications, and thus it's clear that it infringes s. 2(b)
- Court held that hate speech is not a form of violence, and thus is not excepted from s. 2(b)
### Justification
- Stated that we should not be applying s. 1 too rigidly
- Also pointed out that US constitutional doctrine is irrelevant here, but we can look at Canada's international law commitments to support the analysis
- Interpretation of s. 2(b) is *Irwin Toy* was very broad, and argued that content should be irrelevant to determining if communication is protected
- However, s. 1 has to deal with the content of expression out of necessity
- Argued that the expression banned under s. 319(2) does not exactly match with the rationale for protecting free expression
- Very little chance that statements intended to promote hatred are true or would lead to a better world if adopted
- Arguing that these kinds of statements are important for the pursuit of truth or political development is pretty misguided
- True that s. 319(2) might limit self-fulfillment by developing and articulating thoughts and ideas
- However, a big part of self-fulfillment comes from holding an identity derived from membership of a group, which speech criminalized under s. 319(2) opposes
- Finally, the limitation of participation of a few individuals in the democratic process based on their language is overall not too substantial
- Hate propaganda is not what we would generally call political speech, its goals are opposed to the democratic aspirations of equal treatment
#### Substantial and Compelling Objective
- Refereed back to the hate speech committee, pointing out that hate propaganda is prevalent enough in Canada to warrant concern
- Causes two main kinds of injuries
	- First, harm is done to the members of the target group, usually through psychological damage or social consequences
	- Second, the dissemination of hate propaganda has a negative impact on society as a whole by attracting individuals to adopt these views
- Looked at international human rights instruments to determine the strength of the objective, as well as the other parts of the Charter like s. 15
- Found that these is certainly a substantial and compelling objective
#### Rational Connection
- Three main reasons why s. 319(2) may not have a rational connection with Parliament's goal
	- First, it might actually promote the cause of people spreading hate propaganda
		- People accused of spreading hatred are often seen as martyrs and will generate sympathy from other members of their community
	- Second, the public generally views government restrictions on expression with suspicion, granting a degree of truth to the propaganda being spread
	- Third, Germany used to have similar hate propaganda laws to Canada's and we all know how that turned out for them
- Court mostly rejected these reasons, can't argue there is no strong connection between suppressing hate propaganda and criminalizing it
- Section 319(2) cases have drawn a lot of media attention in the past, but it's mostly just shown that hate propaganda is harmful to society
- Pretty doubtful that most Canadians will have sympathy for these people or their ideals
- Porn is not dignified for being suppressed by the government, same goes for defamation and other prohibited speech
- Also no one is arguing that hate propaganda laws on their own can prevent the rise of an ideology like the Nazis
- Seems that this step is clearly met
#### Minimal Impairment
- Argument is that the legislation is overbroad and too vague, and will lead to the punishment of expression that is not hate propaganda
- In practice, it will be used to just limit the expression of unpopular communications
- May also have a chilling effect on public discussion by leading people to censor themselves
- Some of these concerns are unfounded because s, 319(2) only applies to public statements, and requires subjective intent or recklessness for promoting hatred
- Can argue that the problem is that the offence doesn't require proof of any actual hatred resulting from the impugned communications
- However, this would ignore the psychological trauma that be caused to the groups targeted by hate propaganda, even where no hate was actually promoted
- Seems that the term "hatred" here means intense emotion associated with vilification and detestation, based on insensitivity, bigotry, and destruction of the target group
- Overall, the terms of the offence create some pretty clear limits that will only capture activity that is hostile to Parliament's objective of suppressing hate
- Did point to a couple incidents where s. 319(2) and similar customs legislation have been applied too broadly in the past
- However, the court argues that these were misapplications of s. 319(2) and the reasons in this case will hopefully clarify how it should be applied
- These actions were basically illegal police harassment, and should be considered to weigh negatively on s. 319(2) and Parliament
##### Alternatives
- One of the strongest arguments is that discriminatory ideas are best met with information and education, not criminal penalties
- Human rights states are also considered less severe and more effective than criminal law
- This is because in those cases, the accused is encouraged to cooperate with tribunals to mend their conduct instead of simply being punished
- While it is true that s. 319(2) is fairly limited in its ability to actually advance the goals of having equality and tolerance in Canada, it's not expected to do that alone
- There are a variety of measures we can use, and there's no need to choose just one
- Recall that where there are a number of options available to further an objective, the government can use a more restrictive measure as long as it is not redundant
### Conclusion
- Court also held that the objective is important enough to outweigh any potential limitations placed on freedom of expression
- Infringement was saved under s. 1 and s. 319(2) was upheld
### Dissenting Opinion
- Points out the real harms created by hate propaganda, and how it goes against many valued principles of Canadian society like tolerance and equality
- Also argued that this is a universal problem not limited to just Canada
- Can threaten social stability and is intrinsically offensive to the majority of people who believe in equality of all people regardless of race or creed
- Different jurisdictions have taken different approaches to this issue
- American law requires clear and present danger before free speech can be overridden, while the international approach sees free expression as less important than suppressing hatred
- Charter seems to follow the American approach, freedom of expression is broad and fundamental, need to balance that right with others under s. 1
### Justification
- Quickly found that s. 319(2) infringes freedom of expression, and also easily concluded that protecting social harmony and individual dignity is a compelling and substantial goal
#### Rational Connection
- Pretty clear that the legislation does further Parliament's objectives to some extent
- Also accepts that prosecuting these people may affirm their values
- Need to look at the actual effect of the legislation to find a rational connection
- Prosecutions for racist expression have been covered extensively, and grant incredible amounts of publicity and sympathy to the accused
- Arguing that we should prosecute hate speech to reduce racism means there is a risk that otherwise, many people would hear that speech and believe it
- Means that it's probably just as likely that they would believe these statements are true because the government is trying to suppress it
- These kinds of hate propaganda laws have also been notoriously ineffective in the past
- In pre-Hitler Germany, Nazis often explicitly used criminal prosecutions to spread their messaging, taking advantage of these anti-hate laws to drive anti-state sentiment
- Can argue that the rational connection is actually pretty tenuous here
- At the very least, there doesn't seem to be a strong and evident connection
#### Minimal Impairment
- Can also make a pretty strong case that s. 319(2) is overbroad
- Majority argued that the "wilful promotion" requirement rules out statements made for honest purposes like saying something you believe to be true or contributing to a debate
- However, these things are not always mutually exclusive
- Entirely possible that something might be true, important to social and political debate, and also be intended to promote hatred of an identifiable group
- Creates the possibility of people being convicted when their statements were made mostly for non-nefarious reasons
- Real answer to whether this is overbroad comes from its track record
- There have been several examples where unpopular viewpoints that can be construed as hateful statements against groups have been criminalized
- Examples are a ban of a pro-Zionist novel, barriers on the import of a Salman Rushdie book, and similar issues with importing a movie about Nelson Mandela, even arrests for distributing pamphlets that say "Yankees Go Home"
- Also raises concerns regarding the potential chilling effect, as the more vague the provision is and the more broadly it's applied, the more people will censor themselves
- May lead writers to avoid creating potentially controversial characters, scientists from publishing research on ethnic groups, and may even affect political debate
- Criminalization may be an excessive response to the problem of hate propaganda, as the additional penalties may not be deterring to those who want publicity
- Human rights legislation might be a better alternative
#### Proportionality
- Infringement of the freedom of expression is a serious issue, and s. 319(2) seems to reach views in many different domains due to its broad application
- Consequences of breaking these prohibitions, even where it is not intended, can be serious
- The objectives at play here are certainly valuable, but the claims of what is actually gained are pretty dubious
- Any questionable benefit should be outweighed by a broad and significant infringement on the freedom of expression
- Overall, the limit created by s. 319(2) cannot be justified
