- Case involved a challenge to regulations preventing commercial advertisement directed at children under 13
- Determined this by looking at the nature and intended purpose of the good, manner of advertising, and the time and place the ad was shown
- Claimant in this case claimed that this was a violation of s. 2(b) among other things
### Was the Plaintiff's Activity Protected by s. 2(b)
- Clear that not all activities are protected by freedom of expression, need to determine if advertising is protected speech in the first place
- Activity is expressive if it attempts to convey meaning, which is found in the content of the communication
- Cannot exclude human activity from free expression simply due to the content, any activity that conveys a meaning is *prima facie* protected
- Can be through almost any form of expression including the written and spoken word, the arts, and physical gestures or acts
- However, violence is not considered a protected form of expression
- In this case, advertising aimed at children certainly does aim to convey a meaning and contains expressive content, so it qualifies
### Purpose of the Government Action
- Main question here was whether the government action sought to limit freedom of expression
- Can argue that almost all human activity is expressive, and thus all government actions seek to restrict expression in some way
- On the other hand, the government can always claim their purpose was some policy objective, not limiting the freedom of expression
- Generally speaking, wherever the government is singling out specific meanings that cannot be conveyed, that's a limit on s. 2(b)
- Where they are only trying to control the consequences of human activity, this generally isn't a limit on expression
- For example, a rule against handing out pamphlets is a restriction of expression and is tied to the content of the pamphlets even if the goal seems to be avoiding litter
- However, a rule that just restricts littering is not "tied to content"
- Overall, we should be looking if the thing the government is trying to restrict comes from the meaning of the activity or its influence on others, or the direct physical results of the activity
#### Effects
- Court can also look to see if the effect of the action limits free expression
- Burden is on the plaintiff to prove this, need to make the claim in accordance with the principles underlying free expression
	- First principle is that seeking the truth is an inherently good activity
	- Second is that we should encourage participation in social and political decision-making
	- Third is that diversity in forms of self-fulfillment and flourishing should be encouraged
- Plaintiff needs to show that their activity promotes at least one of these principles
- If they can do this, may be able to show infringement even where govt purpose is neutral
- Court found that s. 2(b) applied, the govt purpose was to limit certain content to protect children, this is a clear limitation of free expression
### Justification Under s. 1
- Question is whether these laws are justified on the basis that children under 13 can't distinguish advertising from traditional communication
#### Pressing and Substantial Objective
- Main concern here is protecting children, a group that is especially vulnerable to advertising tactics of manipulation
- Argue that children cannot easily distinguish between reality and fiction and cannot understand the persuasive intent of advertising
- Relied mainly on a US report made by the FTC that showed these facts
- Dealt with younger children and tv ads specifically, but the findings can be generalized to older kids and other forms of advertising
- Mostly deferred to the legislature on this issue, it's a complicated line-drawing exercise based on conflicting social science evidence, their specialty
- Sufficient evidence that 13 and younger is a fair line, and they won't contest that
#### Minimal Impairment
- Court mainly glossed over the rational connection test, it was easily met
- Referred back to the concern raised in [[R v Edwards Books and Art]] that the Charter can become a tool for powerful groups if we don't focus on protecting the vulnerable
- This is an example of a case where vulnerable groups seek the protection of the government where others say they should not intrude
- Question is whether the government had a reasonable basis for deciding that a ban on children's advertising impaired s. 2(b) as little as possible given the objective
- Referred back to the FTC report as evidence, which determined that the best solution was a ban on kids advertising (which is impossible because of the First Amendment lol)
- Also pointed to the fact that the Quebec laws only banned kids advertising during times where children make up most of the audience
- Respondent argued that self-regulation measures would be more effective and less impairing
- Overall, the court found that the decision made by the Quebec govt was reasonable
- This ban was actually less strict that those found in other democratic nations, and there was solid evidence supporting the need for a full ban, not just self regulation
#### Proportionality
- Did not find any evidence that the effects of the ban were bad enough to outweigh the objective, advertisers are free to target older age groups
- Can also do educational advertising instead if they wish
- Only real concern is that some advertisers will have their revenues cut
- Overall, the impugned provisions were upheld under s. 1
### Dissenting Opinion
- Disagreed that the laws could be saved under s. 1
- Did not believe that the welfare of children was at risk due to being advertised to
- Children grow up, and we only have solid evidence that 2-6 year olds can't distinguish reality from fiction
- Suggests that the main concern is from irritated parents, children are not really harmed
- Argued that the freedom of expression is too important to be set aside here
- The majority of limits on freedom of expression are justified on the grounds that it is in the best interests of the people having their rights limited
- This was why the church limited the spread of reading scriptures not in Latin, and a big reason why women were excluded from education
- In the absence of any evidence that children are actually harmed by advertising, we should not be considering this a justified infringement
- Freedom of expression should not be suppressed except where urgent and compelling reasons exits showing why it's necessary to protect the community
