### Introduction
- SCC previously held that the Charter only applies to government action, meaning it doesn't apply to private actors or to litigation between private parties
- Based this decision on s. 32(1) of the Charter which states that the Charter applies to Parliament and all matters within its authority, as well as the legislature of the provinces and its matters
- Debate is over whether this provision was meant to restrict the Charter to public actors or if it only intended to specify that all government bodies are bound by it
- Couple arguments for this interpretation, for example the state is the biggest threat to individual liberty and courts would be overwhelmed otherwise
- However, this raises the issue of what the divide between state and private actions is
- There are two main issues with this
	- Private actors can only act in the ways that the state permit them, when does a state grant or delegation make an entity a government actor?
		- Its previously been established that universities and hospitals are not government actors, but if that actor is implementing a specific government program, those actions can be considered government action under the Charter
		- Raises the issue of what level of delegation makes something government action
		- In the US, they test whether the actions of the party being delegated to are performing a public function
		- For Canada, the test is whether the institution is effectively under the direction of the government, or in other words how much autonomy it has from the govt
	- Where private power depends on state authority, if the state was implicated in a private wrong, does the Charter apply?
		- Example would be the state enforcing a private agreement to ban the sale of property to members of a certain religious or racial group
- First thing to address when looking at the application of the Charter is to look at which entities are considered to be part of the government in Canada
- We know that the executive branch is definitely part of the government, but outside of that we use two main determining factors
	- Does the government exercise significant control over this entity?
	- Does the entity exercise governmental functions?
### McKinney v University of Guelph
- Some faculty and librarians from the university challenged the mandatory retirement policies of Guelph and multiple other Ontario universities based on age discrimination
- Preliminary issue was whether universities are considered governmental actors
- Majority of the court concluded that they are not, and their use of mandatory retirement policies didn't turn out to be government action
#### Analysis
- States that the exclusion of private activity from the Charter was an intentional choice that should be respected
- Argues that the government is the body that is most able to infringe freedoms, and can regulate other actors that do so
- Also points out that allowing judicial review of private actions would put too much of a burden on the courts
- It's true that a government body would not be able to create a mandatory retirement statute
- Appellants relied on a textbook that says that universities are government institutions, but although they were created by statute, these universities are acting to facilitate choices of private individuals
- Clear that universities can freely negotiate contracts and collective bargains with their employees, and did this entirely on their own initiative
- Canadian universities are closely related to the government, and are limited by it in several important ways, namely reliance on government funding
- However, these entities have their own governing bodies which act in the interests of the university itself, not the government
- Government has no power to control the universities directly
- If they tried, they would almost certainly face major backlash for attempting to violate academic freedom
- Concluded that universities aren't part of the government and their actions aren't subject to the Charter
- Needs to be routine or regular governmental control of the entity to be considered part of the government
- Appeal dismissed
### Godbout v Longueuil (City)
- Even a non-governmental entity fails the routine or regular control test, can still be regulated by the Charter if the entity is exercising a governmental function
- In this case, the city adopted a resolution requiring new permanent employees of the city to live within city limits
- Respondent signed an agreement promising to live in the city, and was fired when she moved to a neighbouring city
- SCC held that the city's requirement violated s. 5 of the Quebec Charter, but also addressed whether it violates the federal Charter
#### Analysis
- Previously held that a provincial Law Society was subject to the charter because it was performing a governmental function
- Makes sense, if we didn't apply the Charter to these entities, then the government could avoid the Charter by delegating duties that violate it to non-governmental third parties
- Test from *McKinney* is insufficient to catch these cases
- Municipalities are governmental entities because they are democratically elected in a similar fashion to Parliament and the legislatures
- Additionally, they have powers of taxation generally indistinguishable from those of Parliament or the provinces
- Finally, they can create and enforce laws in their jurisdiction
- Basically what a municipality does is receive its powers from the province, and exercise a mini provincial government
- Since the entity receives its power from statute and is performing acts that are "governmental" in nature, they're subject to the Charter
- This decision was the minority opinion, majority didn't address the Charter issue at all
### Eldridge v British Columbia (AG)
- Three people born deaf wanted a declaration that failure to provide funding for sign language interpreters for medical services violates the Charter
- Under the *Medical and Health Care Services Act*, the Medical Services Commission decides what services are "medically required" and are considered a benefit
- For the *Hospital Insurance Act*, hospitals could decide what services are free
- Commission and hospitals didn't offer interpreters as an insured service
### Analysis
- First issue is finding the alleged violations of s. 15(1) of the Charter
- Does the breach come from the legislation itself, or from the decisions of authorities empowered by the legislation
- Court found that the source was the actions of the hospitals and the Medical Services Commission
- Second issue is determining whether the Charter applies to these entities
- Legislatures can't enact laws that violate the Charter, but they can give authority to non-Charter bodies
- Recall that this can be established by finding that the entity is implementing a certain government policy or program, or is otherwise governmental in nature
- Can also be established by determining the level of government control exercised over the entity
- In this case, we know that hospitals are not considered part of the government because of the decision in *Stoffman*
	- This was another retirement age case regarding VGH
- In that case, hospitals in the province had a variety of retirement policies, but in this case it's different
- The *Hospital Insurance Act* is designed to give services to the public, and those services are determined by the government
- Basically uses hospitals as a vehicle for delivering a range of medical services paid for by the government
- Based on the private insurance model, but is more like a government service
- For the Medical Services Commission, the government was basically delegating their power to determine what's medically required
- Thus, the Commission was acting on the behalf of the government is also subject to the charter
- Held that failure to fund sign language interpretation violates equality rights under the Charter
### Statutory Powers of Compulsion
- Charter applies to non-government actors exercising statutory powers that are considered coercive
- Recognized in *Eldridge*
- This is because any entity that derives all its powers from statute in unable to make an order that violates the Charter
- This is because the power to infringe the Charter cannot be granted by legislation, since that legislation has to comply with the Charter
### RWDSU v Dolphin Delivery Ltd
- SCC stated in this case that courts are not considered part of the government when it comes to s. 32(1)
- This lowkey makes no sense because that would mean a bunch of the Charter provisions regarding criminal law are pointless
- However, this case does kind of establish that the Charter doesn't apply to the common law when litigation is happening between private parties
- Issue in this case was whether a court order preventing a union (RWDSU) from picketing at the respondent company's premises
- Union was in a dispute with Purolator and wanted to picket at Dolphin because they were scabbing for Purolator
- *Canada Labour Code* doesn't deal with this kind of secondary picketing, so the common law had to decide it
- BC court issued an injunction, and the union appealed
- Majority held that Dolphin was not actually related to Purolator, and the injunction was reasonable limit on the right to picket (s. 2(b))
- Also decided on the issue of whether the Charter applies to the common law
- Said that yes it does, but it doesn't apply when it comes to litigation between private parties
