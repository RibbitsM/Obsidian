- Case deals with the extent of remedial jurisdiction under s. 24(1)
- Court found that s. 24(1) is not strictly limited by statute or common law, but court can look to statutes and common law to determine what remedies to choose
### Appropriate and Just in the Circumstances
- Seems that this indicates that the remedy under s. 24(1) should be one that the court considers to be appropriate and just given the circumstances
- This calls for an exercise of discretion from the judge based on their perception of the nature of the right, the infringement, the facts, and relevant legal principles
- We should seek to keep this discretion, and therefore it would be inappropriate to set a strict definition of what is "appropriate and just"
- Generally, we can say that this will be a remedy that meaningfully vindicates the rights and freedoms of the claimants
	- Should be relevant to the experience of the claimant and address the circumstances that caused the violation or denial of the right
	- An ineffective remedy would be one smothered by procedural issues that does not meaningfully vindicate these rights
- Can also say that a good remedy will employ legitimate constitutional means
	- Means the court should not be departing from its role and stepping on the legislature's toes when it comes to individual remedies
- Capacities of the courts can be inferred from the tasks that they are normally charged with and have procedures and precedent for them
	- Should not go beyond these capacities when granting remedies
- Next, a good remedy should fully vindicate the claimant, but should also be fair to the person the order was made against
	- Should not impose undue hardship unrelated to securing rights
- Finally, need to remember that s. 24 should be able to evolve given that it's part of the flexible framework for applying and enforcing the *Charter*
### Application
- Trial judge appropriately used their discretion to choose an effective remedy
- Given the serious rates of assimilation and historical delays in providing French education in the area, it was prudent to take steps to avoid further delays
- Without any reporting hearings, the appellants would have to make a new claim in response to any further delays in the process
- Trial judge also could have just given a declaration, like the court did in *Mahe v Alberta*
- Assumption when issuing a declaration is that the governments will quickly and completely addressed the issues raised in the declaration
- Evidence in this case showed that the government understood its obligations but had consistently refused to comply with them
- Main issue was not a lack of recognition, but a lack of priority
- Created a significant risk that a declaration would be ineffective
- Shows that where governments have failed to comply with obligations they already understood, the courts will be justified in not issuing a declaration
- Court of Appeal dismissed this issue by saying that the claimants could deal with any further delays by making an in contempt proceeding against the Crown
- Debatable over whether this is actually more respectful to the executive branch than just setting up some reporting hearings
- When we consider all the relevant circumstances, becomes clear that the trial judge chose a remedy that reduced the risks that minority language rights would be smothered
### Respect for Democratic Framework
- Remedy granted in this case did not depart from the role of the courts
- They did consider the progress made by the government, and the order only required "best efforts" to account for unforeseen difficulties
- Court of Appeal argued that issuing an injunction against the government may not be constitutional
- However, we already know that courts can issue injunctions under s. 24(1) and there should be no reason why this doesn't also let them issue injunctions against the executive branch
- Pretty hard to enforce Charter rights if the courts can't do this
### Functions and Powers of a Court
- The reporting order wasn't super common, but does call on known powers
- Courts can order remedies that keep them involved in the relations between the parties
- Superior courts have all the powers of common law and equity courts, which includes remedies like preserving evidence and managing parties assets before a trial
- Courts can also supervise complex processes like commercial transactions in bankruptcy cases
- Difficulties of supervision has sometimes been raised as reasons to not give [[Lecture 46 (Contracts)|specific performance]] orders, but courts of equity accept these issues when truly needed
- Academic commentators have also noted that some degree of ongoing supervision is consistent with past practices of the courts
- Only change here is applying this flexibility to protecting constitutional rights
- Order here was consistent with the judicial function, court did not offer to manage the construction projects or anything, just to hear evidence and examine progress reports
### Vindicated Right by Fair Means
- Respondent argued that the remedy was too vague, but there seems to be no evidence of this
- Would have been wise to give more guidance to parties as to what the reporting sessions would consist of, but overall is pretty clear
- The order clearly indicates that the government is only there to report
- This is also a fairly original remedy, and while future judges will be held to a higher standard, this was within the scope of their discretion
- Trial judge did not have to choose the single best remedy, it's enough that this remedy was appropriate and just given the circumstances
### Conclusion
- Superior courts can craft any remedy it considers to be appropriate and just given the circumstances, as long as they follow the principles mentioned earlier here
- This remedy was just and appropriate, court of appeal erred by interfering with and striking down the part where the court has jurisdiction to hear progress reports
## Dissent
- Argue that we should not be ignoring the need to create understandable orders when trying to enforce constitutional rights
- Judicial intervention should end where the case is brought to a close
- Drafting of the reporting order was flawed because it failed to give the parties clear notice of their obligations, and the nature and purpose of the reports and hearings
- Because non-compliance with injunctions can lead to severe punishments, it's important that all the parties involved know what's going to happen
- Reporting order was very short, and lacked many crucial details
- Unclear how the reports should be delivered, same with the level of detail needed
- Parties only learned shortly before the hearings that they needed to file affidavits
- Trial judge also seemed unsure about what the process would involve, evidenced by their quotes from the hearings
- This was supposed to be the final order, but they suggested several times that they could make further orders to the aprties
- The schools were ultimately built/renovated, but it's unclear if these meetings actually helped or if they were just confusing to all the parties involved
