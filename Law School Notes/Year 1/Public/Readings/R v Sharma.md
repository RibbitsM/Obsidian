- Conditional sentences are used to allow offenders to serve a sentence in the community instead of in jail
- In 2012, Parliament made some offence ineligible for a conditional sentence
- In this case, the accused brought almost 2 kg of cocaine into Canada, plead guilty to importing a schedule 1 substance and asked for a conditional sentence
- However, she was ineligible because the relevant offence (s. 6(1) of the *Controlled Drugs and Substances Act*) carries a max term of 10 years, the cutoff for drug offences
- Normally, the cutoff for a conditional sentence is 14 years
- Argued this violated ss. 7 and 15(1) of the Charter, dismissed at trial, on appeal the majority found a violation for both, Crown appealed
- Sharma is of Ojibwa ancestry, confessed her partner paid her to smuggle the coke and she agreed as she was facing eviction, had no prior criminal record
- Judge ordered a *Gladue* report which revealed that Sharma was a single mother at 17 (now 20) and was worried her child would become homeless if she didn't comply
- Her grandmother was also a residential school survivor, her mom was in the foster system, and herself had been sexually assaulted and dropped out of school
### Law
- Conditional sentences are allowed under s. 743.1 of the Code, initially created in 1996 a few years before *Gladue* reformed the sentencing provisions
- Formerly, you weren't eligible for a conditional sentence for four reasons
	- Offence punishable by minimum term
	- Court would impose a term of over 2 years
	- Imposing a conditional sentence would endanger the community
	- Imposing a conditional sentence would violate sentencing principles
-  Later on, conditional sentences were disallowed for specific crimes under s. 752.01
- Current version took effect in 2012 with the *Safe Streets and Communities Act* and applies if the court imposes a sentence of less than two years and these requirements are met::
	- Sentence would not endanger safety of community and is consistent with principles
	- Offence does not have a minimum term
	- Offence isn't an indictable offence with 14 years max term (742.1(c))
	- Offence isn't an indictable terrorism or crim organization offence with max term 10
	- Not indictable offence with max term 10 that resulted in bodily harm, involved import, export, trafficking, or drug production, or involved the use of a weapon (drug one is 742.1(e)(ii))
	- Not an indictable offence in one of several provisions
- Overall the main requirements for a condition sentence are not being convicted of an excluded offence, punishment of less than two years, and safety of community is not endangered
- Whenever these prereqs are met, the court has to consider a conditional sentence
### History
- Barriers for Sharma were a mandatory min sentence of two years, and the two parts of the conditional sentencing regime blocking her request
- At trial, sentencing judge held that the mandatory min sentence was grossly disproportionate and imposed an 18 month sentence instead
- However, he rejected her challenge of the conditional sentence regime based on s. 15
- On appeal, all of her arguments were accepted
### Analysis
- Sharma challenged ss. 742.1(c) and 742.1(e)(ii) on appeal, but she is not affected by the latter provision, it only applies to offences with max term of 10 years
- Going to assess it anyway because the Court of Appeal considered it
- Two step test for s. 15(1) applies here, impugned law must
	- Create a distinction based on enumerated or analogous grounds through words or impact
	- Impose a burden or deny a benefit in a way exacerbating a disadvantage
### First Step
- Issues here are what the standard for impact is, and how claimants prove it
- Need to prove a disproportionate impact on a protected group, claimant has to show evidence proving that the law does this
- in [[Fraser v Canada (AG)]], Court said that evidence on the context of the group's situation and evidence on the outcomes of the law in practice are helpful
- Recall that this evidence doesn't have a specific form and doesn't need to prove full causality
### Second Step
- Courts need to examine the historical disadvantage of the group, will look at the impact of stereotyping, prejudice, and arbitrariness
- Law might further these stereotypes and prejudices, or be based on something outside of the actual capacities of the individual
- Recall how in *Fraser*, the RCMP pension scheme exacerbated a present issue of pensions being designed to benefit male employees more
- Claimant doesn't have to prove intent to discriminate, and the Court can infer that a law exacerbates a disadvantage if that inference is supported
- Also, Court can take judicial notice of undisputed facts, in this case that was colonialism
- Courts also need to consider the broader legislative context here, meaning we need to look at the conditional sentencing regime in light of the wider sentencing scheme
- In this case, a particularly relevant part of that scheme is s. 718.2(e), which was the section addressed in [[R v Ipeelee|Gladue]]
- Parliament has the exclusive right to set sentencing policies, and there exists no constitutional right to any specific sentence
- Section 15(1) doesn't impose a positive obligation on the state to remedy social inequalities
- Additionally, when the state does try this, it can do it incrementally
### Application
- The claim fails at the first step because the impugned law does not disproportionately affect Sharma as an Indigenous offender
- This is why the claim was rejected at trial, Sharma had no evidence of a distinction
- Appeal court argued that because Indigenous offenders are treated so badly in the criminal justice situation, this created a distinction
- Impugned provision effectively removed the option of a conditional sentence under s. 718.2(e) which exacerbated this discrimination
- However, this collapses the test into a single step, using historical evidence as the justification for both parts of the test
- First step is supposed to focus only on the disproportionate impact, not historical factors
- Overall, the Court of Appeal erred here by removing the evidentiary burden for the first step of the test
- Section 15(1) not infringed, decision of trial judge restored
### Dissenting Reasons
- Argues that the division between s. 15(1) and s. 1 is the burden of justification which is intended to fall on the state
- Burden on the claimant is only to prove a discriminatory impact
- For this section to function, we need to approach it broadly and generously, leaving most of the work to the government to justify their actions
- This has consistently been affirmed throughout past cases, but this decision revises the test
- Created additional causation requirements by replacing a requirement to prove the law "created a distinction" with "created a disproportionate impact"
- Also weakened the test by arguing the government does not have a positive obligation to fix social issues, and that it's not enough for a law to limit an ameliorative program
- This is functionally a revision of pre-existing s. 15 precedent
