# Dissent in Part
- Issue is with the restrictions on campaign advertising spending limits for ordinary citizens
- Argument is that limiting advertising mainly to political parties and their candidates restricts the ability of normal citizens to effectively communicate political ideas, limits s. 2(b)
- Limit is placed by s. 350 of the *Canada Elections Act* and places a limit of $3000 per person in each electoral district for a countrywide total of $150,000
- Additionally, s. 350(2)(d) prevents spending more than this amount on any issue that a candidate is particularly associated with
- Effect is to prevent citizens from effectively communicating their views on issues during an election campaign
- Also prevents effective use of national media, it costs $425k to put a full page ad in most major Canadian newspapers
- Mail advertising is similarly limited, bulk mailing rate for some ridings is over $7500
- Overall, any messages communicated by a single person is limited to minor local ad campaigns, only parties and candidates can do larger campaigns
- Seems clear that this prevents citizens from effectively communicating their views on election issues, and is a serious infringement of political free expression
## Justification
- First thing to do is to look at the seriousness of the infringement, we know that political speech is the most important form of expression
- Participation in political debate is the primary way a normal citizen can engage in the debates that determine social policy
- This is a right to effective participation, playing a meaningful role in the process
- The *Canada Elections Act* is undercutting this right by withholding the right to substantive analysis and commentary on political issues
- Only sustained messages that voters see during the campaign are from political parties
- Important thing here is that without effective communication, the ability to freely express your political views is pretty much immaterial
- Also can't argue that citizens should just join a party, because what if their views do not match any registered party
### Pressing and Substantial Objective
- Crown argues that the objective is to promote fair elections, with three subgoals:
	- First, favour equality by not allowing those better off to dominate debate
	- Second, foster informed citizenship by ensuring all positions are not drowned out by others
	- Third, enhance public confidence by ensuring an appearance and reality of fairness
- Common sense shows that we should be promoting electoral fairness
### Rational Connection/Minimal Impairment
- Without having a good idea of the extent of the issue, really hard to tell the true seriousness of the concern regarding free and fair elections
- Crown provided no evidence that wealthy people would dominate political debate if the spending limits were removed
- On the other hand, we know how severe the infringement is, the only space left in the marketplace of ideas here is for political parties
- No demonstration that these severe limits are required to meet some perceived dangers of inequality and an uninformed electorate
- In fact, they may actually exacerbate the issue by limiting communication of ideas not advanced by registered parties
- Court recognizes the need for these kinds of limits, but the current limits are too much
- Seems that nothing on the evidence suggests that a virtual ban on effective citizen communication is required to avoid these hypothetical harms
### Proportionality
- Given the unproven nature of the danger, the potential benefits are not clear
- Make three comments on the majority decision:
	- First, whether or not citizens meet the spending limit is irrelevant
	- Second, spending limits the freedom of all Canadians, regardless of wealth
	- Third, even if spending limits only affect a few citizens, this is still a breach of s. 2(b)
# Majority
- Three purposes for outside groups to participate in elections:
	- First, to influence the outcome of an election
	- Second, they can add a fresh perspective on one or more issues addressed by a candidate or political party
	- Third, can add an issue to the political debate and perhaps force people to address it
### Libman v Quebec AG
- Previous example of the SCC considering limits on election spending, this time for referendums in Quebec under the *Referendum Act*
- Court found that the limits were not justified, but did endorse spending limits in general
- Endorsed five key principles of independence
	- First, spending limits are required to prevent the rich from monopolizing public discourse
	- Second, they're necessary to guarantee the right of voters to be informed of all political candidates and parties
	- Third, for limits to be effective, they need to apply to all election expenses
	- Fourth, actions of independents can directly or indirectly support a party/candidate, creating an imbalance in the resources available to each party
	- Fifth, independent spending should be limited more than official spending, as there are many more independents than candidates/parties
- This model of elections is a core part of Canadian society
- Process can equalize participation by either reimbursing candidates and parties or by providing broadcast time to them
- Current regime is clearly based on this model, with the goal of promoting the equal dissemination of all points of view by limiting independent advertising
## Justification
- Seems quite clear that the limits on advertising violate s. 2(b) of the Charter
- Main issue to address now is whether the evidence raised by the AG is sufficient to show that the limits on freedom of expression are justified
- This is a case of conflicting social science evidence, so we can rely on what a reasonable apprehension of harm would be based on this info
- Nature of the harm and the effectiveness of the current regime is impossible to measure scientifically, but several commissions have suggested fairness will be undermined
- It will make wealth dominate discourse, give parties a way around their spending limits, and any election with unlimited spending will not be fair
- Absence of spending limits can degrade confidence in the electoral process
- Goal of these limits is to protect the Canadian electorate and give them an informed vote
- Several surveys also indicate that Canadians see third party spending limits as an effective means of advancing electoral fairness
- Advertising is political expression, but in some circumstances manipulative advertising will be less deserving of protection
- However, there is currently no evidence that third party advertising seeks to be manipulative
- Imposition of these limits also enhances the right to vote under s. 3 by making the political process more balanced
- Seems clear that we should address this issue with a great deal of deference, Parliament has the right to choose our electoral model and deal with its issues and nuances
### Pressing and Substantial Objective
- Goal is electoral fairness, which can be achieved by restricting the participation of those who have access to more resources
- The Lortie Report is an important piece of evidence showing the potential harms of uncontrolled third party advertising
- We can look at the findings of this report to see if the limit is justified
### Rational Connection
- Lower courts erred by requiring an empirical connection between the spending limits and the objectives of the legislation
- We have sufficient evidence connecting these two things here
### Minimal Impairment
- Dissenting opinion claims that without spending much more than the existing limit, citizens can't effectively communicate their views to their fellow citizens
- However, this ignores the fact that advertising is only limited once the election period begins, outside of this period third parties can spend as much money as they want
- There are also few barriers to joining existing political parties or creating new ones
- Majority of Canadians cannot spend the maximum amount, the limiting factor for most is not the legislative restrictions
- Goal is to make sure discourse is not dominated by the rich, and the focus should be protecting the right to meaningful expression for the whole electorate
- This is a minimal impairment, allows meaningful participation while also respecting free expression
- The limits allow third parties to advertise on a small scale on expensive forms of media, but also to output a high volume of low-cost products like flyers
- Also, the definition of 'election advertising' does not apply to forms of communication like the internet, editorials, debates, speeches, interviews, etc.
- Limits are below the party and candidate limits because otherwise a candidate targeted by a third party may not have the resources to respond
- Third parties also have lower overall expenses, and tend to be single-issue
- Can imagine any number of less impairing limits by just changing the number, but this stage of the analysis is satisfied regardless 
- They already allow third parties to spread their message in a way that does not drown out the voices of others, allowing meaningful participation in elections
### Proportionality
- Overall, s. 350 promotes political expression of the 99%, and protects the integrity of the other spending limits, and promotes fair and accessible participation
- Downside is that third parties cannot engage in unlimited political expression, and are limited to informational but likely not very persuasive campaigns
- Overall, this is a justified limit
