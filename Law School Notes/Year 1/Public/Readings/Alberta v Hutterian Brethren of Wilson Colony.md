- Alberta *Traffic Safety Act* requires all drivers to have a licence which also requires a photo of the license holder
- However, there is an exemption for people who object to having their photo taken for religious reasons, these people can get a non-photo license
- In 2003, the photo requirement was made universal
- About 56% of the non photo license are held by members of Hutterian Brethren colonies
- These are religious communities who live rurally and communally and genuinely believe that the 2nd commandment prevents them from having their photo taken willingly
- Province offered some alternatives, but all options involved a photo to be put in the province's facial recognition database
- Community claimed this was a breach of their religious freedoms, government argued that the new requirement was necessary to prevent identity theft
### Limit on the Right
- Colony members who want to get a drivers license currently need to choose between violating their religious beliefs and getting a driver license
- Because the latter is basically required to live in a rural community, this effectively limits their religious freedom and violates s. 2(a) of the Charter
- Freedom of religion is relevant on both the individual and collective level, but we focus on the individual impact for s. 2(a) and mainly consider the collective effects for s. 1
- Recall that s. 2(a) requires a sincere belief in a religious belief or practice and an interference with the claimant's ability to follow these beliefs
- Province conceded the first element, and courts seem to assume the second element is met
## Justification
-  Government needs to make difficult decisions, and inevitably some of these choices may lead to violations of constitutional rights
- The court will accordingly give a degree of deference to the legislature on these matters, as we should not set the bar high enough to deter reasonable solutions to difficult problems
### Pressing and Substantial Objective
- Maintaining the system of drivers licensing to minimize identity theft is pretty clearly a pressing and substantial objective
- By requiring every individual to take a photo for their license, this ensures that every license is connected to a single individual, with one license per individual
- Also designed to comply with international and interprovincial photo ID standards, but not all provinces have this same requirement
- Still a relevant factor
### Rational Connection
- Government argues that have a universal photo id system will make preventing identity theft more effective compared to allowing religious exemptions
- This is pretty sound reasoning, court found a rational connection here
### Minimal Impairment
- Standard for minimal impairment is not perfection, won't fail if the courts can find a slightly better alternative
- But if the govt can't justify why they didn't use this less impairing alternative, then the law may fail
- Only requires that the government chooses the least drastic means of achieving its objective, so less impairing alternatives that don't meet the objective don't count
- Again, this alternative does not have to meet the exact same standard as the existing scheme, getting relatively close is usually good enough
- Claimants have already objected the offered alternatives, making it abundantly clear that any scheme involving taking photos of them for a data bank is a deal breaker
- But without having these photos in the data bank, the use of the data bank is basically moot, it's no longer universal
- Increases the risk that the license holder's identity can be stolen
- Claimants argue that this effect would be negligible due to the low number of people who would request an exemption
- Court found that any solution keeping the photo exemption significantly compromises the government objective
- Means that the universal photo requirement minimally impairs s. 2(a)
- Can also argue that the way bigger issue for the database is the number of people without driver's licenses, which is way more than the number of religious exemptions
- However, government objective seems to be tied specifically to limiting identity theft within the drivers licensing system
- Lower courts did a rights balancing inquiry for this step and also did a reasonable accommodation analysis instead of the *Oakes* test
- This kind of analysis is limited to human rights and government action cases, if we're dealing with the validity of a law you need to use *Oakes*
- Reasonable accommodation is also different from minimal impairment as the latter is specifically designed to deal with the legislature
- Government action gives a lot more leeway compared to where you're enacting laws of general application
- Also hard to apply "undue hardship" to laws, which is a core component of reasonable accommodation
### Proportionality
- Are the effects of the law on the claimants disproportionate to the government objective?
- This step is actually pretty rarely used as most laws that pass the previous three will pretty much always pass
- However, the other steps only consider the purpose of the law, only this bit considers the effects on the individuals/groups in question
#### Salutary Effects
- There are three main salutary effects of the universal photo requirement
	- First, enhancing the security of the driver's licensing system
	- Second, assisting in roadside safety and identification
	- Third, harmonizing Alberta's licensing scheme with other jurisdictions
- First one seems to be the most important here
- Pretty much uncontested that having all license holders take a photo to represent them in a data bank meets this goal more than having an exemption for religious objectors
- Unclear by how much exactly, but this would compromise the integrity of the system
- Seems that the salutary effects are sufficient to support some restriction of the right to religious freedom
#### Deleterious Effects
- No consistent way to assess how serious a limit will be to a religious practice
- We do need to consider the concerns of religious claimants when it comes to passing laws, but we also need to understand that Canada is a multicultural society
- Need to make sure that a limit on their right is actually serious enough to warrant making some other tradeoff
- Recall that the state cannot directly compel religious practice, but things get more complicated when it comes to indirect effects of the law on religious practices
- Effects of a law can be significant enough to deprive the affected individual of a meaningful choice, or they can even be compulsory
- On the other hand, they can only impose costs or inconveniences on those affected
- Court found that this is not the kind of case where the law is depriving an adherent of a meaningful choice of religious practice
- Regulation is trying to support a social good of minimizing fraud at the cost of a few people not being able to drive on the highway
- This does not rise to the level of depriving the claimants of a meaningful choice
- Arranging other methods of highway transport would not end the Colony's way of life as they know it, they could just hire non-Hutterians to drive them
- This would be an economic cost on the colony, but probably not a prohibitive one
- Law does not compel having your photo taken, only if you want a driver's license
#### Weighing the Salutary and Deleterious Effects
-  We are weighing the benefit of reducing the risk of identity fraud against the impact of certain costs on the operations of Hutterite colonies
- Overall, it seems that the limit of religious practice here is proportionate
### Dissenting Opinion
- Need to consider both the individual and collective aspects of religious freedom here
- Majority rejected the proposal of issuing photoless licenses with a stamp saying that they can't be used for identification purposes as a significant compromise of the govt objective
- No real evidence that this would actually interfere with the objective
- Easy for the state to say that their approach will maximize the objective, but shouldn't we also argue what alternatives may also fulfill that objective?
- Pretty hard to argue that a scheme that completely extinguishes the right of freedom of religion with no wiggle room is a minimal impairment
- Previously established that where there is a full ban on advertising, the government needs to show that this full prohibition is necessary for its goal
- This is not the end of the analysis tho, also need to consider proportionality
#### Proportionality
- Government has not shown that the salutary effects of this scheme are anything more than speculation
- Positive impact of the photo requirement is that it will prevent identity theft, but facial recognition technology is not exactly reliable
- No real evidence that the existing system was causing any harm to the licensing system
- The court also dismissed the issue of non-licensed Albertans to easily
- There are also a number of other documents you can use for identity requirements like birth certificates, social insurance cards, and health cards which have no photos
- If the government is willing to tolerate 700,000 Albertans without license photos, surely having about 250 licenses with no photos is fine
- The salutary effects seem to be slight and mostly hypothetical
- Harm to the Hutterite community is clearly a lot more important
- Colonies attempt to be self-sufficient, but certain members of the community still need to drive to sell products, buy raw materials, and to get medical appointments
- Their independent lifestyle is reliant on this practice to a large extent, and this style of living is crucial to the spiritual community
- This is a form of indirect coercion forcing colony members to either give up their religious beliefs or sacrifice their self-sufficient lifestyles
- The majority also suggests that the scrutiny is different for compulsory programs and conditional programs that are a "privilege"
- Clearly inconsistent with [[Eldridge But Again|Eldridge]] where the court held that when the state provides a benefit they have to do so in a non-discriminatory manner
- Licenses can have a profound significance on the lives of individuals and communities, we can see this as early as *Roncarelli*
- Given the disproportionality between the harms and the benefits, the government has not proven this infringement is justified under s. 1
- Would dismiss the appeal and suspend the declaration for one year
- Other judges said the restriction wasn't justified but disagreed with the notion that you need to wait until the last step to consider competing interests
