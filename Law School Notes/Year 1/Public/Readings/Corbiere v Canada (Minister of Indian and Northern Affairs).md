- Main argument against the enumerated and analogous grounds analysis is that it ignores the idea of intersectionality
- Trilogy of cases following [[Andrews v Law Society of British Columbia]] gave rise to an argument that the courts should focus on groups rather than grounds
- This has been adopted occasionally by the SCC, but not consistently
### Dianne Pothier
- Sought to defend the usage of grounds, arguing that they are not disconnected from real experiences as some judges claim they are
- The grounds of discrimination are not purely legal, they are intended to reflect political and social realities
- Grounds are what separate people who experience discrimination from those who don't
- Having them explicitly listed out like this reminds us why discrimination is banned
- They are markers of the dynamics of power, and identifying new analogous grounds does the same thing, developing those grounds over time
### Analogous Grounds
- Have historically been identified using a few criteria
- Has to be something immutable to the people that possess it, and usually requires a notable lack of political power or representation
- *Andrews* recognized citizenship as an analogous ground, and since then, only three more grounds have been recognized, sexual orientation being the next one in *Egan*
- However, that case ultimately did recognize a discrimination, but was upheld
- [[Vriend v Alberta]] and [[M v H]] have since been more successful
- Marital status is also an analogous ground, recognized by *Miron v Trudel*
- Reasons for this were the difficulty with affecting this unilaterally, as well as the historical patterns of disadvantage connected to marital status
- Finally, this case recognized "Aboriginality-residence" as the final analogous ground, which means being an off-reserve status Indian
## Case
- Section 77(1) of the *Indian Act* requires band members to be ordinarily resident on reserve in order to be able to vote in a band election
- Non-resident band members challenged this under s. 15(1), arguing that their residence status is a personal characteristic that is being used to limit their rights
### Analysis
- Court held that this is an analogous ground, but commented on the claim that it may not be analogous depending on the circumstances
- Also discussed the criteria for analogous grounds
- Enumerated grounds are the markers of what qualifies as discrimination by giving examples of common grounds of discrimination
- These are only indicators, and the same goes for analogous grounds
- Just because you can distinguish based on analogous grounds and not be discriminatory does not means that the ground is not analogous in that situation
- Therefore, a marker of discrimination cannot change status from case to case
- If Aboriginality-residence is an analogous ground, then it is always an analogous ground
#### Criteria
- Obvious answer is to look to potential grounds of distinction that seem to be analogous to the enumerated grounds
- What those grounds have in common is that they are immutable, and are often used as the basis for stereotypical decisions not based on merit
- Section 15 targets the denial of equal treatment on grounds that are either actually or constructively immutable, religion is an example of the latter
- Everything else, like the adverse impact or historical discrimination, is downstream from this
- Here, the distinction made is based on something essential to a band member's identity, which can be considered constructively immutable
- Decision to live on or off reserve is a major decision, different from just choosing where to live
- Finally, this ground only applies to Indigenous Canadians, but this is overall fine, there's no reason why we can't have grounds like this
### Concurring Opinion
- Took a purposive approach to the analogous grounds analysis
- Also talks about how analogous grounds have to be immutable, but talks more about how the grounds have to be a crucial part of a group's identity
- Said that being indicative of an oppressed group is also required
- Also uses a whole bunch of other criteria
- Recognizes that not all groups are discriminated against in the same ways, so different grounds meet different criteria
- Holds that resident on reserve is an analogous ground because it's choice that's crucial to identity, and creates disadvantage
- Entire court agreed there was a discrimination, just for different reasons
- Also agreed it wasn't justified because the restriction was not minimally impairing, though it had an objective of ensuring those most affected vote
- As a remedy, the residence requirement was struck from the statute after 18 months to allow for consultation with Indigenous groups
