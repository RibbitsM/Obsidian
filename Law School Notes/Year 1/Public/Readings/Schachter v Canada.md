- Issue was with the *Unemployment Insurance Act* which gives mothers and adoptive parents maternity benefits
- For adoptive parents, either parent can make the claim and split the benefits between them
- Father tried to claim for paternity benefits for his natural born child and failed, challenged the decision under s. 15
- Trial judge found that s. 32 of the *Act* discriminated between natural and adoptive parents, by only allowing the latter to split benefits
- Granted the claimant declaratory relief under s. 24(1) of the *Constitution Act of 1982*, extending the same benefits to adoptive and natural parents
- Issue was whether or not the trial judge could order this remedy
### Severance
- Section 52 of the *Constitution* requires the court to strike down any law that is inconsistent with the Constitution, but only to the extent of the inconsistency
- Means that the court can strike the law down, strike it at a set point in the future, or read down or read in the provision
- This option of "reading down" is also called the doctrine of severance, basically you pretend the unconstitutional part of the law doesn't exist
- Especially common remedy in *Charter* cases where the violation tends to be only one part of the law
- If what is left after the bad parts are read out cannot survive on it's own, will probably need to scrap the whole thing
- Assumption is that if the legislature could have passed the law without the unconstitutional bits, then it's okay for severance
### Reading In As Severance
- Reading in is also pretty close to severance, except instead of an improper inclusion of language, we have language that was wrongfully excluded from the law
- Example of where reading in is needed is where a statute grants a benefit or right to one group only
- Example of where reading out would be needed would be if all groups except one are granted a benefit
- Seems clear that these are almost identical cases, and the final result would be the same after reading in/out
- Keep in mind that s. 52 does not make the words of the law of no force or effect, it makes the law itself of no force or effect to the extent of the inconsistency
- Inconsistency can include both what is in the text and what is not
### Purposes
- First is the respect for the role of the legislature, the purpose of reading in is to be as faithful as possible to the scheme enacted by the legislature, within the limits of the Constitution
- Can't always assume that we are honouring parliament's intentions when we are reading in, especially for underinclusion
- Second is the respect for the purposes of the Charter
- Without reading in, the standards of the Charter would not be able to be applied without accidentally hurting itself sometimes
- For example, in a case where welfare benefits were available to single mothers but not single fathers, the Charter would be ok whether the benefit was given to both or neither
- Because the court could not extend the benefits for some reason, they had to remove the benefits for single mothers
- In a situation like this, reading in helps the court act in a manner that fulfills the purposes of the Charter
- It's a legitimate remedy like severance, and should be available under s. 52 whenever appropriate
- Reading in can create issues by intruding into budgetary decisions, which the court is not allowed to do
### Conclusion
- No easy formula for determining whether severance or reading in is the appropriate tool to use
- Main guiding principles are respecting the roles of the legislature and the Charter
- Without a clear legislative objective, not smart to read in the excluded group, especially because of budgetary concerns
- Excluded group vastly outnumbers the group that already had benefits
- Unfortunately, the best option is just to declare the provision invalid but suspend the declaration to give the legislature time to figure out the best way to redo this
- Parliament did amend this provision by giving equal benefits, but for a lesser amount of time for all (15 weeks to 10 weeks)
- Overall, appeal allowed and trial judgement set aside
- Section 32 of the *Unemployment Insurance Act* is struck down by s. 52 and is of no force and effect
- Don't need to suspend the declaration, provision had already been repealed
