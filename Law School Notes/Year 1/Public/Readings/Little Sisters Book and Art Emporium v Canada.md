- This is the obscenity case dealing with books for a queer bookstore being seized at the border
- Appellants established that the burden of proving obscenity under s. 152(3) of the *Customs Act* lies on the Crown or whoever alleges it
- Also proved several infringements of s. 2(b) and s. 15(1)
- Affirmed that the trial judge was correct in holding that the offending provisions violated the Charter, only issue left is the remedy
- Court concluded that granting an individual remedy under s. 24(1) is inappropriate
- Since the trial decision finished, Customs has addressed most of these issues, and it seems like these measures should be sufficient
- Appellants also didn't suggest any further measures that would help here
- Court already held that it would be inappropriate to declare the laws inoperable
## Concurring/Dissenting Opinion
- Majority found that there were grave systemic problems with the law here, but only gave a declaratory remedy instead of anything substantial
- Point out that the history of Customs not responding to court declarations and improperly censoring materials show that we should strike down the legislation
- Main issue with the current laws is that they don't have any safeguards for preventing abuse of the discretion given to Customs
- All the appellants have got here was the word of the respondents that they won't do it again
### Declaratory Relief is Insufficient
- Declarations are often preferable to injunctions because they are more flexible and are more hands off in terms of what the govt needs to do
- Problem is that they're usually pretty vague and suffer from a lack of monitoring compliance
- In a case called *Mahe v Alberta*, the court held that Edmonton's school system did not have enough control over French language education
- Impugned provisions here were permissive, the school board could have followed the Charter but they chose not to, and the laws did not force them too
- Didn't strike down these laws, instead issued a declaration
- Same approach was used in [[Eldridge But Again]] by just ordering that services should be provided to the deaf, don't need to nuke the whole legislative scheme
- Neither of these cases were facing the same situation we have here, in both of those cases there would be real harm caused by striking down the legislation
- Doesn't seem to be a similar issue here
- Appellants also don't really have any way to enforce the declaration on their own, they've already spent way too much money on this case
- Only way to enforce it would be to go through the whole process again
- Also differs from the majorities assertion that the new reforms are enough
- Made a couple recommendations for reform
### Conclusion
- Appeal allowed, and declared the offending part of the Tariff Code invalid under s. 52, but suspended for 18 months
