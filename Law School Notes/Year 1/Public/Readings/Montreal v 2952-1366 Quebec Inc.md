- Issue is with art. 9(1) of the *By-law concerning noise*, which was adopted by Montreal and challenged by a club using loudspeakers to advertise to pedestrians
- Charged with producing noise that could be heard outside using sound equipment
- Not disputed that the City had the power to define and prohibit nuisances
- Seems clear that this is expressive content
### Excluded Expression
- Recall that certain kinds of expressive activity are banned due to the method or location of the communication, violent expression is the main example
- Public property is government-owned, and the prohibited sound was being broadcast onto the street, a public space
- A key aspect of freedom of expression is the ability to express yourself in public places
- Issue is whether s. 2(b) protects not just the content of the expression, but also the right to do that on a public street
- Can argue that all limits on the use of public property for expression are limits on s. 2(b)
- However, there are many government-owned places that are actually private, and are viewed similarly to private offices or businesses
- In a previous case called *Committee for the Commonwealth of Canada*, almost all judges came to the conclusion that these places did not allow for public expression
- Multiple different tests proposed for dealing with this, at the very least it seems that in this case, the expression is protected under s. 2(b)
- A speaker emitting noise onto a public street does not impede the function of the street or fails to promote the values of s. 2(b)
- As long as the noise does not interfere with walking or talking on the street, it's fine and that does not seem to be the case here
- Expressive activity should only be excluded from s. 2(b) if its method/location undermines the values of s. 2(b)
- Main example is violent expression ofc
- We should take a similar approach here, does expression in a given place undermine the values of s. 2(b)?
### Novel Test
- Court proposes a test for the application of s. 2(b) to public property, onus is on the claimant
- Main issue is whether the relevant location is a public place where you would expect protection for free expression
- Determined by whether expression in this place conflicts with the purposes of s. 2(b), which are mainly democratic discourse, truth finding, and self-fulfillment
	- Also need to consider the historical/actual function of the place and whether aspects of the place suggest that expression there would undermine the values of s. 2(b)
- Means that in places where free expression traditionally happens, unlikely that protecting expression now would undermine these core values
- Actual function is important because it determines how accessible and private the place is in reality, would public access affect it's purpose?
- Most cases will be determined by these factors, but other factors may also be relevant
- Another potential issue is that the protection is over or underbroad
- Goal of the test is as a preliminary screen for s. 2(b) to weed out places that are outside the protected sphere of s. 2(b)
- Future developments may move debate to the private sector, or online
- Court deems the current test flexible enough to adapt in the future
- Applying this test, we can clearly see that free expression has been long accepted on streets, no reason to think values would be undermined here
### Infringement
- Purpose of the by-law does not infringe expression, but the effect definitely does
- Amplified noise was encouraging people to attend the club for leisure purposes, which can promote values like self-fulfillment
- Overall, seems clear that the ban on amplified noise is a limit on s. 2(b)
### Justification
- Objective of the by-law is to combat noise pollution, a compelling and substantial objective
- City prohibits noises exceeding a certain volume generally, and some noises specifically, including noise produced by sound equipment that can be heard from the outside
- This is not an absolute ban, City can grant licenses to give exceptions
- No evidence that this scheme has been used arbitrarily or to limit discourse
- Additionally, art. 9(1) is only supposed to apply to noise interfering with the peaceful use and enjoyment of the surrounding environment
- Seems clear that this is rationally connected to the objective
#### Minimal Impairment
- Keep in mind that we are giving deference to the City, they are not obligated to find the best possible solution, just a reasonable one
- Unclear that regulation based on volume will effectively curb noise pollution, finding and prosecuting violations is hard and noise can overlap cumulatively from different sources
- This kind of regulation would not achieve the goal of eliminating specific kinds of sound, regulating on volume alone would be logistically impossible
- Really no other practical way to deal with the issue other than this method
#### Proportionality
- Expression in this case is noise created by sound equipment that interferes with the peaceful use and enjoyment of city property
- Benefit is the reduction of noise pollution in the area
- Do need to consider that this was on a busy street with an active nightlife, but this does not mean the enjoyment of the environment can be abused
- Everyone is entitled to a health environment, noise control is part of this
- Overall, beneficial effects outweigh the downsides, by-law is constitutional
