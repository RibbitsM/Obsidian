- Claimant ran a porn store
- Issue for the Court was whether Parliament can criminalize obscenity, which at the time was handled by s. 163 of the *Criminal Code*
### Interpretation of s. 163
- *Criminal Code* says that for a work or material to be "obscene", must have undue exploitation of sex, and this must be its dominant characteristic
- To determine what is "undue" exploitation, the courts use something called the "community standard of tolerance"
- Means that the standard is based on the local community, and is determined by what the average person in that community would let other Canadians see
- Generally, any material that exploits sex in a degrading or dehumanizing way will fail the community standards test
- Fails because it is perceived by the public to be harmful to society, especially women
- Don't really need solid proof for this, as long as most people think that
- Last step is to consider the artistic defence
- Even if the material offends community standards, if that material is required for the serious treatment of a theme, may be exempted
- Look at the work and see if the exploitation of sex has a justifiable role in advancing the plot
- Jurisprudence has failed to relate these tests to each other, for example how do we apply the community standards vs the degrading and dehumanizing test
- Will the artistic defence always redeem a work, even if it fails all other tests?
- Porn can be divided into three different categories
	- Explicit sex with violence
	- Explicit sex without violence, but with dehumanizing and degrading treatment
	- Explicit sex without violence or dehumanizing and degrading treatment
- Seems that only the first two categories are covered under s. 163(8)
- First category will almost always be undue exploitation
- Second category may be undue if there's a substantial risk of harm
- Third category is generally fine
- Courts are responsible for determining what the community will tolerate others being exposed to based on the degree of potential harm
- For example, conduct like this would encourage anti-social behaviour or violence towards women
- The artistic defence or "internal necessities" test is only applied if the work contains sexually explicit material, that would itself be undue exploitation
- Need to look at the sex in context, is it the dominant theme of the work?
- When you're looking at the work as a while, would the community tolerate it?
- Recall that artistic expression is protected under s. 2(b) so we should be giving it the benefit of the doubt
### Does s. 163 Violate s. 2(b)?
- Majority of the appeal court held that s. 163 does not violate s. 2(b)
- They erred by saying that purely physical acts are not expressive
- In the context of [[R v Keegstra]], seems there is actually a clear violation
### Justification
- First question is whether it's a limit prescribed by law
- Appellant argues it's too vague and imprecise to be considered a law at all
- However, while it may be confusing, we have been able to get a judicial standard out of it
- Some vague terms like "undue" are just kind of inevitable in the law
#### Substantial and Compelling Objective
- Main objectives raised are the need to avoid harm coming from antisocial behaviour caused by exposure to indecent materials
- Can also argue that the state is the "moral custodian" and has a duty to prevent threats caused by depictions of sex
- This latter argument is not really applicable, violates the Charter by trying to set a universal standard of morality
- However, Parliament does still reserve the right to legislate on fundamental concepts of morality
- Holds that the overriding objective is avoiding harm to society
- Pointed to a committee report on porn that would that it reinforced unhealthy gender stereotypes and makes degradation and violence seem normal
- Notions of moral corruption and harm to society are not distinct, so it's not inconsistent to say that the purpose of s. 163 is avoiding harm
- Original purpose when it was drafted was to address harms linked to obscene materials
- Similar to *Keegstra*, this is a substantial objective because it seeks to encourage equality
#### Proportionality
- Recall that the values underlying freedom of expression are the search for truth, political participation, and individual self-fulfillment
- Only one you can really argued for here is individual self-fulfillment through arousal
- Can also argue that it can also prompt political discussions about sexuality
- Kind of porn we're trying to regulate here definitely isn't prompting any discussions
- Also, in most cases the primary motive for expression is money, and we know that restrictions on expression for economic motives are easier to justify
#### Rational Connection
- Obscenity is somewhat similar to hate propaganda in the sense that it's a form of expression that creates social damage by degrading and dehumanizing its subjects
- Rational link between banning this and limiting harm should be fairly evident
- We don't have an actual causal connection, but we can presume that one exists
- Know that because of [[Irwin Toy v Quebec (s. 2(b))]], we can defer to the legislature because this is a case of balancing social science evidence
- Also said in *Keegstra* that a lack of causation won't be fatal
- Parliament was entitled to have a "reasoned apprehension of harm" that would come from desensitizing people through obscene materials
#### Minimal Impairment
- Recall that the legislature does not need to find the perfect scheme
- Four factors explaining why s. 163 is minimally impairing, or close enough
	- First, s. 163 is designed to only catch material that causes a risk of harm to society
		- Means that non-violent and non-degrading porn is okay
		- Parliament acted reasonably in not requiring proof of harm, given they reasonably presumed that this would lead to harm
	- Second, any materials with scientific, artistic, or literary value are excluded through the "artistic defence" which is to applied generously
	- Third, past versions to make the definition more specific have failed pretty poorly
		- Seems that the best option is to have an abstract definition
	- Fourth, it only applies to the public distribution and exhibition of obscene materials
- Intervenors argued that this purpose could be better suited by taking action to limit violence against women instead
- Court said that there is no need to choose, Parliament can pursue both avenues
#### Balancing
- Infringement is not to a particularly valuable form of expression, only benefits are a small amount of individual fulfillment, and it's mainly motivated by economics
- Objective is to avoid harm caused by the distribution of obscene materials and enhance respect for all members of society
- Restriction does not seem to outweigh the objective
- Section 163(8) is a violation of s. 2(b) but is saved under s. 1
