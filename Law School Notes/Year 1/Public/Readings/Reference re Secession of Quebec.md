- Three core issues for this case
	- Can the Quebec government unilaterally decide to secede from Canada?
	- Does international law give them this power, likely through self-determination
	- If there is a conflict between domestic and international law on this topic, which takes precedent?
- To determine this, the court looked at the four core principles of federalism, democracy, constitutionalism/rule of law, and respect of minorities
- Start by looking at the historical context of the Constitution
### Historical Context
- Origin of Canada is one of compromise, based on the decisions made by the representatives of New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, and Canada
- To get everyone on board, certain agreements and concessions had to be made
- Only a couple years after Confederation, Nova Scotia tried to secede but was blocked by the Colonial office
- Said that the other provinces entered the agreement in reliance on the other provinces
- This kind of agreement was required to handle the diversity of Canada
- Federalism was an important part of that process
### Constitutional Principles
- Four principles exist in symbiosis, and no single principle takes precedent over another
- Not explicitly created or mentioned in the Constitution, but are nonetheless key
- Uses the "living tree" definition, arguing that these principles are important for dictating how the Constitution will inevitably evolve over the years
- Principles can also create legal obligations, they have binding force
#### Federalism
- Even before federalism was really put into practice, it was a key principle
- Original Constitution gave the fed much more power, but over time these powers have been removed or limited by the principle of federalism
- Original Constitution has previously been interpreted to have the goal of representing the provinces through a central government
- Provinces were vested with exclusive authority and retained their autonomy
- Recognizes that law will be applied differently across provinces, and also serves the goal of protecting minorities
#### Democracy
- Presence of democracy as a core principle can be shown through the presence of elected bodies at multiple levels of the government
- Doesn't need to be explicitly stated because it's so obvious, Canada was clearly intended to exist as a constitutional democracy from the early years
- Also example of the living tree idea as Canada's democracy has evolved over time
- Can trace its origins back to the *Magna Carta*
- Democracy also means encouraging self-governance
- Works on both the institutional and individual level
- Legislatures and Parliament are both elected publicly, and the right to vote is now guaranteed to all citizens of Canada
- Integrates into federalism by ensuring that majorities in different provinces are given equal weight
- Legitimacy of these democratic institutions relies on the legal foundation that ensures equal participation, which ties into the rule of law principle
- Another key part of democracy is the "marketplace of ideas", which comes in the form of the legislatures who hold the executive accountable
- Also the reason why the 1982 Constitution allows any province to initiate an attempt to amend it, but requires a vote to be successful
#### Constitutionalism/Rule of Law
- On the individual level, this principle serves to protect citizens from state actions
- Basically means that all government action has to comply with the Constitution
- Rule of law means the government has to follow the law as well, which includes the Constitution
- Right to lawful authority comes from the Constitution, and applies to all levels of government
- Three reasons why the Constitution imposes restrictions past the requirement for democratic consensus on an issue
	- First is because it serves as an extra safeguard for rights and freedoms
	- Avoids situations where the majority may try to get around these rights to achieve some kind of collective goal
	- Second is to protect minority groups from the majority
	- Third is to enable divisions of political power across levels of government
	- Without this, certain levels of the government could unilaterally empower themselves
- Can argue that allowing a provincial majority to make decisions would be a recognition of the principles of self-governance and democracy
- However, it's pretty clear that in Canada, a simple majority isn't enough to do some things
- Having a constitutional democracy requires political representatives to bind the future decisions of the province via the Constitution
- Constitutional rules can and should be amended, but only after significant negotiation
- To make a serious decision like this, need to consider the minority interests as well as the desires of the majority
#### Protection of Minorities
- One of the key demands for Confederation was the protection of minority religious education rights in most provinces
- Minority language rights has also been a long-time issue in Canada
- Provisions were initially created through negotiation and compromise, but reflect the broader principle of respecting these minority rights
- Existed long before the Charter was formed, and is still a key concern
- These are the reasons for new constitutional provisions like ss. 25 and 35 protecting the rights of Indigenous peoples in Canada
- Court ultimately held that unilateral secession is unconstitutional, but it does indicate that the other provinces should negotiate amending the Constitution
