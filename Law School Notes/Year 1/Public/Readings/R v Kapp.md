- Recall that s. 15(2) of the Charter ensures that s. 15(1) also applies to ameliorative programs
- Appellants in this case were commercial fishers who argued their s. 15 equality rights were violated by an exclusive fishing license given to three Indigenous bands on the Fraser River
- Argue that this fishing license discriminated against them on racial grounds
- Crown argues the goal of the license was to regulate the fishery and ameliorate the conditions of a disadvantaged group
### Analysis
- Recall that not all distinctions are discriminatory, ameliorative programs will inevitably exclude individuals not belonging to disadvantaged groups
- Appellants established that they were treated differently based on race, but because of the Crown's argument we have to look at s. 15(2)
- Fishing license seems to meet all the requirements of s. 15(2)
- Previously stated that the purpose of s. 15(2) is to reinforce that improving the conditions of disadvantaged individuals is not discriminatory even if it requires distinction
- Protects the government when it chooses to make these kinds of remedial schemes
- This section has not been recognized to have independent force, but it's possible
- Can either be read as an interpretive aid to s. 15(1) or as an exception to it
- Court in this case argues that if the Crown can prove that a program falls under the definition set by s. 15(2), it is immune from s. 15 analysis
- Goal is to promote substantive equality, 15(1) does this by actively preventing discrimination and 15(2) does it by letting the govt curb discrimination
- Source of the discrimination in the present case was an ameliorative program, if this program is covered under s. 15(2) the claim should fail
### Section 15(2) Test
- A program does not violate s. 15 if the government can prove that the program has an ameliorative purpose and it targets a disadvantaged group on recognized grounds
- Key phrases in s. 15(2) are "has as its object", "ameliorative", and "disadvantaged"
#### Has As Its Object
- Issues here are whether we are looking at the purpose or effect of legislation, and whether or not an ameliorative purpose needs to be the sole goal or not
- Seems that it's better to look at the intended effect instead of the actual effect because it will limit court interference with legislative intent
- Can determine this by looking at the statements of those drafting the legislation as well as whether the legislature chose rational means for the purpose
- Don't want this latter requirement to turn the whole thing into an effect analysis, so we should try and focus only on finding a connection between means and the disadvantage at issue
- Also pretty hard to justify needing the ameliorative purpose to be the sole goal of the program
- Generally programs will have several purposes, or will be part of a broader scheme
- As long as it's one of the purposes that should be ok, but we should consider the importance of the ameliorative purpose compared to the other options
#### Amelioration
- This should be easy to interpret, but it has been applied broadly in the past
- For example, a bylaw preventing people under 16 from operating an amusement park ride without supervision was held to be for the benefit of young people
- Laws that restrict or punish behaviour will not qualify for this test, and we will look at intended effect of amelioration, not the actual effect
#### Disadvantaged
- This is already established under s. 15(1) to mean vulnerability, prejudice, and any other negative social characterization
- Not all members of a group need to be disadvantaged to qualify
### Application
- Court found that the purpose of the licence program was to recognize Aboriginal fishing rights, and the means the govt took to fix this were rationally related to the purpose
- Program was giving the community a source of income and employment as well as a path for long-term self sufficiency
- Impugned provision was protected under s. 15(2)
