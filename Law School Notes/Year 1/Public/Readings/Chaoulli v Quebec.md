- Appellant was a doctor who tried to get their home visit service licensed and get a license to run a private hospital
- Teamed up with a guy who was upset about wait times in the public health system
- Issue is with the a statutory ban on private health insurance for public health care services
- Argued that this deprives them of services with lower wait times, which violates s. 7
- Trial court dismissed the claim after finding a deprivation but said that it was in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice
- Appellants also argued that it was a violation of s. 1 of the Quebec Charter which also provides the rights to life and personal security
### Analysis
- Court argued that the main issue is if Quebeckers who want to pay more money for health care can be prevented in doing so by the state
- Need for a good public healthcare system is clear, but does preserving this system justify violating people's rights like this?
- Often times, this argument is framed as a class struggle, but the issue here is a purely legal one
- *Canada Health Act* does not ban private health services, neither does it set out what wait times are excessive when it comes to making healthcare accessible
- Appellant argues this brings the right to life into question because some patients may die because they waited too long for care
- Common knowledge that long wait times are a major issue, healthcare has a potentially infinite demand and wait times are a way of rationing that resource
- While patients can go outside of Quebec to get services that may be more accessible elsewhere, this is not a full solution
- Trial judge did not err in determining that this was a violation of the right to life/security
### Justification
- Objective is to promote the highest quality care for all regardless of the patient's resources
- The state is pretty much exclusively responsible for providing these services
- Prohibition on private health insurance means that providing private health care to people with average incomes is basically impossible
- Overall seems pretty clear that this is a pressing and substantive objective
- There is also a clear rational connection between the objective and the legislation, the government has been more or less doing this unchallenged
- Judge held that the prohibition on private insurance was not minimally impairing
- Looking at how other similar countries have addressed the same issue, there are other methods we can use other than banning private health insurance
#### Deference
- Next issue is whether this is a case where the courts need to defer to legislature
- Courts have a duty to ignore political debate and social policy, but will step in where social policy is violating protected rights
- Not exercising the powers in the Quebec Charter would be denying the citizens of the province their rightful protections under the law
- Government could argue that this case is too complicated to warrant the court ruling on it, but the courts seem to have all the evidence and knowledge they need here
- They have failed to act despite many committees and working groups on the issue, and now it is time for the courts to step in
- If the government has failed to act, they cannot demand deference on this issue
## Concurring Opinion (McLachlin & Major)
- Focused their analysis on the Canadian Charter issue, agreeing with the majority opinion on the Quebec Charter issue
- Charter does not grant a right to health care, but where the government provides health care, it must do so in a way that complies with the Charter
- Existing regime limits access to private health services by banning private health insurance
- Creates a monopoly on the part of the state which negatively affects protected rights
- In practice, what this does is ensure that only the richest, who can pay without insurance, are able to skip the long waiting lines and seek private healthcare
- Because of the physical and psychological consequences of these long wait times, this is a violation of s. 7, same as in [[R v Morgentaler]]
- Access to a waiting list is not access to health care, there is a real and serious harm being done by the current healthcare system
### PFJs
- Principle of arbitrariness requires a real connection between the factual consequences and the stated objective of the legislation
- Government argues that allowing private health insurance will divert resources away from the public system, further reducing the available quality of care
- Appellants argue that giving people the choice will avoid a monopoly and result in better care for all
- Pointed to the examples of other countries with public health care that allow access to private health care that do not have this issue
- Shows that a monopoly is not necessary for good public healthcare
- This means that there isn't necessarily a connection between the legislation and the stated goal
- Just because Canada's decisions were based on a series of reports on healthcare does not mean that the courts can't review that decision
- Overall, this is an arbitrary piece of legislation, and violates a fundamental principle
### Section 1
- Due to the lack of evidence tying the ban on private insurance and the need to protect the healthcare system, there is no rational connection here
- Whenever a provision fails for arbitrariness, it pretty much automatically fails s. 1 too
## Dissenting Opinion
- Argues that the question is whether Quebec can prevent the creation of a second tier of the health care system based on private care
- No clear standard for what the Constitution considers to be "reasonable health care"
- What are the benchmarks for waiting times or the number of specific tests available?
- Point of the *Canada Health Act* is to provide health care based on need first, not other considerations like wealth or status
- If you take this view, then it makes complete sense to ban private health insurance, it's a rational consequence of the legislative scheme
- Liberty and security of citizens who cannot afford private insurance is not put at risk here, only those who can already get it
- Current prohibition serves the purpose of maintaining the single-tier system and preventing it from becoming a *de facto* two-tiered system
- Can't easily draw an analogy to earlier cases like *Morgentaler* which dealt with criminal law, not social policy
- Proper place to address this issue is the National Assembly, there's no PFJs that apply here
- There is a reason why all the lower courts were unanimous in holding that this prohibition was not arbitrary
- The argument that this is arbitrary is based mainly on generalizations about the system and a misplaced optimism in the superiority of a private healthcare system
### Health Policy Debate
- Not all provinces choose to ban private health insurance, but all of them do take steps to discourage the use of private healthcare
- Underlying logic behind all these measures is that health care should be based on need first
- Main argument for the two-tier system is that it would help ordinary Canadians who are already being put at the forefront of the public system
- Seems pretty clear that private systems like in the US, minority groups are affected terribly by the system and suffer significant violations
- Quebec could do this, but it would go against the core policies of Canada
- Such a dramatic shift cannot be compelled by either Charter
### Analysis
- Argues that the comparisons to *Morgentaler* are inappropriate here
- That case dealt with internal inconsistencies in the *Criminal Code*
- Accept the finding that a two-tier system would create negative impacts on the functioning of the current public health system
- Generally speaking, private health care emphasizes access based on wealth rather than on need, which is inconsistent with the goals of the *Canada Health Act*
- Private insurance is required to avoid a significant alternative to the public system, which is what the Canadian government is trying to avoid
- Argued that s. 7 doesn't apply because the claimants do not have grounds to establish a breach of a PFJ
- However, it does seem that in some cases, the current system will limit life and security rights
- Does not violate liberty rights, because liberty in the context of the Charter does not extend to freedom of contract, or any other economic rights
- Also means that Dr. Chaoulli is not having his liberty limited by being unable to deliver private health care
- Previously established that career choices and who you do business with are not fundamental choices for the purposes of s. 7
- Still violations of two out of three rights, so need to consider PFJs
### PFJs
- To have a principle of fundamental justice, it has to be a basic tenet of the legal system and has to be generally accepted by reasonable people
- Also needs to be able to be precisely identified and applied in a way that creates predictable results
- Principle of having healthcare in a reasonable time is not a legal principle, no consensus on this, and it's obviously too vague to be applied precisely
- Expert evidence shows that there is no consensus of what "timely treatment" is
- Waiting list problem is certainly serious, but the available data on the issue is pretty contradictory and unclear overall
- Also hard to generalize what the impact of these waiting times are, they are found not just in public systems but in pretty much all healthcare systems
- In general, it seems that whenever health care is absolutely needed in Canada, it will be given to those who need it
- In the event that Quebeckers do need to go outside of the province or country to get insured services, they can be reimbursed under s. 10 of the *Health Insurance Act*
- Court is currently too overburdened with conflicting evidence regarding it's own healthcare system to seriously consider evidence on systems outside of Canada
- Dangerous to start looking at foreign systems that Canadians have little experience in
#### Arbitrariness
- Definition of arbitrariness from [[Rodriguez v BC]] is that a law is arbitrary where is bears no resemblance to the objective or is inconsistent with it
- Concurring opinion argues that this provision is arbitrary because it is unnecessary for the objective, but this is not the proper definition
- Law needs to logically contradict its objectives to be arbitrary
- Changing this definition would unnecessarily open up the scope of s. 7
- State interest is to provide high quality health care at a reasonable cost to as many people as possible in a way consistent with the principles of efficiency, equity, and fiscal responsibility
- System should not be overbuilt, neither should it be insufficient
- The principle here is to create a healthcare system where access is determined by need first to not leave those uninsured behind
- To accomplish this goal, Quebec has banned private health insurance to discourage the growth of privately delivered "insured" health services based on wealth
- This prohibition is rationally connected to the objective
- Practically speaking, the ban is based on the view that private insurance would have a harmful effect on the public system
- Both lower courts correctly found that the expansion of private healthcare would negatively affect the public system
- For these reasons, it was not arbitrary to ban private health insurance
- Reliance on the many authoritative and comprehensive reports on the importance of the single-tier healthcare system is not arbitrary, though it is able to be questioned
- Overall, while in some situations s. 7 may be violated, there is no violation of any legal principle of fundamental justice
