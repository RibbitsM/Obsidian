- Insite safe injection site in the DTES was giving medical services to unhoused drug users
- Seen as the best response to the spread of bloodborne diseases and increasing OD rates
- Government failed to extend Insite's exemption from criminal sanctions in 2008
- Claimants tried to argue that applying the CDSA to Insite violates their s. 7 rights
- Claim that the prohibitions on possession and trafficking under ss. 4(1) and 5(1) violate their rights to life, liberty, and security of the person
### Analysis
- Without an exemption under s. 56 of the CDSA all the staff and clients of Insite would be guilty of possession, but not trafficking
- Possession provisions infringe on rights, but are consistent with the PFJs because s. 56 exemptions allow for some wiggle room
- Crown argues that the negative health risks faced by the drug users if Insite shuts down are caused by their drug use, not by the CDSA
- Contradicts the findings of the trial judge who held that drug addiction is an illness that deprives its victims of the ability to choose to avoid drugs
- This is supported by the common medical definition of addiction
- Crown argument also seems to have a moral element
- However, this is irrelevant when it comes to finding a violation of s. 7
- Can also argue that the Crown is saying that the decision to allow safe injection is a policy question that cannot be reviewed under the Charter
- This kind of argument can only be raised at the PFJ or s. 1 steps
- Only issue before the court here is whether or not Canada has limited the rights of the claimants in a manner inconsistent with the Charter
### Was Decision to not Renew a Violation?
- Established that the statute itself is valid, but was the decision to not renew the s. 56 exemption for Insite valid?
- Court can consider this issue, given the special circumstances of the case as the claimants have already established that their s. 7 rights are at risk here
- Minister of Health does not have absolute discretion, they have to follow the Charter
- Decision to not renew the exemption does engage the s. 7 rights of the claimants
### PFJs
- Court held that the decision to refuse the exemption renewal was both arbitrary, and had a grossly disproportionate effect
- Decisions of the Minister must follow the principles of fundamental justice
- First step to deeming the application of a law arbitrary is figuring out what the goal of the law is
- Goal of the CDSA seems to be protecting health and public safety
- Relationship between this law and the state objective was already proven in *Malmo-Levine*
- Recall that the claimant needs to prove that the limit is inconsistent with the PFJs
- Trial judge found that the previous Ministers had been granting exemption orders to Insite based on a couple of facts
- First, the criminal law has been ineffective in reducing drug use in the DTES
- Second, safe injection reduces the risk of death and disease in these communities
- Third, Insite did not lead to increased crime rates, public drug use, or relapse rates
- Seems overall that giving them an exemption is actually supporting the goal of protecting public health and safety
- Unclear what makes a decision arbitrary, but generally as long as the limit is unnecessary to the objective or is otherwise disconnected from the objective, it's arbitrary
- No matter what definition you pick, this is arbitrary
#### Gross Disproportionality
- Also grossly disproportionate in the sense that Insite has had no negative impact on the state objectives, and denying this benefit to the public is disproportionate to any potential upside
- Only real upside here seems to be a symbolic avoidance of any sort of program that may promote the possession and use of drugs
- Don't need to consider overbreadth, these two violations are already enough
- Seems clear that the claimants have proven that this decision is not in accordance with the PFJs
### Conclusion
- Decision to not renew Insite's s. 56 exemption has prevented injection drug users from accessing key health services, threatening their health
- This engages their s. 7 rights, and creates a limit that is not in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice
- Breaks PFJs because it's arbitrary and grossly disproportionate
- No s. 1 arguments were made, but would not be justified anyway because of the aforementioned lack of connection to a state objective
- Overall, the appeal was dismissed and Insite got it's exemption back
