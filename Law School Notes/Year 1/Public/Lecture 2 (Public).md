- Constitution is the fundamental rules regulating the governmental institutions of a country
- In Canada, it's made up of the common law, statutes, conventions, and entrenched constitutional texts
- Goal is to both empower and limit the branches and levels of government
- Example of interpretation is how the courts define the word [[Judicial Review and Interpretation#Reference re Meaning of "Persons" in S. 24 of the 1867 BNA Act|"persons"]]
- Courts refer to history, text, doctrine, prudence, principles, and structure when interpreting constitutional texts
- Also need to follow four principles for interpretation
- Need to ensure the primacy of federalism, democracy, the rule of law, and the protection of minorities
- Some decisions interpret the Constitution very narrowly, limiting its scope to what was originally intended in 1867 (frozen rights)
- Others allow it to develop over time as culture changes (living tree)
- There are three branches of government, the legislative, executive, and judicial
### [[Antecedents of the Charter#Roncarelli v Duplessis|Roncarelli v Duplessis]]
- Statute in question here gave the power to grant and withhold licenses, but did not grant the power to do so arbitrarily
- Issue for this case is the rule of law
- Public power comes from law, and the statute does not grant an unlimited remit to distribute and revoke licenses
- Shows how common law constitutionalism functions in practice
- Statutes can be interpreted in many ways, and will be interpreted in a way that protects the rights of citizens
### Federalism
- Constitution gives different levels of government the exclusive power to regulate specific things within their jurisdictions
- Section 91 of the 1867 Constitution deals with federal powers, s. 92 deals with provincial powers
- Recall that *intra vires* means within the powers of a level of government, *ultra vires* means outside the powers
- To determine this, look at the purpose of the law and see where it lies between ss. 91 and 92
- The traditional view of federalism divides all power between either the federal or provincial government
- However, there seems to be some powers like those over human rights that don't fall into either category
#### [[Antecedents of the Charter#Union Colliery Co v Bryden|Union Colliery Co v Bryden]]
- A provincial statute was deemed *ultra vires* because it's purpose related to naturalization
- Law was for banning non-Canadian Chinese men from working in coal mines
- Wasn't made for the purpose of managing coal mines (s. 91), messed with naturalization which is a federal matter
#### [[Antecedents of the Charter#Cunningham v Tomey Homma|Cunningham v Tomey Homma]]
- A provincial statute banning Japanese people from voting was valid because its purpose related to electoral law
- This is a matter within provincial jurisdiction, and since it divided people based on race not nationality, didn't end up like Bryden
#### [[Antecedents of the Charter#R v Quong Wing|R v Quong Wing]]
- Provincial statute was valid because its purposes related to employment and morality, which are local matters
- Issue was a law preventing Chinese-owned business from hiring white women, supposedly to protect them
- Presumed to be in response to a local issue, and also race-based so outside of federal jurisdiction
### Implied Bill of Rights
- In [[Antecedents of the Charter#Switzman v Elbling|Switzman v Elbling]], a debate rose over whether a law preventing houses from being used to propagate communism was valid
- Majority said this was a criminal issue, which is a federal responsibility
- Another judge said it was a free speech issue, which cannot be regulated by any level of government
- Dissent said that this law is designed to prevent future crime, which is a matter of local jurisdiction
- Law ultimately ruled invalid
- In [[Antecedents of the Charter#Dupond v City of Montreal|Dupond v City of Montreal]], a municipal law banning public gatherings for 30 days was deemed to be valid
- Despite the potential limit on free speech, that freedom is never explicitly stated in the Constitution
- Therefore, it doesn't apply to the local level since it's only explicitly stated in the *Bill of Rights*
### Parliamentary Sovereignty
- Not legislature can bind future legislatures
- Therefore, if a later statute is inconsistent with an earlier statute, then the later statute can take precedent
- Doesn't matter if this is explicitly stated in the new statute or not
### Bill of Rights
- Quasi-constitutional, it's a fundamental statute of Canadian law and regulates the federal government
- However, it's unclear how exactly it affects other statutes because of parliamentary sovereignty issues
- Also, it has no effect on provincial and municipal law
- Protects rights through "manner and form" restrictions
- Later statutes have to explicitly state they are derogating from the Bill of Rights, otherwise they are bound by it
- Parliament can also argue that "manner and form" only affects procedure, and not substantive manners
- Better than nothing, but not very strong protection
### Recap
- We need to be able to describe the rule of law and common law constitutionalism
- Understand the division of power and debates surrounding the implied bill of rights
- Also need to understand what parliamentary sovereignty is, and the status of the Bill of Rights
