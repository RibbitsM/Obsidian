## Objective Theory
- Basically determining whether parties have reached a mutual understanding
- Without understanding and intention from both sides, you cannot have a bargain
- Acceptance must always perfectly match the terms of the offer
- Studying contracts often involves determining what constitutes an offer, and what constitutes an acceptance
- The test is to first see if something we could reasonably consider as an offer was made, then to see if that offer was understood
- Then, we must identify a clear example of an acceptance, which is also understood by the offeror
- This process is called the Objective Theory of Contract Formation
- Applies when at least one person reasonably believes that a contract has been made, which would require a reasonable offer matched by a reasonable acceptance
- Protects against creating contracts when neither party knew there was a contract
### Bilateral v Unilateral Contracts
- Bilateral contracts generally take the form of an exchange, while unilateral contracts are a promise given in exchange for an act
- Usually comes in the form of offering a reward for a specific act or object
### Offers
- Issue with determining offers is telling what is a preliminary negotiation and what is an offer
- Plus, we need to distinguish if the kind of offer taking place even needs to be explicitly stated
- We call the initiation of bargaining an "invitation to treat"
- This is different from an offer as it doesn't indicate a willingness to be bound by the offeree's response
- Two different cases we can use as examples: *Harvey v Facey* and *Canadian Dyers Association v Burton*
- In *Harvey v Facey*, Harvey asked for the lowest price for a piece of land, expressing that he would pay whatever price was named
- Facey replied with the price, but argued in court that simply by responding with the price he was not agreeing to the offer set out by Harvey
- A similar case occurred with opposite results, called [[Lecture 3 (Contracts)|Canadian Dyers Association v Burton]]
- This case also establishes that a written contract is not always required for an offer
- An example of a unilateral offer comes from *Lefkowitz v Great Minneapolis Surplus Store*
- An advertisement for a first come first served offer to buy a black stole for $1 was seen as binding, as it specified a specific time
- If someone met the conditions (showing up first at a certain time), the store was obligated to sell the stole to them for $1
- Owner tried to later specify that this offer was for women only, but as this was not in the ad, it is considered changing the terms of an offer after the fact
