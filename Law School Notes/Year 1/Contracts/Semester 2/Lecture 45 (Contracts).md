### Expectancy Principle
- Sometimes argued that you can't include both capital and income in expectancy calculations
- For example, if a breach deprived the plaintiff of a capital asset, they would not get both the cost of that capital as well as the income generated by the capital, that's double recovery
- However, there are some situations where we do want to include income and capital
- Example would be where a purchaser has defective equipment for a given period of time before they returned the equipment to the seller
- If they lost profit from having defective capital, could give them both the net profit loss as well as the money needed to replace that capital with a similarly depreciated replacement
- Here we are using both capital and income, and this is perfectly legal
### Cost of Performance vs Diminution
- If a builder refused to build a structure, could calculate damages by looking at the difference between the contractor's price and the cost of a replacement contractor
- This is assessing expectancy based on the cost of performance
- On the other hand, you can calculate the difference between the value of the property if the work was performed properly vs improperly
- This approach calculates expectancy based on diminution in value
- Cost of performance is the traditional approach in these kinds of cases, but if the work was done defectively or not in compliance with the contract, may use diminution in value instead
- Especially want to use diminution when it seems clear the owners not fixing the defects
- Another reason to use diminution is where the cost of fixing the construction work would be unreasonable compared the drop in property value
### Loss of Chance
- Courts will sometimes place value on the chance to make a profit for expectancy damages
- In a case called *Chaplin v Hicks*, the plaintiff was a finalist in a competition for actresses
- There were 50 finalists and 12 winners, each would get a three year contract with the manager running the competition that paid about 4 pounds per week
- Final step of the competition was an interview, but the defendant failed to tell the plaintiff where and when the interview was
- Jury awarded 100 pounds in damages, judge said that just because the damages weren't certain, the defendant should still have to pay for their breach
- Calculated damages based on their assessment that she had a 25% chance to win, and the damages were roughly 1/4 of the wage the contract would give her
### Limits on Expectancy Damages
- Three main limits are causation, remoteness, and mitigation
- Damages for breach of contract are limited by the duty to mitigate as well
- Means that you can't recover losses you could have avoided by taking reasonable steps (*Richter v Simpson*)
- Court can also change the date that damages are based on, but it's normally the day of the breach
- Test for remoteness is the same as torts, was it reasonable foreseeable that the loss would result from the breach of contract (*Gagne v McCarthy*)
### Intangible, Punitive, and Liquidated Damages
- Intangible damages are non-physical damages like mental distress
	- Parties need to reasonably expect that one of the goals of the contract was to give a psychological benefit that would cause mental distress if lost
	- Degree of suffering caused by the breach must be enough to warrant compensation (*Fidler v Sun Life Assurance of Canada*)
- Punitive damages are awarded in cases of exceptional malicious or oppressive misconduct
	- Conduct must also offend the court's sense of decency
	- Mostly limited to breaches of fiduciary duty or intentional torts, but also sometimes apply to breach of contract cases
	- Case for this is *Whiten v Pilot Insurance*
- Liquidated damages are pre-set by the parties to be paid upon breach of contract
	- Courts will enforce these kinds of provisions as long as the clause can be construed as something other than a "penalty", which is slightly different
	- Also need to be an actual pre-estimate of damages, won't be enforceable if the liquidated amount is less or more than the actual amount
	- If the pre-estimate is too high, likely to be interpreted as a "penalty" instead and won't be accepted since it doesn't accurately reflect reality and return the plaintiff to their origin
	- Case for this is *Canadian General Electric v Canadian Rubber Co*
