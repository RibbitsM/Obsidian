- The primary rule of construction is that when interpreting a provision of a contract, you should look at the entire agreement
- Individual terms are often explained in relation to the purpose of the agreement, and their relationships with the other terms of the agreement
- Comes from a landmark case called *BG Checo International v BC Hydro & Power Authority*
- Even if there is an obvious meaning of the term when looking at it in isolation, it may mean something else in the context of the whole agreement
- Sometimes, zooming out may make a seemingly clear term vague, in this case you will have to resort to other means of interpretation
- If a term is used in multiple contexts, need to consider all the uses of a specific term
- The way a term is used in one part of an agreement may be used as evidence to explain what it was intended to mean in another part of the agreement
- One place where the principle is especially common is where there are inconsistencies between two terms
- To resolve an inconsistency between a general term and a specific term, can read the specific term as being an exception or qualification of the general term
- In *BG Checo*, the agreement required the defendant to clear the land where the parties wanted to build a hydro transmission line
- General part of the agreement stated that the contractor was responsible for satisfying themselves with the site conditions and were responsible for any misunderstandings relating to the site conditions
- Contractor claimed damages for the hydro company not clearing the site, and said that the general term didn't prevent the suit because the specific term was an exception
- Contractor's claim was ultimately successful, specific term qualified the broad exclusion of liability for matters relating to the work site
### Give Effect to All Parts of the Agreement
- Means that no term of the agreement should be considered redundant
- We assume that all the terms are in there for a reason, and interpretation should be done in a way that gives meaning to all terms
- In some cases, there will be unavoidable redundancies in the contract, recall that this is just a presumption
- Overall, similar terms will be distinguished from each other, and all terms should ideally have some sort of meaningful interpretation
### Avoid Unreasonable Outcomes
- Whether a certain interpretation leads to an unreasonable result is evidence against that interpretation
- More unreasonable the result, the more unlikely it is that the parties intended that
- If parties to intend to create a term with an unreasonable or absurd outcome, they need to make it abundantly clear that they intended to do this
- Kind of an extension of the principle that the intention of the parties is the primary goal
- Mostly arises when deciding between multiple different interpretations, look at what the potential outcomes are and remove the one with the most unreasonable result
- Reasonability is assessed given the context of the agreement, so things like the kind of agreement and industry practice are relevant
### *Contra Proferentem*
- Means "against the offeror", engages when there is an ambiguous term in an agreement
- Basically means that an ambiguous term will be interpreted against the interests of the person who put that term in the contract
- Goal is to protect people from being hurt by ambiguous terms put in by the other party by giving the innocent party the benefit during interpretation
- Especially relevant for limitation of liability clauses, almost always there will be one party that put in the clause to the detriment of the other party
- Again, this is mainly used for choosing between multiple interpretations of a term
- Look at the outcomes, and pick the one that benefits the party who didn't put it in
- This is a tool of last resort, should only be used if other methods of resolving ambiguity have been exhausted
- Also only applies if you can pretty clearly tell who put the provision in the contract
- Does technically work for non-limitation of liability cases, but it's mostly limited to those cases
- Need to have pretty clear proof that the term was inserted by one party, and it's really easy to prove that with limitation of liability clauses
- If you have multiple "last resort" doctrines, doesn't really matter which one applies first, but this is unusual because most of these doctrines have specific requirements to apply
### Specifics Followed by Generalities
- Also called *ejusdem generis*
- Applies where a provision lists a number of items that share some common characteristic and ends with a general term, the scope of the general term is limited by the characteristics of the terms that preceded it
- For example, no liability for injury caused by fires, floods, storms, and other inevitable accidents
- In this case, the "other inevitable accidents" would be limited to natural disasters (*Saner v Bilton*)
### Restrictive Effect of Explicit References
- An explicit reference to a particular person, place, or thing may amount to an exclusion of alternatives that were not mentioned explicitly
- For example, a deed transferring some properties mentioned that the fixtures were included in the transfer for some properties
- Because this was only mentioned for a few properties, the implication is that the fixtures were not included in those other properties
- Suggests some degree of intention on the part of the drafter to exclude unmentioned things
### Preference for Amendments of Printed Terms
- If parties have made physical written or typed amendments to the terms of the contract that cause some sort of conflict, the amendments take precedent
- Idea here seems to be that the act of making a written amendment shows a clear intention
- Also applies mostly in cases where the contract is a standard form and the parties had to resort to cruder means to reflect their intentions
### Preference for Earlier of Two Inconsistent Terms
- Courts will try to find an interpretation that removes conflict, but where this is impossible, the first provision appearing in the agreement takes precedent
- This is a last resort principle, and is only used because one term must be repugnant to the contract because there is no other way to resolve the conflict
- Idea is that the first term that was put in the contract is more reflective of the initial intention of the parties
- Could also argue that the later term is equally important because it may reflect the parties changing their initial intentions over time
- Overall it seems like the courts just needed to make an arbitrary rule to resolve this issue
### Preference for Interpretations Maintaining Validity
- Similar to the avoiding absurd results principle, except here we are trying to avoid an interpretation that makes the contract illegal or invalid
- If there is no other option, obviously the contract is just illegal then
- Again, this is used for choosing between multiple interpretations by eliminating interpretations that meet some requirement, in this example that requirement is illegality
- Court wants to give effect to an agreement, and can't give effect to an illegal agreement
- Thus, they will interpret the agreement in a way that makes it legal, as long as that interpretation is still reasonable
### Correction of Obvious Errors
- If it seems obvious that the parties made an error in drafting the agreement, court can just ignore the error and pretend it was written correctly
- Requirement for evidence here is pretty strong, needs to be a very obvious error
- Also applies to obvious omissions, if a phrase used multiple times in a contract is missing a word in one context, that was probably an error
