- Couple different remedies for misrepresentation, one is rescission and a restoration of the original positions of the parties
- In thee case of fraudulent or negligent misrepresentation, can sue in tort
- Final remedies arise where a misrepresentation inducing a contract contains an implicit promise that the representation was true
- In this case, that promise may be considered either part of the contract, or a collateral contract subsidiary to the main contract, allowing a claim for breach of contract
- Damages are based on the expectation of the parties, which can be more than what you would get for suing in tort
### Heilbut, Symons & Co v Buckleton
- To establish this claim that a promise is part of a contract, plaintiff must show the representation was intended to be a warranty
- This is a collateral contract where the defendant promises some matter is true
- Different from how we use the word "warranty" in the context of warranties and conditions
- In this case, the defendants were rubber merchants selling a bunch of shares in something called the Filisola Company
- Plaintiffs bought a bunch of shares, defendants told them that the Filisola Company is a rubber company, but this wasn't true
- This ended up being an innocent misrepresentation
- Court found there was no intention to create a collateral contract regarding the nature of the Filisola company and thus there was no breach of contract
### Dick Bentley Productions v Harold Smith (Motors)
- Lord Denning held that a representation that a car had only 20k miles after being refitted with a new engine was a warranty
- If a representation is made to induce the other party into joining a contract and the inducement is successful, creates a *prima facie* case for making a warranty
- Seems to suggest that if the misrepresentation was truly innocent, this fact can be raised as a defence to a claim of breach of contract
- On these facts, the access to information on the part of the defendant meant that the classification of the misrepresentation was irrelevant
- But it seems possible that the defence of innocent misrepresentation does exist
- Seller would have been able to easily find the history of the vehicle
- Means that where the representor has access to information the representee does not and the latter party relies on the truth of the former's statement, that's a warranty
- Also relaxed the previous requirements by not needing mutual intent to create a collateral contract, this was the case in *Heilbut*
### Representation as Warranty
- Sometimes, courts will find a warranty to provide information pertinent to a contract
- Where this information was misrepresented, to establish breach of the warranty, also need to show that the misrepresentor acted carelessly
- In a case called *Esso Petroleum v Mardon*, a tenant was induced into a lease on a service station because Esso presented a report overestimating the volume of business at the station
- Unrealistic that the company was guaranteeing there would be that much business
- However, there was an implicit warranty that the report they gave would be accurate and prepared carefully, instead it was done sloppily with incorrect information
- This was enough to be a breach of contract'
- Usually these kinds of pre-contract misrepresentations can be defeated by an "entire agreement" clause, but not always
- In *Sodd Corporation v Tessis*, the agreement specified that no warranty or condition was expressed or could be implied in the contract
- However, the court found that the clause didn't exclude a warranty in a collateral contract
### Contractual Representations
- Representations can also appear as explicit warranties in an agreement, this is especially common in commercial agreements
- Example would be where the assets of a business are being sold
- These agreements usually have a "Representations and Warranties" section that specify that the seller represents whatever it contains and that the buyer is relying on these representations
- Just because these are described as representations doesn't mean they're not also part of the agreement itself
- Since they are subject to a promise of truth, can allow breach of contract if they're not
- Innocent party would be able to sue for damages, and in the case of a repudiatory breach, can disaffirm or terminate the agreement
