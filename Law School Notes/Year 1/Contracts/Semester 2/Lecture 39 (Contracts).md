### Tercon Continued
- Recall that we're talking about the enforceability of exclusion clauses
- First step is to determine if the exclusion clause blocks the claim 
- Second step is to see if the clause is unconscionable (focus on inequal bargaining power)
- Third step is to see if policy dictates that the clause should be removed
- Contract A of a tendering process contained an exclusion clause, key phrase was that participating in the process implied acceptance of the clause
- Tercon argued that since two bidders had formed a joint venture during the bidding process to submit a singular bid, they were no longer a qualified bidder (round 1 limited to 6 specific bids)
- Recall that there's an implied term in Contract A to only accept qualified bidders
- Province raised the exclusion clause to defend themselves
- Majority of the SCC (5-4) found that the test failed on the first step
- Clause was not available because it didn't apply in these circumstances, minority found that all three steps were met
#### Majority Reasoning
- Majority mainly relied on the implied obligation to only accept compliant bids
- Once the province had breached it's obligations by accepting a non-compliant bid, the process had changed from the original process created by Contract A
- Therefore, the remaining bidders were no longer participating in Contract A and were no longer bound by the exclusion clause
- Clause barred parties "participating in this process", court found that this language was at least ambiguous as to what the process was
- Because of *contra proferentum*, can resolve this ambiguity in the favour of Tercon
- Dissenting judges rejected this argument, saying that the exclusion clause applied to everyone originally in Contract A, the meaning was clear
- Main disagreement was whether allowing a non-compliant bid fundamentally changed the nature of the process the bidders were participating in
### Dissenting Opinion
- When it came to the analysis of step 2 of the *Tercon* test, both parties had roughly equal levels of bargaining power, so no unconscionability concerns arose
- This is pretty common when it comes to commercial transactions, businesses and other commercial actors generally have equal bargaining power/ability
- Unclear whether this term-specific version of unconscionability applies to consumers
- For step 3, the court found that the bad conduct in this case was not serious enough to warrant violating the exclusion clause
- Argued that the standard would be conduct approaching criminality or fraud, pointed to *Plas-Tex* as an example of case where conduct was much more serious
### Post-Tercon: Step 1
- Most courts have decided these issues by using the normal principles of contractual interpretation, mainly *contra proferentum*
- In a case called *Chamberlin v Canadian Physiotherapy Association*, the plaintiff was suing for injuries caused during a physiotherapy training program
- Court interpreted a waiver of "any and all claims" of not specifically barring claims for negligence
- Generally speaking, exclusion clauses are designed to prevent claims relating to the contract like claims for breach of contract, not negligence claims
- This is why courts have often interpreted broad exclusion clauses to not exclude negligence claims unless that is specifically mentioned
- Also argued that the contract was designed for an education course on physiotherapy, but the injury came because the plaintiff was being subjected to physiotherapy treatment
	- Lot of weight put on this point, seems that it was important that the injury came from something arguably outside the contemplation of the contract
- Another example of courts allowing negligence claims was *Canada Steamship Lines v The King* 
- Court held that if you can interpret an exclusion clause to not include negligence claims, you should interpret it in that way
- Even if the exclusion clause explicitly prevents negligence claims, can interpret it in a way that allows a claim for willful misconduct (*Meditek Lab Services v Purolator*)
- Court wants exclusion clauses to be extremely specific to be successful
### Step 2
- Recall that the idea of having an "unconscionable term" is unique to the *Tercon* test
- However, the test you use for this is identical to the *[[Lecture 23 (Contracts)|Uber v Heller]]* test
- Need to find inequality of bargaining power, and an improvident bargain
- Seems that the court will assume there is some improvidence coming from the exclusion clause, and mainly focuses on the equality of bargaining power
- Before *Uber*, the standard for determining inequality was pretty high, arguable that this case actually lowered that standard
- Also important to note that *Uber* created the possibility of using unconscionability to remove specific terms of the contract, hasn't come to pass yet
### Step 3
- Dissenting opinion suggested that conduct approaching serious criminality or egregious fraud for the policy concerns to kick in
- Up until this point, all you needed was that the clause had an unfair and unreasonable effect on the case
- Dissent did also raise *Plas-Tex* as an example, which was less serious but still involved significant risk of damage to property and health
- Stated that policy concerns should only beat freedom of contract where the harm to the public health
