- First enforceability topic
- Tool used to draw the line between promises that are enforceable and not enforceable
- Key question for the law of contracts is which agreements we should uphold
- Principle of consideration does this by stating that a promise has to be bought
- Promise must be given for something of value in exchange
- This is what consideration means, something must come from each person in a transaction
- A gift is not a contract, value only flows one way
- Another rule to use is by looking at the formality of the agreement
- For example, common law establishes that agreements made under seal are always enforceable
- Pretty uncommon now, but used to be a lot more common
- Don't use red wax seals anymore, usually done with a stamp or a sticker
- Second major tool used to divide between enforceable and non-enforceable promises, but consideration is the main one
- Described as an act or refrain from an act by one party or promise to do so, as a price for a promise on the other party
- Uses the term "forebear", basically means refrain or don't do
- In bilateral contracts, this is pretty simple to establish
- In a unilateral contract, performing the requested act is interpreted as providing the consideration in exchange for the reward
- Consideration can be anything considered to be of value to the promisor
- Some things may not be objectively considered valuable, but this is a subjective measure
- Can even constitute a disadvantage to the other party, as long as it has value to one party
- Examples could be promising not to do a certain thing as consideration
- Another interesting scenario is with bank guarantees, where a benefit is conferred by the promissee on a third party
- Promise is made by the guarantor to protect a loan in exchange for a benefit to another person
- Guarantor doesn't get a benefit, but the bank suffers because they need to give money to the borrower
- Benefit in an agreement doesn't need to move between the contracting parties, can go to a third party
- As long as consideration moves from the promisee, it's all good
- In pretty much every case, the promisee will suffer some kind of detriment for consideration to be established
- Famous case for consideration is *Hamer v Sidway* where a nephew promises to not use alcohol or tobacco or swear until 21, and will receive $5000 from his uncle
- Uncle didn't pay out when he turned 21, but the nephew tried to get the money after he died
- Uncle's estate argued that the promise actually benefitted the nephew
- However, the court determined that the promise restricted his lawful freedom of action, which can constitute consideration
### Certainty
- If a promise is too vague, it won't count as consideration
- Uses the example of a promise to give a "good price"
### Firm Offers
- This is an offer to buy something at a fixed price asked to be open for a certain amount of time
- Held to be not enforceable unless the owner does something to "purchase" the promise
### Illusory Consideration
- Relates to agreements where there is only the appearance of consideration
- Classic example is a conditional acceptance, agreement to buy something depending on a future decision, like "I'll buy it after I take a look at it"
### Implied Consideration
- In some cases the court will imply a promise in a case to establish consideration
- Classic example was *Wood v Lucy Lady Duff-Gordon* where a fashion designer made an exclusive agreement with a promoter, but the agreement had no promise to promote the defendant's designs
- Court implied a promise to do so in the contract in order to establish consideration
### Peppercorn
- Means that one promises have been exchange, law doesn't care about the proportionality of the exchange
- Doesn't have to be equivalent, can offer a "peppercorn" for a house if both parties agree
### Nominal Consideration
- However, the court will not uphold contracts where there is a very small amount of consideration in some cases
- For example, if there is evidence of fraud or coercion
### Forbearance
- Usually, a promise to refrain from doing something is considered proper consideration
- Uses the example of *Royal Bank of Canada v Kiska*
### Charitable Subscriptions
- Gifts are not considered proper consideration, but promises to make a gift to charity may be
- However, according to *Dalhousie College v Boutilier Estate* this is not the case unless the charity promises to do specific things in return
- May be able to argue that the purpose of the charity can implicitly do this
- Also been cases where a building or something is named in return
### Warranties
- At common law, warranties are not enforceable unless the buyer buys it directly from the manufacturer
- Statutes that adjust this for consumer protection, but this is how it works at common law
### Past Consideration
- What happens if one of the benefits is something that happened in the past?
- Generally, past consideration is unenforceable
- Leading case is *Eastwood v Kenyon*
- Guardian of the defendant's wife made a loan to pay for the wife's education after her father died during infancy
- When the defendant married her, he promised to pay off the loan
- Held that this was unenforceable as the actions had been taken in the past
- Another example was where an employer promised to provide a retirement bonus in exchange for their past service, held unenforceable (*Blair v Western Mutual Benefit Association*)
- One exception is where services are made and there is an expectation of compensation and the promise is made afterwards (*Pao On v Lau Yiu Long*)
- Example would be moving some stuff for a friend, and they promise to pay you back after
- Also, a promise can revive an obligation that was no longer legally actionable
- You can promise to pay an existing debt that had an expired statute of limitations, and this will work, rendering the original debt enforceable
