- Done with intention to create legal relations, moving on to bargaining in good faith
- This is not a particularly well-defined area of law, spread out across a number of different cases
- Considered in three different situations
- First is issues of certainty of terms
- When parties have made a [[Lecture 6 (Contracts)|agreement to agree]], one party may sue the other party for failing to negotiate on those terms on the ground s that they breached their duty to bargain in good faith
- Second issue is when an agreement has a renewal clause
- In these situations, the agreement will include a clause requiring the parties to negotiate some future agreement
- Usually an extension of the existing agreement or a renewal of an existing agreement at a different price
- A party seeking to enforce this clause can argue the other party must negotiate in good faith
- Final example is in tendering processes
- According to *[[Lecture 4 (Contracts)#Tenders and Auctions|Ron v Engineering]]*, issuers of a call to tender must treat all bidders "fairly and equally"
- Generally considered very similar to a requirement to bargain in good faith
### Agreements to Negotiate in Good Faith
- Leading decisions of UK courts on this matter have consistently come from cases dealing with agreements to agree and uncertainty of terms
- These courts tend to reject the notion of the requirement to negotiate in good faith
- Judge in *Walford v Miles* argued that the idea of negotiating in good faith goes counter to the basic concept of negotiations, which are inherently adversarial
- This precedent has been pretty influential on Canadian decisions
- Canadian courts will usually find agreements to agree unenforceable, unless there are other factors at play
- Usually means that terms to negotiate in good faith are unenforceable, but there are a few exceptions
- If you actually have a clause in your agreement that specifically requires negotiation in good faith, this is enforceable in Canada in two contexts
- First example is with renewal clauses
- Agreements to negotiate in good faith on a renewal clause may be enforceable if there is some sort of objective standard to evaluate the reasonableness of that agreement
- For example, when negotiating the renewal of a rental, the agreement must mention that the negotiation will be for a price around the "market rate" or "fair market value"
- However, there has recently been a movement towards recognizing agreements to negotiate in good faith without this "objective standard" requirement
- Comes from a very important SCC case called *[[Bhasin v Hrynew]]* 
### Objective Standard
- Canadian courts have held that an objective standard for measuring the outcome of a negotiation is required to uphold an agreement to negotiate in good faith
- Main example is *Empress Towers v Bank of Nova Scotia*
- There was a 5 year lease between the parties that could be renewed for an additional 5 years at a "prevailing market rental rate" at the time of renewal as agreed on by the parties
- The fact that this rate had to be agreed on mutually was found to imply that both parties must negotiate in good faith to come to an agreement on the rate
- Additionally, the expectations of said rate would not be unreasonable compared to the market rental rate
- Therefore, the court found that this clause implied a requirement to bargain in good faith and not to reject a reasonable offer from the other party
- Otherwise, one party would be able to avoid renewal by asking for an unreasonably high or low price, which could be considered bargaining in bad faith
- Here, the presence of the objective standard was key, because otherwise there would be no metric to measure whether or not the parties negotiated in good faith
### *[[Bhasin v Hrynew]]*
- Case determining how rights are exercised within a contract
- For example, if a contract grants one party the ability to terminate the agreement, that party must use that right in good faith only
- Otherwise, the person who used the right improperly will be held liable
- SCC argued that the performance of contracts in good faith is a fundamental part of contract law
- Mainly comes from the precedent of previous "performance" cases
- SCC identified four rules for performing a contract in good faith
	1. Parties must cooperate in order to achieve the objectives of the contract
	2. Parties must not improperly exercise a discretionary power under the contract
	3. Parties must not seek to evade contractual duties
	4. Parties must perform the contract honestly
- This last one means that the parties cannot knowingly lie to each other or mislead each other
- Does not apply to bargaining before a contract is made, but may apply to agreements to agree
- *Bhasin* rules only apply to negotiations that are already partly governed by contract
- For example, a renewal agreement like we saw in *Empress*
- Overall, this decision helps mitigate the argument against agreements to agree that they are too vague, the *Bhasin* decision helps establish clear rules for these situations
- However, this doesn't provide a precedent to overrule the requirement of an objective standard
### *BC Ltd v TimberWest Forest Corp*
- Dispute over the negotiation of annual logging rates in a timber agreement between a logging company and a land management company
- Agreement settled the logging rates for the first year, and determined that the rates would be negotiated in good faith for the following years
- This worked for two years, and on the fourth year, the defendant wanted to end the agreement
- To do this, they offered much more difficult terms for the rates that year, and ended the agreement when the plaintiff did not agree
- Case went to the BC Supreme Court, arguing that the defendant did not negotiate in good faith
- The defendant's terms for the fourth year were unreasonable compared to their previous terms, and were advanced with the ulterior motive of ending the agreement
- The Court applied *Bhasin*, and found that the defendant had potentially broken all four rules
- Was not trying to cooperate, were improperly using their power of negotiation, were trying to evade their duties, and were acting dishonestly to do so
- Ultimately ruled for the plaintiff
- No objective standard provided in the initial agreement, but the court still upheld the requirement to negotiate in good faith
- However, remember that this is still a renewal situation, not a pre-contractual agreement
### Summary
- Canadian courts have established that requirements to negotiate in good faith are enforceable in two situations
- First, they are enforceable when the contract sets an objective standard that can be used to measure the outcome of a negotiation
- Second, all agreements to negotiate in good faith are subject to the [[Bhasin v Hrynew|four rules]] set out in *Bhasin* regardless of whether there is an objective standard or not
- Still fairly unclear exactly how the interaction works out with the *Bhasin* rules and the objective standard requirement
- Likely that the Supreme Court will iron out this issue some time in the near future
