- Appellate courts have limited jurisdiction
- Usually don't just look at the evidence and agree or disagree, they are brought in to rule on *errors of law*
#### Error of Law
- While the accused has more opportunity to appeal, either the accused or the Crown may appeal on grounds of a *error of law*
- This can mean an incorrect ruling by the judge, incorrect reasoning, or an [[Evidential Burden and Burden of Proof|improper explanation to the jury]]
- Not all errors will overturn a verdict, less serious errors result in a new trial or a different verdict
- Appeal for an *error of law* is the most common basis of an appeal
- Results in findings of law, meaning the appellate court may overturn the decision of the lower court
- The other options is a finding of fact, which is very rare and only results if there is a palpable and overriding difference in findings of fact between trial and appellate court
#### Unreasonable Verdict
- Accused can also appeal a verdict unsupported by the evidence, also called an *unreasonable verdict*
- Test for this is that no reasonable jury with proper instruction would have convicted on the evidence presented
- High threshold, but not impossible to meet
- Case that set out this test was *R v Yebes*, where the appeal was carried out by the UBC Innocence project
- Usually results in a acquittal is successful
#### Miscarriage of Justice
- Accused can also appeal if there is a *miscarriage of justice*
- This means that the jury was not impartial, or the Crown engaged in misconduct
- Basically means that something went wrong during the trial, often meaning the jury was affected in a way that they could not deliver an unbiased verdict
