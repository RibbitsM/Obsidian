- Elements of a criminal offence are found in the provision defining it
- Also subject to any complementary common law rules
- Usually divided into *actus reus* and *mens rea*
- *actus reus* is the act in question, and the *mens rea* is the mental elements required for the crime
- For example, taking someone else's belongings is the act behind provisions against theft, and the knowledge that the object was owned by someone else is the mental element
- These may not be explicitly stated in the statute, especially the *mens rea*
- The section of the [[Criminal Code]] that creates an offence will also include the *actus reus* of that offence
- Several possible components of the *actus reus*
### Conduct
- Sometimes multiple acts can satisfy the requirements of an offence
- For example, the offence of murder can be done with any method, but the offence of discharging a firearm with intent to harm is a lot less specific
- A offence can also involve a failure to act, also called an omission
- No matter what, an act or omission must be voluntary for criminal liability to be imposed
### Circumstances
- Some offences will require a specific context
- For example, driving is normally legal, but not in a circumstance where you are under the influence of alcohol or drugs
- Offences can also be outside of specific circumstances, for example sexual assault requires the other party to be non-consenting
### Consequences
- Some offences prohibit conduct regardless of the outcome
- For example, intent to commit theft is just as much of an offence as if the theft was fully carried out
- However, some offences require specific outcomes, murder requires proof that the victim died
- An offence may include any and all of these three kinds of elements
- Categorization of elements is not necessary, this is just a framework to help you identify the elements
### *Mens Rea*
- Means "guilty mind" in Latin, refers to the fault element of an offence
- May correspond to elements of *actus reus* or may be unrelated
- Fault can be measured on a subjective or objective basis depending on the offence
- Subjective means the accused had the intention to perform the *actus reus*
- Most offences require this, and is usually connected to one or all of the other elements
- Objective fault considers what the average person should know or would intend in a given circumstance
- Has a higher standard compared to negligence, meaning it requires proof of conduct markedly different from a reasonable person
- Some offences explicitly mention the requirement, for example s. 332 (theft) mentions "with intent to deprive"
- If there is no indication of a *mens rea* requirement, assume that there is a requirement for subjective fault BRD in at least one element
### Intent
- Means that a person purposely carried out an act, not accidentally
- They acted with the purpose of bring about a consequence that they were substantially certain would happen
### Knowledge
- Awareness that a circumstance did or did not exist
- Does not include wilful blindness
- Means that if you suspect the existence/non-existence of a circumstance but do not try to confirm, that is knowledge
### Recklessness
- Happens if you foresee a specific risky consequence, but proceed anyway
- Less than full intention or knowledge, but is usually enough to satisfy subjective mental fault
- These different variations of subjective fault correspond to conduct, circumstances, and consequences
- Some offence have *mens rea* requirements unrelated to any of the concrete elements
- At the same time, not every act element requires a mental element
- Sexual assault requires proof that touching was sexual, but not knowledge or intention that the actions were sexual
- Some negligence offences like manslaughter and dangerous driving require objective *mens rea* as opposed to subjective
- Some offences are [[Strict Liability]], meaning they do not require *mens rea*
- Try to relate possible mental elements to concrete acts
- Also, where *mens rea* is required, it must be at the same time as the act
- The intention must correspond with the time of the act
### Use and Interpretation of Statutes
- Naturally, the most used statute in criminal law is the Criminal Code
- The most recent version of the Code was revised in 1985, along with all other federal statutes
- All statutes are divided into sections and subsections
- At the beginning of a sentence, you should spell Section in full
- Because the revision was so long ago, some new provisions have been added using decimal numbering
- First step to interpretation is finding the relevant offence
- To do this in the *Criminal Code*, look for the section number or in the relevant Part of the Code
- Make sure to find meanings for every word in the provision(s)
- Key words will also have statutory definitions, usually in the section or the first section of the Part
- Also may be in the general definition section of the Code (s. 2)
- The *Interpretation Act* also contains definitions of key statutory words, and each province also has their own *Interpretation Act*
- When this fails, look for judicial definitions
- If this fails, start with the dictionary definition of the word and look to other methods of interpretation, the *Interpretation Act* can help with this
### Introduction to Parties to a Crime
- Crime is often committed by multiple parties, each with varying levels of participation
- Main parties provision of the Criminal Code is s. 21
- Party is anyone who commits a crime and anyone who aids or abets that person
- Abets means encourages
- All of these people are equally guilty under the law and liable to full punishment
- The person who actually committed the crime is referred to as the principal offender, or just the principal
- There can also be co-principals in some cases, like if three people rob a bank they are all principal offenders
- Aiders and abettors do not have to commit any elements of the offence, they just have to do something to assist the principal in committing the offence
- For example, the getaway driver for the bank robbers, or the person that sold them their guns (assuming they knew they would be used to rob a bank)
- If someone becomes involved after the crime was committed like someone sheltering the robbers at their house, they are considered an *accessory after the fact*
- Still subject to punishment, but to a lesser degree
### Included Offences
- Generally, the offence can only be convicted of the offence they are charged of
- Only exception is that they can be convicted of an offence "included" in the one they are charged of
- Sometimes this is explicitly stated, for example second degree murder is included with first degree murder according to s. 662(2)
- Means that if the Crown cannot prove planning for first degree, they can still convict for second degree murder
- Section 662(1) states that a count is divisible in three cases
- First, it is included if it exists "as described in the enactment creating it"
- This means that if there was an offence that has to be committed for another offence to be present, it is included
- For example, assault is included in the offence of assault causing bodily harm
- Is a lesser offence is a necessary step to prove a more serious offence, the lesser offence is included
- Second, it is included if it exists "as charged in the count"
- The charge drafted by the Crown may be worded in a way that includes multiple offences
- For example, a charge that says "attempted murder of X" only includes murder, but "attempting to murder X by stabbing them with a knife" includes assault with a weapon
- Third, "an attempt to commit" is always included (s. 662(1)(b))
- This means that if an attempted version of an offence exists, it is always included in the full offence
- For example if the charge is theft of a motor vehicle, this also includes attempting to steal a motor vehicle
- Can be relevant if the accused is charged of an act but evidence only proves that they attempted that act but were not successful
- This is why it's so important to read the indictment, the drafting of a charge can include and exclude charges you would never think of
- If the indictment includes a section number, read that section and see if there is an included offence
- Finally, you can also look at case law to see if an offence has been interpreted as containing an included offence as well
### Air of Reality and Included Offences
- In a trial by jury, the judge decides whether or not an included offence should be "left with the jury", meaning that they can use it as a possible path to conviction
- This is determined by the "air of reality" test, the same test used to evaluate defences
- On one hand, it's a bad idea to leave included offences with the jury as it can lead to wrongful conviction when there is light evidence of an included offence
- On the other hand, juries may appreciate a middle route when acquittal is considered unfavourable, but they do not feel confident on the principal offence either
- In this case, we run the risk of the jury convicting on the principal offence simply because they feel they cannot acquit the accused
- By running the air of reality test, we ensure the jury have access to all included offences that are reasonably plausible
- Unique in the sense that either the prosecution or the defence may introduce an included offence, depending on the situation
- For included offences, an "air of reality" means there is a real chance that the accused could be acquitted on the principal offence and convicted on the included offence
- To determine this, a judge must see if a reasonable jury could have reasonable doubt about the elements separating the included and principal offences, but no reasonable doubt of the elements of the included offence
