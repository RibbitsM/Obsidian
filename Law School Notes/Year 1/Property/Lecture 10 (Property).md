- Under the *Expropriation Act*, persons having their property taken have no right to legal action against the expropriating authority
- However, they do have the right to notice, to be informed ahead of time
- Also may request an inquiry into the reasoning behind the taking and the availability of any alternative options
- Will decide whether the expropriation is necessary to achieve the objective of the project, not whether or not the project is necessary
- If there is no inquiry, the approving authority must approve the expropriation, which may or may not be the same as the expropriating authority
- Finally, all landowners are also entitled to compensation for expropriation at market value determined as the value it would have sold at on the open market on the expropriation date
- This is an objective measure of value, not a subjective measure
### Grand Forks
- After mass floodings, the existing flood defences in Grand Forks were overwhelmed
- Described as a once-in-a-century flood, the city asked for money from the government to reinforce their flood defences
- However, there was one neighbourhood called North Ruckle that was located on the floodplain which would need to be demolished to create a dike
- All residents were offered compensation for the expropriation of their property, but three or four residents refused
- Preliminary inquiry found that the approach being used was the best possible option, and no alternative solutions were available
- Recall that compensation is paid at the date of expropriation, so the compensation would be for the value of the property after they had already been flooded
- Estimated as about $80,000 less than the pre-flood value, which was a significant chunk
- City of Grand Forks tried to make existing housing cheaper to compensate for this loss
- Reveals the existence of drawbacks surrounding compensation as a protection for expropriation
### Balmoral and Regent Hotels
- These were two single-occupancy buildings in the DTES that were both owned by a notorious slumlord family and had significant health code violations
- However, city was reluctant to foreclose the properties as they were some of the only low-income housing available in the area
- Appraisal determined that the cost of renovations to raise the buildings to code standards greatly exceeded the value of the properties, meaning the net value was less than zero
- Therefore, the city could expropriate the buildings without paying compensation
- Family sued the city, and they ended up settling for an $11 million payout instead
## Constructive Expropriation
- In direct expropriation, the expropriating authority is seizing your property rights, they now have the interest in your property
- However, there is also indirect expropriation, which is called regulatory taking in the US
- Means effective expropriation, removing property rights via statute
### Pennsylvania Coal Co. v Mahon
- A coal company sold a bunch of land to developers, but reserved the right to continue mining coal on that land
- Any builder who constructed on top of the coal mine has to waive all claims for damages
- Of course, the houses eventually started sinking into the ground because they were built on top of the coal mine
- State government passed a statute banning mining that would cause houses to collapse
- Penn Coal argued that this was effectively taking their property since they sold all the surface rights on their land
- Court defined this as regulatory taking, stating that while property can be regulated, but at a certain point regulation will become taking, meaning compensation is required
- Dissenting judge argues that restrictions imposed to protect public health and safety cannot be considered a taking
- First US case regarding regulatory taking
### Penn Central v New York City
- In the 60s, Penn Station was demolished to build Madison Square Gardens in its place
- In response, the government passed the *Heritage Preservation Act* to ensure that no other old beautiful landmarks were redeveloped into ugly buildings
- Later, the Pennsylvania Railway named "Penn Central" bought the New York Central Station with the intent of redeveloping it into some modern monolith
- City blocked this on the grounds of the *Heritage Preservation Act*
- Argued that this was regulatory taking, but the city pointed out that this did not stop Penn Central from continuing to operate Central Station as a train station
- 3 factors for determining regulatory taking
- First, does the regulation significantly diminish the value of the property?
- Second, is there a reasonable expectation of profit backed by investment, and was there prior notice or an expectation of regulation?
- City pointed out that this was already a very regulated area
- Third, was the government action intended to single out a particular property, or was it general?
### Lucas v South Carolina Coastal Council
- Centers around two coastal developments in a region called the Isle of Palms
- Area is basically just a bunch of sandbars
- State created a beach management act intended to limit waterfront development and preserve the integrity of the sand dunes
- Lucas purchased two plots of land on the Isle of Palms, right before the beach management act was passed
- Argued that it amounted to regulatory taking as they could now not develop on their land
- Existing precedent required a taking to physically prevent access to their property
- Also had the requirement that regulatory taking only occurs when all economic value is removed from the land
- Question is to what extent a state can regulate land use without being required to compensate
- Court argues that if all economic value is gone, makes it more likely that the owner is suffering disproportionately from the regulations
- Also means that their land is now economically idle
- Can only regulate without compensating to the extent that the regulations align with the principles of nuisance
- Pretty significant restriction on the ability of the state to regulate land use
- Effectively advocating for additional protections for private property
- Lucas ended up building on both plots, but of course the lots were extremely low-lying and were swamped by the next hurricane
### Belfast Corp v O.D. Cars Ltd
- City of Belfast refused an application from a car company to build a car factory
- Car company sued, arguing this was de facto taking
- Existing rule was that compensation must be given for taking unless the statute explicitly says so, but this technically allowed the possibility that compensation could be given even in the case that the owner still held property rights
### Mariner Real Estate Ltd v Nova Scotia
- Concerns a beachfront property on Kingsburg Beach
- Low-lying beachfront property, very similar to the *Lucas* case
- Province of Nova Scotia passed an act restricting development on beachfront property, and the owner sued arguing this was *de facto* taking
- Court determined there were two governing principles for *de facto* taking
- First, valid legislation may significantly restrict an owners enjoyment of private land
- Second, courts can order compensation for this restriction only if the legislation authorizes it
- Different from the US context where the Constitution contains property rights
- For direct expropriation, you have to compensate unless specified otherwise
- For indirect expropriation, you don't have to compensate unless specified otherwise
- Argues that in Canada, restrictive regulation is normal, and to determine *de facto* expropriation you need to consider reasonable use in the Canadian context
- Set out a two part test
- First, have all reasonable private uses been taken away by regulation
- Second, has the regulating authority received some corresponding benefit
- Pretty clear divide between this and the US approach in *Lucas*
- Scalia stated all "economic value" has to be removed, Cromwell says that all "use" has to be removed
### [[CPR v City of Vancouver]]
- Used all of the principles established by *Mariner Real Estate*
- In this case, the CPR argued that there is a common law basis for compensation for indirect expropriation, pointing to *Mariner Real Estate*
- Supreme Court determined a two part test that was almost identical to Cromwell's test
- Order is reversed, and regulation has to remove all reasonable uses of property
- In this case, the City did not really get anything, just an assurance that the land will be used according to its wishes
- Same as how the City regulates any other land in Vancouver
- Even if people are walking on the railway, the City didn't turn it into a park
- Also, not all reasonable uses have been taken as it can still be used as a railway, which is arguably a very reasonable use of a rail line
- Recall that in Canada, even if all economic uses have expired, it doesn't matter
### R v Tener
- Defendant ran a mining company in Wells Gray, which was soon after turned into a provincial park which prevented him from mining the land
- In this case, the court determined that this was a loss of all reasonable uses, and compensation was justified
