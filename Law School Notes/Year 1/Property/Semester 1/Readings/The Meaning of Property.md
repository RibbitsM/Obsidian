- The meaning of property is flexible, and changes over time according to the needs and desires of society
- When this meaning changes, it becomes controversial as people view it differently
- The idea of property as something more than physical possession signifies property as a right
- This can be seen even in many fairly primitive societies
- Property can be an enforceable claim to the use of something, or a right to share in a common resource or right
- Property is possession backed up by law
### Common, Private, and State Property
- Common property functions very similarly to private property
- Even among public property like parks and roads, there are often limits to how they can used and how often
- This can be speed limits, hours of operation, water rationing, etc.
- Rather than the state allowing citizens to use their property, it's more like they are enforcing the individual right to use that property
- Common property comes from the state enforcing the guarantee that all individuals can access that property
- On the other hand, private property comes from the enforcement of the right for individuals to exclude others from using their property
- In this case, individuals can also be fake people like corporations ([[Multinationals as Political Actors|Santa Clara vs Southern Pacific]]) 
- There is also property that is not common or private, which comes in the form of state property
- This comes from rights that the state creates and reserves for itself or takes from individuals/corporations
### Misconception of Property as Things
- Property does not refer to the real thing, but rather the legal title to that thing
- This is most evident when we look at leases or property to land in general
- We began to start referring to the objects themselves as property mainly due to the spread of market capitalism
- As land became more commodified, we began to speak more of selling land as selling a property
- While market really exchanged legal rights, it created the illusion of selling the land itself
- As we took for granted the rights to property upheld by the government, the illusion grew stronger
### Need for Justificatory Theories
- Property is thought to need moral justification, as legal rights are often backed up by moral obligations
- Property is not just a right because it comes with enforceable rights, but because it is a fundamental human right
- The legal right to property in any of its permutations must be backed up by public belief that it is morally right
- Failing this, enforcing claims becomes almost impossible
