- Starts with a quote from *Pottinger v Miami* that describes the tension between the need of homeless people to survive and the desire to have nice streets
- Issue is the ban on placing temporary housing in public that comes from the *Parks Regulation Bylaw* and the *Streets and Traffic Bylaw*
- Defendants argue that this provision goes against the right to life, liberty, and security enshrines in s. 7 of the Charter
- Case was at the BC Supreme Court btw
- AG claims that this law does not fall under s. 7, and even if it did it is justifiable under s. 1
### Facts
- Over 1000 homeless people in Victoria, and 104 shelter beds
- Bylaws do not prevent sleeping in public, only making a temporary house which includes tents, boxes, or any makeshift shelter
- Exposure to the elements can cause health risks including hypothermia
- Some form of temporary housing is necessary to prevent exposure to the elements
### Holding
- Therefore, the bylaws in question are a violation of s. 7
- They impose severe health risks on an already vulnerable population, and is an unjust punishment on the most unfortunate members of society
- Court held that the violation is also not justified under s. 1
### History
- City of Victoria went to court to receive a civil injunction allowing them to enforce the bylaws
- Did this because in October 2005 a tent city began to form in Cridge Park with about 70 people setting up a community of about 20 tents, and two kitchens powered by public outlets
- Notice was distributed to the residents informing them of the impending injunction and advising them to disperse and move to local homeless shelters
- The Acting City Manager argued that police are not instructed to awaken any homeless people who are sleeping during daytime hours or are not obstructing sidewalks or bus shelters
- Also said that non-structural weather repellant covers like tarps are permitted for sleeping
- Overall, the number of shelter beds is much smaller than the homeless population
### Analysis
- One question to answer is whether or not the defendants are making a claim about property rights
- Victoria and the AG claim that the defendants are making this about property rights by advocating for camping on public property, which is not a Charter right
- Claim that defendants are appropriating public property for private use
- While this case started because of Cridge Park, the defendants just want to right to shelter themselves, not necessarily camp on public property
- Main issue is that homeless people cannot even have a temporary shelter to be taken down in the morning
- Homeless people must sleep, and inevitably many of them will have to sleep on public property
- Whether they do this under a tarp or a tent should not be an issue
- Argues that the use of public parks for sleeping does not diminish the use of that park during the day by other people
- SCC has consistently held that public property cannot be used in the same way as private property
- Public property is held for the benefit of the public, which includes homeless people
- Depriving the homeless people of the use of this property deprives them of the right to protect their bodies, which is unjustifiable under the Charter
- Analysis of this provision saw it fails the [[R v Oakes|reasonable limit test]]
- Many other BC cities like Abbotsford have had similar cases that conclude that homeless people are allowed to construct overnight shelters
