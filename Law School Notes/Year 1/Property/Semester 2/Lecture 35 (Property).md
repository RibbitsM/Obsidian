- Four main kinds of Indigenous lands in Canada
- Aboriginal title lands and treaty lands are governed by s. 35 of the Constitution
- Indian reserves and First Nation lands are governed by s. 91(24) which grants the federal government authority over Indians and lands reserved for Indians
### Aboriginal Title Lands
- First example of a formal recognition of this kind of land was between the Tsihlqot'in and BC in 2014, based on the precedent from *Delgamuukw*
- Court recognized aboriginal title to a subset of this claim, and rights on the remainder
- Another example of this is the Nuchatlaht land near Ucluelet
- Comparatively simple title land, only claimed the land, not the sea or foreshore and did not include any land that was already allocated in fee simple
- Claim was based on the watershed, the community had used the watershed for centuries
- SCC rejected this claim, but it could be modified to a smaller claim
- New, much smaller claim was allowed in 2024
- An even more recent claim came from the Cowichan in Richmond in 2025
- Gained a small portion of land along the Fraser River, still under contention because most of the land is owned in fee simple
- Probably the largest aboriginal title claim is the [[Lecture 6 (ISLR)|Haida title agreement]]
- Agreement with BC included a promise to honour existing fee simple interests, which would continue under BC jurisdiction
### Treaty Lands
- Different from title lands because they are the result of more comprehensive title agreements
- Main example for this is the [[Lessons for Indigenous Property|Nisga'a Final Agreement]]
- The Maa-nulth and Tsawwassen agreements are also good examples of this
- Protected under s. 35, but are established mainly through negotiation, and are held differently, for example the Nisga'a hold their land in fee simple
### Reserve Lands
- BC is unique in terms of reserve lands as they are much smaller and more numerous here, different from the system of fewer and larger reserves in other provinces
- Majority of these small reserves are allotted in order to secure access to fish
- Reserves are held by the federal government for the benefit of the Indigenous nations
- Under the *Indian Act*, a reserve is held in common for a band, which are not necessarily nations but are divisions created by the federal government
### [[Nicola Band v Trans-Can Displays]]
- David Shuter claimed to hold 80 acres of land in the Joeyaska reserve next to Merritt
- Signed an agreement with the defendant to put billboards in that land, two years after the Coquihalla was opened which runs through the reserve
- Shuter died about a decade after, and his descendants claimed the right to the land and the income from the billboards
- The Nicola band refused to recognize these claims as they wanted to develop the land themselves
- Shuter acquired some land in a trade with a man named Spahan from another reserve
- Spahan had inherited a bunch of land in Joeyaska, and Shuter had previously bought a portion of land on the Coldwater reserve where Spahan lived
- Two decided to trade their land claims because they could only use land on reserves where they were members
- These land claims were based on traditional and customary use of the land
- How do we reconcile this with the fact that reserve land is owned collectively?
#### Indian Act
- Going back to the *Indian Act*, s. 20 established that possession of reserve land can be allotted to an individual by the band council with the approval of the Minister
- This kind of interest is called a Certificate of Possession that gives the holder the right to possession of whatever land it was issued for
- Closest thing to having a private property interest on a collective reserve
- These certificates could be transferred between band members, but only if it was approved by the Minister
- Only a person entitled to reside on reserve can hold a certificate of possession, but reserve land can be leased to these people
- Two kinds of leases, a designated lease where the band gives the land to Canada who will lease it to a non-band member which partially surrenders the land
- Enabled by s 28(1), and can be authorized for one year by the Minister, and more by the council
- Section 53 enables the management of designated lands, as well as the lease of land held under a certificate of possession via s. 53(3)
- This is the second kind of lease that appears on reserves
#### Outcome
- Shuter didn't have a certificate of possession, but wanted to lease the land to a non-member
- Court found that he can't do this with a traditional or customary claim
- Would only recognize leases for land which was registered according to the *Act*
- What Spahan and Shuter had was never registered and none of the transfers were approved, so none of them had lawful possession of the land
- Court stated that traditional or customary ownership does not exist independently of the *Act* and these kinds of claims are not recognized by the Court
