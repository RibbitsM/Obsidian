### [[Robb v Robb]]
- Recall that the issue in this case was whether the couple held their interest in a co-op housing unit as joint tenants or tenants in common
- Issue was whether the property went into the husband's will or not, if it's joint tenancy then the property goes to the wife instead
- Nothing in the documents formally suggests an intention to hold the land as joint tenants, so we need to look at what the presumption is
- This is determined by statute, s. 11(2) of the *Property Law Act* says they're tenants in common unless the transfer instrument says otherwise
- However, this only applies to land transferred in fee simple, charged, or contracted to be sold
- Court did some statutory interpretation to determine if this applies here, in BC we know that it only applies to fee simple
- Overall, the statutory presumption didn't apply here
- Moved to the common law presumption, which presumes joint tenancy with a couple exceptions
- Only potential exception that applies here is an unequal contribution to the purchase
- Wife paid the full amount, but the husband later contributed by transferring her another property he owned as consideration for her buying the shares/lease
- Overall, it seems that there was joint tenancy here, nothing in the will
### Joint Tenancy or Tenancy in Common?
- Start with the intention, what does the instrument indicate?
- If the intention is to create a joint tenancy, look for the four unities
- If it's unclear, then you need to use a presumption
- If it's fee simple, the statutory presumption of tenancy in common applies
- If it's not, the common law presumption of joint tenancy applies
- If it's a presumption of joint tenancy, then you need to see if an equitable exception applies
	- Unequal contribution, commercial transaction, or mortgage
- Finally, if it's still joint tenancy, need to see if joint tenancy has been severed
## Severance of Joint Tenancy
- There are three main ways to do this, come from *Williams v Hensman*
	- First, the act of one joint tenant on their own share can do it
	- Second, can be done through a mutual agreement
	- Third, can be inferred through mutual dealings between the parties treating the property like it was severed
- Rationale of the first one is that each joint tenant can dispose of their own interest from the joint tenancy, revoking their right of survivorship
### [[Sorenson Estate v Sorenson]]
- Question was whether a joint tenancy between a former husband and wife was severed
- Initially held two adjacent city lots jointly, one vacant and one with the house
- Settlement agreement during the divorce divided the title with the assumption that the vacant lot could be sold
- Mariam got half title to the house and tenancy for life through a lease agreement
- Effectively a life estate, but done through a leasehold interest
- Marshall was to give her $100 per month to support their disabled son, and in exchange she got a security interest in his share of the house
- As he fails to make the payments, his share of the house diminished
- Mariam was diagnosed with terminal cancer, and sought to transfer the beneficial interest in the house to her disabled son
- Signed a will leaving the house to her daughters in trust to her son for his life, to be divided between the children when any of them died
- Finally, she brought an action for partition, ending the co-ownership agreement which usually involves a mandated sale of the property
- On the day of the scheduled hearing, she died and the petition was never heard
- Issue for the court was whether any of these actions severed the joint tenancy
- Key question we need to ask is if any of the four unities were removed
#### Settlement Agreement
- Court dismissed it as the actions of the parties were inconsistent with the argument that the parties intended to sever at this point
- Mariam took further action to try and sever, shows that they didn't intend to sever at this point
- Created separate titles to the two lots, didn't affect the relationship between the co-owners
#### Lease
- Because it was a lease for life, the right of survivorship was not affected
- If either party died, then the surviving party would still get full ownership
- Theoretically, a lease that could last beyond death might sever a joint tenancy, but the case law on this matter is pretty inconsistent
#### Mortgage
- At common law this would sever the tenancy, as the deed to the title is being transferred
- But in the title registration system where a mortgage was just a charge, probably doesn't
- Didn't matter in this case because it was a transfer between the joint tenants
#### Will
- Since wills only distribute property that's part of the estate, it will not affect the joint tenancy because the right of survivorship will take precedent
- Property will never go into the estate if it's still a joint tenancy
#### Partition
- If the action had gone through, the co-ownership relationship would clearly be severed
- Court held that commencing the action alone was not enough
- It was a statement of intention, but it was not an act affecting their share of the tenancy
### Trust
- This was the only action that the court found was an action severing the tenancy
- Declaration of the trusteeship wasn't enough, but the act was
- Because of the [[Pecore v Pecore|presumption of advancement]], this was a valid gift to the son
	- Recall that the presumption only applies to a gift from a parent to a minor child
	- Otherwise, if the child is an adult they hold it as a resulting trust for the parent unless you can show intention to transfer the legal and equitable interest
- But in this case, Mariam was only trying to transfer the equitable interest, not the legal one
- Transfer of an equitable interest will actually sever the joint tenancy, as it is an act on their title
### Operating on Own Share
- Transfer to another counts as severing
- Transferring the property to yourself will also sever tenancy under s. 18 of the *Property Law Act*
- Some provinces require the other joint tenant to be informed, but BC doesn't
- Reason why transfer to yourself works is because it violates the unities of time and title
- The [[BCLI Report on Joint Tenancy|BCLI]] says we have to reform the law here, why do we still have the four unities?
- Granting a life estate gives you a reversionary interest, which severs because the unity of title is disrupted
- Granting a mortgage will sever at common law, but unclear how it works in the Torrens system
- Granting an easement will not sever a joint tenancy, doesn't act on the share
- Granting a leasehold interest will postpone the right of possession, but does not change the interest held by the joint tenant
- However, it can postpone the right of survivorship which may sever tenancy
### Terminating Co-Ownership
- Section 2 of the *Partition of Property Act* allows joint tenants or tenants in common to be compelled to partition or sell their land
- If the majority (or half) of co-owners want a sale, the court must direct the sale of the property
- Exception to this is if the court has a good reason to not do this
- If the property is physically divisible, might be better to partition instead
- In a case called *Bradwell v Scott*, the BCCA said that if the sale would cause serious hardship to one party, that could be a good reason to not sell
- This is just one of many possible reasons though, need to look at the facts of each case
- Also said that if the applicants for partition are acting maliciously, will probably reject
- Section 7 of the *Act* says that if a sale would be more beneficial to the interested parties than a partition, any of the interested parties can ask for a sale
- This can happen even if the other parties don't want it
### [[The Partition Act]]
- Four main important points here
- First, courts will prefer partition over sale whenever possible
- Second, courts will order a sale instead where the sale will be more advantageous to the parties, and will try to balance the relative harms
- Third, courts are willing to order a sale on the request of a minority even where the majority disagrees
- Finally, the court will prefer an open market sale over a sale to one of the co-owners
- However, they may grant a party an option of first refusal for the open sale
