### Question 2
- This case deals with whether a transaction is effectively a mortgage or not, the facts of the case are very similar to Sartor v Boon
- Issue is whether or not Bill can recover his bungalow
- The first mortgage was entirely legal, it was registered mortgage and it seems that everything you would need for a mortgage is there
- The second agreement was a lot more complicated
- This is functionally similar to the deal that Boon offered the Sartors, with a few differences
- Here, Loretta discharged the mortgage she held on the property, but in *Sartor*, Boon bought the mortgage and held both at the same time?
	- Also, Loretta did not act in bad faith, but Boon did
- In both cases, the person selling the property became a tenant and paid rent on their former property, and had a time-limited option to repurchase the home
- House is also worth more than the purchase option price
- Bill may be able to claim that the agreement to sell his house was effectively a mortgage, pointing to the precedent of *Sartor v Boon*
	- It has most of the component pieces of a mortgage, Bill exchanged his interest in the bungalow for a loan, and could regain possession by paying a certain amount of money
- However, the time limit on when Bill can buy the house back may be considered a clog on the equitable right of redemption
	- This case is not quite as straightforward as *Sartor* since the parties were dealing in good faith and the agreement was not unconscionable
	- However, it's likely that the court may still consider this a clog on the right to redeem
- Even if Bill can convince the court that this is a clog on the equitable right to redeem, it's still possible that the purchase option is an unacceptable restraint on alienation
- Purchase options like this are usually allowed if the price is set at market value, but courts will be more hesitant to honour them if it's at a set price
	- Test for this is whether or not the restriction "substantially takes away the whole power of alienation"
	- In this case, we know that the option price is substantially less than the market price
- In *Trinity College School v Lyons*, the court found that an option to purchase land at an option price far below the market price was void
	- However, this option also needed to be exercised at a certain time, the option we're dealing with here only has a time limit
- Overall, it seems like Bill's claim will probably not be successful
- The basis for his claim is that the agreement between him and Loretta is effectively a mortgage, and that he should be able to use the equitable right to redeem to buy it back
- He does seem to have a solid basis for this claim at first glance, as the agreement does closely resemble a mortgage, and the courts have allowed transactions like this in the past
- However, there are a number of factors pushing back against this
- First, Loretta is the registered owner of the property, and has indefeasible title
	- Theoretically Bill could still recover the property through the equitable right to redeem, but the court will likely want to respect the registered interests
	- Keep in mind that the mortgage is gone, only things registered are Loretta's title to the bungalow, and Bill's purchase option
- Second, there was no bad faith conduct on Loretta's part
	- A major driving force behind the decision in *Sartor* was that Boon was taking advantage of a vulnerable couple and gave them an unconscionable deal
	- In this case, both parties were dealing in good faith, and of their own free will
	- Unlikely that the court will render the transaction void based only on the potential clog on the equitable right to redeem
- Third, the court may be hesitant to allow Bill to rebuy the property using the option price, as this may be considered an unacceptable restraint on alienation
	- Normally, the remedy for this case would be to make the transaction selling the house void, but the court may not do this without bad faith conduct
	- This means they probably wouldn't let him still exercise the option either
