- Recall that the title registration system will not protect a fraudster, specified under s. 23(2) of the *Land Title Act*
- Main debate is who the title system prefers, the original owner or the innocent buyer
- Also, is infeasibility granted immediately, or deferred until the purchaser is at least one step removed from the rogue
- Under this latter system, you need to have made a transaction with another innocent party
- One of the main arguments for deferred indefeasibility is that it encourages purchasers to determine whether the person their dealing with got their interest legitimately
- Theoretically, these people are the ones best positioned to do this
- For immediate indefeasibility, it removes a lot of duties from the purchaser, as long as they dealt in good faith with a title holder, their interest will be protected
- Shifts risk onto the original owner
### Example Question 1
- One party holds a fee simple, and their interest is sold by a rogue posing as them to a third party
- If the third party participated in the fraud, original owner can get their interest back
- If the third party was innocent but it's an immediate indefeasibility system, can't get the interest back
- If the third party was innocent but it's a deferred indefeasibility system, can get their interest back, the third party wasn't one step removed from the fraud
### Example Question 2
- Same situation, but the innocent buyer then tries to sell it to another party and at this point, the fraud is discovered but the second sale has not been registered yet
- Owner cannot recover title if the system uses immediate indefeasibility
- However, they can still recover title if the system uses deferred indefeasibility, because the second sale has not been registered yet
- Therefore, they are still not one step removed from the fraud
- If they had registered the second sale before the fraud was discovered, original owner can't get their interest back
### BC Land Transfers
- Examples of title fraud are not super common in Canada, but it does happen
- Transfer instruments are supposed to be witnessed by a lawyer or notary
- Signature of the officer on a transfer instrument is a certification that the individual told the officer that they are the person named in the instrument
- Signature is an affirmation of this fact (s. 43)
- In a case called *Singh (Re)*, a lawyer commissioned several transfer forms without witnessing the signing of those documents
- Disciplinary hearing found that this was misconduct, need to by physically present when the document is signed to keep the system secure
- Some accommodations were made during COVID, but at a minimum you need to be certain about the signing requirements
- Generally, you need to present two pieces of government issued photo ID to prove that you are the person named in the document
- Ended up recommending that the lawyer be disbarred
### Immediate vs Deferred Indefeasibility
- For a while, it was unclear whether the *LTA* used immediate or deferred indefeasibility
- In the 2005 amendments to the *LTA*, BC made it clear that the system used immediate indefeasibility under s. 25.1(1)
	- Person who purports to acquire land using a void instrument does not acquire any interest in the land
- Goes on to say in s. 25.1(2) that even if a transfer instrument for fee simple is void, a person who is named in the instrument and got it in good faith is deemed to have acquired that estate
- "Deemed" has been interpreted to mean a rebuttable presumption of ownership
- Seems to establish an immediate system of indefeasibility
- Doesn't explicitly give indefeasible title, but what evidence would you be able to raise to rebut the presumption other than proof of a void instrument?
- Under s. 25.1(3), this also applies retroactively to all already registered instruments
### Nisga'a Land Title Office
- Seem to use the same system as the BC *LTA*, uses immediate indefeasibility
- The Tsawwassen on the other hand, did not create their own title office and register their land in the BC Land Title Office
### Assurance Principle
- In the title registration system, where an owner is deprived of title, they may be entitled to compensation through the Assurance Fund
- A person who is deprived of any interest in land because of the registration system where:
	- they normally would have got title under common law, 
	- was deprived because of fraud, and 
	- who cannot bring an action to recover the land by the registration system,
	- can seek compensation (s. 296)
- Barred from bringing an action requirement is usually met by s. 25(2) which prevents action against the registered owner of indefeasible title
- If an owner whose interest was sold through fraud to an innocent buyer discovers the fraud after the new title has been registered, can they get assurance?
- What's the difference between an immediate and deferred system?
- Original owner would be able to get compensation if they can't get the land back (in an immediate system)
- If the original owner got the land back (deferred system), the innocent buyer can't get compensation because they wouldn't have normally got the land under common law
