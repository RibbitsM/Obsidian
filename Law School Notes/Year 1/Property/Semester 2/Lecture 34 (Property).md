### [[HJ Hayes Co v Meade]]
- Recall that the two main issues in this case were whether a transfer was subject to a condition precedent or subsequent, and whether the condition was uncertain
- Recall that a vested interest subject to a condition subsequent can become a defeasible interest or a determinable interest
- However, for this case we can ignore determinable interests
- In the case of a defeasible interest, the interest is vested but it is subject to a condition subsequent, meaning some future event can end that vested interest
- If that happens, the transferor has a right of re-entry and can take it back
- Also not responsible for remainder rules for contingent interests, as well as the rule of perpetuities, which basically sets a time limit on how long an interest can be contingent
- All we need to know is that conditions precedent are not vested interests, the interest only becomes vested once the condition is met
### Application
- Applying the rules of construction, the intentions of the testator are paramount
- Intended for all his sons to benefit, Harold got half of the farm, and the other two each got either a quarter of the farm or $1000
- As for the presumption in favour of early vesting, this prioritizes conditions subsequent
- Finally, the presumption against intestacy also applies because if it was a condition precedent, then if the condition wasn't met and the money wasn't paid, land goes to no one
- Overall, all three rules of construction seem to favour a condition subsequent, meaning the land was vested in James until he failed to meet the condition
#### Uncertainty
- Next question was whether the terms of the conditions failed for uncertainty
- Standard for certainty is that the court must be able to precisely predict what event(s) will determine the fate of the vested interest
- Ziff describes this as a "practical level of clarity", but this standard changes based on what kind of condition we're dealing with
- For a condition subsequent, the donee has to be able to predict what actions will lead to the loss of the interest, otherwise it becomes an unconditional transfer
- For a condition precedent, the condition has to be able to be given some plausible meaning, otherwise the transfer fails
- Reason for the varying strength of these standards is the difference in the outcome, the court is trying to encourage vesting, so they apply a high standard when it's subsequent, and a low standard when it's precedent
- Condition here was if James does not want to live on the property or cultivate it
- Court found that this was too uncertain, generally speaking a requirement to reside on a property will fail for uncertainty
- Can you go on vacation? Can you buy a second home? To what extent do you need to cultivate the land? These questions are all unanswered here
- Because of this, it failed the practical level of clarity test, and the transfer was unconditional
- James's heirs thus were the owners of the property, and won the case
### Restraints on Alienation
- This is an another example of property law trying to balance private power to dispose of land as you wish with the public interest
- What limits can a donor place on the transfer and sale (alienation) of land?
- Three kinds of restrictions, mode of alienation, class of recipients, and time of alienation
- As for the mode of alienation, there are a lot of possibilities here
- Can leave it in a will, use it as collateral, lease it, sell it, gift it, make a life estate, etc.
- For the class of recipients, these would be restrictions on who can or cannot receive the property, can be specific to one person, or apply to a group of people
- For time of alienation, this is usually a period of time where the property cannot be alienated
- At what point do these restraints become too severe for the court to uphold
- Generally, the standard is where the condition substantially takes away the whole power of alienation, which is pretty vague and leaves plenty of room for interpretation
- As usual, the courts will be looking at the substance of the restriction, not the form
### [[Trinity College School v Lyons]]
- Trinity College is a boarding school in Port Hope, the Bennetts were a farming family that lived adjacent to the school
- Agreed to sell some of their land to the school, and also gave the school the option to purchase the rest of the land at a fixed price for $12,000 in 1963
- Stipulated that the school would pay the taxes on the land until the sale, but if the Bennetts decided to sell to someone else they would reimburse them
- In 1965, the school acquired some of the land under the option, and the option was renegotiated to allow them to purchase the remaining land upon the death of the surviving member of the couple for $9375
- In 1976, the Bennetts started paying taxes on the land, and in 1991 the last survivor, the wife, died and the market value of the land was now $135,000
- When the school tried to buy the land, they realized that the land had been gifted to their daughters in 1978, which was not prevented by the contract
- Court mostly ignored this, said that the Bennetts can't avoid the option by doing this
- Main issue was whether an option to buy at a fixed price was an invalid restraint on alienation
- A condition that effectively takes away the incidents of land is considered void, because doing so would keep the land out of the free market and support the concentration of wealth
- In this case, the issue was that the vast disparity between the market value and the option price may have effectively removed the Bennetts' right to earn profits from the property
- Economically speaking, the party that will have the best use of the property is the one that is willing to pay the highest price, so there's a policy justification here too
- First option was considered fine, but the second option was considered void, why?
### Application
- Court never explicitly states this, but the issue seems to be that the second option effectively turned the Bennetts interest from a fee simple into a life estate
- This is probably what they meant by saying the conveyor does not have the power to alter the character of the interest
