### Question 1
- When the judge talks about a "perfect Torrens system", what they are referring to is a system where all property interests are immediately indefeasible
- In the BC system, we have immediate indefeasibility for fee simple interests, but for charges, the registered owner is only "deemed to be entitled" to the interest
	- This means that they're presumed to be the owner, but you can rebut that presumption by providing evidence that they're not the rightful owner
- Possible middle option would be to keep immediate indefeasibility for fee simple and have deferred indefeasibility for charges, or deferred indefeasibility for everything
- I personally think that the better option is deferred indefeasibility as I think strictly enforcing dynamic security can lead to unfair outcomes for homeowners
- However, the assurance fund does a good job of balancing out these negative side effects
- Probably good to have some form of indefeasibility for charges, as it would bring more people under the protection of the assurance fund and lower the need for title insurance
- Having a public scheme for compensation instead of a private scheme helps more people
### Question 2
- This case is very similar to [[Soulos v Korkontzilas]], the issues we're dealing with here are institutional constructive trusts and common law priorities
- In order to determine what claim/remedy Pepper has, need to determine what interests Pepper and Eleanor hold
- Starting with Eleanor, since she has signed a contract of purchase and sale but has not transferred the land yet, she has an institutional constructive trust and may be able to sue for specific performance if the apartment is not given to her
- Pepper may also have an institutional constructive trust, four requirements for this
	- We have a duty of loyalty, Rocky promised to act as Pepper's fiduciary by signing the Buyer's Agency Agreement
	- Rocky's sister also acquired the assets based on that breach of duty
	- Pepper has very specific requirements for what apartment he wants, so it's unlikely that monetary compensation will be an appropriate remedy here
	- However, we do have an innocent third party who will be affected here
		- Eleanor has already paid a 50k deposit for the house
- Assuming that Pepper does have a constructive trust here, it would have been established at the point that Rocky broke his duty to him
- Pepper and Eleanor would have the same kind of equitable interest to the apartment, so Pepper's interest takes precedent because it was first in time ([[Rice v Rice]])
- However, if Eleanor had already completed the sale, she would have a legal interest, and without any notice of Pepper's interest and bad faith conduct on her part, Pepper would not be able to disrupt her claim
- Overall, Pepper would be able to claim a constructive trust on the property if he can convince the court that it would not be unjust to Eleanor to grant this
	- Given the fact she's already paid the deposit, court would probably not allow this, but could still give him damages for the breach of duty instead
### Question 3
- Need to determine if Joan has a right to the full beneficial interest, whether she can convert it to tenancy in common, and whether she can partition and sell the lot
- Starting with the first claim, we need to see if the joint tenancy was ever disrupted or if the beneficial interest ever changed
	- If this was an ordinary joint tenancy from start to end, then both parties would have half the legal and half the equitable interest
- First, this was clearly intended to be a joint tenancy, so we can presume that's the case
- Next, need to look to see if all the four unities are met
	- We may have unity of possession, though this might be disrupted by the fact that Joan initially collected all rent from the land, and later Bob did the same
		- This isn't a possessory right, but indicated that the incidents of ownership were not split equally
	- Interest held by both parties is presumably the same, and was created at the same time
	- Also came from the same act, the initial Form A filed by Joan
- Assuming the four unities are present, this is a joint tenancy
- Still need to see if it was severed
- Only way this could have been severed was through one tenant acting on their own share, only indication of the mutual agreement was the switch from Joan getting all rent to Bob getting it
- Only action that Joan took on the property so far was changing her will and telling Bob to make sure his brothers got their share
	- We know from [[Sorenson Estate v Sorenson]] that changing a will does not affect joint tenancy, since right of survivorship happens before the will is executed
- However, the partition order could allow her to later claim the full beneficial interest, but merely intending to partition the property isn't enough
- Joan also has the option of converting the agreement into a tenancy in common by simply transferring her share in the property to herself under s. 18 of the *PLA*
	- No way for Bob to block this, Joan is allowed to act unilaterally on her share
- However, until this has happened, Joan only holds half the legal and half the equitable interest
- Other option is that Joan can argue that she holds the full beneficial interest because a resulting trust was created when she created the joint tenancy
- Since the transfer was for nominal consideration and Bob didn't even contribute to the expenses for the property for the first 5 years, can argue that it was actually a gift
- Under the common law, we presume that a gift actually creates a resulting trust, advancement doesn't apply here because Bob was an adult when it happened
	- Joan can probably point to the fact that she collected the full amount of rent as evidence that she did not intend to also transfer the full beneficial interest
- If Joan can successfully establish a resulting trust, this also breaks the unity of interest
- Bob can try and counteract this claim by pointing out that he was receiving the full benefits of the property for a significant portion of time
- Overall, Joan would probably be successful with this approach
- Final thing to consider is if the property can be partitioned
- Assuming that the partition request is happening before Joan's claim for the full beneficial interest, the only joint tenants on the property are Joan and Bob
- Can't sell it based only on the request, because there's no majority
- Since the property cannot be subdivided, court will either sell or not sell, no partition
- Court can do a sale if they find it would be beneficial to the owners
	- This could go either way, but overall the declining prices indicate that the court would probably not choose to sell the property here
### Question 4
- Deals with conditions in wills
- Two conditions here, one is precedent, other is subsequent
	- Not explicitly stated, but because there's no contingent language in the "use of the house" condition, we presume that it's vested (condition subsequent)
	- Other condition does say "when and if" which strongly suggests a contingent interest, also supported by the rule from *Re Francis*
- Condition subsequent probably fails for uncertainty
	- Need to be able to predict what actions will lead to the interest being lost
	- Couple things here are unclear, who counts as "all my family", what does "sharing in the upkeep" mean, and does this mean denying any family member the use would make the entire property interest void?
- When a condition subsequent fails for uncertainty, person getting the gift receives it unconditionally, so Gordon, Anne, Neil, and Joni get the house with no conditions
- Condition precedent probably doesn't fail for uncertainty, it's not super specific but it can definitely be given a plausible meaning
- Main question is whether Tom can get the tiny house, it seems that at the time of death he met the condition, so the court will probably give it to him
	- If he does not get the property, then Martha would be intestate, and we want to avoid this
