- Overall, the development of the Torrens system of title registration has not been very controversial in Canada
- There are a couple holdouts in Quebec and the Maritime provinces, but on the West Coast especially, it's pretty firmly established
- Most of these systems have a couple features in common
- Generally, the person registered as the title holder is the title holder under the law
- Usually abolish the common law doctrine of notice, and have assurance funds to compensate victims of fraud
- Result is a system that gives purchasers and lenders accepting property as security confidence in the value of the interests they require
- System originated in Australia in response to concerns over the common law making land transfers to complicated and risky
- In Canada, each province and territory had a fairly different path to and final version of a Torrens system
### BC
- First jurisdiction in Canada to adopt this was Vancouver Island which got the Torrens system in 1860
- Not exactly what we have today, indefeasible title was only granted at least five years after registration
- BC adopted the system a decade later, and when the province joined Confederation, the system was unified
- Two-tier system was removed in 1921, afterwards all titles were indefeasible
### Ontario
- Easy in BC because of the short history, much harder in Ontario
- Implemented the system for Toronto and York in 1885, but registration was optional and the regions were small
- Expanded this to some less-settled areas a few years later, where all new registration was made mandatory
- This pattern continued, most interests were registered only in the last 30 years or so
### Alberta/Saskatchewan
- Things went pretty smoothly here, the fed passed the *Territories Real Property Act* in 1887 which imposed a title system on most of west and north Canada
- When the prairie provinces joined in 1905 they passed their own registration statutes immediately to confirm the system
- Things were a little harder in Manitoba where people fought against registration costs and having a central registry
- Initially optional, but over time more registries were opened, allowing for easy conversion
### Quebec and Maritimes
- Little desire for a title registration system in Quebec, mainly because of how hard it would be to make a Torrens system work with civil law
- Because there was less land speculation here because of how early the land was settled, a need to protect transfers was not a priority
- This was also the reason why the Maritimes didn't feel a need for a Torrens system, pretty much everything was already settled
- Legal profession out east was also very opposed to the change mainly because of the loss of work, but also because it would make settled interests less secure
- Over time as the digital age made the title system more appealing, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick have started switching over
