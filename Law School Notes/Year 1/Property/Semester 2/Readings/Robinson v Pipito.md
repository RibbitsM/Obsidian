- The Robinsons owned two lands in Mission, gave an easement from the owner of lot 1 (dominant) to lot 2 (servient)
- Lot 2 was later sold, and the court declared the easement to be invalid, the case was appealed
- Lot 2 is a long, narrow property bounded by lot 1 on the west side
- Easement extends over pretty much all of lot 2, 80% of the area
- Easement is for free access to use the land for farming and recreational purposes, and gives full rights to the timber and gravel
- Lot 1 owners wanted to use the easement and get an injunction preventing the lot 2 owners from interfering with them
- Also wanted a structure on the easement removed
- Lot 2 owners wanted a declaration that this wasn't an easement
### Analysis
- Every easement will at least partially exclude the servient owner and prevent them from exercising their property rights
- Degree of occupation/possession is dictated by the contract giving the grant, not the activities of the parties
- Generally, a grant of a right that's essentially exclusive occupation is inconsistent with an easement, but drawing that line is pretty hard
- Argument here is that the easement does not give exclusive possession
#### Nature of the Grant
- Jurisprudence of the manner shows that we need to look at the facts of the case, and we should try to interpret the grant so that none of the servient rights are effectively extinguished
- Usually, court will try to interpret the grant so that the servient gets as many rights as possible instead of removing the easement
#### Application
- Right to use the easement for farming and banning the servient owner from inconsistent uses is basically exclusive use
- Also allows the dominant owner to plant crops in the easement
- Removal of all timber without restriction is also a pretty major power restricting the servient owner from interfering with that
- Some problem arises with the rights to the gravel
- Grants the dominant owners dominion over the easement that is clearly inconsistent with the rights of the servient owner
- Common sense shows that this is a complete derogation of rights to possession of the easement
- Consistent with the jurisprudence, previous case called *Clos Farming Estates v Easton* dealt with a similar issue
- That easement gave the right to cultivate a vineyard, and similarly removed the rights of the servient owner to influence what was happening on their agricultural land
