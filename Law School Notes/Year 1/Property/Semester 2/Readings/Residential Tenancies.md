- Landlord/tenant relations were governed by feudal land law until the 70s
- Changed when the Ontario Law Reform Commission held that courts had been applying land law too rigidly
- Previous legal order had some problems
- First, a lessee can't get a court order for possession unless they had actual possession, even if they had a valid lease
- Also meant that if a landlord didn't give possession, they could only be sued for minor damages
- There was also no common law duty for landlords to perform repairs
- Even if the dwelling is destroyed, the landlord can keep collecting rent without rebuilding
- If a tenant unlawfully leaves the lease, the landlord is not obligated to lease to a new suitable tenant and can keep the original tenant liable
- Finally, landlords weren't responsible for harm caused to the tenant resulting from the dangerous condition of the premises
- All these rules had to be reformed to grant tenants greater security
- Ten main elements for the reforms:
	1. Greater security of tenure
	2. Increased termination notice
	3. Reallocation of standard obligations for landlords/tenants
	4. More remedies for tenants
	5. Less remedies for landlords
	6. Ban on bargaining away statutory rights
	7. Elimination of other anachronisms
	8. Creation of better dispute resolution procedures
	9. Creation of landlord/tenant advisory boards
	10. Creation of rent control mechanisms
- In Canada, rent control has actually proven pretty controversial
- There are several issues with both constraining and allowing free increases of rent
- If a cap is too low, landlords, may reduce maintenance to increase profits
- Additionally, this may prompt some landlords to convert residential rentals to commercial uses
- Unless further steps are taken, controls can just make renting conditions worse overall
- Finally, low rents encourage tenants to stay in their current accommodations, hurting future generations
- However, rent control is also an important part of security of tenure in general
- Theoretically, if a landlord wants to evict their tenants for personal reasons but has no grounds to do so, they can just drastically increase their rent
- Without rent controls, other security provisions become moot
- Additionally, if landlords know that their tenants have nowhere else to go, they can hike up the price and gouge them
- Finally, not all kinds of leases fall under local residential tenancy legislation
- For example, in Saskatchewan rental agreements for care homes and student residences don't apply
