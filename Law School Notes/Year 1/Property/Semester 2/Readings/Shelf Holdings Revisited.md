- Trial judge's finding in [[Husky Oil v Shelf Holdings]] that Husky got a possessory interest was based on a previous SKCA decision called *Re Interprov Pipe Line Co*
- Court in that case held that a pipeline easement granted exclusive possession of part of the subsoil
- ABCA in *Shelf* didn't follow this decision, mainly because the grant did not include any language of exclusive possession
- Unclear whether they were talking about whether Shelf was deprived of access to the subsoil, or just the surface land
### Exclusive Nature of Pipelines
- Main problem with this decision was that you can circumvent the fourth rule of easements by carefully crafting the grant
- Any right of way for a pipeline is pretty clearly an exclusive grant, no way for the landowner to use the subsoil if there's a pipeline there
- Also, some pipelines need surface structures like valve stations to function property and you can't build stuff on top of them
- Pipeline construction can also permanently damage crops and drainage systems, and the grantee will always have maintenance rights
- Commentary on this case urged a re-examination of the fourth rule, we should focus on interference with the grantor's use of the **entire** land
- Pipelines can cause a lot of problems that can be devastating for the surrounding land, should not be considered just easements
### Easement Must Accommodate the Grantee
- Heart of this rule is that the easement is accommodating the land held by the grantee, not the grantee themselves
- If it just gives a benefit tailored to the owner, that's a license not an easement
- This is pretty hard to apply when it comes to refinery pipelines
- None of the decisions in *Shelf* actually identified what the grantee's land was, but we can assume it's the refinery lands
- A pipeline clearly benefits Husky, but what happens if the land the refinery was on was given to someone else?
- Pipeline would be just a liability if it's connected to an orange juice factory or an orphanage for example
- Pipelines only accommodate if the owner is an oil company, can't be considered a general benefit to the dominant tenement
- *Shelf* did not consider this, they just glossed over this step
### Lessons of Shelf Holdings
- This should be a lesson for how we use the common law for environmental activism
- Never acknowledged that the trial decision could potentially threaten the oil sector, but this was probably considered
- We also had a similar case around the same time where the ABQB found that powerlines are a common law easement
- No reasons given in that case for exempting energy companies from private law rules
