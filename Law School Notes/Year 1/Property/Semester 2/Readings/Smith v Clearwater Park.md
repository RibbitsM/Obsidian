- The Smiths want to prevent the construction of a townhouse development next to their property in Squamish
- Argue that it's prevented by the building scheme placed on that property, and other properties in the area
- Can only build single family residences, not multi family
- Want a permanent injunction against the owner preventing them from building anything other than single family housing
- Defendant wants an order modifying/cancelling the building scheme
- Rely on the ability of courts to order this under s. 35 of the *Property Law Act*
- Main issue is whether the scheme should be modified/cancelled
- Has also drawn interest from surrounding property owners, sets a precedent for all properties affected by the building scheme
- Squamish says the project complies with the property zoning, and supports Clearwater
- Court found that the building scheme should be cancelled
- Adequate notice has been given to the people entitled to the benefit, Clearwater served all the owners in the immediate vicinity and posted the petition and affidavits online
### Facts
-  Building scheme was created prior to the LTA, and applies to lands in a subdivision plan registered in 1959
- Most of the properties within the building scheme also have restrictive covenants registered that replicate the restrictions under the building scheme
- Hard to confirm full extent of the lots affected without individual title searches, but seems it affects about 200 properties
- Original owner of the lot consisting of the Smith's lot, the lot at issue, and 65 other properties was sold to the Sorbans in 1964, included a number of restrictions
- Allowed the seller (GPEL) to control anything built or modified on those lands
- Includes the right to waive, modify, or cancel the building scheme restrictions
- GPEL could either use these rights themselves or through a delegate, never appointed a delegate and dissolved in 1983
- Clause 3 of the restrictions state that non-single family housing units can be constructed unless authorized by GPEL or their delegate
- Prohibition is not absolute, subject to the authority of GPEL
- GPEL has previously released certain lots from the building scheme
- Some prohibitions in the scheme prevent trade or business, and restricts how close buildings can be built to roads/property limits
- The lot (Lot 36) became part of Squamish in 1964
- In 2024, Squamish rezoned the reason to allow up to four residences per lot
### Evidence and Positions
- Smiths live in a single family home on a cul-de-sac, initially moved into the neighbourhood because it was low-density, private, safe, and quiet
- Pointed to the building scheme as a major reason for this, and argued that making the townhouses would change the character of the neighbourhood and reduce their enjoyment of the property
- Like the neighbourhood as is and oppose ant changes in its character in general
- Several other residents in the area have sworn affidavits expressing similar concerns
- Other residents support the project, citing the need to address affordability issues
- Squamish has also issued building permits for accessory buildings and secondary suites in the area in the last 10 years or so
### Analysis
- Section 35 of the PLA allows the court to modify or cancel building schemes
	- Can do this if the application is not premature in the circumstances and if changes in the land or neighbourhood make the registered charge obsolete
	- Also a number of other factors, can do it if reasonable use of the land will be impeded if the charge isn't changed/cancelled
	- If people entitled to the benefit have expressly or impliedly agreed to the change
	- If the change will not injure the benefit holders, and if the registered instrument is invalid, unenforceable, or has expired
- Party trying to rely on this provisions needs to prove the charge is obsolete
	- This test is based on a consideration of the nature of the change in the circumstances of use
- Meaning of obsolete has been defined as the ordinary meaning of the word
- To figure this out, need to look at the purpose of the building scheme
- If the scheme only works with consent of a non-existent grantor, owner seeking relief needs the consent of the other owners or can apply under s. 35
#### Prematurity
- Application is not premature, Clearwater already has approval for the project
- No pending decisions or other circumstances that will affect the decision in the future
#### Obsolete
- Purpose of the building scheme is not stated, needs to be inferred
- One goal seems to be limiting construction to single-family houses but this is not really committed to
- Also allows GPEL to authorize duplexes and other multiple dwelling units with no limit on this authority
- Seems that the discretion given to GPEL makes this a pretty soft commitment to single family housing
- Overall objective is more to give GPEL control over anything built on the lands rather than placing hard restrictions
	- Even something as simple of a fence requires approval from GPEL with no limits on discretion
- No assurance was given the residents that there would be no multifamily residences, GPEL could always do this unilaterally
- Request for a permanent injunction goes way beyond the terms of the building scheme
- Also, anything built in the area after GPEL dissolved does not comply with the building scheme because no one is around to authorize it
- Gaining unanimous consent of the owners of all 200 properties is also practically impossible
- Since administration and compliance with the building scheme is now impossible, can safely call it obsolete
### Neglect of Building Scheme
- Also no provisions for what to do if GPEL stops paying attention or ceases to exist, and no time limits
- Seems that the scheme was not intended to last permanently
- Can interpret the scheme as allowing GPEL control until their interest or ability to administer the scheme ended
- This has clearly ended, scheme should now be cancelled
- Clearly seems to be a material circumstance under the PLA
- Even if the scheme remains in force, the absence of GPEL or a delegate should mean the scheme should be modified to make Squamish the new delegate
- Seems pretty clear that Squamish intends to allow for multi family housing in the area, evidenced by its zoning policy
- Concerns about the negative effects are also pretty overblown, the circumstances have changed significantly over time
- Squamish's planning and development rules will address most of these concerns including flooding, tree canopy, and safety
### Discretion to Refuse Relief
- Smiths also argue that cancelling the scheme would be unjust, and the court should refuse to exercise its discretion here
- This is supported by the common law
- Smiths argue that the neighbourhood will be harmed, and Clearwater bought the property to make a profit with full knowledge of the restrictive covenant
- Clearwater admits they hoped the building scheme would not limit them, pointing to the new rezoning and development permit
- Nothing to really criticize here
- Anyone who actually read the building scheme can see it doesn't really apply here
- Smiths and other witnesses never got any assurances it would be enforced, their reliance on the building scheme has little weight
- Desires of the residents are overall outweighed by the need for additional housing in Squamish given its fast-growing population
### Conclusion
- Orders sought by the Smiths are dismissed, notation relating to the building scheme on Clearwater's property are cancelled
