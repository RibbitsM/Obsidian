- *Partition Act* is a piece of Ontario legislation that deals with co-owned properties
- Section 2 allows all joint tenants, tenants in common, mortgagees, or anyone else with land interests to be compelled to partition or sell their land or part of it
- Section 3 allows any person with a interest in land to apply for partition or sale if that would be more advantageous
	- Any owner or creditor with an interest can bring this action, including mortgagees and execution creditors
- Therefore, all joint or common tenants can be forced by the court to agree to a partition or sale if the court finds a sale more advantageous
- This can happen despite objections from one of the co-owners
- A bunch of cases deal with this, mostly family law
- Result is pretty vague and simple directions for very high-stakes cases
- Current environment is very rigid, party wanting out can effectively hold the other party for ransom
### Case Law Rules
1. There is a hierarchy of remedies under this act
	- Parties are entitled to partition as a primary remedy, property will be divided between the parties equitable
	- Court determines how land is partitioned, and partition is the primary objective
	- Only move on to sale if partition isn't ordered
2. Court will only order a sale where the applicant is entitled to a partition but a sale would be better for both parties
	- Court has a lot of flexibility in determining whether to order a sale
	- Generally, they will only order a sale where it's impossible to divide the land equitably
	- If one wants partition and the other wants sale, court balances the relative harm to each party
3. Court decides at the start of the application to see if the applicant is entitled to partition
	- Will generally only refuse an application if the applicant was acting vexatiously or oppressively
4. Mortgagees can be ordered to partition or sell mortgaged properties
5. Court can give a right of first refusal to a sale order
	- One party can match any legitimate third party offer of purchase
6. Minority interests can compel majority interests to force a sale even where the majority objects, regardless of the relative interest size
7. Any party to a sale order can bid in the sale process
8. Court will generally not order either party to sell to the other party, prefers an open market sale
9. Courts are reluctant to order sale of a part interest
	- If applicant holds 30% and wants the whole thing sold, while the respondent has 70%, court won't just sell the 30%
	- Either sell the whole thing, or don't sell it at al
10. In at least one case, courts will not allow commercial applicants to further their commercial goals at the expense of the co-owner
11. Clause in the co-owning agreement obligating parties to not the *Partition Act* to exit will be upheld by the court
