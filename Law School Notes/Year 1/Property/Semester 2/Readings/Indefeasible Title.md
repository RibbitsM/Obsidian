- BC government changed the *Land Title Act* in 2005 to ensure immediate certainty of title for someone who acted in good faith but got a property through a fraudulent transfer
- Raises the question of whether the BC title registration system works on immediate or deferred indefeasibility
- In the former case, the registered owner is immune from challenges that their title was acquired through fraud or a void instrument
- In the latter case, indefeasible title is only acquired if they are at least one step away from the fraud
- Person who gets the interest through a void instrument can have their ownership challenged by the person wrongfully deprived
- The intention behind these new amendments seem to suggest that BC is moving towards an immediate system
### Common Law Conveyancing
- Under the common law, the Latin maxim of *nemo dat* holds that the risk falls entirely on the purchaser
- If the seller did not have legal title to transfer, the transfer was void
- If any transaction in the chain of acquisition was fraudulent, then the person who was defrauded can claim the title back
- Purchaser only has cause of action against the person who committed the fraud
- Prioritizes settled interests in land over those acquired through a transfer
### Indefeasible Title
- Principle of indefeasibility secures the registered owners interest absolutely unless they personally participated in fraud
- Rogue's interest will always be subject to the claim of the person they wrongfully deprived
- Other than this, whoever is named in the registry as the owner is the owner by law
- Makes the transfer of property much less risky, this provides dynamic security to sellers and buyers
- Recall that immediate indefeasibility secures an interest immediately where the buyer acted in good faith
- Deferred indefeasibility delays the interest until it can be determined that the person getting the interest got it from the registered owner
- Where the wronged party cannot regain their title, they will be compensated monetarily from an assurance fund
- No matter which version you choose, this is a departure from the common law system which prioritizes stable security
### Immediate or Deferred Indefeasibility
- Unclear which of these systems is used in BC
- Section 23(2) of the *Land Title Act* states that indefeasible title is conclusive evidence that the registered owner is entitled to the land in fee simple
- The amendments only added the "registered owner" language, which seems to clarify that the registered owner is the person with title, not someone with a lesser interest
- Participation in fraud is an exception to this under s. 23(2)(i)
- Seems to suggest that BC runs on an immediate system
- However, another section of the *LTA* states that the person who got an interest through a void instrument is not a purchaser (s. 297(3))
- This is hidden in the section on the assurance fund, while the immediate defeasibility language is in the core part of the *Act*
- Case law seemed to confirm that this section applies to the whole Act, and thus BC had a deferred system
- But in practice, the system worked on an immediate model
### Immediate Indefeasibility in BC
- A provincial task force recommended adopting immediate indefeasibility in 2001 because it would increase confidence in the system
- Courts should still have some discretion to rectify the title to the person defrauded if not doing to was unjust
- In these cases, the purchaser is the person who gets compensation
- 2005 amendments repealed s. 297(3) and added a new section in s. 25.1 to affirm the immediate system
- This new section asserts that the registry will not assist the person who gets an interest based on a void instrument, but a person who gets the interest in good faith is the legal owner
- This only applies to estates in fee simple, other interests are treated differently which is consistent with the rest of the *LTA*
- Doesn't specify that the person who got the land through a void instrument has indefeasible title, only that they are deemed to have acquired the estate
### Indefeasibly Entitled vs Deemed to Have Acquired
- Once the owner of a fee simple interest registers it, the holders of all lesser interests can register their interests as charges against the fee simple interest
- A charge is any interest less that fee simple, and the person registering the charge is deemed to be entitled to the estate (s. 26(1))
- This is not indefeasible title, as long as the registered owner was not party to a fraud, they can defend the claim of the person wronged
- But if the owner of a charge acquired the interest through fraud at some point in the chain, they are subject to the claim of the person wronged
- Registering non-fee simple interests will establish priority, but does not give indefeasible title
- Does this mean that s. 25.1 grants the owner of a fee simple interest acquired through fraud something less than indefeasible title?
- Overall, the use of the word "deemed" seems to only raise a rebuttable presumption of ownership
- Where the registered interest is a charge, evidence that a void instrument is involved is enough to rebut the presumption
- But s. 25.1 specifically rules out this kind of evidence, it says that the registered owner is "deemed" to be the owner notwithstanding a void transfer instrument
- Because of this, the ownership established in this section is actually closer to being indefeasible title
- Means that overall, BC seems to operate on a system of immediate indefeasibility despite the lack of explicit language granting indefeasible title
- May still be circumstances where the owner who got their interest through a void instrument may be vulnerable to a claim
- The system definitely isn't deferred indefeasibility, but it's not quite immediate indefeasibility either
### Conclusion
- The 2005 amendments mean that the informing principle of the BC title registration system is now immediate indefeasibility
- Distinction between fee simple and other interests remains, indefeasibility only applies to fee simple interests
- Changes stopped short of fully adopting immediate indefeasibility
- Still possible for indefeasibility to be deferred if the registered owner got the interest in good faith from a rogue
- This did not necessarily increase security overall, but it does mean that the costs are distributed instead of being focused on the victims of fraud
