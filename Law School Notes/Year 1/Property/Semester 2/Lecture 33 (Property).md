## [[Welcome the Newest Unworthy Heir]]
- The case of [[Re Leonard Foundation Trust]] raised a lot of issues over how public charitable trusts will be handled in the future
- The case there was about as clear as you can get, but it won't always be like that
- In the case of *Ramsden*, the court had to address whether UPEI could carry out a trust given only to Protestant students who wanted to go into the ministry
- This was discriminatory, but not necessarily against public policy
- Bunch of cases like this, there were also some scholarships at UVic that could only be given to Roman Catholic students
- Again, these trusts were upheld and not found against public policy
- One more complicated case was a trust to the University of Western Ontario that could only go to white single male straight students in the sciences, with preferences given to students who did hard manual work during the summer
- Also were not allowed to play intercollegiate sports for some reason
- They also had one for women, which was similarly restrictive, candidates had to be hard-working single which girls who are not feminists or lesbians, with preference given if she is an immigrant, but not necessarily a recent immigrant
- Also had to be in the sciences, but not medicine
- This is almost certainly against public policy in some way, but what parts are the problem?
- Court said that the qualifications of race, marital status, sexuality, and philosophy were void for being against public policy
- While the guy granting the scholarship did not explicitly say he was a white supremacist or homophobe, we can infer this from the terms of the scholarship
- In the end, this scholarship was determined to be void
### Private Trusts
- In the case of *Fox v Fox Estate*, the executor of a will deprived her son of his interest in an estate because he married a non-Jewish person
- The text of the will gave the executor absolute power of discretion to do this, but is it against public policy?
- If the will explicitly said that it could not go to someone who married a non-Jewish person that would certainly be against public policy, even though it was private
- Therefore, if the writer of the will couldn't do it, then neither can the executor
#### McCorkill v McCorkill Estate
- This was an even muddier case, as it involved an unconditional grant of a full estate
- However, the problem was that the estate was being granted to the National Alliance, a white supremacist organization in the US
- When the issue came to court, it was proven that the primary intent of the organization is to promote white supremacy via disseminating propaganda inciting hatred against identifiable groups
- This is illegal in Canada, giving the court grounds to refuse the gift as being contrary to public policy
#### Spence v BMO Trust Company
- In this case, a father specifically disinherited her daughter, which in the will said it was because she had not communicated with him for several years
- However, evidence suggested that the reason for this disinheritance was because the daughter had a daughter with a White man
- Although there was evidence that this went against public policy, ultimately the court found that the testator was allowed to do this
- This is basically directly against the decision made in *Fox v Fox Estate*
- Held that the common law principle of testamentary freedom protected the testator's right to dispose of their property, even on discriminatory grounds
## Economic Efficiency
- Public policy restrictions on transfers are exogenous to property, but what about endogenous restrictions?
- Main issues here are uncertainty, and restrictions on alienation
- Some conditions are simply too vague to execute, and other conditions may unduly restrict the transfer or sale of the property
### [[HJ Hayes Co v Meade]]
- In this case, a father granted half of his land to one of his sons (Harold) and gave the other half split in two between his two younger sons, one named James
- James got his quarter on the condition that he lived on the land and cultivated it, but if they didn't want to do that, he could give it to Harold for $1000
- One issue is whether or not the requirement to live and cultivate was a condition precedent or a condition subsequent
- Can either see this as James needing to live on the land and cultivate it to receive the land, or needs to stop doing that in order to lose the land
- Additionally, what happens if this condition fails and Harold doesn't pay the money?
- James ended up not living on the land until 40 years after the father's death, and Harold never paid James the $1000
- Harold's descendants claim the condition was precedent, and the land goes to them
- James' descendants claim the condition was subsequent, and the land goes to them
- Court points out that a condition subsequent allows for immediate vesting which a condition precedent doesn't
- If a condition subsequent is uncertain, it can be deemed void
### Decision
- In cases like this, we have to turn to the rules of construction
- Recall that the main concern is to give effect to the testator's intention
- Also, we have a presumption of early vesting and a presumption against intestacy
- Intention seemed to be to benefit all parties, all of his sons were supposed to get something
- Because it was uncertain, a condition subsequent was preferred because it would allow for early vesting
- Finally, because the money wasn't paid and if the condition was precedent it wasn't met, in that case the property would transfer to nobody
- Looking at the differences in results between precedent and subsequent, the latter option seemed like the best option
- When it comes to uncertainty, we usually talk about this for conditions subsequent
- This is because if a condition precedent is uncertain, then the gift fails, but if a condition subsequent is uncertain, then the gift is absolute
