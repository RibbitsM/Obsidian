### Question 1
- Question deals with fraud and title registration mainly
- Need to figure out what will happen with every interest, and any available remedies
- First thing to do is to make a list of the interests involved
	- Fraser Bungalow
		- Currently subject to a mortgage to Prudence, who is trying to foreclose
	- Bungalow Mortgage
		- Created through fraud, by Racoon, who is now deceased
	- Quesnel House
		- Currently owned by Eleanor, an innocent purchaser
		- Sale was done legitimately, but the property was transferred by fraud from George to Martha, who sold the house to Eleanor
			- Martha has also fled with all the money
		- Also subject to a mortgage to Penny Lane taken out by Eleanor
	- Quesnel Mortgage
		- Legitimate mortgage, Eleanor was the registered owner when she took out the mortgage
- Start with the Fraser Bungalow, which George can get the mortgage removed from
- Because of s. 27(3) of the *LTA* and *Credit Foncier*, the *nemo dat* rule applies to mortgages, so since Racoon did not have the right to grant the mortgage
	- Prudence is only deemed to be entitled to the mortgage, George can have it removed as long as he can prove the power of attorney was forged
- Means that the Bungalow Mortgage will be rendered void, and Prudence will likely be out of luck, as Racoon is deceased and she can't get assurance
- The Quesnel house is more complicated, but it seems that George may not be able to get it back, and instead will have to make a claim to the assurance fund
- Under s. 25.1, Eleanor's interest in the Quesnel house is immediately indefeasible as it's a fee simple interest, and she acted in good faith when acquiring it
- The transfer of the house from George to Martha was illegitimate, but the sale from Martha to Eleanor was legitimate as far as Eleanor is concerned
- However, George meets all the requirements to claim from the assurance fund
	- He normally would have gotten the house at common law because the *nemo dat* rule would prevent Martha from selling the house to Eleanor
	- He was deprived of his interest in the house due to fraud
	- He cannot sue Eleanor to recover the house because of s. 25(2)
- Finally, since Eleanor gets to keep the Quesnel House, then the mortgage on that house is also unaffected
### Question 2
- Pepper wants to enforce the covenant, as well as to exclude Alice from the easement area and stop the pickleball
- Need to deal with the covenant and the easement separately
- Easiest thing to determine is the covenant, which is not enforceable
	- While restrictive covenants can run with the land in Canada, positive covenants cannot
	- We know that this is a positive covenant, because Alice is currently doing nothing to fulfill the covenant and is violating it
	- Should never have been allowed to transfer when the land was sold
- Easement is more difficult to deal with, need to use the test from *Re Ellenborough Park* to determine if the easement transferred over
	- First element is met, there's dominant and servient tenements
	- Second element is also met, the easement benefits the dominant tenement by allowing its owners to make use of the tennis court
	- Third element would normally fail, as Betty made the easement while she owned both properties, but this has been abrogated by s. 18(7) of the *PLA*
	- Fourth element is the most complicated, but probably also met
		- The requirements of the easement are fairly clear, probably the only vague thing is what all tennis related activities are
		- The easement does grant a real utility, seems to be a mere right of recreation, but has not been interpreted this way
- Only thing that could potentially make the easement not run with the land is the argument that it takes away the right of exclusive possession
	- The easement gives the dominant owner "uninterrupted use" of the tennis court, for playing tennis and all related activities
	- Could maybe argue that this means that the servient owners lose exclusive possession of the tennis court
- Pepper would have to raise *Robinson v Pipito* as support for their case here
	- Overall, I don't think the court would invalidate this easement, as while it does cover a substantial portion of the property, it's less than in that case
	- Also, exclusive possession is not significantly hampered, the only thing the easement extends to is the tennis court and tennis-related activities
	- Could also try and draw an analogy to *Husky Oil v Shelf Holdings*
- Once we've established this, need to see if Pepper can exclude Alice from the court or prevent her from playing pickleball on it
- Pepper likely cannot exclude Alice, as the easement gives her the right to "uninterrupted use" of the court, at least for playing tennis
	- We know the easement is valid, so Pepper is out of luck here
- However, the terms of the easement do say that it only applies to the "playing of tennis and all related activities"
	- I can imagine that Pepper can argue that Alice went against the terms of the easement by converting the tennis court into a pickleball court
	- While the sports are similar, I imagine that the burden this new situation places on Pepper, as well as Alice's conduct, would lead the court to ruling in Pepper's favour here
- Overall, Pepper will not be successful in asking Alice to pay for the property taxes and plant the hedge, nor will he be able to exclude here from the easement
- However, he can likely get a court order requiring Alice to limit her use of the easement area to tennis only, not pickleball
