### [[Governance Property]]
- Main point of this article is dividing property into two main categories: exclusion property and governance property
- Exclusion view focuses on the relationship between the owner and the rest of the world, focused of course on the right to exclude
- Problem is that is assumes a single exclusive owner, does not work when it comes to quasi-common property or properties with multiple owners
- In practice, most land is not held by a single owner, think about corporate partnerships, marital homes, and strata properties
- Right to exclude has a blind spot when it comes to the internal realities of property
### [[Overlapping Claims in the Douglas Treaties]]
- Connects to the article we read about [[The Principle of Sharing]]
- How does co-ownership work under Coast Salish law?
- Here, the right to local resources is derived from your village, but also the kinship group that you belong to which generally extends across many villages
- When it comes to the *Indian Act*, only those distinct villages and bands are recognized as legal entities, conflicts with the reality of Coast Salish life by preventing overlapping claims
- No such thing as dual citizenship for bands, so where individuals could traditionally exercise their rights in villages connected by kin, the *Indian Act* system doesn't allow this
- Co-ownership in the Salish context means access based on both kinship and village membership
- Modern example of how this works is the MST Development Corporation, which deals with land that is simultaneously claimed by all three bands
- Work together to claim Crown land without competing for claims
### Co-Ownership at Common Law and Equity
- When we have a transfer to two parties (to A and B), what do we do?
- We've looked at how this works when we are granting two different interests, but here we are dealing with the granting of two equal interests
- When A dies, how that land transfers is determined on whether the grant was in joint tenancy or tenancy in common
- Determined by looking at four factors
	- Language in the grant
	- Four unities
	- Presumptions at common law
	- Actions of co-owners after the grant
#### Joint Tenancy v Tenancy in Common
- In both cases, each tenant has an equal right of possession to the whole, subject to the rights of the other tenants
- Can also transfer your interest in both cases, although anyone who buys it has to also enter into the joint or common tenancy situation
- However, only in tenancy in common can a tenant devise their interest in their will
- In the case of joint tenancy, the deceased is removed from the tenancy, and the joint tenancy is reassigned to all surviving tenants
- In effect, where there is only two joint tenants, the survivor is the sole owner and it's now a normal interest
- This is called the "right of survivorship", and is unique to joint tenancy
- Business corporations can be joint tenants, listed specifically under s. 31(1) of the *Business Corporations Act*
- However, corporations don't "die" in the same way as people, so instead when the company dissolves, then the normal rules of right of survivorship apply to their partners
### [[Ontario Law Reform Commission|Common Law Presumptions]]
- We've seen examples of joint tenancy before in [[Pecore v Pecore]], most common in family law
- Can create these interests by transferring land "as joint tenants" or "as tenants in common"
- No magic words required, as long as the intent is sufficiently clear
- If it's not clear what the intent was and the four unities are present, then we presume joint tenancy
- The four unities are as follows:
	- Unity of possession
		- Each owner is entitled to possession of the property subject to the equal right of other owners, applies to joint and common tenancy
	- Unity of interest
		- Holdings of the owners must be equal in nature, extent, and duration, otherwise you have reversionary/remainder interests or tenancy in common
	- Unity of time
		- Interests of the owners need to arise at the same time, except for where joint tenancy is created in a will
	- Unity of title
		- Interests of the owners need to come from the same act or instrument, meaning where one joint tenant transfers their interest, new person has tenancy in common and the remaining joint tenants keep the right of survivorship
		- Say you have B, C, and D, and C transfers to E
		- B and D are joint tenants and have right of survivorship, E has tenancy in common
		- If B or D dies, then the survivor gets their interest as tenancy in common because there are no joint tenants left to share with
- These are required at common law even when there is clear intention
### Equity Presumptions
- Equity uses different presumptions compared to a joint tenancy
- Three main situations where we presume tenancy in common instead of joint tenancy
	- Where co-owners contributed unequally to acquire the property
	- In commercial transactions involving business partners
	- Where co-owners were giving someone a loan for a mortgage, and held the property interest as security for a loan
		- Two people give someone money together for a mortgage and get the property presumably as tenants in common in exchange for the loan
- Recall that these are presumptions, they only apply where there is no clear intention
### Statutory Presumption
- Under s. 11(2) of the *Property Law Act*, land transferred or devised in fee simple, charged, or contracted to be sold to two or more persons is presumed to be tenancy in common
- This does not entirely displace the common law presumption
### [[Robb v Robb]]
- This is an example of a case where the common law beats the statutory presumption
- Deals with an apartment in Ocean Towers, a building co-op in English Bay next to the Sylvia Hotel
- This was fairly novel at the time, no real way to actually own a unit in a strata property because we didn't have the *Strata Property Act*
- You make a company who owns the building in fee simple, and to buy a suite you have to buy shares in the company that give you exclusive possession of a unit via a long-term lease
- Can buy and sell shares to transfer ownership of the suite and the lease
- The lease did not include payment of rent, only was there to transfer possession
- Issue in this case was whether Mr. and Mrs. Robb held these shares in joint tenancy or tenancy in common
- When Mr. Robb died, he left part of his estate to his children, if there was right of survivorship that determines what is in his estate
- If there was no right of survivorship, they may have a right to those shares
