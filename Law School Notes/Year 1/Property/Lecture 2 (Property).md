- First reading from [[The Meaning of Property|CB MacPherson]], a Canadian writer who had a specific interest in property
- Property changes both in terms of how we describe it and how it should be
- Originally, property was generally only owned by a single person, with land divided horizontally
- Population growth and increased density changes the way that ownership is understood
- One condominium owned by a realty group may have hundreds of stacked sub-owners within the larger property
- Property is about rights with respect to things, and the enforceability of those rights
- Includes [[The Meaning of Property#Common, Private, and State Property|common, private, and state property]]
- Private property is fairly self-explanatory, the owner has the right to exclude all others
- Common property has reverse exclusion rights, members of the community have the right to not be excluded from use
- A weird consequence of common property is that it appears as private property if you are outside the community allowed to use it
- State property is held by the state, and can often come in the form of common property like parks and roads
- Property does not justify itself, we have private property because it provides us with utility
- Encourages labour, grants privacy, etc.

### [[Property and the Right to Exclude]]
- Written by TW Merrill
- Comes up with points of consensus and contention on property
- 5 primary points of consensus
	1. Property refers to the rights of individuals with respect to resources
	2. These rights take precedent over possession
	3. The rights are enforced
	4. Property can be private, common or public
	5. The right to exclude is key to property
- Scholars differ on what the actual content of property rights are
- Three main theories which are [[Single-variable Essentialism]], [[Multiple-variable Essentialism]], and [[Nominalism]]
- Single-variable essentialism believes in a single view of property that revolves entirely on the right to exclusion and its reinforcement by the state
- This is a necessary and sufficient condition for property rights, it's all you need
- Property interests are distinct from contractual rights because they apply to all of society, not just to the person that you signed your contract with
- Multiple-variable essentialism believes that property requires multiple different rights that all form a part of property rights
- Different scholars have created lists of varying length of rights that come along with property
- Nominalism believes that there are no necessary conditions for property, and is instead determined by the structure of society
- Makes things very difficult to distinguish property rights from any other rights
- Simply just a word that can refer to any number of rights held by people
### [[Yanner v Eaton]]
- There were four different opinions on this case
- The majority opinion was authored by four judges, including Chief Justice Gleeson
- There was also a concurring opinion which came to the same conclusion but for different reasons
- Besides these two there were also two distinct dissenting opinions, one authored by Justice McHugh
- In Australian law, native title rights are statutory law, granting property of all free land to the Aboriginal peoples of Australia
- However, inconsistent interests will extinguish these native title rights
- In Canada, a statute needs to explicitly state that it is distinguishing a native title right, which makes things much more difficult especially because they are protected by the charter
- The key piece of legislation in this case was the Fauna Conservation Act which states that all fauna except for those taken during open season is property of the Crown and under control of the Fauna Authority
- The whole case hinges on whether or not this property right extinguishes native title rights
- Unless this property right is "absolute", it will not be inconsistent with native title rights
- CJ Gleeson specifically mentions in his opinion is that property is not a thing, but a legal relationship with a thing and how it relates to other people
- Property can refer to any number of interests, and does not always carry the same rights
- To try and find what form this property right takes, Gleeson looked to other examples of wild animals being property
- One example is that under common law, wild animals are not property until they are killed and taken
- This synergizes with the finding that Queensland mainly passed the Fauna Act to allow the government to collect royalties on fauna taken in the state
- Gleeson seems to take a [[Nominalism|nominalist]] view of property, talking about how property can only been defined by its context
- Property in this case is the power to regulate, but not to absolutely control
- As such, there is still room for native title rights to coexist alongside the Crown's property interest
### [[Yanner v Eaton#Dissenting Opinions|Dissenting Opinion]]
- J McHugh argues that property must involve the right to exclude
- This means that the Crown has the right to exclude Yanner and the Gangalidda from taking crocodiles because it is their property
- Disagrees with Gleeson on a fundamental level because of their differing views on property
- McHugh is a [[Single-variable Essentialism|single variable essentialist]] while Gleeson is a [[Nominalism|nominalist]]
- In his mind, there is nothing else that property could mean except for exclusive ownership of the object(s) in question
