- When we talk about trespass the chattel, the term "chattel" includes basically any good that is private property but is not real estate
- Think of it as anything that can be physically moved
- Has the same weight in public policy as any other intentional tort
- Just as tort law prohibits interference with the body of a person, it also prohibits interference with their possessions
- As always, intentionality is a key element, but trespass to chattel can also be performed negligently
- Plaintiff must establish that the defendant interfered with their possessions either intentionally, or negligently
- Must also be direct interference with the chattel
- Either defendant acted with the desire to trespass on chattel or acted in a way where trespass was **substantially certain** to occur
- Therefore, in trespass on chattel cases, mistake of fact is not a defence
### Everett v Martin
- Plaintiff was walking by a car and her coat was caught on the defendants car which was in disrepair and had parts sticking out, causing her coat to tear
- Was this an actionable trespass?
- Court held that the contact between the coat and the car was accidental, but the defendant still had to pay the plaintiff for not taking care of their car and acting negligently
### Ont Ltd v Canada
- Department of National Revenue obtained an order against someone and mistakenly seized the plaintiff's goods instead, thinking the order was against them
- Held the goods for three days, and returned them when the mistake was revealed
- This is an intentional trespass, mistake is no defence so the fact that they acted with the intention to seize the goods is sufficient to prove trespass
### Case Study 3
- Jane arrives at a party and places her purse on a table, and another guest does the same thing with an identical purse
- If Jane accidentally picks up the other guest's purse while leaving, is she committing trespass?
- No, because there was no **desire to interfere** with the goods of another
- In the *Ont Ltd* case, even though the Department of National Revenue had a legal right to the goods of the person they had a judgement against, they were still acting with a **desire to interfere**
### Legal Possession
- Trespass to chattel is defined by interference with legal possession of chattel
- Legal possession is defined by physical control over a good, and intent to control it
- Therefore, sometimes the court will even protect the possession of a wrongful possessor
- Done because the law wants to discourage violent dispossession
- Think about squatter's rights, you're not allowed to violently remove a squatter, you have to get a court order first
- Other examples why someone holding your property may not have to legally give it to you
- You take your car to the mechanic but don't have any money to pay them afterwards, the mechanic has a legal right to hold your car until you pay
### Detinue
- Wrongful detention of goods from a person that has a right to immediate possession
- No need for the plaintiff to establish title to the goods, they just have to prove legal possession
- In the case above, the mechanic would have right to possession of the car
- Difference between trespass and detinue, in the latter case the plaintiff is always not in possession of their property, while trespass cases can just relate to interference
- Key element of detinue is there has to be a demand by the plaintiff to the defendant to return their goods which must be rejected by the defendant
- Without this element, you can't establish detinue
- If the defendant is holding stolen goods, detinue still applies
- Trespass is considered lesser than detinue, which is lesser than conversion
- Wrongfulness is implicitly established when the good is stolen, so the plaintiff does not need to make a demand for return in these cases
### Conversion
- Relates to a positive and intentional act of interference with legal possession or right to immediate possession
- Basically applies when someone unlawfully holding your property tries to do something that would further interfere with your property rights such as selling or destroying it
- These are all methods of denying or seriously interfering with the possession rights of the plaintiff, "converting" the right of possession from the plaintiff
- As long as the physical consequences of the act are intended, no defence of mistake
- Recall that the act of interference has to be intentional, so destruction or loss of property through negligence may not qualify as conversion
- If there is refusal to return the goods, must establish that the refusal is done with the intent to convert
- Interference can be determined by the extent and duration of the ownership
- In *Dzaman v Riggs*, a car was held for three days and request to return was refused, but conversion was not established
- There was no intent to convert, and three days was not a substantial amount of time
- Of course in this case the plaintiff got their car back, clearly showing there was no conversion
- Conversion can be justified by good faith, in *Robertson v Stang* the plaintiff removed the goods of another to keep them safe
- Modification of a good can also constitute conversion
### Bolwell Fibreglass v Foley
- Plaintiff ordered and paid for a yacht, and commissioned the defendant to build it
- Bolwell requests the delivery of the yacht before it was completed, and Foley refused to deliver it as Bolwell had not paid them in full yet
- Not liable for conversion
### [[Penfolds v Elliot]]
- Plaintiff was an Australian wine maker that would sell wine in their marked bottles with the expectation that they would be returned to them once used
- Defendant was a hotelier who had a practice of filling bottles that customers would bring to the hotel, including many Penfolds bottles
- Trespass could not be established, since Penfolds did not have legal possession of the bottles
- They were essentially off on bailment to the customers
- Penfolds also argued that by filling the bottles with non-Penfolds wine, there was action for conversion
- However, since Elliott was not systematically using their bottles, he was just filling the bottles brough to him, didn't constitute a substantial interference
- Finally, there was also no detinue because Penfolds failed to demand return of the bottles, and even if they did, Elliott certainly would have complied
