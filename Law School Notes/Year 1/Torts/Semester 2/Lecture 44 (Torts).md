### Question 1
**Defendant concedes that he owes a duty of care and breached that duty, look for reasons why he should not be liable for the injuries**

- Main issues for establishing negligence are thin skull, vicarious liability, and causation
- Duty and standard of care are not relevant, conceded by the defendant
- Also have clear evidence of harm, seems that this is mainly a causation issue

The main issue we need to discuss to determine if Cleveland can be held liable for Purdue's injury is causation. Out of the four elements of negligence, three have already been clearly established here. Cleveland has admitted to owing Purdue a duty of care, and that he breached that duty of care through his actions. Additionally, there is clear evidence that Purdue was harmed by the knife handle hitting his temple, either by creating a significant bruise that began to bleed profusely due to his Hemophilia A, or by causing a subarachnoid haemorrhage. Overall, it seems that Purdue's claim will succeed, although they likely will not receive the full amount of damages.

Factual causation is easy to establish in this situation. If Cleveland had not placed the cutting machine on the table, or had simply clocked out at his assigned time, he would not have caused the knife to fly up and hit Purdue on the temple. Given that we only have one defendant in this case, this is a straightforward application of the "but-for" test for factual causation. (idk what case to cite to show that the but-for test is the standard)

When it comes to legal causation, there are a number of other factors we need to consider. The most important factor is Purdue's underlying medical condition of Hemophilia A, which clearly caused the hit to his temple to have much more severe consequences compared to an ordinary person. We know from [[Athey v Leonati]] that in these kinds of cases, the defendant will still be liable even though the plaintiff is suffering from a medical condition that exacerbated their injury. This will not be an issue in establishing legal causation. We also know that causation does not have to be identified with scientific precision from [[Snell v Farrell]]. This is not an issue that is directly raised on the facts, but could potentially arise if Cleveland attempted to argue that some aspect of Purdue's medical care contributed to the subarachnoid haemorrhage.

The next thing we need to consider is remoteness, was the risk of injury reasonably foreseeable given the circumstances. This is the test established in [[Wagon Mound No 1]], and applies in this case given that we only have a single plaintiff and defendant each. While the situation leading up to the injury was rather unusual, it seems that the risk of injury was likely reasonably foreseeable by Cleveland. Given that he was placing what was likely a moderately sized and fairly heavy piece of machinery on a table the plaintiff was working at that had several knives placed on it, it was reasonably foreseeable that this could have injured Purdue in some way. It was not necessary for Cleveland for foresee the precise mechanism of injury, as long as the injury was within the reasonable range of risk created by his activities. We know this from [[Hughes v Lord Advocate]], in that case the plaintiff was not expected to prove that the exact way they were injured was reasonably foreseeable, only that it was a potential risk of the breach of duty.

Finally, we should also consider the crumbling-skull rule. This principle was also explained in [[Athey v Leonati]], and can reduce the damages paid by the defendant if there is a measurable risk that the plaintiff would have suffered an injury in the future regardless of the defendant's actions due to an underlying medical condition. In this case, we know that due to the inhibitors that Purdue's body had developed, there was a 60% chance that he would suffer further complications from his medical condition, including spontaneous bleeds. While it is unclear whether or not these further complication could lead to a subarachnoid haemorrhage, there is a measurable risk that Purdue would be injured in the future regardless of Cleveland's actions, though potentially not to the same extent. The purpose of this principle is to avoid restoring the plaintiff beyond their original position. Given the sizeable chance of future injury, it is likely that Purdue's damages will be reduced at least slightly, though this will not disrupt legal causation altogether.

Overall, it seems fairly clear that Purdue will be able to establish legal causation here, which is the fourth element required to establish a claim of negligence in this case. However, there are still additional things we need to discuss including potential defences and vicarious liability.

Regarding defences, Cleveland does not appear to have any strong defences against Purdue's claim. There is no evidence of contributory negligence on Purdue's part, nor is there any evidence of a voluntary assumption of risk outside of choosing to go to work and accepting the inherent risks of working at a blade manufacturing company, or evidence of illegality. Voluntary assumption of risk is the strongest defence here, but is unlikely to have any impact on the case due to the lack of evidence supporting it, and the high bar for this defence in general which was established in [[Dube v Labar]].

Regarding vicarious liability, there is a chance that X-Blades can be liable for the injury instead. The Salmond test for vicarious liability has three parts, and requires a tort to be committed by an employee who was employed by a defendant business, and the tort must be committed during the course of employment.

The first two parts are clearly established. We have already shown that Cleveland can be held liable for negligence, and he is employed by X-Blades. The only issues arise in determining whether or not this tort was committed during the course of his employment. Cleveland was working at the manufacturing company at the time the tort was committed, but was working over time contrary to company policy. Given that the employer did not authorize this, it seems likely that Cleveland will still be personally liable for the injuries suffered by Purdue. While the Salmond test does allow for liability for injuries caused by unauthorized means of carrying out an authorized activity, Cleveland's actions seem to be entirely unauthorized by the company. Purdue can argue that the place and purpose of the incident were clearly related to the employer, X-Blades has a strong argument when it comes to the time of the offence, which is the third relevant factor for vicarious liability. 

Overall, it seems that while this argument may be convincing, it's more of a technicality than anything. It depends on how the court sees the issue, but the fact that Cleveland was at the workplace and carrying out activities expected by his employment when he breached the duty of care seems to weigh strongly in favour of establishing vicarious liability here.

In summary, Purdue's claim against Cleveland is likely to succeed. He will be able to prove damage and causation, which are the only two elements of negligence contested by Cleveland. Cleveland also has no viable defences against this claim. However, it is also likely that the damages Purdue is claiming will be reduced slightly by the crumbling skull rule. Additionally, Purdue has the option of making a claim against X-Blades instead of making a claim against Cleveland. (I'm not sure if vicarious liability makes the employee immune to liability or not, but I don't think so)
