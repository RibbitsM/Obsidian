- Topic for today is the standard of care
- In these cases, the issue is not whether the defendant owes a duty to the plaintiff, but whether or not the actions of the defendant breached the expected standard of care
- What is the standard of care? How is it different from case to case?
- Once we establish that a duty is owed, you need a standard to show whether that duty has been adhered to
- There is one main standard of care, and then several special cases within that standard
- The general standard is the objective standard, that of the reasonable person
- What would a reasonable person do in the circumstances?
### [[Vaughan v Menlove]]
- In this case, the defendant built a haystack on the border of his property
- Neighbours warned him he might set it on fire, but the defendant stated that he would take the chance, but did build a chimney inside it
- It set on fire spontaneously, and burned down a bunch of neighbouring buildings
- Court applied the reasonable standard, saying he should have exercised all reasonable care that a prudent person would have
- Defendant argued that the standard did not apply to him as it was too uncertain, how can you tell if it was met or not
- Pointed out that he should not be held accountable for not being as smart as the average person, and the standard should be set according to that
- Thus, the test should be if he took as much prudence as was capable within his capacity
- Judge on the case said that this new subjective test would be so vague that it would be no rule at all, emphasized that the objective test is the traditional rule
- Here, the defendant is asking for an exception to the traditional test to allow for his level of intelligence, which we generally do not do, especially for adults
- Does create an exception in the case where the defendant has a mental disability, that's one of the only legitimate grounds where we can modify the standard for intelligence
- Court will not insist on this label, defendant has to raise the evidence
- The definition of the reasonable person has been a major point of contention in modern critical theory, suggested to modify it to account for the diversity of the human race
- Generally, the standard has been implicitly and explicitly interpreted to place white men as the "reasonable person", reflected by the language of early British jurisprudence
- This case held that all peoples should be assessed by the same standard to guarantee consistency in the application of the law
- However, we now know there are a number of exceptions
## Medical/Mental Impairment
- First example of an exception to the reasonable person standard
### Buckley v Smith Transport
- Truck driver got into a crash, company argued that the driver had some sort of severe mental impairment rendering him incapable of controlling the truck
- Died of syphilis sometime after the incident
- Appeared that he had a mental break caused by syphilis while driving, which made him unable to understand and discharge the duty of care
- Seemed like he genuinely believed that the truck was being remotely controlled and that he was not actually driving the truck
- Court held that the employer was not liable because the employee was affected by the disease to the point where he could not understand or discharge to duty of care
- This is the standard that has to be met to create an exception for mental impairment
- Just because the defendant has some sort of mental health issues, this does not mean that they get an automatic exception from negligence
- Only applies where there is a connection between their mental condition and their ability to understand and discharge the duty of care
- Therefore, even if an individual shows some evidence of insanity, the standard objective test can still apply to them depending on the facts
- Overall, defendants are assumed to be able to choose between negligent and non-negligent acts, and liability will be imposed for making the wrong choice
- However, if the defendant is unable for some reason to make the right choice, they will not be held liable for negligence
- In *Vaughan*, the defendant had the capacity to see the risk, but decided to accept the rsik
- But in *Buckley*, the syphilis in the defendant's brain removed this capacity
### Mansfield v Weetabix
- Employee of the defendant had a condition causing his brain to malfunction when his blood sugar was low
- However, the employee was not aware of this condition
- Caused a number of accidents as a result, he was a driver for the defendant
- Court held that the standard of care was that for the reasonable driver unaware that he was suffering from this condition
- Imposing the objective standard here would be a form of strict liability, can't impose liability because awareness is required for foreseeability
## Age/Youth
### [[McHale v Watson]]
- In this case, the defendant was a 12 year old boy who hit the plaintiff with a sharpened welding wrong while playing with her
- Issue on appeal was whether the trial judge erred by applying a different standard of care
- Court looked at American tort law, which has three different categories of care for youths
- One category is for babies and infants, which are children that they are incapable of exercising any of the qualities required to perceive risk
- For this category, they cannot be held liable for negligence
- Next category is young adults, they are still minors but are fully capable of foreseeing the possible consequences of their actions
- For this category, the normal objective test applies
- Third category is everything in between, where the capacity of the child varies greatly
- For this category, the standard is what you would expect from a child with the same age and level of experience/intelligence
- This is a substantial modification to the objective test, and is somewhat subjective
- Judges ultimately mostly agreed with the trial court, and held that the defendant acted as a normal 12 year old boy would have, and did not breach the standard of care
- For these cases, the test is whether the defendant did anything a reasonable boy of 12 would do while possessing and exercising the foresight and care of that group
### Concurring Opinion
- Concurring judgement agreed that we need to modify the standard to account for childhood, but we should not rely on the intelligence or experience of the child
- Only relevant factor should be age, we don't want to consider intelligence for risk of committing an error similar to the defendant's argument in *Vaughan*
- Childhood is a stage of development, it's not a specific idiosyncrasy of the person
- Legitimate limit on the capacity of the defendant
### Dissenting Opinion
- Judge Menzies argued that the normal standard should be used here, there is no room for variation
- Even if the standard was that of the reasonable boy, the defendant would still be liable
- Overall, majority said we consider age, intelligence, and experience, Kitto said we should consider age only, and Menzies said we use the normal standard
- This is an Australian case, we'll look at the Canadian standard later
- Ireh prefers to look only at the stage of development as a factor
