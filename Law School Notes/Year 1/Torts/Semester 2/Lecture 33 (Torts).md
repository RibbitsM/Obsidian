### [[R v Hill]]
- Crim case dealing with how the ordinary person test works with provocation
- Dissenting opinion points out how the legal standard should increment with age according to the developmental the accused youth is in
- Standard of care for children is semi-objective, because it's adjusted for age
- Points to the three-stage approach used in [[McHale v Watson]]
- Affirms the arguments made by Kitto in that case, only uses age, not experience/intelligence
- However, in earlier cases, the SCC used the more comprehensive semi-subjective test used by the majority in *McHale v Watson*
### McErlean v Sarel
- Used again in an Ontario case a few years after *Hill* called *McErlean v Sarel*
- This case involved a collision between two teenagers riding trail bikes
- Court argued that the law should recognize that children have vastly different capacities, and the court should include subjective factors to account for this
- Treating children more leniently than adults under the law is in the interest of children and serves to protect them, which is in the public interest
- However, children should be held to the same standards as adults when engaging in adult activities that are inherently dangerous like trail riding
- Supported by previous case law as well as academic articles recommending this approach
### Summary for Age/Youth
- Majority in *McHale v Watson* support a semi-subjective test that takes age, intelligence, and experience into account when assessing liability for young defendants
	- *McEllistrum v Etches* and *McErlean v Sarel* are the main Canadian cases advocating for this approach, albeit not if the defendant was doing dangerous adult activities
	- This is the approach preferred by the SCC
- Concurring opinion from Kitto in *McHale v Watson* and the dissent in *R v Hill* prefer the modified objective approach, where age is the only consideration
	- This approach is not used at law, but is preferred by Ireh
### [[The Reasonable Man]]
- Traditional standard is the man of ordinary prudence, the man on the Clapham omnibus
- We should not be relaxing this standard for defendants who cannot measure up to it, as the victims would still have to suffer the harms caused by their substandard behaviour
- Point of having an objective standard is to set a bar for society to be measured by
- Can still have some aspects of subjectivity through modifications to this standard
- Changes can come from the case law or policy
#### Knowledge
- Except in the case of children, we will not consider a lack of knowledge in the objective standard
- However, if the defendant is expected to have superior knowledge of the situation, for example if you are an expert in the field or experienced this situation before, held to a higher standard
- For people with superior knowledge, they are measured against what would be expected by a reasonable person with the same knowledge
	- Not just knowledge in the academic sense, also applies to your experience of risk
- Doctors will be judged by the standard of an average practitioner in their field, meaning that specialists have different standards from GPs
	- Especially based on how you represent yourself, perception of your specialization is very important here
	- If you hold yourself to have some special skill, you will be liable if the plaintiff is relying on that skill to their detriment
- Similar to the knowledge case, beginners are held to the same standard as someone reasonably skilled in that field/activity, not to the standard of other beginners
	- Reason for this is the need to compensate victims, similar to people with less knowledge, we don't want victims to suffer for the incapacities of beginners
- For any task requiring an expert skill, no matter who was performing the task, they will be held to the same standard as an expert in that task
	- Similar to the rule on adult activities from *McErlean v Sarel*, you should be judged based on the activity you participated in, not your individual skill
	- This is particularly important when it comes to any task that impacts public safety
#### Physical, Intellectual, and Emotional Impairments
- This is where we see the most modification of the objective standard
- People with physical disabilities and those lacking the use of some senses are held to the same standard as the reasonable person sharing that disability
- Some other modifications may be made as well, we don't expect blind people to perceive things visually, but are expected to be better able to perceive with their other senses
- This requires a careful balancing act between preserving the freedom of disabled people while also protecting public safety
	- This is why blind people are allowed to walk around unassisted, but can't drive, a reasonable blind person would not be driving a car
	- We also know that drivers who lose control due to some physical condition or become involuntary are generally not liable
- Mental and emotional impairments are generally not accounted for
- *Vaughan v Menlove* states pretty clearly that the law does not account for the infinite varieties of temperament, intellect, and education
	- Only exception is where a mental condition prevents the defendant from being able to assess the standard of care like in *Buckley*
#### Age and Lunacy
- Already know that we can modify the standard for minors
- Some authority for using a modified semi-subjective standard for the elderly and infirm
	- For dangerous activities like driving, normal standard still applies
- Also need to make adjustments for their lack of mobility as pedestrians
- Mental illness is more controversial, Fleming says that it should never be taken into account
- However, there does seem to be case supporting the view that we should excuse people who are unable to appreciate the duty of care and modify their behaviour
