- Recall that harm is required for an action in negligence, no liability without injury
- Additionally, the act or omission of the defendant must result in an injury to the plaintiff, we must establish causation
- Third major element of negligence after duty and standard of care
- What we're doing here is establishing that the breach of the standard of care led to the harm suffered by the plaintiff
- To establish that the defendant is at fault, we need to show that they caused the injury to the plaintiff, otherwise they have not wronged them
- Even if the defendant did something wrong, if they didn't cause harm, there's no action
### Factual vs Legal Causation
- Recall that the injury also has to be foreseeable, we can have factual causation, but not legal causation
- Similar concept as in criminal law, think about [[Lecture 11 (Crim)|Maybin]]
- Factual causation will not necessarily lead to legal fault if the connection is too weak
- Issue for factual causation is simply whether the defendant's act/omission caused the injury suffered by the plaintiff
- Several issues for legal causation, also called proximate cause or remoteness
- Did the outcomes of the defendant's risk include the injury suffered by the plaintiff?
	- Basically asking if the harm was foreseeable given the nature of the risk
	- Harms resulted from a bizarre Rube Goldberg machine chain of events are not reasonably foreseeable, even if there was causation in fact
- Other way to think about this is what the limits are on the defendants liability
	- Example of this was a case of a father who slapped her daughter, and she fell into a coma as a result
	- Court found that this injury was not within the risk of the activity
	- Would have been liable if the coma was also a result of an underlying condition, this is because of the thin-skull rule
### Factual Causation
- This is always a question of fact, not law
- Need to look at the history and the known facts of the incident, and see if the events leading up to the injury were in some part caused by the defendant's actions
- Not a simple topic, there are a number of different kinds of tests and doctrines that we apply to resolve causation in certain situations
- One of these is the "but-for" test, which just tries to assess whether or not the injury would have happened without the defendant's intervention
	- Example for this is a patient with sciatica who goes to a chiropractor, and is inured as a result
	- Did the injury come from the chiropractor, or the underlying condition
	- Doesn't just apply to underlying condition cases, anywhere there are multiple potential factors of causation that could have created the injury
- Another issue we have to deal with is where there are multiple different people who could have caused the injury
- In underlying injury cases we also need to consider the thin-skull rule
	- Example of this could be a patient with a condition that thins their blood getting a blood test and then bleeding out
- Can also have cases where a plaintiff is for some reason pre-disposed to future injuries
	- For example, they got in a climbing accident and then later suffer a disc herniation
	- Should the damages consider the plaintiff's potential vulnerability to the herniation because of their previous accident?
	- Called the "crumbling skull rule", slightly different from the thin skull rule
- Also possible to have cases of factual uncertainty, where no factual causation can be established
### But For Test
- Primary and simplest test for factual causation, did the injury occur but for the defendant's action?
- Can also think about it like whether the injury would have occurred if the defendant was never involved at all, if it would have, there is no causation
- Not particularly helpful where there are multiple negligent parties involved or multiple potential factors of causation
- In these situations, we have to resort to considering the material contribution of the different tortfeasors or causation factors
- Plaintiff holds the burden of proving factual causation, like almost all other elements
- Where the plaintiff can't establish a causal connection, this is causal uncertainty
	- In cases like these, the court may look at the type of risk and nature of the injury
	- Depending on the situation, defendant may still be liable
	- If the injury is associated with the type of risk associated with the defendant's act, this may be enough for negligence despite causal uncertainty
	- For example, say a worker suffers a burn at their workplace where molten metal is present, the type of injury would be similar to the type of risk
	- Other example could be a lung condition suffered by a plaintiff working in a workplace where noxious fumes are usually present
- This is the type of case that is likely to come up on an exam, factual causation is too easy
### [[Athey v Leonati]]
- Example of several causes of injury causing complications for factual causation
- Appellant suffered back injuries from 2 consecutive car accidents, and later suffered a disc herniation while stretching their back
- Defendants accepted liability for the accidents, but argued that the herniated disc was caused by the exercises and a pre-existing condition
- At trial the judge said that the accidents were a minor contributing factor, and awarded only 25% of the damages, arguing the other 75% was due to other factors
- Appeal court also dismissed the case, but then the case was appealed to the SCC
- Found that the trial judge erred, and the plaintiff should get full compensation
### General Principles of Causation
- The principles set out in this case have been widely adopted as the tests for causation
- Where a plaintiff proves on a balance of probabilities that the defendant either caused or contributed to their injury, they are liable
- Use the but-for test to determine this, but it's not a conclusive test
- If you have a situation that makes this test insufficient, need to consider other factors
- Don't need to prove that the defendant's act was the sole cause of injury, it's enough for the defendant to merely contribute to the injury
	- Can have other non-tortious contributing factors like exercise or a underlying condition
- For cases with multiple tortfeasors, need to determine if the actions of the defendant were a material contribution to the injury
	- However, this principle does not apply where there is only one defendant, not established in this case so it's use in this case was retroactively inappropriate
- Respondents argument was that because there were both tortious and non-tort causes, the damages should be awarded in proportion to their contribution
	- Hence the 25% figure from the trial court
- SCC said that you can only do this for multiple tortious causes, the full value of damages should be awarded for any tortious conduct
- Can't split causes with non-tort conduct, because that's not full compensation
- Recall that negligence can be based on either full causation or a contributing cause
- Rationale for this is the complexity of the situation, and the need to give the plaintiff full compensation to return them to their original position
- By dividing damages by degree of causation, the original position is not restored
- You can apportion damages between defendants because in that case, there is still full compensation, it's just split between multiple tortfeasors
- If the plaintiff contributed to their own negligence, then you can split the damages between the plaintiff and defendant, still technically full compensation
- But in this case, there was a single and indivisible injury
#### Thin/Crumbling Skull Doctrines
- Did the crumbling skull rule apply because the plaintiff was predisposed to a disc herniation?
- Likely the strongest argument advanced by the defendant
- Applies where the plaintiff is especially susceptible to injury for some medical reason
- When considering damages for negligence, we need to take this into account
- Therefore, the defendant is liable even where the injury is unexpectedly severe because of a pre-existing medical condition
- Despite the fact that the injury was unforeseeable, still liable
- This is the thin-skull rule, used to apply liability in situations where the plaintiff suffered a severe injury because of their pre-existing condition
	- Also applies where the defendant's actions exacerbated a pre-existing condition
- This principle also recognizes that the pre-existing condition was inherent to the plaintiffs original position, and we need to consider that when restoring that position
- Law will not seek to put the position in a better position, if they were already suffering in some way, we need to take that into account
- Defendant is not expected to pay for any effects of their pre-existing condition, because the plaintiff would have experienced those harms anyway
- This is a forward-looking principle, were trying to predict what might happen in the future
- Overall principle is if there's a measurable risk that this condition will affect the plaintiff in the future irrespective of the defendant's actions, can use this to reduce damages
- In this case, the court determined that this principle didn't apply because there was no measurable risk that the herniation would have occurred anyway
### Decision
- Court found that the 25% award at trial was not enough, and was only based on the material contribution rule (which shouldn't have been applied anyway)
- Found that there was enough to render the defendants fully liable
### Summary
- The "but for" test is the general test for causation
	- Doesn't require negligence to be the sole cause of injury
	- Defendants are only liable for injuries caused by their negligent acts
- Defendant is liable for the full extent of an injury even if it was partly due to an pre-existing condition (thin skull rule)
- Injury from a pre-existing condition that would arise regardless of the accident is considered when it comes to calculation damages (crumbling skull rule)
	- Future possibilities are weighted depending on how likely they are
- Causation has to be either proven or not proven, can't just be speculative
	1. If defendants acts caused or contributed to the injury, causation is proven
	2. If the herniation would have occurred at the same time as the accidents, no causation
	3. If you need both the accident and the condition for the injury, causation proven because of the thin skill rule
	4. If either the accident or condition could cause the injury, unclear causation
	5. If the herniation would happen in the future without the accidents, causation proven but damages are limited by the crumbling skull rule
