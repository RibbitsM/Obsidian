### [[Bolton v Stone]]
- Plaintiff was an old woman who got hit by a cricket ball while walking down the street and was severely injured
- Ball had gone onto the road six times in the last 28 years, not unprecedented but quite rare
- Traditional test was applied, but a debate arose over whether the standard was based on the foreseeability of the incident or the probability of the incident arising
- The latter is what Posner suggests in [[The Learned Hand Formula]]
- Court found that the standard of care only extends to preventing harms that were reasonably risky, and not to preventing any foreseeable harm
- Defendant was ultimately not held liable
- Outlined the main framework for using this kind of "learned hand" analysis
- Main distinction being drawn here is between unforeseeable risk and foreseeable real risks
- This decision is arguing that we have to consider more than just foreseeability
#### Foreseeable and Unforeseeable Risks
- An unforeseeable risk will never impose liability, these are accidents so unusual that no person could have foreseen it, example was a dog busting out of a car, sending broken glass into someone's eye
- A foreseeable risk generally does impose liability, but the court argues that in reality, a reasonable person will take a chance on a foreseeable risk
- Technically anything that has happened before is foreseeable, no matter how unusual or rare
- It would be a breach of care to not take steps to prevent these past risks
- Court in this case argued we should refine the ambit of foreseeable risk to account for the probability of that risk
- Divides between small foreseeable risks, and substantial foreseeable risks
- Argues that people must guard against reasonable probabilities, but are not bound to guard against fantastic possibilities
- Recall that the law does not protect all people from all risks, defendant is only bound to avoid from creating a substantial risk
- Question before the court was whether the risk of someone being hurt was small enough that a reasonable person would disregard that risk
- This is basically just the learned hand formula, B > P x L
- Don't explicitly state the burden element, but the latter part is definitely there
- Imply that the L element is important too, a greater harm will elevate the need to avoid even an unlikely harm
#### Summary
- Reasonable person avoids imposing a foreseeable risk on others
- Reasonable person takes greater care where there is a higher likelihood (P) of damage and where the severity of the threatened harm is great (L)
- The reasonable person takes less care where there is a small likelihood of damage and where the severity of the threatened harm is slight
- Trier of fact must exercise their own judgement on the amount of risk and severity of outcome to determine if the standard of care was met
- As mentioned earlier, this analysis ignores the burden part of the learned hand formula
- Court says no because it would not be right to take into account the difficulty of remedial measures when it comes to determining the standard of care
- If the risk is so severe and the consequences are so serious, burden is irrelevant
### Wagon Mound No 2
- Tanker called the "Wagon Mound" was docked to take on oil, but because of a poor fitting on the oil tank, the oil was discharged into the harbour
- Dock owner was welding at the time, set the oil in the water on fire, burning the dock and nearby boats whose owners sued the tanker crew
- At trial, judge found there was no liability because the risk of this happening was so low
- House of Lords held that the trial court was wrong, distinguished it from *Bolton* because in that case, there was an expense associated with eliminating the unlikely risk
- Argues that it's only reasonable to neglect attending to even a miniscule risk unless there is some sort of valid reason to ignore that risk
- In this case there was basically no cost to avoiding the risk, and the damage was so severe that a reasonable person would not have disregarded even that small risk
- Probability of the risk in this case was quite small, but because the cost of precautions was also so low, the defendants should have taken those precautions
- States that *Bolton* recognized that it's justifiable to not eliminate a real risk if the probability was small, and a reasonable person would also neglect that risk
- Does not deviate from the traditional principles of negligence
### Latimer v AEC
- Factory flooded, after draining the water the floor was still slippery, they sawdusted the floor but didn't have enough sawdust
- Employee slipped on a part of the floor with no sawdust, injured themselves and sued
- Defendant was not liable because the cost would be shutting down the plant
- Based the decision on *Wagon Mount*, because burden was greater than the PL, defendant did not need to take maximum amount of care
- Overall, cost of avoiding risk completely was too high, the injury was an acceptable cost
- Plaintiff didn't establish that a reasonably prudent employer would have shut down the factory
- Probably the biggest example of the impact of the learned hand formula
## Rule of Statutes
- Where a plaintiff is injured where a statutory obligation has been breaches, is the breach the basis for a negligence action?
- Basically, does a breach of statute impact the reasonability assessment
- Statutes impose a duty and penalty for the breach, but does not explicitly say this impacts a civil action
### [[R v Sask Wheat Pool]]
- Defendant breached the *Canada Grain Act* which led to economic losses on the part of the plaintiff by delivering infested grain to them
- Court held that there is no tort for breach of statutory duty in Canada
- If the statute is silent about creating a civil duty on top of a penal offence, then there is no possibility of an action in negligence
- In the case of a common law duty of care, courts can point to statutory standards as evidence for determining the standard of care
- On the basis of this decision, the plaintiff had no cause of action because they did not allege there was a breach of a common law duty
- Relying exclusively on the statutory breach, and the Act did not create a civil remedy
- Other cases have tried to distinguish this, but overall this rule has persisted
- Overall, the outcome was that an action cannot be based purely on a statutory breach, but a common law action can rely on statutory standards as evidence
- Defendants can also raise statutes as evidence, recall that the defence of [[Lecture 22 (Torts)|statutory compliance]] works this way
### [[Ryan v City of Victoria]]
- Statutory standards are relevant for determining reasonability, but compliance with statutory duties does not automatically lead to exercising reasonable care
- Number of factors to consider
	- Where statute is specific, this is very compelling evidence
		- Was the statute aimed at the exact activity exercised by the plaintiff?
	- Was there little discretion on how to perform this statutory duty?
		- If the statute is general or the circumstances were unusual, the court will give less weight to the statute as evidence of reasonability
#### Role of Customs
- When we talk about customs, we are talking about the general practice of those engaging in a specific are of practice like law or medicine
- These customs are very relevant for creating the standard of care for this practice
- A custom that the defendant follows as a matter of practice would not be labelled as negligence
- Basically saying it's not their fault because they were complying with general practice
- On the other side of the coin, not complying with general practice can be grounds for liability
- In either case, the court will still have to do a traditional assessment of negligence, compliance or non-compliance with custom does not alone create a *prima facie* standard of care
- Court has to consider a number of factors
	- When was practice established?
	- Has the circumstances changed?
	- How many people involved
	- How long has the practice continued?
- If a sufficient number of people have been following these practices over a certain amount of time, that is enough to establish a custom
- Court can also take *judicial notice* of the custom, which means that the presence of that custom is considered common knowledge and can be brought in without proof
- Practice must be carefully and deliberately adopted and applied
- Means you need to actually have a reason beyond adhering to tradition for following the practice, needs to be a deliberate reason for adoption
### [[Ter Neuzen v Korn]]
- Doctor was practicing artificial insemination, gave the patient HIV by injecting them with infested semen
- Argued they were not liable because they followed the customary standard of practice
- Court considered whether this practice itself could be considered negligent
- Jury was instructed on two issues, either the doctor could be found negligent for failing to comply with standards, or they could find the standards negligent
- In the end, the jury found that the doctor was negligent
- Appeal court said that at the time, specialists had no knowledge of the risk of infection and there were no established screening practices or warnings
- Very little knowledge on how HIV spread or how it could be prevented
- One of the donors the doctor did ask about their sexual practices lied, and thus the defendant could not be found negligent despite complying with the insufficient practices
- Reasonable person test for experts is the conduct of a reasonable similar expert
- In this particular case, the conduct of doctors must be judged by knowledge that would be reasonably possessed by experts at the time of the incident
- Can't look at a 1947 accident with 1954 spectacles, hindsight is always 20/20
- Even if the standard at the time was clearly insufficient now, it was the best and most accepted process at the time, and the defendant should not be held liable for that
- Important note is that a common practice can be deemed negligent if it is fraught with obvious risk, the jury had the option to do this in this case
- As long as it wasn't a highly technical process, the jury can make the same decision a reasonable person would come to
#### Summary
- While compliance will usually exonerate someone for negligence, the practice itself can be found negligent where a reasonable person can look at it and see the flaws
- If the jury can look at the common practice at the time and identified the clear failures without any sort of scientific knowledge, then practice is negligent
- Supreme Court agreed with the Court of Appeal that the case involved difficult and technical scientific questions
- Evidence on the prevention of HIV was variable and the proper instruction for the jury was whether the doctor acted reasonably
- Two ways to find negligence were if the doctor violated the custom, or if the custom itself was negligent
- Doctor met the custom, and court found the custom was too complex to rule on
- Overall, the defendant was not found negligent
