- For a negligence action, the plaintiff must prove their case against the defendant on the civil standard
- This means a balance of probabilities, lower than the criminal standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt
### [[Prosser Proof of Negligence]]
- Plaintiff must have sufficient evidence to support their claim, need proof of facts to establish duties or standard of care
- Trier of fact holds the responsibility for weighing the evidence provided by the plaintiff and determining its value
- First element is duty of care, need to establish two things:
	- Is there a relation between the parties imposing legal obligations on the defendant?
	- Is the violated interest of the plaintiff entitled to legal protection?
	- This is a question of law, determined by the court
- Second element is the standard of care
	- Generally, this is what the reasonable person would do
	- Law does not have set rules for all possible situations, will probably need to do this on a case by case basis
### [[Byrne v Boadle]]
- Direct evidence of negligence is obviously preferred, but there are limited cases where circumstantial evidence can be used instead
- Latin phrase means "the thing speaks for itself", refers to cases where the trier of fact can infer negligence based on the facts
- In this case, a barrel of flour rolled out of a warehouse window and landed on a pedestrian
- Court found that there was no other explanation for how this happened without some negligence from the defendant
- Thus, the judge drew an inference that the defendant was likely at fault, and ruled for the plaintiff
- Jurisprudence on this topic has made the mistake of tying two distinct principles into one
	- First principle determines where circumstantial evidence is sufficient to prove negligence
	- Second principle determines where the burden of proof shifts to the defendant to disprove their negligence
- Traditional principle is that where something under the management of the defendant causes an accident that could not have happened without negligence, that's evidence of negligence
- Unless the defendant can prove otherwise, the court will decide for the plaintiff
### [[Baker v Market Harborough]]
- Husbands of both plaintiffs were killed in a head on collision in company cars, plaintiffs sued the companies
- There was little evidence as to how the accident actually happened as both drivers were killed and there were no witnesses
- Court found that the available facts showed that there must have been some negligence from each driver
- If either one of the drivers or both were to blame for the accident, must have been negligence
- In the absence of any conclusive evidence, both drivers were found equally liable and each plaintiff received half of the damages
### [[A Res Ipsa Loquitur Nutshell]]
- There has been continuous discussion of this principle, and there are multiple complications associated with it
- Two main debates surrounding *res ipsa loquitur*
	- First, does the judge have to infer that the defendant's conduct caused the plaintiff's injury?
	- Second, what does the defendant need to show to rebut the inference?
- Seems that in Canada at least, the judge is never compelled to make the inference, and the strength of the inference depends on the case
- Also, the ultimate burden of proof will always lay on the plaintiff
- Defendant does not need to prove their innocence, just to prevent the inference
- If the jury cannot decide which side is more compelling, they should side with the defendant
### [[Fontaine v BC]]
- Husband and his friend went on a hunting trip, but did not return and were reported missing
- Later found dead in the friend's truck, the friend was driving and it appeared the car had gone off the highway during a storm
- Plaintiff argued that the fact the car left the highway was *prima facie* evidence of negligence on the driver's part
- Trial court dismissed the case as the plaintiff could not prove on a BOP that the friend's negligence led to the husband's death
- Appeal to the BCCA was rejected
- Court found that *res ipsa loquitur* only applies where there is no direct evidence, and we only have circumstantial evidence
	- If you have direct evidence, use that instead
- When we are using this circumstantial evidence, the strength of the inference being drawn depends on the strength of the evidence
- Trial judge was within their right to say that the evidence was not strong enough to permit an inference here
- Situations where the facts only whisper negligence, an others where they shout it out
- There were a bunch of unknown factors here, for example the court knew a bad storm happened during the time period, but it was not known if that was when the two men were driving
### Modern Relevance
- This case is around where the principle stands today
- Circumstantial evidence will rarely prove the case on its own, unless it's very obvious that there was negligence
- Plaintiff will need a significant amount of evidence to prove this
- Defence can also present evidence to the contrary, including providing alternate explanations for how the incident occurred
- This is what happened in *Fontaine*, the defence claimed the accident could have happened because of the storm
- Principle still exists, but its relevance has shrunk significantly
