### [[McGhee v National Coal Board]]
- Recall that Reid argued that the plaintiff will only succeed if they can prove that the defendant caused or materially contributed to the injury
- Distinguished this from material contributions to the **risk**
- This should be considered distinct from the material contribution test
- Court said that when we have two separate causes, it is enough to decide for the plaintiff if one cause arose from the defendant's fault
- Couldn't prove at the time exactly how dermatitis occurred, but the evidence suggested that the plaintiff biking home without washing could have contributed
- Court needed to take a "broader view of causation" to determine the result since they were not confident of the actual mechanisms leading to the injury
#### Wilberforce
- Argued that the key question was this:
- If the plaintiff can show a breach increasing risk, but cannot prove a material contribution to their injury while the defendants can't prove otherwise, should their case fail?
- Who should bear the consequences in this scenario?
- Injury occurred within the area of risk created by the defendant, so the defendant should bear the loss unless evidence of another cause of the injury can be brought
- Where medical evidence cannot segregate the compound causes of an illness, the creator of the risk should "suffer from the inherent evidential difficulty" and bear the costs
- Creator of the risk must have foreseen the harm
- Court knew that hot air and dust could probably lead to dermatitis, but did not have the science necessary to explain that link
- Because the defendant had not addressed created by the hot air and dust, they breached their duty of care, and the injury caused was within the risk created
- Keep in mind that the rule of "injury occurring within the risk" and the rule of being unable to separate causes are distinct
#### Simon
- Said that where an injury is caused by multiple cumulative factors and at least one is a breach of duty, where one makes it impossible to determine the proportion of harm, plaintiff should not be expected to prove the impossible
- Thus, defendant should bear the costs
- Failure to take steps to materially reduce the risk contributed to the injury
- Lord Salmon came to a very similar decision
- Court ultimately held that the distinction between materially increasing a risk and materially contributing to the risk is too vague to be recognized by the law
### [[Snell v Farrell]]
- Elderly patient was having vision problems, needed surgery to remove a cataract
- Long story short, something went wrong with the surgery, causing a blood vessel to burst in the eye which eventually led to a stroke and the atrophy of the optic nerve
	- Doctor detected the burst during surgery and waited for it to abate as he was supposed to, only discovered days later that more blood had escaped than expected
- This was also partly due to an underlying medical condition, specifically the stroke
- Unclear whether this was the sole reason for the stroke or not
- Facts were very complicated, made more difficult by the fact that the plaintiff was unconscious which is a typical issue for medical malpractice
- Key issue in this case was whether malpractice suits required the same standard of causation as other negligence cases because of this difficulty
- Court rejected using the same approach from *McGhee* because it shifted the burden of proof to the defendant, this is what the trial judge did
- Suggested that the plaintiff could prove that the defendant created a risk that the injury which occurred would occur, or defendant has the burden of disproving causation
- Taking this approach could result in deciding for the plaintiff without a clear connection between the injury and the defendant's conduct
- Because of the possibility that the harm could be disconnected from the defendant entirely, we should not assume the defendant played some role in the injury
- Different from *McGhee* where it was clear the defendant did something wrong
- After *McGhee*, there were multiple example of malpractice cases in the UK where this approach was attempted, but it generally failed
### Summary
- Main issues with the usual causation rules was that it was being applied too strictly
- Causation does not need to be determined with scientific precision (key part of the ratio)
- Cited a UK case that said that causation was better determined by "ordinary common sense rather than abstract metaphysical theory" (Alphacell v Woodward 1972, Lord Salmon)
- Generally, where the defendant has the majority of knowledge of the facts, they may be required to prove it in lieu of the plaintiff (most malpractice cases/other technical cases)
- Court is doing this to try and encourage a looser interpretation of causation
- In the absence of evidence to the contrary adduced by the defendant regarding causation, approach of the court should be to draw an inference
- Without scientific proof of causation (or proof against causation), can draw an inference to determine what happened
- Means that medical experts do not have to give firm opinions supporting the plaintiff
- Ties back in to what we said in *McGhee* about difficulties with proving scientific causation, court is allowed to fill in the gaps left by science with an inference
- Court determined in this case that the doctor knew what had happened during the operation and the operation was done negligently, so the trial judge was allowed to draw an inference that the injury was caused by the complications from the surgery
- In the absence of any evidence to the contrary, decision goes to the plaintiff
- Defendant needs to establish a plausible alternative theory to defend against this inference
