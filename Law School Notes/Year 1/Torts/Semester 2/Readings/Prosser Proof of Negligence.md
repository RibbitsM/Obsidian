- Determining whether a particular action is negligence is a question of both law and fact
- Generally speaking, the court will address the questions of law, and the jury determines the questions of fact
- Raises the issues of what questions we need to answer, and which are questions of law vs fact
- Couple of different distinct questions when it comes to proving negligence
### Sufficiency of Evidence
- Need proof of facts that give rise to a standard of care
- Once the standard has been set, then you need proof of departure from that standard
- These are questions of fact, but the court reserves the right to determine which questions of fact will be left with the jury
- This is all part of the general rules of evidence that apply for all cases
### Weight of Evidence
- Once it's determined that there is a question of fact, jury's role is to determine what the value of the evidence is and what conclusion to draw
- Includes assessing the credibility of testimony and drawing inferences from that testimony
- Again, this is the same in most other tort cases
### Existence of a Duty
- Do the facts suggest that one party has legal obligation to the other party
- This is a question of law, and is determined only by the court
- If the court cannot find the existence of any duty, then decision goes to the defendant
### Standard of Conduct
- Facts and context will vary greatly from case to case, but the general standard is what a reasonable man would have done in the circumstances
- Generally a question of fact, the court will set the standard and then hand it to the jury
- Means that court will sometimes need to remove a certain standard to account for the contexts of the case
- In some cases, the court finds that the actions were clearly beyond the reasonable standard and the issue will not be left to the jury
- Reason for this is to avoid rash judgement by the jury in clear cases
- This is one of the few cases where the court is allowed to decide questions of fact
### Burden of Proof and Presumptions
- Basically means that where one party is unable to raise enough evidence, they fail to meet the burden of proof
- In rare cases, neither party will be able to induce enough evidence to meet this burden
- In cases like these, the presumption is for the defendant, as the plaintiff is the one asking the court for relief
- However, this is pretty rare considering that the burden is only a balance of probabilities
### Res Ipsa Loquitur
- The fact that an accident or injury has occurred is not on it's own proof of negligence
- Evidence that there was some aspect of negligence is required to even consider the issue
- Where any definite conclusion would be based solely on speculation, the court needs to tell the jury that the burden of proof has failed
- Don't need direct evidence, the court can accept inferences and make them themselves
- Evidence must make it fairly clear that negligence is more likely present than not
- Need to have evidence for all the aspects of negligence
- *Res ipsa loquitur* translates to "the thing speaks for itself" and refers to a doctrine of evidence for negligence
- Basically means that where it's proven that whatever caused the incident was under the control of the defendant, the incident could happen if proper care wasn't taken, and the defendants cannot explain otherwise, this is evidence of negligence
- Very unwieldy and unpredictable, many jurisdictions want to get rid of it
