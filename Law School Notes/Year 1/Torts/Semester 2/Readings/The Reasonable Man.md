- Point of the reasonable man standard is to create a model that all citizens should conform to
- Because of this external standard, people are often expected to live up to a standard that they may not be able to actually meet
- The law cannot always identify moral blameworthiness, the law only applies to external conduct, not the internal conscience
- Society expects a certain standard, and those who cannot attain that standard will have to bear the burden of the hazards they create
- In reality, although we are supposed to ignore the idiosyncrasies of those subject to the law, these will be included unconsciously regardless
- Plus, the standard includes the circumstances of the act as well
### Moral Qualities and Knowledge
- People have varying degrees of foresight, but the reasonable man is free from both overapprehension and overconfidence
- Perception of risk generally relies on past experience in the given situation, we assume the reasonable man has the same information the defendant could gather
- Substandard experience or knowledge is not an excuse, but someone with more knowledge will be held to a standard of someone equally knowledgeable
- What was ignorance yesterday may be negligence today
- This experience will also evolve as the public as a whole learns more and more
### Physicians, Beginners, and Experts
- Doctors are judged by the standard of an average practitioner in their field
- Beginners will cause more than their fair share of accidents, but for compensation purposes will be held to a standard of an intermediate person
- Especially important when the law requires licensing, like for driving
- Some circumstances may also require the reasonable man to consult an expert
- In the case of things like medical treatment or engineering, this is obviously required
- For small private matters like maintaining the household, this is not required
- But for any task that would normally require expert skill like maintaining large trees or telephone lines, the standard is that of the expert in that field
### Physical, Intellectual, and Emotional Characteristics
- Easiest cases to apply a subjective standard is where the defendant differs significantly from the reasonable man in terms of physical attributes
- Physically handicapped are judges on the standard of someone else with their disability
- May be required to compensate, a blind man is not required to see but is expected to use their other senses to a higher degree
- Policy concerns come into play here, we need to balance between allowing these people reasonable freedoms while also protecting safety
- This is why blind people are allowed to walk around unassisted, but are not allowed to drive
- For mental and emotional characteristics, these are not considered in the test
- Too many variations, and too difficult to identify and verify
### Age and Lunacy
- Law makes significant concessions for children, judged relative to other children with the same age and experience
- Parents and authorities have responsibilities of supervision, which also limit liability here
- However, any minor engaging in adult activities like driving will be held to the standard of an adult, similar to a beginner in that activity
- Some leniency is allowed for the old and sick, but for dangerous activities like driving, the ordinary standard must still apply
- As for those with mental disorders, it's unclear how this works
- Some courts excuse defendants who could not appreciate the duty of care, as they are unable to make rational choices and are thus blameless
- Others say that these people at least can foresee harm, but overall the need for compensation to the victims requires some liability
- Reflects the principle that requiring moral fault must take a back seat to fairness for victims, especially where compensation comes from insurance, not personally
