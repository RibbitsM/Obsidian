- Respondent were shipowners, appellants chartered a steamship from them to transport gas from Lisbon to Casablanca
- Rough seas, to there was a bunch of gas vapor in the storage chambers when they got to the port
- A stevedore hired by the appellants knocked a plank into the hold when unloading the cargo, causing massive explosion that destroyed the ship
- Arbitrators employed under the charter agreement found that the fire was caused by the stevedores and the appellants were vicariously liable for negligence
- Appellants argued that the damages were too remote, sent the matter to court
### Bankes
- Arbitrators found that the falling plank was caused by the negligence of the stevedores and the fire was directly caused by the plank falling
- Doesn't matter that the plank had to create a spark to light the gas fumes
- Appellants tried to distinguish reasonable foresight of the extent of damage from reasonable foresight of the type of damage
- Court rejects this distinction, if a breach creates negligence and the damage is a direct result of the negligence, doesn't matter what they anticipated
- Damages are not too remote
### Warrington
- Argues that the presence or absence of reasonable foresight of damage determines whether an act is innocent or negligent
- If it's considered negligent, then you need to consider if the damage was a direct consequence of the act to assess damages
### Scrutton
- Restates the test that negligence depends on whether a reasonable person would have foreseen that the act would cause damage
- If you found that the act would probably cause damage, doesn't matter what form the damage comes in as long as it can be traced back to the negligent act
- In this case it was negligent to knock down the planks as they could have caused damage to the workmen, cargo, or the ship
- The fact that such an unexpected catastrophic event happened instead doesn't mean they were not negligent in knocking down the planks
