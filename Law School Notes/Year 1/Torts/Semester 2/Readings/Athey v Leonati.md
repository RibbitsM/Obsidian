- Appellant suffered back injuries in two car accidents and soon after herniated a disc during a stretching exercise
- Injury was caused by the injuries from the accidents and an underlying condition
- Respondent argues that you can split the damages in terms of tortious and non-tortious causes, no you can't do that
### General Principles
- General test for causation is the "but for" test
- Plaintiff does not have to establish that the defendant's negligence was the sole cause of their injury, can be liable even if they were only part of the cause
- If we apportioned liability like this, then the plaintiff would only be fully compensated in cases where the defendant was 100% the cause of their injuries
- Contrary to the purpose of restoring the plaintiff to their original position
### Inapplicability of Analogies
- Claimed that we can apportion liability like this because the court already apportions liability in cases where there are multiple tortfeasors
- However, this ignores the core rule of restoring the plaintiff to their original position
- Court can also apportion liability where the injuries inflicted by the defendants are divisible and you can tell who caused which injury
- This not actually apportioning liability, it's just how those cases are supposed to work
- Can also apportion liability like this where some injuries were not caused by tortious conduct, but in this case there is only one indivisible injury
- Defence then argued that the court apportions liability when it comes to potential future events, for example awarding 30% damages for an injury with a 30% chance to worsen
- However, this is exclusive to future events, can't be applied to past events
- Next analogy is to intervening event cases, where you can reduce damages if something disrupts the chain of causation
- For this, we can look back at the "original position" principle
- In those cases, the intervening event was disconnected from the original act, so it was not considered part of their "original position"
- In this case, the herniation was a product of the accidents, so reducing the damages does not restore the original position
#### Thin/Crumbling Skull
- Next argument was that the plaintiff was predisposed to a disc herniation, so the "crumbling skull" rule should apply
- This principle basically means that the defendant doesn't have to put the plaintiff in a better position compared to their original position
- Means they are only responsible for the injuries **they** caused, but not necessarily for injuries caused by an underlying condition
- Also means that if there is a chance that the injury would have happened regardless of the accident, we can reduce damages accordingly
- This was their strongest argument, but was still rejected
- Findings of fact did not find any measurable risk of the disc herniation happening even without the accident, so there's no basis to reduce liability
#### Loss of Chance
- Argued that the accidents only increased the risk of herniation, and thus they are only liable for the increase in risk, not the accident itself
- However, the principle is mainly only used for cases where the plaintiff loses a chance at a favourable opportunity or to avoid a detrimental event
- Can argue that here we have a loss of a chance of avoiding a disc herniation (holy double negative)
- But again, the findings of fact do not support this argument
- Therefore, we don't need to consider it
### Application
- Can summarize the applicable principles like this:
	- If the injuries sustained in the accident caused or contributed to the injury, then the defendants are fully liable
	- Plaintiff needs to prove causation using the "but for" test
	- Future or hypothetical events can be factored into damages based on degrees of probability, but causation is either proven or it is not
- These principles mean three main things:
	- If the herniation was likely to happen at the same time without the injuries, no causation
	- If you need both the accident and the underlying condition for the herniation to happen, fully liable because they were a necessary contributing cause
	- If the accidents alone were a sufficient cause, and the underlying condition alone could be sufficient cause, it's unclear what caused the injury
		- Court would have to determine material contribution on a BOP
- Findings of the trial judge show it was necessary to have both the pre-existing condition and the injuries, since she assigned damages to each cause
- We can apply the thin skull rule here, the defendant exacerbated an underlying condition, and they need to take their victim as they find them
- Trial judge could have reduced damages if she found there was a chance the injury would have happened in the future anyway
- In the absence of this argument, plaintiff is entitled to full damages
