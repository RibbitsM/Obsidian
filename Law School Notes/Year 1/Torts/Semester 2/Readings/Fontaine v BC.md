- Two men, Fontaine and Loewen, went for a hunting trip but did not come back
- Bodies were found in a truck in a creek bed next to a highway, no witnesses to the accident but it's clear that they crashed off the road
- There was really bad weather on the day of the crash and a bunch of highways were closed for various reasons like landslides and flooding

### When Does Loquitur Apply?
- Need to have circumstances where an inference of negligence is possible
- Can't clearly define what these cases are, need to look at the context
- Can look at previous cases, but this is not determinative
- Several other cases have held that the presence of a vehicle leaving a road suggests negligence
- The principle applies to provide evidence of negligence where there is no explanation for the cause of the accident
### Effect on Application
- Recall that the burden is on the plaintiff, and *res ipsa loquitur* doesn't shift it
- Where it applies, the judge has to instruct the jury on negligence, and the jury will be able to infer negligence from the facts
- If it's still unclear at the end of the case, judgement goes to the defendant
- The the trier of fact does make an inference, this will help the plaintiff depending on the strength of the inference and the explanation from the defendant
- A good explanation can negate the inference
- Court needs to determine whether a reasonable trier of fact could draw an inference of negligence to give the charge
- This is a question of law, does *res ipsa loquitur* apply here?
- Court in this case held that the principle is pretty useless now, previous attempts to apply it have been more confusing than they are helpful
- Now only applies in cases where the facts allow an inference of negligence and there is no other reasonable explanation for the incident
- Should no longer be considered a full doctrine or a separate component of negligence cases
### Application in Present Case
- Trial judge found that the only potential evidence of negligence was for Loewen, based on the fact the car left the road with such significant momentum
- We also don't know for sure when the accident actually happened
- Presumably, it happened during the severe wind and rain storm
- While these conditions generally impose a higher standard of care, we can't say that the accident was definitely a product of negligence
- Any inference of negligence here will be modest at best
- Defence was successful in giving some adequate alternate scenarios where there was no negligence on the part of Loewen
- This is a reasonable assessment of the facts, no overriding and palpable error here
- Overall, trial judge did not err in finding no negligence, decision for defendant
