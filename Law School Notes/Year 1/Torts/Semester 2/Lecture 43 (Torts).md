### [[Hall v Hebert]]
- Recall that *ex turpi* is extrinsic to morality
- *Ex turpi* does not deal with the relationship between the parties, others do
- Once the plaintiff has proven a valid cause of action, defence can attempt to prove *ex turpi* to defend themselves, if it's proven then onus shifts again
- Court pushed back against the idea of moving *ex turpi* to the duty analysis
- First issue with this was the reversal of burden of proof
- Second issue is that it's an "all or nothing" defence
- By attaching it to the duty, either the duty of care will be negated by the illegal acts, or it will not
- Means that instead of applying the defence to specific heads of damages, it has to be applied to the entire case, and if it fails, it does nothing
- If we use the defence properly, it can be applied to specific heads of damages
- Recall that the third issue was the overlap between tort and contract
- Creates procedural problems where the plaintiff is suing both in tort and contract, hard to track the onus shifts, especially if we're dealing with contract illegality at the same time
### Conclusion
- Appellant only wanted compensation for the injuries they sustained
- Compensation can be reduced based on contributory negligence, but not wholly denied due to criminal conduct
- Heavily influenced by Weinrib's article, since the plaintiff is already being punished under the criminal law, also preventing their right of action would be too cruel
### [[BC v Zastowny]]
- 18 yo was arrested, evidence that he had a drug addiction that led him to steal
- Sexually assaulted twice by a prison official while in jail, became addicted to heroin after he was released and ended up in jail again because of his addiction
- Sued for the sexual assaults, including damages for lost wages in prison
- Issue before the SCC was whether the plaintiff was barred from getting compensation for loss of wages because they were in prison
- Applied *ex turpi*, which bars recovery for damages based on time spent in prison, there was no exceptional circumstance allowing the plaintiff to get compensation here
- Court found a connection between the sexual assaults and his further criminality, but did not consider this an exceptional circumstance
- Quotes *Hall v Hebert*, again saying they would not give with one hand what it takes with the other
- Tort law is not the conscience of the criminal law, if the plaintiff was found criminally responsible, then he will not be repaid for the consequences of those acts
- If the plaintiff had been wrongfully convicted, could likely get compensation
- Can't introduce inconsistencies into the law, meaning we will not incarcerate someone and then compensate them for being incarcerated
### Summary
- When it comes to making defences to torts cases, these three formal defences are actually quite narrow
- In most cases, your "defence" will really just be challenging one element of the plaintiff's case, whether that may be the standard of care, existence of a duty, etc.
- Rarely if ever will we have a case where all elements of negligence are clearly met

### [[Cook v Lewis]]
- Recall that the jury answered the following questions
	- Plaintiff was shot by either of the defendants
	- ah whatever
- Dismissed at trial, overturned on appeal, sent to the SCC
- Main issue was that the jury found that one of the two defendants shot the plaintiff and that both defendants were negligent, but did not find full negligence for either defendant
- Trial judge did not charge them property about the onus of proof, and the jury's decision should be set aside
- Because the defendants had removed the plaintiff's ability to prove negligence, the onus was shifted to them instead
- Both shot negligently in the direction of the plaintiff, so defendants should be liable
- Whenever we find that two or more people are responsible for the harm to the plaintiff, they are automatically jointly and equally liable
- This is the default option, and can be adjusted based on the circumstances
### [[British Columbia AG v ICBC]]
- Girl stole a car and led the RCMP on a car chase, during the chase the girl crashed into another car, killing the woman driving it
- Woman's family sued the girl and the RCMP officer
- Girl was an uninsured driver so ICBC was required to pay her portion, RCMP officers are immune to tort liability under the *Police Act* so the BC Attorney General was required to pay the portion for the officer
- Court found that the liability was split 10% for the constable and 90% for the girl
- ICBC of course refused to pay, AG argued that they only had to pay the 10%
- Issue before the SCC was what the scope of the AG's vicarious liability was
- Looked at s. 4(2) of the *Negligence Act*, says that the AGBC is jointly and severally liable
- Also reinforced by another police tort case which found the same thing
- This is different from joint liability, joint and several liability allows the plaintiff to collect their total damages from **any or all** of the liable parties
- Means that if the plaintiff is owed $10,000 and 4 people are jointly and severally liable, they can collect $2500 from each person or $10k from one person, or any other combination
- Victim was entitled to get full compensation from the AGBC
### Negligence Act
- The s. 4(2) part referred to in the above case specifies that where two or more people are found liable, the court must determine the proportion of fault
- These people are jointly and severally liable, and must indemnify each other if the plaintiff does not collect their damages according to the court proportions
- Emphasizes the importance of giving the plaintiff full compensation
- Also reinforces the principles that damages are generally indivisible from [[Athey v Leonati]]
- Where the court does not apportion liability, we assume it's split equally
