## Nuisance by Public Body
- Situation where the defendant in a nuisance case is any public body governed by statutes
- Can be a municipal, provincial, or federal government or any government body
- Main difference is how liability is assessed in these cases because you have to consider that the statutory body has access to the defence of [[Statutory Authority]]
- Many of these cases relate to a one-time breakdown of state infrastructure like electrical grids or sewage systems, which may not be considered a sustained interference
- Under strict liability, the use has to be an unnatural use of land that can cause extreme harm
- Doesn't apply to something like a water pipe bursting
- Also no intentionality, so it can't fit into the [[Trespass]] box
- May have action for [[Negligence]], but it depends on the conduct of the government
- For the sake of convenience, these cases are placed under the nuisance category
### [[Antrim Truck Centre v Ontario Transportation]]
- Plaintiff operated a truck stop that had its business substantially reduced when the Ontario govt restructured the highway system to circumvent his truck stop
- Question here is how we should balance the damages suffered by the plaintiff with the public good created by the highway restructuring
- Plaintiff applied for compensation under the *Expropriation Act*
- Court found that the reduction in the value of the plaintiff's land was substantial enough that the defendant would be found liable in the absence of statutory authority
- If the defendant was an ordinary citizen, this would be an open and shut case
- *Expropriation Act* exists to dislodge the way that the case would resolve under the common law
- In cases like these, the liability will be lifted if the nuisance was created by the inevitable consequences of exercising statutory authority
- Example of the Inevitability Doctrine, which states that the government will not be held liable for nuisance caused inevitably by the exercise of state authority
- Common law doctrine does not apply in this case
- As long as there was no negligence on the part of the government, they will not be held liable
- The *Expropriation Act* exists to award compensation in cases where a private nuisance would exist in the absence of statutory authority
- However, the Ontario Court of Appeal held that the Board of the *Expropriation Act* did not consider the local standard, sensitivity of the plaintiff, as well as the value of the defendant
- Case was appealed to the SCC
- SCC determined that indeed the Board considered all these factors and upheld the initial decision to grant compensation
#### Issue 1
- Established that the interference was both substantial and unreasonable
- It was substantial in the sense that the plaintiff was put out of business, and unreasonable in the sense that one individual carried the majority of the burden caused by the restructuring
- To establish the reasonableness component, the court cited the decision in [[Tock v St John's]]
- In this case, the test used was the [[Rogers v Elliot|ordinary person test]]
#### Issue 2
- Second issue was how reasonableness should be determined in nuisance by public body cases
- Should be assessed in light of **all relevant circumstances**
- Involves the gravity of the harm done to the plaintiff, measured against the value created to the community by the project being carried out
- Takes into account all other elements of a nuisance, but a more robust analysis is required given that the government body is required by law to take the nuisance action
- The utility of the defendant's conduct is considered particularly important in nuisance by public body, but is still considered alongside all the other factors
- Court cited the *Queen v Loiselle* case which established that public purpose does not outweigh significant harm to an individual
- Means that if there is significant harm to an individual, the defendant has to compensate the plaintiff in spite of the inevitability doctrine
- The significant portion of this is important, the court determines whether a negative externality of a public work is a significant burden on one person, or just a general cost created by running the public system
- Important to consider the material extent of the harm
- In this case, the board determined that it was unreasonable for the plaintiff alone to bear the cost of the nuisance as they were uniquely affected by the highway restructuring
- This intention is suggested by the existence of the *Expropriation Act*
### [[Tock v St John's]]
- Appellants basement was flooded after a storm drain overflowed as a result of heavy rain
- House suffered extensive damage, and plaintiff alleged that the flooding was a nuisance
- Defendant argued that they were not liable as their operation of the sewer system was protected under the defence of [[Statutory Authority]]
- Trial judge awarded compensation, was appealed successfully at appeal court, and went to the SCC
### Ryan v Victoria
- In another Supreme Court judgement, the court elaborated on the inevitability doctrine
- Rule is that liability is absent if an activity is authorized by law and the nuisance is an inevitable result of performing that activity
- *Tock* tries to limit the defence to only situations where statute mandates a duty or specifies how to carry out a duty
- La Forest also deviated from the rule by arguing that the defence only applies if there is statutory exemption from liability
- Argues that this is a departure from the traditional rule, and we should not follow this precedent
