As assault and battery are the only potential causes of action arising from these issues, we will need to outline the elements of both torts. These torts are closely related, and have three elements in common. The first common element is intention. For assault, this requires the defendant to have acted with the intent to threaten the plaintiff, and for battery, it requires intention to make physical contact with the plaintiff. The second common element is directness, meaning the defendant's actions must directly result in either an immediate threat to or physical contact with the plaintiff for assault and battery respectively. The third common element is offensiveness, which for assault requires that the threatened conduct be harmful or offensive, defining offensive as an act that is either disgusting or culturally offensive, for example spitting on someone or flipping them off. For battery, the main purpose of the offensiveness element is to consider the context of the battery claim, was the contact offensive considering the surrounding context?

Next we have the unique elements of each tort, beginning with assault. The fourth element of assault is apprehension, which means that the defendant's act must create a reasonable apprehension of imminent harm. This is assessed on an objective standard, meaning the act must have been threatening to a reasonable person. The final element of assault is non-physicality, meaning the act cannot involve physical contact with the victim. Moving on to the unique elements of battery, the fourth element is physical contact. This simply requires that the defendant made physical contact with the plaintiff, which can include contact through a weapon or projectile. The final element of battery is fault, which requires the defendant to have acted wrongfully in some way, usually meaning the contact cannot be accidental

Additionally, the defence of consent is also included in these issues, which can be used as a defence to either assault or battery. This defence requires proof of genuine consent from the plaintiff to the act in question. The consent cannot be obtained by force, threat of force, while the plaintiff was under the influence of drugs, or by deceit or fraud. Consent can also be vitiated by the presence of an exploitative relationship.
