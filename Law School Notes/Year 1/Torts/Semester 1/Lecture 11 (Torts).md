- General introduction to intentional torts
- Key concept in intentional torts are protected interests
- Intentional torts create liability for interference with these interests, interference has to be intentional
- Come in three categories: persons, chattel, and land
- Distinguished from nuisance due to the required element of intentionality, but also the expanded application to different kinds of property
- Also, interference in an intentional tort must be physical
- Originally came from English writs of trespass intended to stop interference with property
- Dealt with anything called a "breach of the king's peace" which included stealing, interference with property, and assault
- Originally all one offence, evolved into multiple different categories
### Elements
- First element is [[Directness]]
- Action by the defendant must directly cause an injury
- Second element is [[Intentionality]]
- Action by the defendant must be committed with foreknowledge of the consequences and a desire to create those consequences
- Does not require intention to harm
#### Negligent Trespass
- Defined as interference with the plaintiff through careless behaviour
- Measured as showing a disregard for the consequences of an action
- For example, handling a gun that you know has the safety off and setting it off in the direction of another person
- Different from an intentional trespass as the plaintiff does not need to prove damages
- Only needs to prove that the interference was careless
#### Accidental Trespass
- In a case like this, there is no intention or negligence, and thus no liability
- Torts are fault-based, cannot receive compensation if there is no fault
- In the example above, if the safety was on and somehow failed, that would be accidental as opposed to negligent
## Assault
- Assault is a direct, non-physical, and intentional act that creates a threat of physical harm
- Distinct from assault in criminal law, which requires physical contact, more analogous to an attempt to commit assault
- No bodily contact at all, purely non-physical tort that is actionable on its own
- Usually a direct threat of physical harm communicated through words or body language
- For example, a feinted punch, brandishing a weapon, or a verbal threat
### Elements
- Five key elements
- First is intention, defendant must make the threat intentionally
- Recall that this means that they must anticipate and desire the consequences of their action
- Must be acting with the intent to threaten the plaintiff
- Second is directness, same as before
- Must meet an element of immediacy, meaning that the threat must be impending
- Doesn't apply to conditional threats taking place in the future
- Immediate conditional threats do apply
- For example, "If you don't pay me back by the next time I see you, you're dead" is different from "pay up right now or there will be consequences"
- Third element is offensiveness, conduct must be a threat of offensive or harmful conduct
- Extends to threats past physical harm, classic example of an offensive act is spitting on someone
- Acts that are not physical harmful, but are disgusting or culturally disliked
