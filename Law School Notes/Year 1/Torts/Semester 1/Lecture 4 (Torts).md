### [[Nuisance]]
- Helpful example to begin with as it allows us to look at many of the fundamental questions of tort law
- Specifically, helps us examine the concept of harm or injury unlike trespass, which does not require harm/injury
- In Nuisance, injury is required to warrant a remedy
- Also demonstrates that the law isn't all about precedent, it's also about directing the law in a way to promote the social good
- Not always about individuals rights, occasionally an injunction will not be issued for the sake of the community
- A nuisance must be indirect, it must be sustained, and it must amount to unreasonable and substantial interference with the use and enjoyment of land
- The second definition of nuisance is the unlawful interference with the use and enjoyment of land
- The element of sustained interference is a key part of the tort
- Definition of "sustained" is up to interpretation, does not specify a period of time that is considered "sustained"
- However, single instances cannot be considered a nuisance
### Torts Protecting Interest in Land
- Different torts can be extremely similar, the line between nuisance and trespass can be very fine
- For the first semester, we need to be able to distinguish nuisance from other land-related torts like trespass
- Trespass is direct and physical interference with the use and enjoyment of land
- Similar to nuisance, but nuisance is generally indirect and non-physical
- Also no requirement for sustained action in trespass, one instance is enough
- Another tort to consider is the example of *Rylands v Fletcher* which is a kind of [[Strict Liability]]
- This refers to incidents where a property owner's non-natural use of land creates something likely to do harm/mischief on the properties of others
- Sounds very unusual, but can be simple as leaving your sink running in your apartment with some bizarre cleaning supplies mixed in
- Ordinary water would be natural use, and would fall into the category of nuisance
- An element of unnaturalness is required to establish strict liability under *Rylands v Fletcher*
- Next tort to examine is [[Negligence]]
- Defined as the damage to land caused by a failure to take reasonable care
- As usual, the key term here is "reasonable", which can be interpreted in any number of ways
### Grounds of Liability
| Nuisance                            | Trespass                                   |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Indirect                            | Direct                                     |
| Continuing                          | Positive                                   |
| Non-physical                        | Physical interference                      |
| Actionable upon proof of damage     | Actionable with or without proof of damage |
| Interference with enjoyment of land | Interference with possession of land       |

### Public v Private Nuisance
- Two main kinds of nuisance
- A public nuisance refers to a series of frequent activities that adversely affect the public interest and are also often criminal in nature
- Usually involves the obstruction of the public's ability to exercise a right
- A public nuisance generally requires the obstruction and interference with multiple members of the public
- Unlike a private nuisance, a public nuisance can just be one activity
- Can either be a pattern of widespread separate private nuisances, or a single event of obstructing the rights to public land
- For example, occupying parks, blocking roads or highways, etc.
- These acts were originally just a crime, and still is a crime but is not also considered a tort
- Allows private action on the condition that an individual was affected disproportionately by a public nuisance
- Called *Locus Standi*, must prove an unusual degree of suffering compared to other members of the public
- Main question about public nuisance is the number of cases required to establish a public vs private nuisance
- However, there is no consistent answer to this
- One consistent element is the public interest criterion for public nuisance injury
- Basically means that any action interfering with property that serves the public interest can be considered a public nuisance
- If there is one act that causes an ongoing interference, that counts as sustained
- A brief period of interference will not elevate to the level of public nuisance
- Nuisance action works in two different contexts
- The first is indirect and sustained interference coming from neighbouring properties
- This is the usual form of nuisance, often in the form of offensive smells or loud noises
- The other form was created to shift the costs of accidents from those affected to those most able to distribute the costs
- Mainly applies in cases of water lines or sewage pipes breaking
- Those best equipped is usually just the government, if a water main breaks it's not your fault, and the government has to pay for it
- Doesn't apply in the case of dangerous or inappropriate use
