- Vicarious means to take the position/place of another, in the context of torts it means being responsible for the actions of another
- Defendant will have no fault personally, their liability comes from their relationship with the person who committed the tort
- Form of strict liability in the sense that the person being held liable may not have done anything personally
- Employers are vicariously liable for torts committed by their employees, as long as the tort was committed during the course of their employment
### Policy Considerations
- Several important considerations underlying this principle, mainly to keep businesses accountable
- Also ensures that those who profited from a tort will also bear the accompanying losses
- Makes it easier for the victim to get compensation too
- Often, the person committing the tort may not be able to pay for damages, but their employer certainly will be and may be easier to get a hold of
- Also just more efficient to do things in this way
- May also prove to be a deterrent effect to ensure that businesses take steps to make sure that their employees are not committing tortious behaviour
- Main problem is that we are indemnifying the employers through their employees
- Indemnify just means securing against losses or damages, businesses have to pay the victims
### Who is an Employee?
- Need to distinguish between acts committed by direct employees compared to contractors
- Usually assessed by looking at the level of control the employer has over the person committing the tort, are they responsible to the business or mostly to themselves?
- For employees, the employer tells them what to do and how to do it, while contractors are usually hired to do one specific kind of work aimed at a predetermined result
- Not always true though, professional employees like lawyers or doctors usually have a lot more freedom compared to standard employees
- Courts also consider other factors like who gets the profits from the action, who owns/provided the tools used for the work, and who is responsible for losses
- Most of the control criteria comes from *671122 v Sagaz Industries Canada Inc*
- Generally speaking, vicarious liability doesn't apply to independent contractors
- Exceptions are for non-delegable duties, which have to be carried out by the person the duty was imposed on
- This means that if they try to delegate these duties to a contractor, still liable
- If there's a strict statutory duty to an act, you can't escape liability by getting a contractor (Cory J., Lewis (Guardian ad litem of) v British Columbia)
### [[Jones v Hart]]
- Original case for vicarious liability, defendant was a pawnbroker who sent their servant to collect the plaintiff's goods
- Servant lost the goods, and pawnbroker couldn't give them back when they paid to get them back
- Plaintiff sued the defendant for trover, an old kind of lawsuit used to recovering the value of property after conversion or detention of goods happens
- Court held that "whoever employs another is answerable for him... The act of a servant is the act of his master, where he acts by authority of the master"
- Can see all the core elements of vicarious liability in this quote
### Elements
1. Employee commits a tort
2. Employee was employed by the defendant
3. Tort was committed in the course of employment
### Statutory Vicarious Liability
- Some statutes impose vicarious liability in specific situations unrelated to employment
- One example would be car owners who are vicariously liable for accidents caused when a driver not registered to their car was driving
- Another example is that parents are vicariously liable for the actions of their children
- Under the *Parental Liability Act*, if a child intentionally takes, damages, or destroys the property of another person, their parent is liable (s. 3)
- If more than one parent is liable, all parents are jointly and separately liable (s. 5)
### Course of Employment
- Likely the hardest thing to determine is whether a tort was committed during the course of employment
- Includes wrongful or unauthorized modes of doing an authorized act (Salmonds)
- As long as the employer authorized, instructed, or participated in the tort, they're liable
- However, if the conduct was completely unconnected or distinct from the employee's work, then there's no liability for the employer
- Courts will consider the time, place, and purpose of the offence to determine this
- Intention is also relevant, as it's much harder to argue that an intentional tort was authorized by the employer compared to a negligence tort
- Sexual assault is also a special case, as it's pretty much impossible to argue that activities like these were supposed to be part of the employee's job
- For these cases we use the test of whether there was a strong connection between the tort and the nature of the employment (Bazley v Curry)
### *Salmonds* Test
- Traditional test was related to the mode of committing the act
- As long as the conduct related to the act was connected to the employee's work, there will be vicarious liability
- *Salmonds* extended this definition to include unauthorized ways of carrying out a task
- Time, place, and purpose of an offence are all important factors in determining whether a tort was committed within the course of employment
- Act of the employee should be intentional, not negligent
- Raises the issue that a legitimate business could not authorize their employees to commit an intentional tort
- A special case for this is sexual assault, which is particularly difficult to rationalize as being part of the course of employment
- Intentionality and seriousness of the tort makes it harder to establish
