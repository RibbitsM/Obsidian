### Disabled Adults and Consent
- Incapacity to make a decision is based on the ability of an individual to demonstrate understanding of information provided by a health care professional and the accompanying consequences
- Because of this, they may not be able to consent to medical treatment
- Comes from the *Health Care (Consent) and Care Facility (Admission) Act*
### [[E (Mrs) v Eve]]
- Mother applied for permission to have her mentally disabled daughter sterilized as she was concerned about her becoming pregnant
- Basically asking the court to consent to the treatment on the behalf of Eve
- Supreme Court determined that the ability of the state to make decisions for those incapable (*parens patriae*) can only be exercised for the benefit of those individuals
- Can't be use to lift the burden of the caretaker, outcomes must be a net benefit for the person who cannot consent
- In this case the benefit to the daughter was questionable
- Main purpose was to lift the burden on the mother, as she would likely have to raise the baby if Eve became pregnant
- Decided to withhold consent
- Person should not be restricted from giving birth for social reasons
### [[In Re B]]
- Similar facts to the previous case, but the health risks caused by pregnancy were considered much more severe
- Decided that the decision of the Canadian Supreme Court was unreasonable, expressly rejected the analysis of the court in *Eve*
- Court determined the best interest of the daughter, goal was to prevent future injury
- Considered some of the arguments made in *Eve*, but ultimately rejected them
- Specifically took issue with the classification of "therapeutic" or "non-therapeutic" sterilization
- Intended to distinguish between medically necessary and unnecessary sterilization
- Most important measure is whether or not the sterilization would help the daughter
- Focused on the impact on the mother, and the capability of the daughter to participate in reproduction
- Argued that the main issue is whether or not the actions would benefit the incapable individual
- Decided to grant consent
### Informed Consent
- Has to be freely given, cannot be coerced
- Patients must know fundamental information to consent to treatment, mainly the potential risks or side effects of treatment, as well as alternatives (s. 17(3) of the Infants Act)
- Need to have all the information required to make an **informed** decision
- For example, Reibl would likely have wanted to know that the surgery was not medically necessary in the immediate future
- Even if there is a failure to inform of risks that would lead a reasonable patient to opt out, cannot charge with battery
- Once the patient knows that the doctor is going to perform the surgery and what it involves and they agree to that, this is sufficient to eliminate the requirements for battery
- However, still have cause of action for negligence if consent was not informed
### [[Reibl v Hughes]]
- Plaintiff underwent surgery, which was competently done but lead to the plaintiff suffering a massive stroke leaving him partially paralyzed
- Consented to the procedure, but alleged he was not informed about the risk of a stroke
- If he was aware of this fact, would not have chosen to undergo surgery
- Plaintiff pointed out that he was 1.5 years away from receiving his lifetime pension, he would have waited until retirement to get the surgery if he knew the risks
- Consent was not vitiated by this failure, so there was no action in battery, but there was a breach of duty of care/negligence
- Therefore, battery is only appropriate in cases where there is no consent at all for the surgery/treatment performed
- For example, battery would apply in cases where the surgeon performs a different surgery than the one the patient consented to
- In this case, the patient had the surgery explained to them and they understood the procedure
- Three things you look for when it comes to battery
- Was there no consent given?
- Did the treatment exceed the scope of the consent given, if there was any?
- Did the information provided by the doctor amount to misrepresentation or fraud?
- Exception to this is when there is an emergency situation, in this case consent is implied
- This is only a presumption, if there is evidence that the patient does not consent, doctor must be careful
- If none of these requirements are met, but there is a failure of informed consent, patient has cause of action for negligence
### Self Defence
- Recognized by the common law
- Allowed to use force to protect yourself from harm
- Force has to be necessary, and proportionate to the imminent harm
- You can't shoot someone who is trying to hit you with a stick
- Unless there is a real threat of harm, you can't justify using force in self-defence
- If there was provocation, this may have an effect on the award of damages
- Also should not be retaliatory in nature, reaction should be almost immediate to show that you are acting only to prevent the incoming harm
- If your attacker is weaker than you, cannot use more force necessary than to simply repel the attack
- Using a weapon in self defence complicates the defendants case, and would have to be proportional to the level of harm you were facing
- Quote from a case called *Veinot v Veinot* that states that shooting an unarmed man will almost never be justified on the basis of self-defence
- What was the attacker doing? What were they holding? Was the use of the weapon by the defendant reasonable given this context?
### Defence of Others
- Same considerations as self defence, except to deter harm to someone else
- Already covered this in [[Babiuk v Trann]]
- Can either arise when the defendant has a duty to defend the person being harmed, but also in the absence of that duty
- Easy to conclude when there is a legal duty to care for a dependant, mostly with children and parents or with elderly people being cared for
- Much harder when there is no legal duty like in the case of friends, co workers or teammates
- In the case of *Babiuk*, the court held that there was no reason why the defence of others should not be extended to people without a legal duty
- Held that the action was reasonable, defendant threw one punch to prevent an imminent and immediate harm, and the confrontation ended at that
- Caused significant harm, but the force was held to be reasonable
### Defence of Property
- Usually applied in terms of defending land
- Owner of the land uses force to remove a trespasser on your land
- Force needs to be reasonable, otherwise the owner may be criminally liable or liable for battery
- In defence of property resulting in bodily harm or death, case will depend on whether the trespasser posed a real threat to the owner or not
### Legal Authority
- Most commonly raised defence against intentional torts
- Defendant will argue that they had a legal right, either granted by statute or common law
- Can be based on authority of state officials like police officers, or civilian rights
- Parent being charged with battery may argue they have a legal right to discipline their child
- Also applies to professions where there is duty of care
- *Health Care (Consent) and Care Facility (Admission) Act* allows physicians to restrain patients through physical or chemical means
- Limited to restrictions where it is justified, not just for convenience
### Provocation
- Argument that the defendant lost self-control due to the actions of the plaintiff
- Not a justification or a complete defence, we are trying to encourage people to control themselves
### Necessity
- Occurs where a person can argue they have legal authority, underlies many other defences
- For example, this is the defence for saving someones life in an emergency situation
- Defence of property is also based on necessity
- When invoked as a stand-alone defence, you have to show that there was great and imminent danger to life or property which could only be remedied by the actions of the defendant
- Only example of an intentional tort where the defendant is the assailant
- Happens when the defendant harms someone else to prevent some other kind of harm that would be severe enough to supersede the rights of the plaintiff
- Can be a complete defence, but may provide incomplete privilege
- Unclear what the definition is, will be defined on a case-to-case basis
