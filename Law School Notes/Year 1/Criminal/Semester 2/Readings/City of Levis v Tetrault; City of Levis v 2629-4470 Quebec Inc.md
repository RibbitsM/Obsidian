- City is trying to appeal two acquittals given by trial judges for *Highway Safety Code* offences on the grounds that they erred in determining the offences were strict liability offences
- Also argue that even if this is the case, the respondents didn't meet the standards for the defence of due diligence
- Appeal court found that the offences were indeed strict liability, but the defence of due diligence was not successfully made in either case
- The company respondent put a car back into operation without paying the SAAQ (Quebec auto association) the appropriate registration fees
- This was counter to s. 31.1 of the *Safety Code*
- Company argued that the former owner had registered the car good until march, they bought the car in January
- Also, a company employee registered the car after they bought it, and the company reimbursed the old owner for the registration money for jan to march
- Company also paid the remaining registration fees and bought another year of registration
- This happened in 2001, registration now expires march 2002
- SAAQ representative told the company employee that they would get a renewal notice 60 days before the expiry date, which was March 31, 2002
- In January 2002, the SAAQ sent the company a renewal notice, but it never arrived because they failed to include an apartment number on the address, which they had on file
- Car in question was pulled over in April, charged with having an expired registration 
### Analysis
- Issue is whether or not the provisions are absolute liability, and whether the defences of due diligence and officially induced error were met
- Two step test for classifying regulatory offences comes from *R v Pontes*
- First step is applying the considerations from [[R v City of Sault Ste Marie]], second step was determining whether legislature intended to allow the defence of due diligence
- However, this is kind of redundant because that's the goal of the *Sault Ste Marie* considerations in the first place, so court says we should just use that
#### Defence of Officially Induced Error
- Company argues the SAAQ gave them wrong information on how to pay the registration fees
- Tried to raise the defence of officially induced error, which may or may not be legitimate in Canada
- Generally speaking, ignorance of the law is not an excuse
- This is a principle of criminal law, but extends to regulatory offences
- Court does recognize that this can cause some issues in cases where the error of law comes from an error caused by the state itself
- Pretty unfair situation overall
- Previously, the defence of officially induced error was proposed as a way to amend this
- Has never been officially recognized, but has been applied in trial and appeal courts to both criminal and regulatory offences
- This defence is excuse, and is similar to entrapment because while the act is still wrongful, the situation shows that the accused should not be held liable
- Defence has six elements in total:
	1. Error of law, partial or complete, was made
	2. Person who committed the act considered the consequences
	3. Advice came from an official
	4. Advice was reasonable
	5. Advice was erroneous
	6. Person relied on the advice while committing the act
- Elements come from *R v Jorgensen*, which didn't rule on this issue but no one has since questioned this approach and it has been used in several cases
- Attorney general did suggest a seventh element that the act was committed soon after the receipt of information, but overall this does not seem necessary
- Advice must be objectively reasonable, and the reliance on the advice must also be objectively reasonable
- Factors to consider include efforts made by accused to get info, clarity of the law, position of the official, and the clarity of the information given
#### Application
- As section 31.1 of the *Safety Code* is written, it's not an absolute liability offence
- Recall that there needs to be a specific indication of absolute liability, otherwise the presumption is that it's strict liability
- Company attempted two defence based on the argument that they were misled by the SAAQ
- SAAQ said they would send a notice two months before expiry, but the SAAQ didn't put the address correctly, and they never received the notice
- Thus, the company thought the car was still registered
- Doesn't meet due diligence, because the company was clearly aware when the registration would have expired, they knew that they renewed it for 15 months in January of 2001
- Should have at least known that they should have gotten a notice in January 2002
- Also didn't meet the standards of officially induced error, there was no legal advice given or consideration of legal consequences
- Most of the stuff at issue here is just administrative practices
- Appeals allowed, convictions entered on both charges for a min fine of $300
