- Accused was convicting of sexually assaulting his daughter and making, distributing, and possessing child pornography
- Trial judge imposed the max sentence for sexual assault as well as a consecutive sentence for the other three charges
- Also found that he was a long-term offender and should be supervised in the community for ten years after being released
- Court of Appeal reduced the global sentence, case was appealed to the SCC
### Analysis
- Three main issues with sentencing here
	- What principles are applicable to intervention in an appeal against sentence
	- What principles are applicable to imposing a max sentence?
		- Do we need to imagine the worst crime committed in the worst circumstances?
	- Should a long-term offender's community supervision period be considered when determining the appropriate term of imprisonment?
- SCC previously held that appellate courts need to show great deference when considering appeals on sentencing
- Standard is that the court needs to be convinced that the sentence is clearly unreasonable
- Could also happen if there was an error in principle, a failure to consider a relevant factor, or an overemphasis of the appropriate factors
### Violation of Sentencing Principles
- Court of appeal was required to show deference in this case
- Majority did not meet this standard, did not show that the accused's sentence was unreasonable, and also varied some findings of fact
- Also disregarded the close connection between the sexual assault and other offences
- Trial judge has considerable discretion in sentencing because of how individualized the process is, need to weigh the principles set out in the *Criminal Code*
- Principles include deterring and rehabilitating offenders, as well as separating them from society, as well as acknowledging the harm they've created
- Sentences must be proportional to the gravity of the offence and offender's degree of responsibility
- Sentences can be increased or reduced to account for aggravating or mitigating circumstances, and should be similar to sentences imposed in similar circumstances
- Least restrictive sanctions should be identified, and need to consider sanctions other than imprisonment, this is where [[Lecture 9 (ISLR)|Gladue principles]] come from
### Maximum Sentences
- *Criminal Code* imposes a maximum sentence, but is not always used
- Judges are often influenced by the idea that maximum sentences should only be reserved for the worst possible offenders and circumstances
- Of course, since these standards are never actually met, maximum sentences are almost theoretical
- In a previous SCC case called *Cheddesingh*, the court rejected this argument, stating that all factors should be considered on a case-by-case basis
- Need to impose a maximum sentence if the circumstances warrant it
- Trial judges will know best what a proportional sentence is, given that they were the trier of fact for any given case
- Court of Appeal should have asked whether imposing the maximum sentence was reasonable given the findings of fact made by the trial judge
### Application
- Trial judge pointed to a number of aggravating factors that justified imposing the maximum possible penalty in this case
- The fact that the accused had done this several times in a position of authority and trust, as well as the quantity of material seized and the central role he played in distribution and production are all clear aggravating factors
- The victim was clearly subjected to severe psychological violence, she had been scarred for life and struggles to fit in with children her own age
- Also clear that these offences were connected, some of the CP he posted were videos of his sexual assaults on the victim, need to consider this as a whole
- These materials were also distributed on the internet, and it can never be sure that these materials have been wiped from the web
- Only mitigating factor was that the accused had shown some regret, but this only happened after he read the pre-sentence reports that mentioned his lack of regrets
- Together, all these factors convinced the trial judge that the maximum sentence was justified
- Judge correctly understood these factors, especially the interconnected aspect of the offences which justified the global sentence
### Unwarranted Intervention
- First of all, the Court of Appeal erred by varying a sentence that was already reasonable
- Also put too much importance on the principle that similar sentences should be imposed on similar offenders, offences, and circumstances
- This principle is important, but does not take priority over the principle of deference to the trial judge, there was no unreasonable sentence or error in principle here
- The sentencing process is very individual, so it's understandable and normal to have different sentences for the same offences
- Seems clear that the circumstances here were pretty exception, justifying the max sentence
- Most of the cases cited by the Court of Appeal did not have the same scope in terms of the quantity of materials and wide distribution over the internet
- Only similar case also involved abuse of a child and materials created and distributed, but only to the friends of the accused, not onto the internet
### Conclusion
- Trial judge's sentence was appropriate, it was proportionate to the acts, and all mitigating/aggravating circumstances were accounted for
- With no reviewable error, Court of Appeal erred in varying the sentence
- Appeal allowed, trial sentence restored
