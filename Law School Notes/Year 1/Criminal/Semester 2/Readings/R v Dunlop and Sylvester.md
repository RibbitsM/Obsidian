- Victim was a 16 year old girl, met the defendants at a hotel bar, who were members of a motorcycle gang
- Victim and her girlfriend went with the defendants and several of their club members to a nearby dump
- Victim was carried away and sexually assaulted by a group of men, named the accused as two of the men in the group
- Accused argued they had left the dump to go get beer and when they came back saw people standing around and a woman having sex with a man they didn't recognize
- Trial judge summed it up by saying that if you accept the evidence of the victim you should find the defendants guilty
- If you have a reasonable doubt that one or both participated in the sexual assault, you should acquit that person
- Also decided to instruct the jury on party liability, suggesting that the accused could be guilty as aiders or abettors
### Analysis
- This was an error of law, if we believe the story of the defendants, they had no common intention with the men involved
- Additionally, passive observance of a crime is generally not enough to make you a party under s. 21 of the *Criminal Code*
- They did not encourage these men or help with the offence by keeping watch or preventing the victim's escape
- Already stated in a previous case called *R v Coney* where walking past a prize fight was not enough to be aiding the fight
- However, actively watching it and cheering on the fighters would make them guilty of assault
- Also two other cases called *R v Black* and *R v Clarkson* where the victim was tortured or sexually assaulted in front of multiple bystanders
- Where there was evidence that the spectators encouraged the harm, they were guilty, but those who merely stood by were not
### Application
- Overall, there was no evidence of any positive act or omission committed by the accused to aid or abet the offence
- Trial judge erred by charging the jury on the possibility of the men being aiders or abettors
- Also important to note that there is no general duty to prevent crime when you see it
- Not considered an accomplice if you let it happen
- Suggested an acquittal
### Dissenting Opinion
- Undisputed that presence at the scene of a crime is not enough to prove aiding or abetting an offence
- However, the trial judge only said that it was necessary to show that the accused understood what was happening and assisted with or encouraged the act
- It was up to the jury to accept or reject the evidence that the accused were only there to deliver the beer
- Jury could also consider the other evidence like what the victim said, and the events leading up to the sexual assault
- Jury could certainly have used this information to draw an inference that the men were more than just innocent bystanders
- Appeals should be dismissed
