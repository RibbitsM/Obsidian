- Accused were charged with offences including blocking roadways, interfering with property, and defying court orders as a result of their protests on various environmental issues
- Argued that their actions were necessary in face of the imminent peril faced by climate change
- Called expert witnesses testifying to the threat posed by climate change and the importance of civil disobedience in the democratic process
### Analysis
- Recall the three requirements for the defence of necessity from [[R v Perka]]
- Trial judge accepted the evidence submitted regarding the threat of climate change, but concluded there was no air of reality to the first element
- Peril was not so imminent that the accused could not have acted freely
#### Reasonable Legal Alternative
- Evidence clearly showed that the accused made choice in advance on which days and times they would engage in civil disobedience
- If these acts were the only realistic option available to them, there would be no choice involved
- Also clearly some choices made in how much interference they would cause
- Made choices depending in their personal lives as to what day they would be arrested
- Also took a break from protesting at one point to recruit supporters, which is lawful activity
- Overall seems clear that the accused were able to specifically choose the time, location, and form of their illegal actions
- Also impossible to tell if their actions were more effective in raising public awareness for climate change compared to legal alternatives
- Accused were not limited for choice, they had multiple viable ways to respond to the peril created by climate change
- Accused did testify that in their personal experiences, lawful alternatives are not as effective
- Overall, still not clear that illegal protest activity is needed to enact change, and Canadian courts have never applied the defence of necessity in this way
- Both two elements of the defence fail, no need to consider the third element
