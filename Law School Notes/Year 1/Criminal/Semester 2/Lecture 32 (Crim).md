### [[R v Chaulk]]
- Recall that the defence of mental disorder can be raised by either party, and can happen during the trial, or when sentencing starts
- When the Crown is raising it, they can only do it at the end unless the accused puts their mental state into question during the trial
- Unclear whether the jury has to rule on NCRMD decisions or not
- Court found that the accused should need to know that their actions are morally wrong, as opposed to merely legally wrong
- This case need to overrule itself, as the previous precedent was the other way around which was set by *R v Schwartz*
- Present case also posed some potential Charter problems
- Theoretically, a mental disorder could violate the *actus reus* by putting the accused in a state of automatism, or the *mens rea* by distorting your perception of right and wrong
- However, in practice the defence does not actually overwrite these elements of the offence, but rather acts as an excuse once the elements of the offence are proven
- Additionally, the law focuses mainly on the capacity of the accused, were they able to ascertain that an action was morally/legally wrong
- What the court is looking for here is a lack of capacity to understand that society as a whole disapproves of their actions
### Facts
- Two young people conspired together to commit murder, both of them knew they were killing someone and that it was against the law
- However, they had delusions that they were vested with some divine power, and killing others was a necessary step to fulfilling that power and ruling the world
### Analysis
- Issue here was whether or not this suffices for the purposes of s. 16
- Previous precedent says no, as the accused knew that what they were doing was legally wrong, but they believed that the laws of Canada were irrelevant compared to their divine purpose
- This case was also unusual because both accused seemed to share the same delusions
- Accused claimed that requiring them to prove they had a mental disorder on a balance of probabilities was a violation of s. 11(d)
- Court rejected this argument, and found that the burden was justified
- Split on the reason why, some said it was not a violation at all, and others claimed it was a violation but it was justified under s. 1
- Main rationale for this was that the purpose of the presumption of sanity (s. 16(4)) was to save the Crown from having to prove sanity
- This is extremely hard to do for two main reasons
- First, they have to prove sanity at the time of the offence, not at the time of the trial
- Second, the Crown cannot compel the accused to submit to a psychological assessment, and doing so would likely raise even more Charter challenges
- This is also not even getting into how hard it is to determine objectively whether someone is "sane" or not, even with a psych evaluation
### Justification
- Court here seems to be giving a lot more deference to the legislature, especially when it comes to the minimal impairment part of the test
- Stated that the legislature is not obligated to find the absolute minimum level of impairment
### Summary
- Lets go back to our framework of analysis for defences, how does this defence measure up?
- It's a statutory defence, found in s. 16 of the *Criminal Code*
- Burden of proof is to prove on a balance of probabilities
- Applies to all criminal offences, and likely some non-criminal ones too
- Outcome is that you are declared not criminally responsible
- Then, the accused goes before some administrative tribunal staffed by psychiatrists and other representatives for a disposition hearing, which has three possible results
	- Can be detained in a secure psychiatric facility
	- Can be released on certain conditions
	- Can be released unconditionally
- Under the previous scheme, the automatic result was detainment with no clear date of release
- Current scheme is designed to favour release with the fewest conditions possible
- If the Crown wants to justify additional conditions, they have to prove that to the Board
- If you are detained, that decision will be reviewed semi-regularly
- There was certainly some backlash to this, as there were several cases of people released this way committing some particularly brutal murders
- Today, there is still this approach except individuals can also be classified as "high risk offender"
- If this happens, detention is automatic and the system is more like the old scheme
- Additionally, emphasis was put on the safety of the public and family members of victims were given the ability to appear at these hearings
