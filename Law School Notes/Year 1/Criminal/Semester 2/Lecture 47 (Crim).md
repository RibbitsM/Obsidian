## Example Question
### Question 1
- Amir thinks Bonnie is taking PEDs, they are running a race together
- Sneaks up to her house in the evening and runs away when spotted, leaving his running shoe
- Police find his shoe and bus pass, charged with "trespassing at night" under s. 177
- Acquitted at trial, question is why
- Assume he has no lawful excuse
#### Answer
- Reason for this is because the definition of "night" under s. 2 starts at 9 pm and ends at 6 am, the events here took place around 8 pm
- This is a good reminder that you should always look for definitions, and without a clear definition you need to use the case law
- Not using a dictionary to answer exam questions, only bring one if you're worried you won't know the meaning of a word
### Question 2
- After Amir ran away, he realizes he lost his bus pass and money
- Goes to a store to ask to use their phone, attacked by a dog
- He grabs a nearby bike and rides it home, intending to return it the next day
- Charged with theft under s. 322
#### Answer
- Crown is likely going to be able to prove the AR and MR of theft
- He took something without colour of right, and he arguably did it with intent to deprive, at least for a short amount of time
- Depriving temporarily counts as well as depriving absolutely
- Only real defence he has here is necessity, which is notoriously hard to establish
	- Urgent and pressing peril would be the dog attacking him
		- This is on a semi-subjective standard, element would likely be met
	- Reasonable legal alternative is the main issue, though it does use a modified standard
	- Final element is proportionality, this is likely also met
		- Harm avoided is fairly significant, and harm created is pretty minimal
- Would likely fail since he had legal alternatives like running into the store
	- Could argue that this was a spur-of-the-moment decision, but it seems clear there were options outside of taking the bike
	- Also can argue that he could not outrun the dog, it was too fast
### Question 3
- Clyde has been diagnosed with a mood disorder takes medication to alleviate this and has been warned not to combine this with alcohol
- Before the race, Amir finds two syringes and a vial in Bonnie's bag, and takes them
- Clyde drank a bottle of brandy before the race to stay warm
- Bonnie confronts and insults Clyde before the swimming part of the race because he's wearing a PFD, not allowed under the rules
- Clyde becomes angry and punches her in the face, she had a bleeding disorder which requires regular injection of a clotting factor, this is what the syringes were for
- Since Amir took them, she can't stop the bleeding and dies before an ambulance arrives
- Clyde is charged with 2nd degree murder of Bonnie
#### Answer
- For culpable homicide, you need an unlawful act causing death
- Important to know all the homicide offences, most good defences apply mainly to murder
- Underlying offence here was an assault, he punched her in the face
- This is pretty simple to prove from the Crown's end
- Main issue here is proving causation between assault and the death
	- Can argue there was an intervening factor, Amir taking the syringes
	- Need to apply the significant contributing cause test from [[R v Nette]]
	- Recall that the existence of the underlying condition doesn't matter because of the thin-skull rule, need to take the victim as you find them
- As for potential defences, there are three possibilities
	- For provocation, unlikely that this would be successful because Bonnie was not committing an offence, just made a mere insult
		- Could challenge this under the Charter
	- For intoxication, would need some more evidence for this, but 2nd degree murder is a specific intent offence, so you don't need automatism
	- Final defence would be mental disorder, also need more evidence for this
- Doesn't really matter how you balanced the facts, but you should consider everything that could apply just to show knowledge, even if you don't think it'll succeed
	- If it could conceivably be raised, at least explain why it doesn't work
