### Question 1
- Patty is mildly intoxicated, but not extremely so
- Saw a guy get hit by a bus, picked up a box containing an engagement ring that fell out of the guy's pocket
- Charged with theft under s. 322(1)(a)
- Question is whether the Crown can prove the offence BAR, and what arguments/defences you can raise for Patty
- First part of the question is whether the Crown can prove the offence, for this we need to look at the AR and MR and see if they're met
- Actus reus of theft is taking something that does not belong to you, this is clearly met as Patty did not own the engagement ring
- Relevant MR requirement is the intent to deprive the owner of it, we know this because Patty was specifically charged under s. 322(1)(a)
- This is a pretty strict requirement, we need intent to deprive, as well as knowledge that the object belonged to someone else (subjective MR btw)
- The strongest argument that the Crown can make here is that Patty knew the box belonged to the guy who got hit by the bus
	- She saw him get hit, and saw the box next to his body, even if she didn't see it fall out of his pocket, the Crown could argue she should have known
	- Recklessness isn't an option here, but this could be wilful blindness?
- AR can be easily established, only issue is with the MR
- If the Crown can prove that Patty saw the ring box fall out of Linus's pocket, then they will be able to prove all elements of the offence BARD
	- Otherwise, there will still be a reasonable doubt that she knew it was his
- In terms of defences, Patty would be able to argue that she simply saw the box lying on the street and picked it up, thinking it was abandoned
- Additionally, she can raise the defence of non-extreme intoxication
- Theft is a specific intent offence, given that you need intent to reach a specific consequence (depriving someone), as shown in *Tatton*
- Could definitely give an air of reality to the argument that Patty was too intoxicated to really know what was going on and manifest specific intent
- Assuming she didn't share that bottle of wine, she's probably hammered
- Can likely successfully raise the defence of non-extreme intoxication, which would grant her a full acquittal
	- No included offences to charge her with instead except for attempted theft which doesn't make sense on the facts
### Question 2
- Arthur has early onset alzheimers, can get disoriented and confused over the time or place
- Accidentally walks across the court at a basketball game, and makes a player miss their free throw, who gets angry and starts yelling at him
- Arthur pushes him to the ground and kicks him in the head, killing him
- Claims he does not remember what happening, only that he was lost and afraid and that someone was trying to kill him
- Charged with unlawful act manslaughter
- First thing to consider is if the Crown can prove this, and what the AR and MR are
- For unlawful act manslaughter, you need an underlying unlawful act
- Unlawful act here is undoubtably assault, no real other options
- AR of assault is applying force to another person without their consent
- MR is intention to apply force, and knowledge of no consent
- Seems that both of these apply here, the intention requirement is pretty clearly met since he shoved the guy and then kicked them, it wasn't an accident
- Also clearly no consent involved here
- To tie a predicate offence to unlawful act manslaughter, actions of the accused just need to be a significant contributing cause, and must be reasonable foreseeability of bodily harm, not death (*Creighton*)
- Also met, this was definitely a significant contributing cause and kicking someone repeatedly in the head should reasonably lead to bodily harm
- Only thing left to do is consider any applicable defences
- Four main possibilities: provocation, mental disorder, automatism, and self-defence
- Can rule out provocation because Arthur seemed to have reacted out of fear, not anger, and the player didn't even commit an offence so it wouldn't work anyway
- Mental disorder could potentially work, but you would probably need expert evidence on how Alzheimer's can affect your perception of reality
- At the very least, you could argue that Arthur couldn't appreciate that what he did was morally wrong because from his perspective, his life was in danger
	- Could also raise necessity on these grounds, but doesn't apply to murder (*Latimer*)
- However, this case doesn't bear much resemblance to most mental disorder cases which usually require something more than confusion over time and place
- This is probably his strongest defence
- Automatism runs into similar issues, would need expert evidence for this, but there doesn't seem to be much indication that Arthur acted automatically
	- The fact that he doesn't remember what happened is indicative of automatism, but could just as easily be Alzheimer's or shock
- Self-defence is another strong option, probably about as good as mental disorder, and it grants a full acquittal instead
	- Main issue is whether the act was reasonable given the circumstances
	- The actual threat posed to Arthur was pretty minimal, and the reaction was obviously incredibly disproportionate
	- Also, unclear if the test would account for his Alzheimer's
- Overall, Crown can prove the case, and Arthur's best chance is to argue mental disorder, which would succeed as long as he can support it with expert evidence
	- As a result, he would be declared NCRMD

- Aria and Mona already have a bad relationship, Mona is frustrated as Aria keeps giving her friends free stuff
- Aria's friend gives her some pills that she takes
- Mona threatens to tell the owner about Aria's stealing
- Aria is now under the effects of the pills, and tells Mona that if she doesn't cover the shortfall, she'll tell Mona's spouse that she's cheating on him with the theater manage

- In the ensuing panic, and 8 year old is trampled by the other patrons and becomes concussed
- Jenna is actually a gang leader who makes Aria give her free food and movie tickets to help pay off her drug debt
- Aria wakes up in the woods and leaves town to stay with her friend Ezra, and uses drugs with them
- She hears a news report that Mona was arrested for murder, but stays in the cabin
### Question 1
- Mona is charged with 2nd degree murder, what defences can you raise on her behalf, assuming Aria is actually dead
- Can't really challenge the AR of murder, only options here are to challenge the MR or raise a defence
- Couple of options for MR, can be subjective intent to kill or subjective intent to cause bodily harm, and were reckless in causing death
- Not much clear evidence of subjective intent to kill, but there seems to be a pretty clear indication of subjective intent to cause bodily harm
	- Real question is whether Mona was reckless or not
- This is arguable, the defence can likely make a pretty convincing argument that Mona only hit Aria a single time, and while it was a head hit with a heavy object, the hit was not hard enough to create a substantial risk of death
	- Likely need for information for this
- Overall, the Crown should be able to prove BARD that hitting someone on the head with a metal flashlight is reckless, as there is a definite risk of death that Mona would have known about
- Next thing to consider is the defences, the only potentially available defences here are provocation and necessity
- For provocation, need to prove that Mona was provoked by conduct constituting an indictable offence punishable by 5 years or more
	- This is actually possible, can argue that Aria's conduct amounted to extortion under s. 346(1), which is an indictable offence with a maximum sentence of life in prison
- She meets the AR of extortion by attempting to induce Mona into paying the shortfall and not reporting her by threatening to expose her infidelity
- The fact that the argument was "heated" and the Mona had "had enough" indicates that this was done in the heat of passion as well
- Only remaining requirement is whether the conduct was sufficient to deprive an ordinary person of their self-control
	- This is assessed on a modified objective test as seen in *Hill* and *Thibert*
	- Only successful case we have for provocation is *Thibert*, and this case seems to meet the standard
	- Falls into the common pattern of a concern over losing one's spouse, and the insults also likely played a role
	- Given the fact that the court was willing to allow the defence in *Thibert* despite the obvious unreasonability of his beliefs, likely that it will be allowed here
	- None of the same concerns with women as property or beliefs that violate Charter beliefs are present here
- Overall, the defence of provocation can be given an air of reality
	- Crown likely cannot disprove any of this, only exception would be if disproving the MR for extortion counts
- Can also briefly cover necessity, but it probably won't be successful for a couple of reasons, most significant is that you can't really justify murder
- In the end, Mona can probably get her charge reduced to manslaughter, either by arguing that the MR for murder is not met, or via the defence of provocation, latter is more likely

## A Hole in One
- Two famous golfers named Panther and Ike are facing off in a major golf tournament, spectators are very drunk
- When Ike is about the putt on the last hole, a spectator burps very loudly and distracts him, making him miss
- When Panther goes to putt, another spectator walks out on the green and starts singing, distracting Panther and making him also miss
	- Second spectator (Bogey) is hauled off by security
- When it's Ike's turn again, the first spectator (Birdie) burps again, causing him to miss again
	- He smacks a tree with his club, sending a piece of bark flying which hits Birdie in the arm
- In response, she runs onto the green and pushed Ike into the water, he dies of drowning because of a defective breathing reflex
### Question 1
- Bogey was charged with causing a disturbance under s. 175(1)(a)(i)
- AR of this is singing, which causes a disturbance in a public place while the accused is not in a "dwelling-house"
	- This just means a house
- MR of this seems to be intent to cause a disturbance, and knowledge that you are not in a house and are in a public place
- AR is met, Bogey was in a public place and disturbed the golf tournament (causing Panther to miss) by singing
- MR is less clear, as the defence can point to Bogey's self-induced intoxication to question whether he had the requisite intent
- Even if the Crown can prove that Bogey intended to create a disturbance through his actions, he may be able to raise the defence of intoxication
	- This is likely a general intent offence, as the circumstances and outcome are not very complex
	- Only option is extreme intoxication, may be able to raise this considering the extreme volume of alcohol he consumed
	- Will need evidence of automatism for this
- Overall, the Crown would likely not be able to convict Bogey here, as the defence could likely raise a reasonable doubt over whether Bogey intended to cause a disturbance
- If he was charged under s. 175(1)(a)(ii), he would be convicted easily
- Difference is that instead of singing, he causes the disturbance by being drunk
- This removes the only arguments the defence had, which were based on Bogey's intoxication
	- Also can't raise the defence because one of the elements of the offence is now intoxication (*Penno*)
### Question 2
- Birdie is charged with 2nd degree murder, does she have a defence?
- Three main things that Birdie can do: challenge the MR, claim provocation, or claim self-induced intoxication
- The AR for murder is causing the death of a human being, which is met here
	- Test for this is the significant contributing cause from *Smithers*, and the thin-skull rule from the same case also applies
- MR for murder includes meaning to cause bodily harm, where the accused is reckless as to whether death ensues
	- It's not clear that Birdie was reckless as to whether death ensued, all she did was push him into waist-deep water
	- Likely the only way this could be considered an unacceptable risk was if Birdie knew about his defective breathing reflex
	- This is possible given that he's a famous golfer and Birdie is an avid golf fan, but need more evidence
- Birdie can also raise provocation, arguing that Ike unlawfully caused bodily harm to her when hitting the tree
	- This qualifies as an indictable offence punishable by 5 years or more
	- We know this is possible, case is similar to *DeSousa*
- Main problem would be establishing that an ordinary person would be deprived of their self-control
	- Even if we incorporate Birdie's intoxication into the test, pretty unlikely that this is met
- Birdie can also raise intoxication, likely the best available defence
- Murder is always specific intent, need more evidence but it's definitely possible that Birdie was too intoxicated to manifest specific intent
- It would at least meet the air of reality, Crown would have to prove that Birdie still had intent to kill here
- Overall, it's very unlikely Birdie would be convicted of 2nd degree murder, it would either be reduced to manslaughter because she doesn't meet the MR or murder, or because of intoxication
