Answer 1 
Archie is likely not to be convicted of this offence, as he can argue that he was intending to disrupt a different, non-social/benevolent meeting, and thus does not have the requisite *mens rea*. First of all, the Crown will have to prove all elements of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt to secure a conviction. Looking to the elements of the offence, the *actus reus* of this offence seems to be fairly simple. The conduct required is that accused must disturb or interrupt an assemblage of persons, and the circumstance is that the meeting must be for a moral, social, or benevolent purpose. The Crown can certainly prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Archie disturbed and interrupted the meeting of the Jane Austen Society, and as they were meeting to discuss *Pride and Prejudice* and knit socks for the homeless, this would almost certainly prove that this meeting was moral, social, or benevolent.

The more difficult element for the Crown to prove is the *mens rea*. This offence is only punishable by summary conviction, but is presumed to be a subjective fault offence by virtue of being in the *Criminal Code* according to *Beaver* (SCC, 1957). Additionally, the text of the offence includes the word "wilfully", which according to *Buzzanga and Durocher* (ONCA 1980) implies subjective *mens rea*, specifically intention and knowledge. Therefore, to prove the *mens rea* the Crown must establish that Archie intended to disturb a moral, social, or benevolent meeting, and would have to know that the meeting would fall under those criteria. Because of the doctrine of transferred intent, even if Archie intended to disrupt a **different** moral/social/benevolent meeting, since the meeting he actually disrupted also met that criteria, the *actus reus* and *mens rea* would still coincide, according to *R v Gordon* (ONCA 2009) Thus, this case will hinge on whether a city planning meeting meets the definition set out in the *Criminal Code*. If the Crown is able to successfully argue that this developer meeting is a social or benevolent meeting as it is intending to help grow the community, making it benevolent, or is a meeting between representatives, making it social, they likely would have a decent case against Archie. However, if the trier of fact can be convinced that the developer meeting is not social, moral, or benevolent and rather entirely business-focused, then Archie is unlikely to be convicted. Overall, since the accused is granted the benefit of the doubt according to *JHS* (SCC 2008), it is more likely that the trier of fact will be left with reasonable doubt regarding the sociality or benevolence of the developer meeting, and is thus likely to acquit Archie, as in this case he would not have the requisite *mens rea*.

Finally, all this assumes that the predicate offence can be proven on it's own beyond a reasonable doubt. In the context of s. 364(1), the Crown must prove that the conduct amounted to a dishonest act, the circumstances were that the dishonest act was used to obtain food from a place in the business of providing food, with the consequence of economic deprivation. Additionally, s. 364(2)(d) holds that evidence that the accused absconded or surreptitiously left the premises after obtaining food or drink without paying for it first is proof of fraud in the absence of evidence to the contrary. 

Given this information, the Crown would likely be able to prove all elements of the *actus reus* beyond a reasonable doubt. There is an argument to be made that leaving the restaurant was not inherently a dishonest act, but the fact that this conduct is mentioned under s. 364(2) seems to indicate that it is considered dishonest.

For the *mens rea*, the Crown only has to prove that one of the options under s. 364(2), and an evidentiary burden is placed on the accused to disprove the presumption that they committed fraud. As mentioned earlier, the most relevant option in this context is s. 364(2)(d). Given that the Crown should be able to easily prove beyond a reasonable doubt all elements of the offence, we must now discuss what counterarguments Albert's counsel can raise. First of all, Albert can provide evidence to disprove the presumption under s. 364(2). Based on the facts of this case, the only evidence Albert can raise here is that he left the restaurant with the goal of entering the restaurant across the street, and gave no thought to the fact that he had not yet paid for his meal. Albert can also argue that he ordered the food with the intention of staying for the meal, and only left after he realized he was at the wrong restaurant after already eating some food, but the fact that he continued eating/drinking after realizing this, and the fact that he could have at least left money for the food before leaving seems to contradict this argument. This would not disprove the facts required for the presumption, but the court may consider this as evidence to the contrary against the assumption he committed fraud. The Crown may be able to argue that Albert was reckless in not considering payment, but as established by *Theroux*, the *mens rea* requirement of fraud only includes intent or knowledge, not recklessness. However, while Albert may have a strong argument that he did not meet the requirement of intention to deprive, the Court is overall more likely to interpret s. 364(2) in a manner that indicates that evidence must be raised to disprove the evidence that the accused obtained food and surreptitiously left the restaurant without paying, not evidence that the accused did not meet the *mens rea* of fraud.

The question remains of whether this violation can be justified under s. 1 of the *Charter*, which requires applying the four part test from *R v Oakes* (SCC 1986). The first part of this test requires a pressing or substantial goal for the violation, which in this case would be making the prosecution of "dine and dash" cases easier. While this may not be a serious offence, it's likely that this requirement will be met on the grounds that the difficulty of proving subjective intent for fraud offences creates hardship for small businesses. Second, and likely most important part of the test is the requirement of a rational connection between the violation and the goal. This also requires an assessment of how likely the evidence of the presumption is to lead to the presence of the presumed fact. As we can see from this case, it seems pretty clear that it's possible for someone to meet the requirements of s. 364(2) without having the requisite *mens rea* for fraud, indicating this element is not met. However, if this argument fails, the other two elements of the test will likely be met as the objective is achieved with minimal impairment as a simple evidentiary burden is probably appropriate here, and given that this offence is only a summary conviction, the benefit would likely be deemed appropriate and the Crown would be successful.
