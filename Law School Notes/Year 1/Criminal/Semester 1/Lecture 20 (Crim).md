### [[R v Javanmardi]]
- Once again raised the issue of the objective test for unlawful act manslaughter, following the precedent of [[R v Creighton]]
- Not itself a Charter case, but is the last major decision on the issue of objective fault
- Accused was charged with unlawful act manslaughter and criminal negligence causing death
- Javanmardi was a naturopath operating in Quebec, who provided the victim with an IV injection of nutrients, which is legal in most provinces but not in Quebec
- The victim had a negative reaction to the injection, Javanmardi stopped immediately and recommended he drink water and have sugar, thinking it was a hypoglycaemic reaction
- Later that night, he was rushed to the hospital and died
- Infection likely occurred because the vial used for the injection had bee used twice already
- Act was unlawful under the Quebec *Medical Act*
- In the absence of this prohibition, the Crown would likely only be able to proceed for criminal negligence, not unlawful act manslaughter
- Issue for the court is what the *mens rea* of these two offences are, including the predicate
- In *Creighton*, there was a requirement that the underlying act be "objectively dangerous"
- Therefore, the Crown has to prove that both the predicate offence was dangerous, and that a reasonable person must have reasonably foreseen bodily harm
- For criminal negligence, we have seen that the standard is a "marked and substantial departure", originating from [[R v JF]]
- Both objective standards but phrased differently, so are they equivalent?
- The Court decided that the difference between "marked" and "marked and substantial" was irrelevant to this case, and again avoided making a decision on that issue
- Overall, the standards for both avenues to conviction were basically the same
- Additionally, finding objective foresight of bodily harm is the same thing as determining whether an act is objectively dangerous, so that is irrelevant too
- In the case where the predicate offence has strict liability, the onus is on the Crown?
- Given all this information, how do we apply the decision from *Creighton*?
- Recall that we need to consider the nature of the act for the objective standard
- What is the standard being applied here?
- Court determined that we should examine what a reasonably prudent naturopath would do
- Javanmardi had extensive experience and education in her field, and knew how to give an injection, but we have to recall that this practice is illegal in Quebec
- In this case, the majority argued that greater care was required of an average person based on the nature of the activity, requiring special skill and care
- If the person carrying out the activity isn't certified to do so, they are unlikely to meet the higher standard
- In this case, Javanmardi's previous training and experience was used as evidence that she met the standard of care, even if that experience was not legally recognized
- Odd argument, as it would seem that given this training it would be more likely for Javanmardi to have objective foresight of harm in this case
- It seems that the argument being made by the SCC was that the trial judge was correct in presuming that Javanmardi's experience showed that using the vial multiple times was fine
- Even though this is supposed to be an application of *Creighton* that just focuses on the nature of the act, the Court is going pretty extensively into Javanmardi's personal experience
- The argument from the dissent was that the act here was administering an IV injection, so we should be focusing on that and not what a naturopath specifically would have foreseen
- As far as the law is considered, Javanmardi is no more qualified to give this injection as any random person that was dragged in off the street
### [[R v Stephan and R v Goforth]]
- In another SCC case, a couple was charged with criminal negligence and unlawful act manslaughter after a foster child died in their care
- Wife was found guilty of second degree murder, husband was convicted for manslaughter
- Husband he should not be found liable as his wife was primarily responsible for the care
- He had little involvement with the two girls, he knew they were picky eaters but never saw them unclothed and was unaware they were malnourished
- Court held that in this case, the fact that he was not involved in the care is not a vitiating factor, but actually further evidence that he was acting negligently
- However, they did point out that there are some circumstances where one parent is rarely home if they work in another location, but this did not happen here
- These kinds of circumstances could potentially be relevant to an issue like this
- Another example of personal circumstances that could be incorporated into the objective test
- It seems that in this case, the Court determined that the husband actually had subjective fault, the objective test wasn't even relevant
- Based on the condition the girls were found in, there was no possible way that the husband could have reasonably been unaware of what was going on
### Summary of Objective Fault
- If you have a statutory provision and you're trying to determine the fault requirement, here's what you do
- First, read the text of the offence in the *Criminal Code*
- There might be an explicit statement of the fault requirement, or there may be key words suggesting what the *mens rea* is
- Also need to consider whether or not the fault requirement is constitutional under s. 7
- It's pretty rare that an offence will violate the Charter, but it is possible
- If there is no suggestion of the fault requirement in the offence, look to the case law
- Often, there will be a previous case that determines the *mens rea* of a specific offence
- If there is no case or the precedent isn't binding, you need to do your own interpretation
- If the offence is a "true crime", there is a strong presumption of subjective *mens rea*
- We saw that this presumption is pretty strong from [[R v Zora]], but it can still be displaced like in the example of [[R v ADH]]
- If there is a suggestion of an objective fault requirement, need to consider whether the standard is a marked departure or a marked and substantial departure
- Do all the elements of the *actus reus* have a corresponding fault element?
- Finally, if there is a requirement of subjective fault, how do we express that
- Is it intent, recklessness, knowledge, or some combination of the three?
- Your goal should be to narrow down the possibilities as much as you can
- For every possible fault requirement, you'll need to make arguments for both the defence and the Crown
- Also, if the facts of the case establish subjective fault, you don't need to do all this
- You should always be considering how the fault requirement affects the outcome of the case
### Practice Questions
	You are a lawyer working for the British Columbia Ministry of the Attorney General.  The government proposes to introduce the following piece of legislation as part of its new _Dangerous Products Act_.  You have been asked to provide an opinion to the Minister on whether the legislation is constitutional.  What will you say?

          44. (1) Any person who makes a firecracker, firework or other incendiary device without lawful authorization is guilty of an offence and liable to a fine of $25,000. 

            (2) For the purposes of subsection (1), evidence that the person was in possession of a firecracker, firework or other incendiary device that does not bear an authorization stamp is proof, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, that the person made the firecracker, firework or other incendiary device.

            (3) It is not a defence to a charge under subsection (1) that the person making the device honestly and reasonably believed that he had the lawful authorization to do so.

- This is a provincial offence created by the BC government
- Subsection 3 of this offence eliminates the possibility of objective or subjective fault, so we can probably assume that this is an absolute liability offence
- The incorporation of the word "reasonably" also eliminates the possibility of the defence of due diligence
- However, this does not automatically make it unconstitutional as it does not carry any risk of jail time, meaning it does not contravene s. 7 of the Charter
- Still may also violate the constitution under s. 11(d), as this places an onus on the accused
- Therefore, there would need to be an application of the *Oakes* test to see if this violation can be saved under s. 1 of the Charter
- Remember that s. 7 isn't the only reason for a provision to be unconstitutional
