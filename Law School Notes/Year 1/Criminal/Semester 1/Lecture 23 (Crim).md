### [[R v Ewanchuk]]
- Recall that even if the victim claims they did not consent for the purpose of establishing the *actus reus*, the credibility of that claim can be questioned
- One of the primary ongoing issues when it comes to sexual assault cases
- A sexual assault case is not a credibility contest, we are not asking the trier of fact to choose between which story they like better
- In many sexual assault cases, the defendant doesn't testify like in [[R v Ewanchuk]]
- This is usually done because otherwise the Crown would be able to cross-examine the defendant on their criminal record
- This would have been particularly bad for Ewanchuk, given that he had a long history of convictions for rape going back to the 1960s
- Sexual assault is a crime of general intent, the *mens rea* requirement is not very complex
- Only has to be intention to touch, the sexual nature of the act is measured objectively
- For the consent requirement, knowledge or at least recklessness in terms of consent is enough
- Carried over directly from the previous offence, but the knowledge/reckless isn't related to the victim's state of mind, it's whether consent was communicated
- Weird double negative, but the standard is proving that the defendant knew that the victim had not communicated consent, either verbally or through action
- If the accused wants to challenge this, they have to raise a defence of honest but mistaken belief in the presence of communicated consent
- Consent is defined by law in the *Criminal Code*, so any mistaken belief based on a different definition of consent is a mistake of law, not fact
### Defence of Mistaken Belief
- Criminal code explicitly states several situations where this defence does not apply
- Doesn't apply if the belief comes from self-induced intoxication, recklessness/wilful blindness, and any circumstance where consent is vitiated
- Also requires the defendant to take reasonable steps to ascertain consent
- Later, another provision was added that the defence doesn't apply if there was no evidence of affirmative consent through words or conduct
- Doesn't really change the law, but emphasizes the requirement for affirmative consent
- Given all this, could Parliament make sexual assault an absolute liability offence?
- Almost certainly not, since the offence carries the risk of jail time and this would create a violation of s. 7 of the *Charter*
- What about making it an objective fault offence?
- To do this, the proportionality between the stigma and punishment of the offence must be weighed against the value of making it an objective fault offence
- Issue was dealt with by the SCC in two cases, *R v Osolin* and *R v Darrach*, where the court found that putting this in an air of reality test is fine, doesn't violate s. 11(d)
- Unlike murder, this offence isn't severe enough that it must be purely subjective *mens rea*
### Concurring Reasons
- Mainly spend time criticizing the reasons of Justice McClung and addressing the shocking prevalence of rape myths in judgements on sexual assault cases
- Even though we've made it a gender-neutral offence, it's still a highly gendered issue
- We should be interpreting the law with that issue in mind
- Specifically criticized the victim for living with another couple and having children outside of marriage, and using this as a justification for the assault
- Minimized the harm created by Ewanchuk's actions, and suggested that the victim should have physically fought him off if she truly didn't consent
- The day after the SCC case, Justice McClung sent out a public letter to the National Post attacking the concurring reasons, partially blaming her for raising the male suicide rate
- This was particularly offensive to the justice, who had lost both her husband and son to suicide
- McClung claimed to not know this, but doubled down on his criticism of the victim
- Complaints flowed in about Justice McClung both about his reasons in this case and another where he made significant homophobic remarks, as well as his response to the SCC decision
- Ultimately, the complaints against Justice McClung resulted in a reprimand, but not a removal as removing a justice is actually very difficult, can only be done by legislature
- Ewanchuk went back to the trial court for sentencing, given a year in prison and was convicted again a few years later for sexually assaulting an 8-year old girl and sentenced to 16 years
