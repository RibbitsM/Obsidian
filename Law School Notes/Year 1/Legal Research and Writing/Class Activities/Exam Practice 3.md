To establish the common law defence of duress, the defence must be available, and the elements of the defence must be satisfied. The common law defence is only available to parties to a crime, not the principle(s) (Keller, para 12). Therefore if Gerry is charged as a party to the theft, the defence will be available. There are four elements to this defence. First, there must be an immediate threat of death/serious harm solely motivating the accused. Second, the threat must be credible and affecting the accused during the offence. Third, the threat must be serious enough that a reasonable person would have complied. Fourth, there cannot be a safe avenue of escape (Gionet, para 25).
#### Threat of Death/Harm

Harley informed Gerry that if he went to the police or failed to assist with the theft, Gerry would "find out what happens". This threat was made 3 hours prior to the theft, which Gerry was unaware of before Harley arrived at his door. The immediacy requirement is not absolute, *Keller* establishes that future threats also qualify (para 21).

Gerry can argue that Harley's statement implies a threat of serious injury/death, and came only hours before the offence. Gerry also had no other reason to assist Harley, he did not know him and unaware of the theft until Harley told him.

The Crown can argue that Harley's statement is too vague to be a threat, meaning the defence fails to create an "air of reality" and is unavailable (Gionet, para 13)

Assuming there was a threat of serious injury/death, evidence suggests there was an immediate threat solely motivating Gerry.
#### Credibility of Threat

Harley physically intimidated Gerry during their meeting, and implied that he had people watching Gerry. Gerry was terrified by this encounter, and likely believed he would be seriously injured or killed if he did not comply. This element allows the threat to be express or implied, and relies on whether the conduct could be reasonably interpreted as a threat (Gionet, para 25). Additionally, *Laughy* establishes that there must be evidence of a real risk of harm (para 20).

Gerry can argue that he interpreted "what happens" as implying death or bodily harm. Further, he can argue any reasonable person would conclude that saying you "don't want to find out what happens" and "we'll be watching you" implies threat to life or limb. Harley's criminal status, as well as his statement that "Darcy owes me money. But Darcy's dead" suggests he is willing to follow through. Only three hours elapsed between the threat and offence, and Harley was present during the offence. Therefore, the threat was affecting Gerry at the time.

The Crown can argue that Gerry has no concrete evidence that Harley would hurt or kill him. In *Gionet*, a similar vague threat was deemed credible. However, the plaintiff in that case had a personal relationship with the threatener, and knew what they were capable of (para 31). Gerry has no such knowledge, and no solid basis to conclude that Harley's threat is credible.

Assuming the first element of the test is met, the threat would be likely be credible and affecting Gerry at the time of the offence.
#### Seriousness of Threat

This element is evaluated on an objective standard that includes the "particular circumstances and human frailties of the accused" (Keller, para 23). Gerry was in a significant state of distress after being threatened by Harley and learning his ex-wife of 15 years was dead. He also had previously developed anxiety.

Gerry can argue that given that he was "so scared [he] could barely think", his only option was to comply. Additionally, any reasonable person would have done the same in the situation. The consequences of complying were enabling the theft of farm equipment, likely valuing a few thousand dollars. A reasonable person would value their life over stopping the theft.

The Crown can argue that a reasonable person would not see the threat as serious. If Harley truly had people to carry it out, he would have no need for Gerry.

Given the subjective circumstances, this element would likely be met.
#### Safe Avenue of Escape

Gerry had two clear avenues of escape. He could not have phoned the police at any point, as Harley confiscated his phone while recruiting him. However, he could have driven to the police during the 2 hours after Harley left and before Gerry went to meet him. Also, Gerry could have driven to the police station on his way to meet Harley. According to *Keller*, the accused also must take reasonable steps to find a safe avenue of escape (para 26). This element is also established using the same objective-subjective test (Gionet, para 26).

Gerry can argue that he could not utilize the first avenue as he was paralyzed by fear. He testified pacing anxiously and has little memory of this period of time. Gerry could not have used the second avenue, as he noticed a car had been driving behind him since he got on the highway, and likely believed it belonged to Harley or his associates. Any reasonable person faced with such fear would have believed they would be killed or seriously harmed if they tried to escape. Additionally, compared to Gionet who had weeks to go to the police, Gerry had only three frantic hours to find a way out of this situation (para 37).

The Crown can argue that even in Gerry's situation, a reasonable person would have recognized a safe avenue of escape. Laughy believed that her sister and niece were at risk if she tried to escape despite a lack of concrete evidence, and could not raise the defence of duress (para 8). Gerry similarly has little evidence to suggest that Harley could prevent him from going to the police. A reasonable person would conclude that his claims that people are watching Gerry are bluffs, and go to the police. The case of Gionet is distinct as Gerry does not have the same concrete evidence and lacks any distrust of the police (para 41). Finally, Gerry could have called the police after Harley returned his phone to him.

Overall, Gerry's state of mind suggests that him and anyone else in the same scenario would be unable to muster the courage to call Harley's bluff, assuming he is lying in the first place. Therefore, it was reasonable for Gerry to conclude he had no safe avenue of escape.
