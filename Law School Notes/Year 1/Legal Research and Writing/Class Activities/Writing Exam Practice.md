Determining whether Gerry can successfully raise the common law defence of duress depends on whether the defence is available to him, and whether or not the elements of the defence can be satisfied.

The common law defence of duress is ordinarily only available if the defendant is charged as a party to an offence (R v Keller (Keller), para 12). Since Gerry has not been charged as a principal offender in this case, the defence is available to him. For the defence to be considered by the judge it must have an "air of reality", meaning that the available evidence must be sufficient to meet the elements of the defence if believed (Keller, para 15). The common law defence of duress relies on four questions. First, was there a threat of serious injury or death solely motivating the victim, and was the threat immediate? Second, was the threat credible to the accused? Third, would a reasonable person done the same in the circumstance and was the threat proportional to the offence? Fourth, did the accused have a safe avenue of escape? 
#### Was There a Threat of Serious Injury/Death?

When Gerry first encounters Harley, before leaving the house Harley tells Gerry that if he get police involved, fails to appear on time, or fails to fulfil his job, he will "find out what happens". For establishing defence of duress, a there must be a threat of death or grievous bodily harm which can be either express or implied, this would be an example of an implicit threat (R v Gionet (Gionet), para 25). While Harley makes no specific threats, Gerry can argue that Harley's threatening demeanour and physical intimidation of Gerry heavily implied violent consequences. However, it would still be possible to argue that this is not a threat of serious injury of death as Gerry has no idea of what Harley intends by this threat. 

Regarding the element of sole motivation, Gerry had no other motivation to participate in the theft outside of the threats made by Harley as he was completely unaware of the plan until meeting Harley.

Gerry knows very little about Harley, except for the fact that he needs a lookout and used to know Darcy. In *Gionet*, the Court was able to interpret a similarly vague threat by pointing to the fact that the accused knew that the person threatening him had previously killed one of his business partners and had considered killing the accused before (para 14). The Crown can thus argue that Gerry has no concrete evidence of whether this implied threat is genuine or not. 

In the case of *R v Laughy*, the accused was denied the defence of duress as there was no evidence that danger would be created if they went to the police. #### Was The Threat Immediate?

At common law, a threat can also be of future death or injury and still suffice for the defence of duress (Keller, 18). In this situation, Gerry was facing a fairly immediate threat as he was ordered to meet Harley three hours after their initial meeting or face consequences. Even if this is construed as a future threat, this would satisfy the requirement regardless.
#### Did Gerry Believe the Threat was Credible?

The evidence suggests that Gerry believed Harley intended to kill or seriously injure him if he did not comply. Gerry is described as being very nervous and developing anxiety after his experience with Darcy, and mentions being nervous before even opening the door when he first meets Harley. Gerry's testimony never specifically mentions fearing injury or death, but makes numerous references to being terrified by the experience, even "so scared [he] could barely think". Gerry also may have believed that Harley killed Darcy, as he mentions that Darcy is dead when first meeting Gerry. Harley also informed Gerry that "we'll be watching you", implying that Harley has a larger organization backing him up and that Gerry may not be safe in his home. Additionally, Harley is clearly a criminal as he is recruiting Gerry to help him commit a theft, and is willing to go to these lengths to collect a debt. 

However, the Crown could argue that Gerry never mentioned fearing physical violence specifically from Harley. As stated in *Gionet*, "Mere fear does not constitute duress in the absence of a
threat, either express or implied" (para. 25). Additionally, he has not witnessed Harley acting violently, and does not have any concrete evidence that he will follow through with his threat.

#### Was the Threat Effective During the Offence?

Harley was not in the physical proximity of Gerry during the theft, but was nearby. Harley also restated his instructions to Gerry before driving off, but did not reinforce the threat. Harley's original threat still applied, as one of the conditions required that Gerry successfully fulfill his lookout role.
#### Did Gerry Act Reasonably?

While reasonableness in this context is measured with an objective test, the test is modified to include "the particular circumstances and human frailties of the accused." (Keller, para 23). Likely the most important factor to consider is Gerry's anxiety. Gerry can argue that given the compressed timeline, any reasonable anxious individual would have complied for fear of being found and killed if they tried to leave the house or fail to fulfill Harley's request. It is unlikely that anyone who was not quite brave would have called Harley's bluff.

The Crown will likely argue that even if put in the same situation, even an anxious reasonable person could conclude that it Harley truly did have people helping him, he would have no need of Gerry. Therefore, his threat was simply an attempt to intimidate Gerry into cooperating and he did not actually have the means to hurt him.

#### Was the Threat Proportional to the Offence?

Harley wanted Gerry to assist him with stealing some expensive farm equipment, likely valuing multiple thousands of dollars. If we assume that Gerry believed that Harley intended to seriously harm or kill him if he did not comply, this is likely to be insufficient to outweigh the value of Gerry's life. But if the threat was not genuine, the proportionality requirement would fail.
#### Was a Safe Avenue of Escape Available?

This element is assessed using the same standard as the previous element, issue is if a reasonable person in the same circumstances would find a safe method of escape. Given the fact that Gerry had only three hours between the threat from Harley and his arrest, there are a limited number of options to consider.

Gerry had one option to get help, and two opportunities to use it. He owns no modern technology except for a cell phone, which was confiscated by Harley, therefore he could not have phoned the police to alert them. He also likely could not have refused to give his phone to Harley as Harley had just threatened to harm him. However, Harley then left Gerry alone for around two hours, during which Gerry had the opportunity to leave the house and drive to the police station. During this time Gerry was overwhelmed with anxiety, having little recollection of what he did apart from pacing and recalling his ex-wife who he had just learned was now dead. When Gerry was driving to the farm to assist Harley, he again had the opportunity to take the exit leading to the police station, but chose not to as he saw a black SUV following him.

Gerry would likely argue that any reasonable person in his situation would be similarly scared to take action immediately, and would also have been deterred when noticing the black SUV following his car. He also would likely point to the fact that Harley pulled up behind him immediately after he arrived as evidence he was being followed.

Besides having an avenue of escape available, another consideration to note is whether or not the accused took steps to find one (Keller, para 25). It is important to note that Gerry made no efforts to resist Harley apart from contemplating driving to the police office and moving towards the exit to the police station.

In the case of *Gionet*, the accused had many more opportunities to go to the police compared to Gerry as the events took place over several months, and was still allowed the common law defence of duress (para 37). However, Gionet was a former criminal who would distrust police and had a long relationship with the man who threatened him, and was well aware of what he was capable of. Given these circumstances, it was held that he had no safe avenue of escape despite having ample opportunity to go to the police (Gionet, para 42).

- There are two different ways to apply the defence of duress
- If the defendant is a principal, the defence is found in s. 17 of the *Criminal Code*
- This version is also called the "defence of compulsion"
- This is the statutory version of the defence of duress, exam question is asking if Gerry can use the common law defence of duress, not the statutory version
- Gerry has been charged **in connection** to the theft, not as a principal
- According to *Paquette v R*, s. 17 of the *Criminal Code* only applies if the defendant is a principal
- Likely don't need to address this much, as the question specifically asks about the applicability of the **common law** defence of duress
- Another really important qualification is that for any defence to be considered, it has to pass the **air of reality** requirement
- The components come from *R v Gionet* but are explained in other cases
#### Is the Common Law Defence of Duress Available?
- As mentioned earlier, if the accused is the principal then they only have access to the defence of compulsion arising from s. 17
- However, it is clearly established that Gerry is a party to the offence and not a principal, meaning that he has access to the common law defence of duress assuming all the conditions of the defence are met
#### Is the Air of Reality Test Met?
- Even if the defence is available to the accused, the defence must have an "air of reality" to it
- To determine this, the judge will assess the existing evidence to evaluate whether the trier of fact could acquit based on the given evidence if believed
- If we take Gerry's account of the situation at face value, are all the elements of the common law defence of duress met?
- To address this we have to go through the elements of the defence
- All of this is set out in *R v Gionet*, as *R v Keller* also mentions the elements of defence, but only focuses on the immediacy of the threat and the fourth element listed here
#### Did Gerry Act Solely as a Result of Threats of Death/Grievous Bodily Harm? (main component 1)
- Was there a threat of death?
- Hinges on the interpretation of "you find out what happens"
- Two possibilities, either court doesn't consider this to be a threat of death/grievous harm and the case is dismissed, or analysis continues
- If this is satisfied, have to establish whether Gerry acted solely because of this threat
##### Was The Threat to Bodily Integrity? (subcomponent 1.1)
- Vague threat, again hinges on the same interpretation of what Harley meant by "you find out what happens"
- Look to case law for how to interpret vague threats
- Subjectively speaking, Gerry definitely viewed this as a threat to his bodily integrity
- In *R v Gionet*, the accused faced a similar threat, and was considered to be an implied threat of bodily harm or death
- However, in that case there was significant history between the principal and the accused
- Accused knew that the principal had killed people in the past, and at least on one occasion considered killing or severely harming the accused
- Gerry has never met Harley before, so this element is not present
##### Was there a Close Temporal Connection? (subcomponent 1.2)
- Alleged threat was made a couple hours before the commission of the crime
- How will the court interpret a "close temporal connection"?
- Very likely this element will be satisfied, can likely brush over counterarguments
#### Was the Threat Credible? (main component 2)
- Should move our analysis from subcomponent 1.1 here, and focus on other things in that segment
- Usually depends on what the accused knows about the person giving the threat
- In *R v Gionet*, the Court determined that the threat given to the defendant was credible as he was aware that the person giving the threat had killed business associates in the past
- Usually not a significant factor, tends to be overlooked in favour of determining whether or not there was a safe avenue of escape
- Need to consider whether the accused initially believes the threats, in *R v Laughy* the defendant initially thinks the threat is a joke or prank
- Also important factor is whether there is circumstantial evidence that the threat will be carried out
- In *R v Keller*, the accused is able to go on a week long vacation to Vancouver without informing the person threatening him and suffers no consequences
- Gerry has no evidence that Harley's threats are genuine apart from his general demeanour and obvious involvement in criminal activities
- *Keller* also establishes that the threat does not have to be immediate, can be threat of consequences in the near future (para 21)
##### Was the Threat Affecting the Accused at the Time of the Offence? (subcomponent 2.1)
- Have to look at how Gerry acted while heading to meet Harley
- This is another element that is pretty trivial to establish from Gerry's testimony
- Also need to consider whether or not Gerry is a reliable witness
- Doesn't seem to be any evidence that he would intentionally lie, though he does have pretty severe anxiety
- No evidence that he suffers from hallucinations though
- Needs to be evidence that the threat prompted the accused to act, in *R v Laughy* the defendant argued that a phone threat prompted her to act out of fear, but later refused police protection arguing that since the police were involved, she would be ok (para 10)
#### Did Gerry Act Reasonably? (main component 3)
- This is a general assessment of whether or not Gerry acted in a similar way to what a reasonable person would have done in the same circumstances
##### Is the Objective Test Met? (subcomponent 3.1)
- This is not a purely objective test, *R v Gionet* and *R v Keller* both specify that this test also takes into consideration subjective factors
- Means that the objective person would be in the same circumstances with the same history, capacity, and conditions of the accused
- "the appropriate objective standard to be employed is one that takes into account the particular circumstances and human frailties of the accused." (R v Keller, para 23)
##### Was the Threat Proportional to the Offence? (subcomponent 3.2)
- Likely trivial to establish, if the court determined earlier that the threat suggested a credible possibility of severe harm or death, it would be proportional
- Crime here was stealing farm equipment, expensive theft but not exactly morally reprehensible
- Also keep in mind that Gerry was just a lookout, he was not involved in the planning or the execution of the theft itself
#### Was There a Safe Avenue of Escape? (main component 4)
- This is the most important element, most cases involving the common law defence of duress hinge on the fulfillment of this element
- Gerry had at least one clear opportunity to go to the police
- First, after Harley left his house, he had a period of a few hours where he could have called the police
- Additionally, while he was driving to the place he promised to meet Harley at, he passed by the exit to the police station without pulling in
- However, the black SUV driving behind him deterred him from taking the exit, as he feared that Harley or one of his associates was driving the car
- Harley also issued a specific threat to Gerry that if he got the police involved, he would "find out what happens"
- This is a key element, in *R v Laughy* the Court determined that the defence could not be established as there was no suggestion that the accused couldn't go to the police
- Also, Harley was nearby while the offence was being committed, though not in Gerry's immediate vicinity
- Compare this case to *R v Gionet*, where the defendant had many more opportunities to go to the police and chose not to
- Recall that the theft took place the same night that Harley and Gerry met
##### Is the Objective Test Met? (subcomponent 4.1)
- Same kind of test we used under component 3, considers subjective factors
- Likely going to hinge on the moment where Gerry could have turned off towards the police station
- An important point to note is that **Gerry did not have any way to contact the police after Harley came to his house**
- The only method of communication he had at his house was his cell phone, which was confiscated by Harley and only returned once they met at 7 pm
- Therefore, **the only time Gerry would have been able to go to the police was when he was driving to the meeting spot**
- Was the presence of the black SUV enough to deter a reasonable person?
- The above quote specifically mentions "human frailties" as an element considered in the objective test
- Very likely that Gerry's severe anxiety played a role in his hesitation to go to the police
- In *R v Gionet*, the court considered the fact that Gionet was distrustful of the police and had a fairly significant criminal record as factors in the objective test
- Also, *R v Keller* establishes that this test extends to the ability of the accused to evaluate their options
- In that case, the defence failed partly because the defendant did not take any steps to find a way out of their situation
- Gerry seemed to be in a state of mental distress after Harley came to visit him, and likely would have been incapable of planning a way out during the time between the visit and the meeting time
- Gerry moving into the right lane to take the exit before choosing not to after seeing the SUV may constitute him taking steps towards a legal escape
