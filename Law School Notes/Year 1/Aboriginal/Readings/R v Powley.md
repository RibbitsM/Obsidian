- Issue is whether the members of the Métis community around Sault Ste Marie have a right to hunt for food
- Powley and his son went hunting and shot and killed a bull moose
- Moose hunting is strictly regulated, but is not enforced against harvest by status Indians
- Neither Powley or his son had a valid license to hunt moose, so they put a tag on the ear of the moose specifying the date, time, and location of the kill, and put his Métis and Aboriginal Association membership number on it
- One week later, the pair were charged with unlawfully hunting moose and knowingly possessing illegally hunted game
- Argued that they have an aboriginal right to hunt for food near Sault Ste Marie, govt denies the existence of a Métis right to hunt for food
- All lower courts agreed with the Powleys and acquitted them
### Analysis
- Keep in mind that the term "Métis" in s. 35 does not refer to all individuals who have mixed Indian and European heritage
- Métis is a distinct culture separate from Indian, Inuit, and European cultures alike
- Across Canada, Métis have developed differently which is recognized by the language of s. 35(2)
- As long as the claimants belong to an identifiable Métis community with enough continuity establish a local aboriginal right
- Respondents are claiming to belong to the Métis community in and around Sault Ste Marie
- Doesn't matter whether this is a distinct community, or part of a larger Métis group across the Upper Great Lakes
### Van der Peet Test
- Core question of this case was how we define aboriginal rights under s. 35(1) of the 1982 *Constitution*
- Emphasis in this case was on prior occupation and pre-contact continuity, but this is not exactly appropriate given the nature of Métis society
- Majority in *Van der Peet* suggested that the Métis were included in s. 35 for reasons different than their pre-contact continuity
- Significant feature of the Métis was that they emerged in the period between first contact and effective European control
- With that in mind, we can modify the *Van der Peet* test to focus on customs and traditions important to Métis communities before effective European control
### Characterization of the Right
- Respondents made a point of documenting that the moose they shot was going to be used as meat for the winter
- Means they were hunting for food, and we can characterize the right being claimed as the right to hunt for food
### Identification of the Rights-Bearing Community
- Trial judge found a distinctive Métis community in the Upper Great Lakes region emerging in the mid-17th century and peaking around 1850
- Métis mainly came to Sault Ste Marie around 1750 after the French established a trading post there
- One of the oldest and most important Métis settlements in the area
- Historical records show an overwhelming number of Métis working at the trading post, seen as evidence of a distinct and cohesive Métis community in the area
- Court found no basis to overturn the trial judge's finding that there was a historic Métis community at Sault Ste Marie
- This community has persisted over the years and while it has decreased significantly, it still exists
- European control did interfere with the community and its practices but did not eliminate it entirely
- Most of the lack of information was because the Métis public were laying low after the Riel rebellions
- Overall, evidence supports the trial judge's finding that there was not a lapse in the community, and there was still continuous occupation
### Verification of the Claimant's Membership
- This is a little harder to determine than finding membership in an Indian band, need to do it case-by-case
- Court is not setting out a comprehensive definition of who and is not Métis in this case
- Just looking at the three broad factors of self-identification, ancestral connection, and community acceptance
- Claimant needs to self-identify as a member of the Métis community, and cannot be too recent
- Evidence of an ancestral connection does not require any measure of "blood quantum", but at the least you should have a link to the community you're claiming rights based on
- Powley's Métis ancestry is not disputed in this case
- Finally, membership in a Métis political organization can be relevant to determining modern acceptance but won't be enough without an understanding of the role of that organization
- Core of community acceptance is past and ongoing participation in a shared culture that constitutes the identity of a Métis community
- Can also provide evidence of participation in community activities or testimony from fellow community members
- All three of these requirements are necessary to claim a s. 35 right, we want to make sure only entitled individuals can claim the rights
- In this case, there is no reason to overturn the trial judge's finding that the Powleys are members of the Sault Ste Marie community
- The fact that their ancestors briefly lived on an Indian reserve does not negate their Métis identity
### Relevant Time Frame
- Recall that the most relevant time frame for Métis communities is the time post-contact, but pre-European control
- Métis rights do not need to come from the pre-contact practices of their aboriginal ancestors, this denies the Métis their distinct status
- Europeans were definitely present in the region early on, but settlement in the area wasn't encouraged until the mid-1800s
- Until this time, the local Métis population was largely unaffected by European law and custom
- Supports the finding that the relevant time period here is between 1815 and 1850
### Integral Practice
- Subsistence hunting and fishing was a constant in the Métis community though the availability of specific species varied
- Residents of trading communities relied mostly on local subsistence instead of European imports
- Lived off the land by hunting, fishing, gathering, and cultivating
- Many of these activities were done in the land around Sault Ste Marie
- Evidence seems to support that hunting for food was integral to the Métis way of life in the region during the relevant time period
### Continuity of Practice
- Recall that s. 35 is a commitment to protecting the former practices of Aboriginal communities, recognizing that these practices can evolve over time
- Right claimed by the Powleys seems to fall neatly into the historical practice of the right, no issue here
### Extinguishment and Infringement
- No evidence of extinguishment, only evidence is from the Robinson-Huron Treaty which excluded the Métis
- However, there was no clear and plain intention to extinguish here
- Ontario does not recognize the Métis right to hunt, the lack of recognition and application of the challenged provisions infringed their right to hunt for food
### Justification
- Main argument for justification was conservation
- This is an important concern, but the record does not support a justification
- No evidence the moose population in Ontario is under threat and even if they did, the Métis would get priority according to the process from [[R v Sparrow]]
- Given the available evidence and current licensing system, the denial of the Métis right for food is unjustified
- Also argue that identifying members of the Métis community is harder, but this is an exaggerated claim
- Justification of other hunting regulations will require evidence for the specific species
- Concluded that the Métis community near Sault Ste Marie have an aboriginal right to hunt for food, appeal dismissed with costs
