- The patriation of the Canadian Constitution in 1982 in some ways froze in place existing issues with Aboriginal rights, but also created opportunities
- Section 35 of the 1982 Constitution Act is the portion dealing with Aboriginal and treaty rights, and is what this entire course is pretty much based on
- *Calder* decision was the first landmark decision for Aboriginal rights, and Aboriginal title was first developed in the Nisga'a Nation case
- *Guerin* case first established the fiduciary relationship between the government and Indigenous nations, dealt with the sale of Musqueam land to build the Shaughnessy Golf Course
- Where the concept of the "honour of the Crown" as it relates to Indigenous peoples first developed
- As Canada's constitution developed, the system began to switch from the supremacy of the Parliament to the supremacy of the Constitution
- Initially, the formation of the new Constitution was seen as an opportunity for Indigenous peoples, it was important for them to get something for them in there
- Simply states that "The existing aboriginal and treaty rights of the aboriginal peoples of Canada are hereby recognized and affirmed"
- Section 35(2) states that "In this Act, "aboriginal peoples of Canada" includes the Indian, Inuit, and Metis peoples of Canada"
- Additionally, s. 35.1 was added in 1983 to recognize Indigenous peoples as partners in governance, allowing them to attend constitutional conferences
- Proposed recognition of the right to self-governance in the Charlottetown Accords, which we know ended up not being accepted by the Canadian public
## [[R v Sparrow]]
- First opportunity for the SCC to interpret s. 35
- In this case, the court tried to determine what it means to "recognize and affirm" Aboriginal and treaty rights
- Is this a true reimagining of the relationship between Indigenous peoples and the government, or is it simply a codification of the existing relationship
- Supreme Court rejected the latter interpretation, arguing that recognition and affirmation goes beyond the previous state of affairs
- Generally speaking, this section is designed to call for a just settlement for Aboriginal peoples
- Justice Dickson was Chief Justice at the time, and led to kind of a high water mark for interpretation in favour of Indigenous groups
- Did not go as far as to challenge the Doctrine of Discovery, sovereign title was still very much unquestionable, though some other parts of the relationship may have changed
### Doctrine of Discovery
- Clearly set out by a US case called *Johnson v M'Intosh*
- Served to significantly impair the rights of the original inhabitants of North America, disregarding their right to sovereignty as independent nations
- Their title was considered subservient to the rights of those who "discovered" the land
- Originates from the Papal Bulls issued by the Vatican during the exploration of the New World
- John Borrows, a prominent Anishinaabe scholar, is known for advocating for the undermining and questioning of the [[Doctrine of Discovery]] as a harmful concept for Indigenous rights
- Argues that a house built on a foundation of sand is unstable, and though moving it to a new foundation may be disruptive, it would overall be better for all its inhabitants
### Case Issues
- This was a regulatory prosecution, the Department of Fisheries and Oceans in BC had been consistently limiting Aboriginal fishing rights for years
- In protest to a limit on drift net length, the Musqueam decided to challenge the limitation by intentionally fishing with a net that was too long and making the argument in court
- There are multiple Aboriginal rights decisions that panned out this way, as well as cases where the apprehension of the accused was incidental and the community got behind it
- Can also be lawsuits based on non-criminal issues, or part of a judicial review
- Several Musqueam fishers were charged, but Ron Sparrow was the main case prosecuted
- Lost their case at trial, as their right only extended to the version regulated by the government
- Goes to appeal, and eventually to the Supreme Court
- Key issues were the meaning of "existing", the meaning of "recognized and affirmed", and what limits the government could impose on these rights
- While s. 35 is not subject to traditional limitations on rights like those from s. 1 of the Charter, in the decision the Court did read in a method for justification
- This is a course that uses doctrinal tests, and we will need to learn those tests
- Something that common law courts are known to do, and a lot of this decision is couched in that familiar language of burdens of proof and tests
### Sparrow Framework for Aboriginal Rights
- Established ways to demonstrate the existence of Aboriginal rights, though it would be developed much further in later cases
- Also established the possibility for the Crown to prove that a right has been extinguished, though it is quite difficult to do
- Creates a method for proving a *prima facie* infringement of an Aboriginal right, with the burden on the claimant
- Also created the framework for justifying this infringement, proven by the Crown
- For the "existing" definition, Court held that an existing right is one that has not been extinguished
- One example of an extinguished right would be in the National Resources Transfer Agreements, made between the government and the Western provinces regarding authority over resources
- Basically took away provincial jurisdiction to manage natural resources
- Within these agreements, it's been interpreted that there was clear indication to extinguish the commercial right to hunt, one of the few examples of such intention
- In exchange, the ordinary rights to hunt were expanded to cover the entire province
- SCC also held that the meaning of "existing" must be interpreted liberally, and is allowed to evolve over time, not "frozen" at the point of contact or in 1982
- Inconsistent with the principles of constitutional interpretation
- Can be argued that this intention was reversed in [[R v Van der Peet (ATR)]]
#### Existing Rights
- To prove the existence of the right, appellant brought oral evidence of Musqueam chiefs and testimony from anthropologists
- Proving the right was fairly simple here, but it much more important in other cases, especially when it comes to Aboriginal title
- Court found that this right was limited to fishing for food, social, and ceremonial purposes, but did not include commercial purposes
- However, they did leave that possibility open and mostly sidestepped the issue
- Argument from the appellants was that the Coast Salish nations were extremely involved in barter between other nations, often using fish as their main commodity
- Common theme in these cases are Indigenous nations trying to push issues away from the courts and towards the negotiating table
#### Fiduciary Relationship
- Also marked a significant change in how provinces dealt with Indigenous peoples, as they were not bound by the same fiduciary relationship
- Now, they have to obey constitutional protections for Aboriginal rights
- The issue of the fiduciary relationship was also addressed here, kind of building off of what was already established in the *Guerin* case
### Infringement of Aboriginal Rights
- Court decided that they need to establish a framework for how the government assesses whether limitations on Aboriginal rights are justified or not
- Stated that even though s. 35 is not subject to s. 1, this is still needed as these rights are not absolute
- This is what people are referring to when they mention the "Sparrow Test"
- Do say that these rights have constitutional status and priority, meaning they are superior to ordinary statutory rights and take priority over those rights
- A *prima facie* limit is established by looking at the right from the Indigenous community's perspective and asking a few questions
- First, does this limit pose an undue hardship to the community?
- Second, does the limit deny the right holder their preferred means of exercising the right?
- From the Musqueam perspective, they wanted to be able to make decisions on how to fish based on the needs of their community
- Additionally, salmon is a fundamental part of Musqueam law and culture
- Other questions are possible, but these are the relevant ones here
- Found that there was indeed an infringement here
#### Justification on Infringement
- Then, the burden shifts to the Crown
- Similar to the [[R v Oakes]] test, requires a legislative objective and minimal infringement
- Must establish a valid legislative objective, which the lower court allowed the "public interest" as one, but the SCC required something more specific
- Found that conservation and managing fishery resources were more valid objectives
- Second step is determining whether the chosen means are consistent with the honour of the Crown, meaning its fiduciary relationship towards Indigenous peoples
- In this case, it means that priority must be given to Indigenous food fishing if conservation goals are to be a valid reason not to make that the highest priority
- In the future, decisions will not be nearly as generous to Indigenous communities
- Court also set out a number of other factors relevant to justification
- There should be as little infringement of the rights as possible
- Fair compensation should be given for any expropriation performed
- Finally, Aboriginal communities should be consulted with respect to measures taken, a requirement further developed in the *Haida Nation* case
### Outcome
- Court never decided whether or not the net length restriction was a justified infringement or not, the charges were withdrawn and a new trial was recommended but never happened
- Government developed an Indigenous fishing strategy, and further challenges developed this aspect of the law as time went on
