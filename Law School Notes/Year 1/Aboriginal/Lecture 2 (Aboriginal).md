- Recall that in [[R v Sparrow]], the Musqueam made an intentional decision to take the issue of the fishing nets to court
- Goal was to get an official interpretation of s. 35 of the Constitution to clarify the meaning behind the recognition and affirmation of those rights
- Big factor in that case was the presence of Chief Justice Dickson, who isn't on the Supreme Court in this case
- Key part of the decision here was the definition of extinguishment, showing that regulation doesn't lead to extinguishment
- Remember one of the key outcomes was holding that Aboriginal rights actually have priority over statutory rights
- Also important part of interpreting s. 35 was doing it in a way that recognizes that Indigenous legal systems exist in parallel to ours
- Right to fish for food and ceremonial purposes was recognized, didn't extend to right to fish for commercial purposes
-  Didn't really spend much time talking about proving the existence of the right, major issue was whether there was a violation of the right and what the extent of the right was
- *Sparrow* test shows us when an infringement of an Aboriginal right is justified, requires a valid legislative objective, which has to be at least somewhat specific
- Objective in this case was conservation, also need to prove that the means are consistent with the Crown's fiduciary duty
- Also mentioned several factors to consider when assessing this, including whether the community has been consulted and whether there was compensation
### [[R v Van der Peet (ATR)]]
- Part of a trilogy of cases, the other two are [[R v Gladstone]] and *NTC Smokehouse*
- All surround West Coast First Nations challenges of the prohibition on commercial fishing when it comes to Aboriginal rights
- Dorothy Van der Peet was caught selling ten salmon for $50 and charged for selling the fish without a license
- NTC Smokehouse Ltd was more of a direct commercial fish selling operation, and *Gladstone* dealt with selling herring roe on kelp
- Van der Peet was from the Sto:lo Nation on the Fraser River, *Gladstone* involved the Heltsuk Nation
- Also some similar cases in Ontario regarding gambling restrictions on Anishinaabe land
- *Gladstone* is also more important because they do establish proof of an Aboriginal right in that case
- However, in the present case, Dorothy was unable to argue there was a violation of her right to fish
- Issue is whether the Sto:lo have an Aboriginal right under s. 35(1) of the 1982 Constitution entitling them to sell fish
- If this right can be established, need to ask if it was extinguished
- Court found that the Sto:lo right to fish does not extend to commercial trade of fish
- Very early case, only the second time the SCC has addressed this
- Recall that the court is trying a purposive approach to s. 35, we need to view this in context of the new deal with Indigenous groups
- Chief Justice LaMere states that the purpose of s. 35(1) is the reconciliation of the preexistence of aboriginal societies with the sovereignty of the Crown
- Basically an explicit recognition of how the Doctrine of Discovery still applies to Canadian law, upholds the assumption of Crown sovereignty
- Cites a lot of academic work on the topic, no Indigenous scholars
- Not really a time when the doctrine was being questioned
- Also, there were two dissenting opinions
### Test
- To be an aboriginal right, an activity has to be an element of a practice, custom, or tradition integral to the distinct culture of the group claiming that right
- Also provides a list of ten different factors to assess, these factors also apply to finding Aboriginal title
	1. What is the Aboriginal perspective on the right?
		- Usually you'll have testimony from Sto:lo elders as well as anthropological evidence
	2. What is the precise nature of the right?
		- Hard to do, these rights are rarely precise
	3. Is it a central or significant part of the distinctive culture?
	4. Needs continuity with the pre-contact society
	5. Approach to finding evidence must be flexible
	6. Also must be specific to each Aboriginal group
	7. Evidence of the right must be independently significant?
	8. Right must be distinctive, but doesn't have to be fully unique
	9. Can't be the product of European influence
	10. Aboriginal title is part of Aboriginal rights
- Recall that this is specific to fish, what happens when it comes to restrictions on other kinds of economic activities
- Usually, the main point of contention is the articulation of what the right involves
- *Sparrow*, and many other cases, all claim to change the landscape of Indigenous-government relations
- This case indicates that *Sparrow* was not as much of a change as it was supposed to be, more like a high water mark for Indigenous rights recognition
- SCC seems to now be retrenching on their previous position
- Now we're seeing another moment like this again with the Cowichan case in BC
- Supreme Court will very rarely speak ill of it's previous decisions, rather they tend to gloss over those decisions
- Suddenly, the test is much more difficult to meet
- Especially, the need for the practice to be integral to pre-contact culture is very hard to prove, and wasn't required in *Sparrow*
- Dissent from McLachlin points out that contact isn't even a consistent standard, it was different for many nations
- Additionally, s. 35(1) also applies to the Metis, a group that didn't even exist prior to European contact
- Why is this requirement needed for other Indigenous groups?
- Court says they need to be flexible when it comes to evidence standards, but in reality that standard is quite high
- Especially difficult to prove continuity in some cases because of the displacement and cultural destruction caused by the Canadian government
- Some of the *Van der Peet* factors don't seem to have much relation to a need for a higher standard of proof, just to limit the number of successful cases
### Application
- Recall that the first step is to find the precise nature of the right
- Court states that the claimed right is the right to exchange fish for money or other goods
- Dorothy claims that the right is to catch sufficient fish to provide for a moderate livelihood
- Court finds that though there is evidence for the Sto:lo fishing for food and ceremonial purposes, but not in exchange for other goods
- Contract in this area would be around the early 1800s
- While the Sto:lo did trade with local forts, this was only episodic and not an engrained part of the culture
- Market for salmon was only created by the presence of Fort Langley in the area and the HBC
- Don't even get to the justification for the infringement, dismissed the case as there
### Dissenting Opinion
- Other dissenting opinion has similar criticisms mainly aimed at accusing the majority of taking a "frozen rights" approach
- Issue should not be whether salmon was being used for trade, but whether it was used for sustenance
- Unfair to expect Indigenous cultures to have a European-style market economy pre-contact
- Real test should be if the salmon fishery was being used to support the community
- Could be defined as the right to obtain from the river or the sea whatever that Aboriginal group traditionally obtained from that source
- Also would eliminate the need to relitigate this for every Aboriginal group wishing to challenge their fishing rights
- This test is also too broad in some ways, for example what is an "integral" practice?
- Argues that the focus should be on historical methods of sustenance for the community
- Ultimately decides that the right should be recognized, is being infringed, and the infringement is not justified
- Keep in mind how this case is different from the more industrial scale in *Gladstone*
