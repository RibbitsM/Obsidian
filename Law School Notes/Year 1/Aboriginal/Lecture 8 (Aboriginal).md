## [[R v Marshall]]
- Case deals with one of the "Peace and Friendship" treaties, different from the numbered treaties but the same principles of interpretation apply
- Motivations here were different, less about gaining land and more about making alliances and managing the war with the French
- Accused in this case was Donald Marshall Jr, charged with three offences for fishing and selling eels
- Marshall is also famous for being wrongfully convicted of murder, and his case was subject to a judicial inquiry
- Major issue with the prosecution was racism against Indigenous people
- This was another test case, probably why they chose Marshall because he was already known in the Canadian justice system
### Treaty
- There was no singular peace and friendship treaty, slightly different versions were signed by different chiefs in the region
- Focus of the agreement was alliance with the British, against the French that the Mi'kmaq had historically worked with
- Treaty also required a promise from the Mi'kmaq to not trade with anyone except for the trade houses established by the British
- This is a restrictive covenant, pretty common in employment law
- However, we need to consider why the Mi'kmaq agreed to this, what did they get in exchange, and does that include a right to trade?
- There was a verbal promise to establish these truck houses or trading posts
- Falls into the broader question of what outside information we can use to determine the terms of the treaty
- Crown argued no, everything is in the text of the treaty, Court of Appeal said the same thing, unless there was ambiguity
### Extrinsic Evidence
- Court held that this kind of evidence was actually necessary, especially for these older treaties
- First, the written text is often incomplete, historical experts attested to this and the trial judge did find the same thing
- However, he found that the subsequent actions meant the treaty right was voided, while looking at the extrinsic evidence
- Recall that these are *sui generis* kinds of agreements, but the rules of contract law do allow for the use of extrinsic evidence
- Court found that it was in the interest of the British to establish these truck houses, both to appease the Mi'kmaq and to make them self-sufficient
- Mi'kmaq only agreed because of a verbal agreement to set up these truck houses
- Also, you can imply terms that are required to make the treaty effective, or are implied by the historical/cultural context
- Makes sense that you need a right to hunt, fish, and gather and bring those resources for trade
- Historians argued that this was unnecessary, it was a given that all people had the right to do this
### Honour of the Crown
- Court points out the importance of the honour of the Crown for treaty interpretation in particular
- All treaty terms setting out rights must be approached with the honour of the Crown in mind
- Assume that the Crown intends to fulfill its promises, no sharp dealing will be sanctioned
- Also need to consider the differences in the reasons why these parties agreed to join these treaties
- Where a treaty was made orally and later written down, we need to consider the oral terms and not focus entirely on the written ones
- Also, try to resolve all ambiguities in the favour of the Indigenous group
- Court found that interpreting the Mi'kmaq's demand for a right to trade as a negative right only is inconsistent with the honour of the Crown
- Need to interpret the treaty in a way that gives meaning and substance to the promises of the Crown
- Interpretation of the lower courts leaves the Mi'kmaq with an empty shell
- Still need to reflect the common intentions of the parties, look at the possible options and pick the one that best achieves reconciliation
- Even once we find that the written treaty is incomplete, we still need to figure out what the treaty rights actually are
### Treaty Rights
- Court talks about the treaty right including an implied right to access to goods used for trade like fish and lumber
- Crown argued that this would lead to excessive exploitation, as this would effectively be a commercial right to trade
- Does not have the same internal limits as the right to food
- Court pointed to a term from the treaty negotiations saying that the truck houses were required to give the Mi'kmaq the "necessaries of life"
- Interpreted this to mean a right to fish enough to give themselves a moderate livelihood, similar to McLachlan's argument in [[R v Gladstone]]
- This argument had been in the jurisprudence, but never adopted by the majority
- Does not mean that this is an unfettered commercial right, it can be regulated by the government to make sure it stays moderate
- These regulations would not be violations of the right because they are part of the right itself
### Infringement of the Right
- Existing system gave the minister absolute discretion over who was granted a fishing license, gave no accommodation to the Mi'kmaq
- Crown did not try to argue justification, because they believed there was no right in the first place
- Court found a *prima facie* infringement, pointed to a previous case that said absolute discretion is never a justified limit
- Because of this, Marshall was acquitted
### Justification
- Still good to think about whether this would actually be justified
- What objectives are likely to be put forward, public safety and conservation are the common ones
- Assuming conservation was the objective, are the existing limits consistent with the honour of the Crown?
- Recall that [[R v Sparrow]] held that priority should be given to the rights holder, need to consider that this is a commercial right
- Still has some internal limits, best comparison is probably the right recognized in *Gladstone*
- Likely that the other objectives like economic and regional fairness will be raised here
- Actually pretty common that the Crown does not provide justification for infringement, mainly because of the focus on the duty to consult
- In this case, consultation was not the appropriate remedy, but in most cases where the infringement was a regulatory decision, that would be what happens
## Marshall #2
- Pretty unique decision in the sense that it was clarifying an earlier ruling
- As soon as the decision was made, Indigenous fishers began using this new right to fish for lobster, which was much more lucrative than eel
- Non-Indigenous fishermen became extremely upset about this, violence erupted with boats and churches being built
- At the time the DFO did not really have any sort of response to the ruling, they were caught off guard by the SCC ruling
- Similar to a lot of what we're seeing now with the Cowichan decision
- Intervenor in the initial case applied for a rehearing and asked for a stay of judgement
- Interveners are very common in SCC cases, a lot of the time the parties won't be able to get the full context of the case
- Intervenor was called the West Nova Fisherman's Coalition
- Rehearing was two months after the original decision, motion for a stay of judgement was dismissed
- Did not need to do anything else, but the SCC took the time to clarify the earlier ruling, whole discussion is *obiter*
### Clarifications
- Couple things that seem to be important clarifications
- First, the ruling did not deal with treaty rights to logging, minerals, oil, or gas deposits
- Issues were narrow and mostly focused on hunting and fishing
- Did not confer a right to a separate commercial fishery, said treaty rights are different from Aboriginal rights which focus on continuous practice
- Court also confirmed the minister's authority to regulate the fishery for other non-conservation objectives like participation in the fisher by non-Indigenous groups
- Regulated treaty right, not an exclusive unlimited fishery
- First Nations leaders saw this as a retreat from the ruling due to the political backlash
### Recent Developments
- Still a bunch of crucial things that were unresolved, for example the government has been negotiating with the First Nations on how to regulate these fisheries for years
- Enacted new policy to give licenses to Mi'kmaq fishers but never came to a decision on how to regulate the moderate livelihood fishery
- Some groups made their own policy and started fishing using that right, led to the issue flaring up again
- Most interesting development is that over time, the fishery industry in the Maritimes has shifted from focusing on lobsters to focusing on elvers, which are baby eels
- Issue of whether this is part of the eel fishery is a big issue, because the position of non-Indigenous fishermen for a while has been that the Mi'kmaq right was limited to the eel fishery
- Recently, one of the agreements between the Crown and a local Indigenous group was challenged by a non-Indigenous fishing group
- Overall, this case is mostly important for the moderate fishing right, but we can see how difficult implementing that has been
- Main problem with the regulation is that the government can't just regulate it however they want, they need to come to an agreement with the Mi'kmaq
- What is a moderate livelihood is a big issue, the Maritimes has been economically depressed since the cod fishery imploded
- Government has not exactly been handling this well, once they allocated most of the elver fishery to Mi'kmaq fishers but didn't give compensation to non-Indigenous fishers
### Exam Review
- Sort your CAN by the kind of right, like aboriginal right, metis right, treaty right, etc
- Then talk about how to find limits for these rights, justification for infringement, and have a section on duty to consult
