### Question
- BC granted a mining permit to a coal corp, probably duty to consult delegated thru a third party
- Mining permit was granted on the sacred hunting grounds of the Salmulth First Nation in Red Elk Valley North
- Mainly hunt for white tailed deer, this practice dates back as afar as living memory allows, archaeological evidence suggests the SFN have lived in Red Elk Valley for 8k years
- In November 2001, Red Elk Valley North was recgonized as the traditional trapping territory of a different nation who mainly trap lynx, and don't hunt white tailed deer
- This other nation, the Nanahmuth, is a neighbour and historical ally of the SFN, main distinction is that the SFN mostly hunt and the NFN mostly trap
- Historical evidence shows that the two tribes have co-existed here since pre-contact and do not interfere with each other mainly because of these different modes of gathering food
- Initially, the SFN hunted the Red Elk, and the white tailed deer were only introduced post-contact by Europeans
- Red elk population was decimated by diseases brought by the deer
- Also, they were negatively affected by coal mining operations in the area, and now only exist as one small herd
- White tailed deer used to live in all parts of Red Elk Valley, now they mostly live in Red Elk Valley North where the NFN live, not in Red Elk Valley South where the SFN live
- Both nations were devastated by smallpox but the NFN had it much worse, entered an agreement in 1849 where the SFN would protect the NFN and the NFN would let them hunt in their territory
- Done through a potlatch, the SFN and NFN have been honouring this reciprocal agreement since the 1800s
- Only changed recently as the NFN support the mining permit due to the economic benefits it brings
- Province consulted with the NFN before issuing the permit and moved the mine location
- Found that the mine would not affect NFN trapping practices, but did not consider the effect on SFN hunting
- NFN are sympathetic but don't want to push the issue at the risk of losing their benefits from the mine
- BC also consulted with the SFN, but they only gave them notification of the permit and allowed them to make a written statement
- Province said they would take measures to protect the deer population by increasing wolf hunting permits
- However, the wolf is a sacred animal to the SFN
- Coal corp also offered jobs for SFN members at the mine but nobody has really taken this offer
- First mine is now being dug, SFN came to your firm asking for a memo discussing the s. 35(1) issues here including remedies
- If you need information, tell us what that information is and why it would be relevant
### Planning
- Primary issue is with the right to hunt in Red Elk Valley North, and whether or not the Crown met the duty to consult
- To figure this out, need to do a full *Sparrow* test with a full D2C analysis in the middle
- Also might need to do an aboriginal title claim within the D2C analysis?
	- Unlikely, the SFN can't make a claim to the northern area because they already recognize NFN sovereignty there
- No Metis issues here, and also no treaty rights?
	- Only exception is the HTA, but that doesn't involve the SFN
- For remedies, preferable option for the SFN would probably be to have the mining stopped for now and get in on the HTA
- SFN have two main goals with litigation, to stop the mining development and to protect the deer
	- A *Sparrow* and D2C analysis should be enough for this
### Answer
- Best option for the SFN is to pursue an aboriginal rights claim
- Can't claim aboriginal title because it's not their land, they use it as their hunting grounds but only as part of their agreement with the NFN
- NFN may have an aboriginal title claim to the north, but not the SFN
- The SFN also can't make a claim based on the HTA because they were not a party to that agreement
- Therefore, their only basis is for an aboriginal rights claim
- First step to an aboriginal rights claim is to prove that the right exists in the first place
- Need to use the *Van der Peet* test as it was modified by *Sappier* and *Gray*
- Key problem will be with characterizing the right
	- Crown will likely argue that the right is limited to hunting White Tail Deer only
	- This is a problem because this is not a practice with continuity to the pre-contact period, the deer were introduces by Europeans
- Overall, SFN can probably successfully argue that their right to hunt applies to elk, deer and other cervids
	- Only cases we have where an aboriginal right is limited to a specific species are fishing cases
	- However, this is likely because of the fact that the fishing industry relies so heavily on distinct fisheries to operate
- Can point to *Powley* as an example, even though they were only hunting moose, they successfully argued that the Metis in the area had broader hunting practices
	- SFN can do the same by drawing comparisons between their pre-contact practice of hunting Red Elk and their modern practice of hunting deer
- Most likely characterization of the right would be the right to hunt for food (maybe cervid-specific) in the Red Elk Valley
	- Fraser does talk about how the SFN relied "primarily" on elk so it's definitely possible this is broader
- If this argument is accepted, also proves continuity
- Distinctive from the NFN based on their food collecting practices, so that element is met assuming there's no other similar nations nearby
- Influenced by European contact, but only because their previous practice was rendered impossible
- Also does not have to be a defining feature of the culture, but it's arguable that this would be met anyway
- Overall, it seems like the SFN would be able to establish an Aboriginal right to hunt all across the Red Elk Valley
- Next step is to let the Crown attempt to prove if the right has been extinguished
- Only potential piece of evidence here is the 2001 agreement, but the right needs to be extinguished before 1982
	- Can probably just skip this step
- Next step is to examine whether or not the construction of a mine in Red Elk Valley North would infringe this right
	- History of the White Tail Deer being driven out of Red Elk Valley South by mining operations is good evidence here
	- Should be able to argue that allowing this mining operation would impose an undue hardship by depriving the SFN of their main prey
- Probably no infringement of preferred means, main one would be undue hardship
- Honour of the Crown is not engaged as a fiduciary duty because there's no interest, so that's not relevant either
- Either way, there will be an infringement here
- Need to move to the justification phase
- Government needs to provide a valid objective for the decision to grant the mining permit
	- Not clear what the objective will be, but it's probably something like the need to make money?
	- It's not for energy, if you're digging up coal in BC the only thing you can do with it is sell it to China lol
		- BC uses hydro power, and using coal power is now really frowned upon in Canada
	- Probably need more information here on why we're mining coal in the big 21
- Entirely possible that the test would fail right here, but for the sake of practice we'll keep going
- Next, we need to consider whether the infringement is connected to this objective, and whether it's consistent with the honour of the Crown
- Infringement is definitely not minimal, the govt didn't really consider the SFN when making the decision and have not done much to remedy that
- No compensation but there was probably none available anyway
- No issues with prioritizing rights here
- The objective is also pretty vague, surely they could have found somewhere else to mine coal
- Final thing to consider is whether the SFN were consulted
- For this, need to consider the duty to consult from *Haida*
- We know the Crown had knowledge that the SFN had some attachment to the region since they consulted with them
- Issuing the mining permit definitely qualifies as potentially adversely affecting the SFN's rights
- Main issue is what the scope of the duty which is based on the strength of the claim and the seriousness of the impact
- We know it's not a particularly strong claim because it's not a settled interest like a treaty or title claim, just an aboriginal right
- Closest comparison is to *Clyde River*, which also dealt with interference with a hunting right due to industrial action harming their preferred prey
- They got deep consultation in that case, but that was mainly due to the conduct of the government and the *NEB*
- In this case, BC at least considered the SFN
- Considering *Clyde River*, the fact that the SFN have a longstanding right to hunt in Red Elk Valley North based on their potlatch with the NFN, and the fact that there is a real chance this could remove the SFN's main food source, seems to be at least a moderate duty required
- Remember that we know that mining in the south already forced the SFN to go north to hunt, good chance it will happen again
- Actions of BC matched up with the lowest level of consultation, but it seems pretty clear here that the SFN need at least moderate consultation
	- Also did not do much to help, the only things they offered were shit the SFN actively don't want like more jobs and wolf hunts
- Seems like there's enough to establish a breach of the duty to consult here
- By extension, that also shows that the infringement on SFN's hunting rights by issuing the mining license was probably unjustified
- In terms of remedies, the simplest outcome is that the SFN should get an injunction preventing the mine from being dug until the mining company consults with the SFN regarding the White Tail Deer
	- This would be the remedy for the breach of D2C
- In terms of a remedy for the infringement, it would make sense for the SFN to be let into negotiations to have themselves added to the HTA to protect their rights in the future
### Class Answer
- When it comes to characterizing the right, starting point should be what the nation believes the right should be, then think about what the Crown will argue
- Need to use terminology like "frozen rights approach" in your arguments
- Can also use the honour of the Crown as a reason for remedying anything that seems "unfair" in the situation
