## [[Haida Nation v BC (Minister of Forests)]]
- Original case for the duty to consult and accommodate, although all subsequent cases have seemed to focus overwhelmingly on the consulting part
- Duty was first mentioned in [[R v Sparrow]], it was one of the factors showing whether an infringement was justified
- [[R v Gladstone]] also made reference to the need for consultation and compensation as a result of the infringement found in that case
- *Delgamuukw* was an intervening case on Aboriginal title, where it was established that the strength of the duty is proportional to the strength of the claim and seriousness of the breach
- Some violations of a potential right only need notice, others may need full consent
- The present case happened in 2004, 14 years after *Sparrow* and 8 after the VdP trilogy
- Everything comes after this case focused mostly on the duty to consult, this was also a trilogy of cases, other two are *Taku River* and *Mikisew Cree Nation*, we'll read the latter in March
- Ongoing theme is that the court wants the government and nations to negotiate, not deciding everything in court
- Haven't been very many modern treaties, or out-of-court agreements on Aboriginal title
- In 2004, Haida title was still pending, this case focused on whether the government can extract resources from Haida Gwaii without consultation in light of this pending title
### Facts
- In BC, forestry is mostly done on Crown land, which is parcelled out to companies through instruments called Tree Farm Licenses or TFLs
- These are long term, exclusive, and very valuable licenses with accompanying restrictions
- Don't give the right to cut on their own, but are an important step
- Every so often, these TFLs have to be renewed, the Haida were arguing that they should not be renewed while the Haida had a pending claim to the land
### Analysis
- Argument from the government is that the Haida should seek an interlocutory injunction as the remedy to stop the cutting from happening
- Couple problems with this, one is that it's an all or nothing solution, other main problem is that it discourages further negotiations
- Key part of this case is the honour of the Crown, which is engaged in all negotiations with Indigenous communities
- However, not all of these cases also require the Crown to act as a fiduciary
- Only applies where the Crown is considering action against particular interests, for example the case where the Shaughnessy golf course was being built on Musqueam land (Guerin)
- In that case, the Crown was acting on a specific Indigenous interest (their reserve land), so the fiduciary duty of the Crown was engaged
- Here, the court found that there was no specific interest at risk so the fiduciary duty doesn't manifest, but the honour of the Crown does
#### Duty to Consult
- Crown can't make the excuse that no rights have been proven or no treaty has been signed as a justification for assuming no rights exist
- Duty is triggered when the Crown has real or constructive knowledge of a potential Aboriginal right or title claim, and they were considering action that could affect that
- Recall that in *Sparrow*, the court made reference to requiring consultation before the duty was established
- Pretty much all the arguments the Crown raised regarding why the honour of the Crown didn't apply were rejected, seems that it applies to these circumstances
- Duty to consult is also not a requirement to make a change, as long as the Indigenous community has been meaningfully consulted, that meets the duty
- Court does its best to give flexibility to the parties, trying not to prescribe what exactly is required
- When we get to established aboriginal title, the right is much stronger as the right comes with the land specifically
- Government cannot delegate the honour of the Crown and the accompanying duty, but often the processes of consultation are handled mainly by the resource industry
- Can be carried out by industry and lower tribunals, but only the government is bound
- Some of those regulatory bodies can be considered part of the Crown, decided in *Clyde River*
#### Scope of Duty
- Can apply to a number of actions, has previously applied to oil drilling, the building of winter roads, and in this case to logging operations
- On the lower end of the scale, duty only requires notice and the discussion of any issues
- On the higher end, formal participation in the decision-making process needs to be given to Indigenous groups, and the govt needs to have written evidence of how Indigenous interests were considered
- Recall that the scope of the duty depends on the severity of the impact and strength of the claim
### Companion Cases
- Both cases regarded roads being built through Indigenous territory
- In *Taku River*, the government was held to have constructive knowledge of a strong claim to aboriginal title
- However, the road did not have much of an adverse impact, but could potentially harm certain important species living in the area
- Duty was definitely engaged enough to require more than the minimum consultation
- Does require some amount of meaningful consultation here, impact was not enough to require deep consultation and full accommodation
- Eventually ruled that the environmental assessment process they did with the road that consulted with Aboriginal groups in the area met the duty to consult
- Duty applied here, but it had already been met by the government
- Keep in mind that BC especially has a lot of conflicting claims to Aboriginal title, the road went through land claimed by several First Nations groups
- Hadn't accommodated all of them, and the Taku River Nation specifically was not happy
- As for *Mikisew Cree* we will address it more later since it's the cornerstone treaty rights case
- Also about a winter road, but through traditional hunting grounds that were adjacent to their Treaty 8 territory
- Under the *Natural Resources Transfer Agreement*, prairie province First Nations have hunting rights throughout the entire province, just not for commercial regions
- Initially planned to put the road through the reserve, changed it to go around and the Cree contested it anyway
- Court held that the duty was triggered, but the scope of the duty was pretty small
- Impact was overall not super significant, but the duty was still not met
- Remedy in this case was to allow the appeal and quash the Ministers approval for the road so it could be sent back for consultation
