- Which ways did the IAP settlement help achieve reconciliation, and which ways did it fall short?

The goals of the IAP settlement were noble, and provided a strong model for how the Canadian legal system can work towards reconciling the harms it caused in the past. However, these efforts were often hampered by the flaws in the evidence process and the actions of the legal profession as a whole. 

According to the decision in *Fontaine v Canada*, the purpose of the Independent Assessment Process (IAP) was to minimize the harm to claimants created by the adversarial system by providing a more inquisitorial environment where claimants are not questioned directly by opposing counsel. In this way, the courts sought to balance the comfort of the claimants against the need to test their claims in court. However, this was marred largely by the actions of the plaintiff-side counsel, as well as the actions of the IAP staff themselves. An excellent example of the kind of abuse this created was the case of David Blott, an Alberta lawyer whose abuse of the IAP system was detailed in *R v Fontaine*. In short, Blott used the IAP as a money-making scheme by partnering with a form-filling organization that used standardized templates to fill in IAP claim forms, and referred their clients to Blott's firm. Then, the claimants would be assigned contract lawyers who knew little of their true experience, and abused the court processes intended to protect claimants like the principle of progressive disclosure to cover up their negligence in filling out the claims. This is a clear example of how the entrepreneurial nature of the legal profession can raise financial incentives over the need to actually serve clients, although Blott was eventually condemned by the Law Society of Alberta. This issue was further explored by Farrow, who noted the need to reform the principles of the legal profession to put more emphasis on the need for lawyers to take more care in balancing the interests of the public with the interests of their clients, and to take a more trauma-informed approach to dealing with clients who have gone through such devastating experiences as the abuse experienced in residential schools. 

In summary, this case shows how the legal profession failed to recognize the goals of healing and reconciliation underlying the IAP and the Settlement Agreement as a whole, instead acting on self-serving impulses that ended up undermining the entire process. 

This is not to say that the IAP was innocent in all this, as they certainly made their own errors. In fact, the purpose behind the *Fontaine* case was not just to address the actions of Blott and his associates, but also to point out the deficiencies of the Settlement Agreement itself. First of all, an issue pointed out by many claimants was that Canada was a party to the agreement and had a seat on the National Administration Council (NAC) which oversaw the settlement as a whole, despite being a defendant in the case, which was specifically pointed out as an issue in *Baxter v Canada (Attorney General)*. Additionally, evidence for the IAP process was reliant on document disclosure from government agencies and church organizations, which were often hesitant and unwilling to provide the appropriate documents needed to properly hear claims. Finally, the IAP also failed to disclose to claimants that their testimony would be destroyed after 15 years if it was not submitted to the Truth and Reconciliation Committee, an important process in preserving the truth of Canada's ongoing colonial legacy for future generations.

Overall, the IAP did succeed in the sense that it provided an opportunity for claimants to have their stories heard and receive compensation without being subjected to the harms of the adversarial system. However, the execution of this goal was consistently hampered by the actions of the people responsible for carrying it out, motivated by misplaced priorities in the legal profession and an unwillingness of the government to accept the consequences of its actions.
