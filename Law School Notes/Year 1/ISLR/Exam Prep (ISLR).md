## READ THE QUESTIONS
- Every exam is also a reading comprehension test, make sure you're actually doing what you're being asked to do
- Don't bother trying to go above and beyond, focus on doing what the exam asks for to the best of your ability
- Focus on the quantity of requirements, and stick to them
- If they say give two examples, give two examples
- Underline the key verbs in the question, and make sure your answer matches the tasks
- Make sure your answer also fits the scope of the question, is it asking about a specific time period or jurisdiction?
- Also might be asking about specific parts of the course
- Don't write down everything, avoid stream of consciousness writing and focus on an essay-style response tailored to the question
- Especially the long answer questions will be multiple-part, make sure you're hitting the requirements for every action verb in the question
- Look to the other parts to see what's being asked of you
- Goal is to test your understanding, use course vocabulary and provide definitions of contested terms like settler colonialism
- Make sure to make specific references to readings and lectures
- Our exam is going to be reflection free, so do it impartial and research focused, not personal
- If you removed your opinions from the answer, would your response still be solid?
### Exam Prep
- Look at each module and take notes on each reading, focusing on the arguments and extract some key quotes related to those arguments
- Minimize the amount of time you spend searching and reading
- Make sure whatever you make for your exam prep is actually usable in the exam context
- Try making a concept sheet that has definitions and references for a bunch of key concepts
- Can also divide your notes by theme instead of by module
- For example, the residential schools week was very closely related to the week on the foster care system
- Exam is trying to test your ability to make connections, so try to put some work into doing that ahead of time
- Don't really need to use FIRAC, it's not that kind of exam
- Start with a high-level prep doc like a mind map, and then move on to writing more detailed materials
- Might be useful to read all the questions first and outline them, then move on to the actual writing process
- You gotta know when to cut it off and move on, it's better to have three full mediocre answers than two really good ones and a bad one
- Focus on having a clear and easily readable structure, professor is going to be reading 50 of these really fast
- Include topic sentences that explicitly state your answer
- Show where you're getting your knowledge from, don't need to cite anything but include the name of the author or book
- "As X said in Y text, blah blah blah"
