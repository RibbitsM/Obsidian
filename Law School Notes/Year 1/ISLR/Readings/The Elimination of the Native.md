- Article by Patrick Wolfe in the Journal of Genocide Research
- Focuses on exploring the relationship between genocide and settler colonialism
- Land is necessary for life, and attempts to seize land can thus become genocidal
- Settler colonialism relies on a "logic of elimination" which may lead to genocide, but not always
- Both settler colonialism and genocide often operate using the concept of race
- Example of how ancestry in America was determined for indigenous vs black people
- Black people had the "one drop" rule, where no matter how distantly related, and relative of a Black person was Black
- For indigenous peoples, there were more complicated blood quantum rules that excluded half-indigenous children from the category
- Purpose of the American system was to create more slaves and less indigenous people, and these policies reflected this
- Primary motive for elimination under settler colonialism is for access to territory
- Recall that settler colonialism does not destroy for the sake of destruction, it eliminates to replace what existed there
- While many settler-colonial societies like Canada and Australia sought to eliminate indigeneity in practice, they still adopt many indigenous symbols
- Author compares this approach to how Zionism implements settler colonialism
- While many places in Australia and North America are having their indigenous names restored, impossible to imagine any region of Israel being restored to its original Arabic name
- Recognition that the settlers ontologically **own** the land, while in Canada we merely took it because we though we would use it better
- However, in every settler colonial context, the eliminated group will still exert some influence on the settler state
### [[Doctrine of Discovery]]
- In many early settler-colonial efforts, rights were given to indigenous peoples depending on the intensity of competition
- In Australia, Britain was the only colonizer and were able to restrict indigenous peoples completely
- However, in Canada there had to be management of alliances with both British and French efforts
- Doctrine of discovery manifested differently in each of these scenarios
- Native sovereignty may or may not be recognized, but the Europeans were always understood as holding dominion over the land
- Doctrine of discovery mainly dealt with the issue of competing land claims between European nations
- Frenzy to seize native land meant that the nominal principles of "buying land" from indigenous groups were rarely followed
- Before land was ever sold, the state had already established legal authority considering it cannot be sold to anyone else
- Land is already theirs, it just has to be transferred into their possession
- From the moment of discovery, the violent seizure of land becomes an inevitability
- But during that period between discovery and seizure, indigenous land rights are briefly acknowledged
### Genocide and Modernity
- Colonialism is a modern invention, it cannot function without the massive state structures that drive it
- Global capitalism also played a key role by creating the incentive for mass extraction industries like cotton and fur
- Industrial revolution demanded the raw material and labour generated by colonialism to function
- Recall that the categories of "Hutu" and "Tutsi" were products of Belgian colonialism in Rwanda
- Settler colonialism created modernity, but this doesn't explain its intense need for more land
- Agriculture explains this partially, as extractive industries usually don't require permanent settlement
- Also has the side effect of eliminating Indigenous ways of life
- While many Indigenous groups did practice agriculture, they are often thought of as nomadic
- On the other hand, the European farmers are firmly rooted to the land that they cultivate
- If the natives are already nomadic, that means they won't mind moving on right?
- Also demonstrates how settler colonialism uniquely targets Indigenous peoples, not just non-European social practices
- Cultural tolerance is a myth, the Cherokee adopted agriculture, owned slaves, and created their own constitution
- Yet, they were violently driven off their land and onto the Trail of Tears
- However, not all Indigenous peoples were treated this way, as long as they abandoned their Indigenous identity they would be allowed to stay
- While Choctaw homesteaders were allowed to remain, the collective land ownership of the Cherokee was intolerable
- Even when the goal wasn't to physically eliminate the Indigenous population, they still had to go
- Turning Indigenous peoples into settlers achieved this goal
- Genocide isn't just elimination of a group of people, but can also be the elimination of the group identity while the people live on
### The Frontier
- Once there were no longer unoccupied regions to relocate indigenous peoples to, elimination truly began
- In most settler-colonial societies, assimilation only really ramped up when relocation was no longer an option
- Intensive effort began to break down Indigenous groups into individuals and absorb them into America
- Same process occurred with Indigenous land, which was parcelled out individually to their Indigenous residents
- Majority of this land would ultimately be purchased by white people
- The policy of "blood quantum" statistically eliminated Indigenous children over time
- Similar to the [[Settler Colonialism in Canada|Canadian approach]]
- The connection between the frontier and genocide can still be seen in the European context
- For example, Israel uses a variety of strategies like pushing Indigenous inhabitants outside of the country entirely
- Also only allows people to marry out of Israel, never to marry in
- Similar to the blood quantum strategy
- Preserves ethnic unity through the Law of Return
- These practices are just as eliminatory as straightforward killing
- Recall that one of the potential elements of genocide is "measures intended to prevent births within the group"
- This is a practice carried out by pretty much every settler colonial state
### Logic of Elimination
- We still use this term so as to keep settler colonialism and genocide distinct from each other
- No use of applying settler colonialism to the Holocaust or Cambodia
- We also don't want to not use the term genocide, calling it "cultural genocide" or  "indigenocide" just weakens the term
- The term "logic of elimination" perfectly explains how settler colonialism seeks to obtain and maintain occupied territory
- Because that territory is occupied, its occupants will necessarily be eliminated
- Larger category than genocide, applies to situations where multiple groups occupy that region, or simply part of a larger group
- Often, this process of elimination is not physical killing
- Makes it look like an "invasion" or a "war" while the reality is that settler colonialism is a structure
- Thinking of it otherwise leads us into the trap of assuming that since the killing is over, settler colonialism is a thing of the past
