- Full title is The Duty to Learn: Taking Account of Indigenous Legal Orders in Practice
- Crown has officially recognized that organized society existed in Canada prior to colonization
- By extension, this signifies the existence of a pre-existing indigenous legal order
- Canada is a multi-jural nation, just not all the legal orders are written out as statutes or common law
- Key question is how can we make space for these legal orders in Canadian law
- Most lawyers and judges in Canada are mostly unaware of indigenous culture and language
- What little knowledge they have is often from Eurocentric depictions of indigenous culture
- The lack of indigenous representation in law makes incorporating indigenous legal principles into law that much more difficult
- Additionally, we run the risk of the majority demographic misinterpreting these principles
- However, we cannot just ignore the existence of these indigenous legal orders, especially in the ongoing context of reconciliation
- In order to achieve integration, the lawyers judges and lawmakers in question need to learn what these indigenous legal orders are
- Achieved through a three step process:
	1. Recognize the true nature and scope of the challenge
	2. Recognize the preconceptions and limitation hampering our current perspective
	3. Recognize the need for humility, respect, and receptivity in our approach to indigenous law
### The Necessity
- Several examples that show the legal precedent for the need to incorporate indigenous perspectives
- Judges have acknowledged the need to respect indigenous legal traditions as part of reconciliation, as well as a part of common law considering the long history of the law
- While determining the source of native title rights in Canada, the Supreme Court determined that it was grounded in both common law and indigenous law practices
- Reconciliation of the prior occupation of North America requires accounting for both the common law and indigenous perspectives on law
- If at the time of sovereignty a society had laws relating to land use, those laws should be used as part of a claim to native title
- Not just indigenous perspectives on property law, but also family, criminal, and civil law among others
- The Crown mandates the duty to approach questions of interpretations generously, as well as the duties to consult and accommodate
- As well, all members of the legal profession should be ready to learn in order to successfully do the work of consulting and accommodating indigenous perspectives
### The Challenge
- Hardest part is accounting for the wide variety of indigenous legal traditions without oversimplifying the circumstances of a single group
- Our task isn't just to understand indigenous legal systems, but to understand them **in context**
- Especially important as many of these laws are closely related to social narratives
- Laws are explained through stories that explain the reasoning behind a certain rule
- Law is not only set by storytelling, but also modified over time by the same force
- Not all the information required to understand a story will be within the story itself, there is an assumption of a high level of contextual knowledge
### The Obstacles
- Two main obstacles: severance and distortion
- For severance, there are many ways in which indigenous law is separated from its context
- First is language, the same meaning is rarely approximated by retelling a story in English or French
- Some words in indigenous languages that are key to understanding their legal orders simply do not translate cleanly
- The second element is the existence of these stories textually
- These are oral traditions, meant to be passed down to specific people in specific ways
- However, our legal system inevitably requires translation into text, a form they were never intended to exist in
- This ties in to the broader severance of laws from their original rules of procedure
- We may have the laws that tell us what to do, but we are missing the "how"
- Finally, we must consider the overall severance of these principles from their original setting and context, especially the original geography they were intended for
- This process also risks ignoring the constantly evolving nature of indigenous culture, which is not the same as it was before colonization
- The second issue is the process of distortion
- If we try to look into the indigenous perspective on almost any issue, we'll inevitably run into a number of non-indigenous sources
- Research can be distorted by relying on non-indigenous sources, or even just engaging with indigenous legal systems as a non-indigenous person
- We should always be considering the perspective of these authors and seeking to understand their biases and reasons for producing their work
- Internal distortion also occurs from our previous programming, our education as Canadian lawyers
- Recognizing and addressing this internal distortion is likely the hardest challenge to overcome, but also the most important
- Our existing biases of what "law" even is may doom our desire to understand indigenous legal orders before we even begin
### The Beginning
- On the other hand, this is something that is entirely within our control
- We must not forget that what we're really doing is not making space in our current legal landscape for new ideas, but rather finding space for Canadian law within this pre-existing framework
- We need to be receptive to these new ideas, as well as respectful for their histories
- Never forget the context, it is the most important part of understanding
- Colonial nations often harbour an inherent desire to forget their pasts, which is an urge we should try our best to resist
- This is a goal that operates on a generational level, hence the targeting of law schools and legal education
