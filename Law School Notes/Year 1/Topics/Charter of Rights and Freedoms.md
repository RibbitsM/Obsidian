- Part of the 1982 Constitution Act
- Came into force on April 17, 1982
- Has several provisions key to the development of Canadian Criminal Law
### Fundamental Freedoms
- Guarantees fundamental freedoms such as freedom of religion, freedom of thought/belief and expression, freedom of peaceful assembly and freedom of association
### Legal Rights
- Grants legal rights as well
- All persons have the right to not be deprived of life, liberty, and security except in accordance with principles of justice
- Also includes right against unreasonable search and seizure and right to not be arbitrarily detained/deported
- On arrest, all have the right to be informed why, to retain counsel without delay and determine the validity of the detention via *habeas corpus*
- People also have several rights when charged with an offence
- They have the right to be informed promptly of the specific offence and to be tried reasonably soon
- They cannot be compelled to testify against someone, and are presumed innocent until proven guilty
- They cannot be denied reasonable bail without cause 
- They have a right to a trial by jury if an offence has a maximum punishment of five years or more in jail, except if it is occurring before a military tribunal
- They cannot be found guilty *ex post facto* and cannot be tried again for the same act if acquitted or found guilty and punished
- Also if they were found guilty and the punishment was varied between the act and the time of sentencing, they receive the lighter punishment
- Moving on, all people have immunity from cruel and unusual punishment
- Witnesses have the right to provide incriminating evidence in testimony without having that evidence used against them except in the case of perjury or contradictory evidence proceedings
- If a party does not speak the language of the proceedings or is deaf, they have the right to an interpreter
### Equality Rights
- As for equality rights, all individuals are considered equal before and under the law
- These individuals are entitled to equal protection and benefit from law regardless of race, ethnic origin, religion, etc.
### Enforcement
- If the rights ensured by the Charter are infringed, an individual can seek an appropriate remedy from any competent court
- If said court decides that evidence was obtained for these proceedings in a manner inconsistent with the Charter, it will be excluded
### Application of Charter
- The Charter applies to the government of Canada in all matters within its authority, including in the Territories
- Also applies to the governments of all the provinces
