- Vertical movement of water is controlled by density
- Density is determined by salinity, temperature, and to an extent pressure
- Dense water sinks
- Density profiles are a graph of the density of water as depth increases
- The "stability" of a depth profile is dependent on how likely it is to change
- Stratified water is less likely to change, if there is a clear divide between layers it is unlikely to fluctuate
- At the surface you have the mixed layer of the ocean that is affected by wind, and then there is the pycnocline that divides that layer from the deep ocean
- Pycno means density, and cline mean rapid change
- The density changes rapidly in a short period when moving from mixed water to deep ocean
- Temperature decreases with depth and density
- Decline is sharper in lower latitudes where the surface is warmer
- Also fluctuates with season in mid latitudes
- Stability depends on how different the top and bottom layers are, and how quickly the density changes between layers
- Therefore the water table is most stable in warmer climates and least stable in cold ones
- The more evenly distributed the heat is in the mixed layer, the sharper the pycnocline
- Even though summer creates the most contrast between top and bottom, it doesn't create as steep of a gradient if there's not wind to mix the top layer

Salinity

- Salinity is affected by precipitation/evaporation, river input, mixing, and ice formation/melt
- Ice forming increases salinity, ice melting decreases it
- Ice is made of fresh water only
- High rainfall/low evaporation, high river input, low mixing, and low ice formation/melt all create fresher water
- Generally when combining salinity and temperature, water columns are least stable in the winter, when the mixing layer is deeper and temperature is more uniform
- They are most stable in the summer, when the surface is in stark contrast to the deep water

Deep Water Formation

- Deep water is formed in areas where the water is very cold and salty
- This is mainly in the North Atlantic, and the Weddell Sea off the coast of Antarctica
- Deep water flows from the North Atlantic underneath the gulf stream, in the opposite direction
- Gulf stream brings warm water to the north, where it sinks and becomes part of the deep ocean where it flows back towards the equator
- In the Antarctic, ice formation and convection creates very cold and salty water
- The AADW (antarctic deep water) is BELOW the NADW (north atlantic deep water)
- The AADW flows to all three ocean basins, the NADW only goes to the Atlantic
- Deep water gets back to the surface via mixing by gyres, and by areas of upwelling
- Where we see our Ekman divergence, that's where deep water returns to the surface
- Mainly in the southern ocean
- The NADW actually joins AADW in the southern hemisphere
- The AMOC is the atlantic meridional overturning current
- This consists of the cycle between the north and south pole in the atlantic
- The spot underneath Greenland where the AMOC sinks is resistant to global warming because of the circulation
- However the AMOC has been slowing down as the north atlantic gets less salty
- One parcel of water takes about 1000 years to take one cycle from the bottom of greenland all the way to the pacific and back
- Deep ocean circulation transfers heat on a much larger timescale than surface transport
