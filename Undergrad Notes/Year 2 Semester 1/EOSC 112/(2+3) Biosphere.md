- Primary productivity is the rate at which CO2 is turned into organic biomass
- Gross primary production is the rate of CO2 uptake by primary producers
- However, even autotrophs require chemical energy, not all of the energy comes from sunlight
- So a small amount of energy is lost during photosynthesis
- Net primary production is gross primary production minus the rate of respiration of primary producers
- Usually respiration takes up less than 10% of gross primary production

Oceanic Biosphere

- In the oceanic biosphere we have two different forms of primary production
- Regenerated Primary Production is where phytoplankton takes nutrients from the water and energy from the sun to produce energy
- Phytoplankton are eaten by herbivores, who are eaten by carnivores
- Fecal matter and dead bodies are recycled by decomposers and turned back into nutrients for phytoplankton
- This is called regenerated primary production because the nutrients are recycled back into the ecosystem, and happens only in the euphotic zone
- However, some fecal matter and bodies sink below the thermocline and cannot be recycled back
- This is called sinking detritus, and transfers carbon from the euphotic zone to the deep waters
- Deep ocean organisms use this detritus and turn it into CO2 and more nutrients
- No photosynthesis in the deep ocean, detritus is main energy source
- Some of these deep nutrients are brought back to the euphotic zone by upwelling, returns the nutrients lost by detritus
- These are new nutrients, and fuel New Primary Production
- Therefore total primary production is new plus regenerated production
- This is a whole new pathway for carbon, where the biological pump moves carbon from the atmosphere to the deep ocean
- We call this export production, the nutrients that escape from the first production cycle are used in the second cycle
- Export production is equal to new production when averaged over time
- The euphotic zone is the top 200 m of the ocean and is where all primary production occurs
- Ocean productivity is highest in areas of upwelling, where new primary production is highest
- Patterns are also seasonal, productivity is highest in spring and end of summer
- This is because of changes in ocean stratification
- As ocean stratification increases when it's warm, less mixing is occurring and fewer nutrients are upwelling
- At the end of the summer, stratification decreases drastically, allowing new production to occur, this is why we see higher production at the end of summer
- Vertical segregation also affects nutrients in other ways
- Nutrients are low near the surface since they are used by autotrophs
- As you go further down, there are less autotrophs and more heterotrophs meaning more nutrients
- Nutrients must be returned to the surface to be used by autotrophs
- Light availability peaks in the summer and decreases in the winter
- In the winter, nutrients are high but light is low, in the summer nutrients are low and light is high
- Therefore, the fall and spring are the best times for productivity, especially the spring
- The biological pump is not related to downwelling

Trophic Pyramids

- In areas of upwelling, food chains are very short
- Phytoplankton can support populations of small fish very efficiently
- Where rate of photosynthesis is low, like in the central gyres, it becomes much less efficient
- Only 10% of energy is transferred per tier

Water Column

- Oxygen concentrations are highest near the surface where photosynthesis occurs
- When nitrogen is high, oxygen is low and vice versa
