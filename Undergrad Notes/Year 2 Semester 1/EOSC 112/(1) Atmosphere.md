- Why do we have wind on earth, and where does it blow?
- Need to explain how energy is distributed differently at different points on the earth
- When looking at the atmosphere, we mainly look at the troposphere
- This is the thinnest layer of the atmosphere, only about 12 km but contains 90% of the mass
- The stratosphere contains the ozone layer and absorbs UV radiation
- Need three variables to define the atmosphere
- Pressure is defined as force per unit area
- Atmospheric pressure is the weight of air above a surface
- Pressure of a gas is same in all directions
- If there are horizontal differences in pressure, air will flow from the high pressure to low pressure area
- F= dP/dx is the pressure gradient force
- Vertical pressure differences are due to gravity, but horizontal pressure differences are compensated for
- Temperature is a measure of kinetic energy
- Molecules move faster and expand in warmer gases and move slower in cold gases
- Ideal gas law is pressure = density x temp x gas constant
- In the atmosphere, pressure is fixed so temp directly affects density
- When warm air expands, it rises above the denser cool air until it reaches a layer with an equal density
- This is how hot air balloons work
- Air warming increases the pressure, which causes it to move towards cool areas with lower pressure
- This creates wind
- Warm air rises, blows at a high level, and settles down   
   
 
- At the lower level, hot air rising creates a low pressure zone
- Cool air from other areas rushes towards the warm core, heats and rises
- This is also present on a global scale
- Whenever air is leaving an area, more air will replace it
- Air warms at the equator and rises, rushes towards the poles where it cools, sinks, and rushes back towards the equator
- Called continuity
- This is a non rotating earth with no continents
- In conclusion, air heats and rises at the equator, flows at high level towards to poles, and then cools and flows at a low level towards the equator
