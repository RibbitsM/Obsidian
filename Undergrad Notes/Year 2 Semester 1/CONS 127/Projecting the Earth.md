Projecting the World

- One of the biggest problems cartographers run into is how to turn the 3d earth into a 2d map
- This is done with a map projection, a set of formulas that allows you to transform a curved surface into a 2d map
- Every 2d map contains some sort of distortion, only a globe can properly represent the earth
- Cartographers primarily project onto one of three shapes
- Cylindrical projection distorts the poles but creates a rectangular map
- Conic projections are good for representing parts of the earth, but not for the whole globe
- Planar projections
- A conformal projection is a projection where the angles between positions are preserved, which is good for sea travel
- The most popular projections are usually conformal and cylindrical
- Equivalent area projections preserve the surface area of countries and are usually associated with conic projections
- Equidistant projections are where all distances from a point are correct, and are usually planar
- However, distances between any other points are incorrect, used for radio communication
- Can also create compromises to create the most visually appealing map possible
- Compromise maps are usually not very good for analyzing area or navigating

Measuring Distances

- Early in human history there was no standardization for measurement
- After the French revolution the meter was defined as one ten millionth of the distance between the equator and the north pole
- After that there was a bar of metal exactly one meter kept in storage that was the standard
- The meter is now standardized based on the speed of light
- Length of the path travelled by light in 1/299,792,458th of a second
- The kilo was also recently redefined from the weight of a certain metal cylinder to the force generated by an electric current
- The metric system is the most common standardized system of measurement
- All length and distance measurements in the metric system are based on the meter
- The metric system was not used in the US because it was too French
- This has caused issues, such as when the Mars Climate Orbiter failed because it was using metric and imperial units simultaneously

Observing Different Scales

- The electron microscope is used to observe incredibly small things on the picometer scale
- Used primarily for looking at cells
- The optical microscope uses light and lenses to magnify things, and can magnify up to 1000x
- Used for pharmaceuticals, biology etc.
- Cameras are optical devices, attempt to create a 1:1 image to what the human eye would see
- Telescopes are used to observe extremely far away
- Initially used optical lenses but now make use of gamma rays and other technologies
- Used to observe planets, galaxies, stars, etc.
- We represent scale on maps with a bar scale
- Bar scales can be graphic, fractional, or verbal
- This shows the ratio of the distance between two points on a map vs two points on the earth
- The scale of worldwide maps is usually 1:40,000,000
- Continents are half of this at 1:20,000,000
- Nations are usually 1:10,000,000 depending on the size
- Provinces and regions are around 1:1,000,000
- Metropolitan areas are 1:50,000, cities are 1:30,000 and neighborhoods are 1:15,000
- Smallest scale maps are cadastrals which are between 1:500 and 1:5000
- Cadastral maps are used to mark land ownership in detail
