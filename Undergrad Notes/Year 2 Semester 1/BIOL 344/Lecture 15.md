Chromosome Mutations

- Genes can be duplicates, the chromosome is extended and you have a duplicate of a gene
- An inversion can happen, where the genes are in reverse order of the wild type
- This usually isn't a problem, except for when the break point of the inversion messes with transcription
- Translocation can occur where a gene is moved from one gene to another
- One of the most harmful mutations is deletion, even if you have a backup gene on the other homologue
- Mutations can result in missing chromosomes, or extra chromosomes
- Gaining chromosomes is generally better than losing them, but it still cause significant problems in humans

Ploidy

- Aneuploidy is when you have subtractions or additions of chromosomes
- 2n plus one (trisomic), or 2n minus one (monosomic)
- Polyploidy is multiplying chromosomes, having 3 or 4 sets instead of 2
- Plants are fine with polyploidy, humans aren't
- Ploidy issues usually arise during meiosis
- Usually occurs in anaphase 1 or 2 when the chromosomes are separated
- Say two chromosomes don't split apart in anaphase 1 (nondisjunction)
- Anaphase 2 works out fine, and we have 2 gametes with extra chromosomes, and 2 gametes with none
- In the 2n plus 1 gametes, there is two homologues from different chromosomes
- If a gamete with extra chromosomes is fertilized, it causes trisomy and the gamete with less chromosomes causes monosomy
- If nondisjunction occurs in anaphase 2, one gamete will have two copies of the same chromosome, one will be lacking one, and the other two are normal
- Polyploid plants could be created by breeding
- Todays wheat is hexaploid, a product of centuries of selective breeding
- Plants with higher ploidy tend to be more productive
