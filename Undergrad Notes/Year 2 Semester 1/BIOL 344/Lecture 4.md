- Direct link between genotype and phenotype
- DNA creates RNA, creates proteins, determines phenotype
- Each cell in the human body contains a full diploid set of DNA
- Human sex cells (gametes) are haploid, they only have one set of DNA

Structure of DNA

- Two main types of DNA, purine and pyrimidine
- The purine nucleotides are Adenine (A) and Guanine (G)
- Purines are larger double ring structures, while pyrimidines are only single rings
- You always need a single ring bound to a double ring to maintain the shape of the DNA
- If two single rings or two double rings bond it'll constrict or expand the structure
- The pyrimidine nucleotides are Cytosine (C) and Thymine (T)
- In RNA T is replace with Uracil (U)
- A and G are present in equal amounts and C and T are present in equal amounts
- A and G are always bonded together and C and T are always bonded together
- All nucleotides share the same backbone made of deoxyribose and phosphates
- The backbone just provides a shell for the nucleotides to occupy
- DNA replicates by splitting apart, and then free nucleotides bond to their respective pairs to create two complete DNA strands
- Assuming no mutations, DNA replication always maintains the genotype
- Even between homologous pairs, each chromatid is not identical
- Even after transcription, RNA still needs to be refined into mRNA by removing introns
- Ribosomes use the information from mRNA to create proteins
- Transcription is similar to DNA replication, RNA polymerase bonds to one half of the unzipped DNA
- This creates single stranded RNA, made of Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, and Uracil
- AUG is the universal start codon for protein synthesis
- Because several different codons code for the same protein, if a mutation occurs it may not affect the resulting protein strand
- The human dystrophin gene is 2.5 million nucleotides long, but the transcribed RNA is only 14,000 nucleotides long
- Most of the gene is intronic and not necessary
