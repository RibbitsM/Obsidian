Origins

- Feminism is often described as coming in three distinct waves
- The first wave focused on the legal status of women
- Issues were mainly differences in legal rights, legal representation, voting rights, etc.
- Mary Wollstonecraft, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Susan B. Anthony
- Most of these goals were met in the US, but not other places
- The 19th Amendment to the US constitution was not the be all end all of global women's rights
- The second wave focused more on the equality of condition
- Emphasis was social and economic equality
- This included the wage gap, reproductive rights, workers rights etc.
- Betty Friedan and Cynthia Enloe
- The third wave focused more in intersectionality and identities
- LGBT feminism, postcolonial feminism, postmodern feminism, environmental feminism
- Angela Davis, Carol Adams, Chandra Mohanty etc.
- In terms of IR theory, we focus mainly on the contributions made by the second wave
- Recently, ideas of intersectionality are starting to be adapted into IR theory

Principles

- Feminism theory focuses overwhelmingly on gender
- Everything is related to gender, and gender matters greatly
- The traditional assumptions of global politics are mainly associated with masculinity
- Despite the name, feminism is really the study of gender, not just the female gender
- Gender can be considered a power relation, which is useful for global politics
- Gender needs to be understood in order to properly predict how actions will play out on a global scale
- Feminists basically all agree that women have been historically marginalized in politics and the economy
- This is due to structures put in place by the patriarchy (male dominated structures)
- Female perspectives have been ignored in the creation of IR theory
- Theories such as realism and liberalism assume a male perspective when talking about actions and policy
- Women have different life experiences compared to other genders due to a combination of biology and social factors
- All social relations are gendered, there is no distinction between gender and any other identity
- It exists in addition to all other identities
- Intersectionality means that all of these identities compound
- Someone who has one marginalized identity has better opportunities than someone who has 4 marginalized identities
- The feminist view of the world invariably comes in the form of statistics related to the oppression of women
- More men are employed than women
- Labour force participation rate is also much higher
- The biggest disparities are in Northern Africa, Southern Asia, and Western Asia by a large margin
- The current estimated global wage gap is 12%, but it's probably being underreported
- Women are generally employees, not employers
- Women often do much more unpaid work than men
- 28% of managerial positions held by women, 18% of CEOs are women
- 20 women are currently serving as head of state, 25% of parliamentarians are women
- Women account for 80% of intimate partner homicides while overall 20% of homicide victims are women
- 1 in 3 women will experience physical or sexual violence from an intimate partner in their lives
- Most Covid-19 cases were among women, who accounted for most front line workers

Future

- Women are being increasingly employed, they are the largest new labour market, and account for two thirds of American breadwinners
- Globally, women are the largest emerging market, twice the size of india and china combined
- In the US women outnumber men by 20% in terms of college degrees, we have no such data from other countries however

Power

- Power is inseparable from gender relations
- Feminists critique other theories for focusing too much on conflict
- Feminism does have several overlaps with post-colonial theory and constructivist theory
- Examine how power has been exercised over women today and historically
- Goal is the emancipation of women and their advancement in global politics
- Women played a key part in the Arab Spring in the Syrian protests, and it is being echoed today in Iran
- From a realist perspective, what's happening in Iran makes no sense, but it's clearly explained through a feminist perspective
- Feminists are especially critical of realists for their focus on hard power and masculine ideas
- Power politics in general are considered to be a product of male thought

Liberal Feminism

- Focuses overwhelmingly on the concept of "bringing women in"
- Give women more opportunity in privileged areas and positions
- Increase social and economic equality
- If you hear phrases like "we need more women in ____" its liberal feminism

Marxist Feminism

- Focus on the impact of capitalism on the oppression of women
- The solution to the female condition is to overthrow the capitalist system
- The problem with this is that the oppression of women obviously predates capitalism
- To solve this marxist feminists bring feminism into marxist class theory

Postmodern Feminism

- Women are socialized into their roles and identities by norms and values
- These roles are usually associated with low status and unpaid work

Postcolonial Feminism

- Focuses on how the legacy of colonialism is a cause for the oppression of women especially women of colour
- Believes that a gendered lens is required to properly understand colonialism and it's impacts

Today

- The Beijing program of action was a conference where a coalition of nations agreed to make progress on a variety of feminist issues
- This program was ambitious, but little progress has been made
- The idea was to have another conference with even more countries, but COVID shut it down
- Some states have created feminist foreign policy, meaning policy based on the wellbeing of marginalized people
- Spain, France, Canada, Mexico, Luxembourg, Chile, and especially Sweden have adopted these policies
- Margot Wallstrom was the pioneer of feminist foreign policy
- She defines 4 Rs, being Rights, Representation, Resources, and Realism
- Realism was added later after the progress of the first three was shown to be painfully slow
- These ideas have continued to gain momentum and support slowly but surely
- Canada sought to use it's foreign aid budget to improve the condition of women worldwide
- Some of the successes of feminist foreign policy have been improving peace agreements by involving women, and also the discovery that women's political involvement has been associated with QOL indicators going up
- The limitations of feminist foreign policy is that it only really exists as a social framework, and it has been extremely underwhelming in action

LGBT and Queer Theory

- LGBT IR theory is still in it's infancy
- Focuses on sexualities, heteronormativity, and their effects on social norms
- Discusses the current backlash of the emergence of LGBT and Queer identities
- For example the Russian rhetoric of America's decadence and acceptance of LGBT identities
- LGBT theory is clearly relevant today
- Also contests the gender binary in global politics, most documents and policies recognize two genders
- Most of the work being done in LGBT theory focuses on the global culture war about LGBT identities

Conclusion

- There is no agreement about what framework is best
- Everyone has different views on what the core problem is
- Different theories look at power differently
- Policy is a product of theory, it is crucial to understand theory if you want to assess foreign policy
- Theories are best used as analytical tools, not religions
- Think of them as interchangeable lenses
