- The purpose of arms control/disarmament is to restrict the development, production, stockpiling, proliferation, and use of weapons
- This applies to all weapons, but we're going to be talking about nuclear weapons
- Disarmament is drastically reducing/eliminating categories of weapons
- Examples of this is the ban on chemical and biological weapons
- Arms control is more managerial, and reduces existing stockpiles of weapons and restricts use
- Arms control treaties are created via diplomatic negotiations between states
- A state that has agreed to participate in a treaty is called a signatory
- Treaties only have force in international law if they are ratified by most or all of the signatories, depending on the terms of the treaty
- Treaties could become law even if only 40% of the signatories ratified it if that's what the treaty says
- If a country ratifies a treaty, that means that they incorporate the terms of the treaty into their government's legislation
- This process looks different for different countries, but would go through the house of commons in Canada
- When the ratification conditions are met, we say that a treaty has "entered into force"
- Generally non-signatories are not bound by the provisions of the treaty, but some treaties are so widely ratified and acknowledged that non-signatories are judged for disobeying

Motives

- Arms control is a conflict management tool
- It aims to reduce the frequency of war by removing dangerous weapons
- Increases feelings of security
- Aims to reduce/prevent the progress of arms races
- By establishing a cap on certain kinds of weapons, it evens the playing field and prevents arms races
- Saves money that states would have otherwise spent on weapons
- Helps build trust and promote peaceful resolutions
- Countries obeying the terms of treaties is seen as a sign of trust
- When conflicts do erupt, the extent of destruction is limited

Problems

- Most issues with arms control were raised by realists
- Why should a country in a treaty trust it's fellow signatories to obey the treaty
- Some treaties have verification mechanism that attempt to ensure that the terms of the treaty are upheld
- Ensuring compliance is one of the largest challenges in arms control
- For example, North Korea was a member of the NPT, but proceeded to acquire nuclear weapons and pull out of the treaty anyway
- Another argument is that as long as the means and knowledge to create weapons exists, they can never be truly banned
- Technological innovations can make treaties irrelevant, for example the Washington Naval Treaty limited battleships, but then aircraft carriers became the most important ship
- And of course treaties only bind their signatories

Principles

- The first principle is transparency
- In order for arms control to exist, countries must willingly volunteer information about their intentions, doctrines, and weapons capabilities
- Rarely do states willingly admit their capabilities and goals, because of the disadvantage it puts them at
- It is difficult to gain this information if it isn't given willingly
- The next principle is verification
- You must be able to determine to what degree a state is following to provisions of the treaty
- Many states are unwilling to violate their sovereignty and let outside inspectors into the state
- There is a constant concern that trade secrets and economic secrets will be leaked by inspectors
- Irreversibility is also a principle
- Arms controls should be irreversible, commitments to a treaty should not be able to be taken back
- If one state withdraws from a treaty, it weakens the commitment to the whole treaty
- However, times change and states are allowed to withdraw from treaties at any time

- Militarization of the Antarctic including nuclear testing banned in 1959 (Antarctic Treaty)
- Tests in the atmosphere, underwater, and outer space banned in 1963 (Limited Test Ban Treaty)
- Placing nuclear weapons in orbit, on the moon or celestial bodies banned in 1967
- Nuclear weapons banned in Latin America in
- The most significant treaty was the Nuclear Non-proliferation Treaty
- Signed July 1968
- Extended indefinitely in 1995
- 173 countries are party to it, but non-signatories include India, Pakistan, and North Korea among others
- Nuclear weapons states cannot transfer nuclear weapons
- Non-weapons countries cannot receive or produce them
- Signatories agree to IAEA provisions
- And also the signatories must pledge to work for universal nuclear disarmament
- This has been an issue lately as many critics of the treaty points to this article as an issue with the treaty
- The other issues include that its only binding on signatories, several nuclear states haven't signed
- Some signatories developed secret nuclear weapons programs while in the NPT
- Application of peaceful use of nuclear power, which has drawn criticism
- And of course the universal problem of enforcement
- The only thing they can threaten is sending the issue to the UN Security Council

IAEA

- Full title is the International Atomic Energy Agency, or Atoms for Peace
- More of a treaty than an agency
- Creates in July 1957 located in Vienna
- 171 current member states
- It is the investigatory arm of the NPT
- Encourages peaceful nuclear power, and safeguards nuclear energy
- Upholds standards of nuclear safety and security
- Three main components to the safeguard system
- The first main component is that any state that wants to build a nuclear facility must send their design plans to the IAEA
- They also monitor the inventory and flow of nuclear material, and hold regular and special inspections
- Special inspections are only used for emergency circumstances
- The IAEA also has access to surveillance systems in nuclear power plants
- Nuclear material in excess of the amount allowed by the NPT is sealed and safeguarded by the IAEA
- These stockpiled are secured with fiber optic cables or tape seals to ensure no tampering
- If inspectors find that a state has breached compliance, it is reported to the Director General and the IAEA board of directors
- This is reported to all member countries and referred to the UN Security Council
- Of course, these safeguards only apply to member countries
- Countries like Iraq, Iran, and North Korea have developed nuclear weapons while under inspection from the IAEA
- However, the NPT still upholds the norm of preventing nuclear proliferation
- There is a near universal agreement to prevent nuclear proliferation
- And while the inspectors methods aren't perfect, they complicate the process of acquiring nuclear weapons

Treaties

- Seabed Treaty ban nuclear weapons or WMDs beyond the 12 mile coastal zone in 1971
- SALT 1 signed in 1972, froze the number of strategic ballistic missiles between US and USSR
- ABM treaty signed also in 1972 restricted ABM systems to one area and no more than 100 systems
- SALT 2 wasn't ratified but still obeyed, limited ICBMs, SLBMs and bombers
- Intermediate Range Nuclear Forces Treaty was a disarmament treaty that outright banned INF systems
- Ballistic Missile Launch Notification Agreement treaty in 1988 established to ensure that ballistic missile tests weren't interpreted as a first strike attempt
- START 1 signed in 1991, post cold war arms control treaty on delivery vehicles and weapons
- Belarus, Kazakhstan and Ukraine joined this theory post USSR collapse
- START 2 signed in 1993, reduced warheads further
- Nuclear weapons banned in Africa, same as Latin America

Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty

- Signed September 10 1996, not in force
- Will not be in force until China, India, Pakistan, North Korea, Israel, Iran, Egypt, and the US ratify it
- Each state cannot carry out any test explosion, and must prohibit nuclear explosions at any place under it's jurisdiction
- Closes several loopholes allowing nuclear tests to continue
- Furthermore, each state must refrain from encouraging or participating in the process of any test explosion or any nuclear explosion
- Closes the possibility of a clandestine nuclear test carried out by a group of nations
- North Korea is the only country known to have carried out a nuclear test in recent history
- If put into force, the CTBT will have the International Monitoring System
- It is currently complete and functional
- The system includes primary and auxiliary seismological stations, radionuclide stations, infrasound/acoustic stations, and hydrological stations all around the world
- This system is currently generating useful information, but the treaty has yet to be ratified
- New Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty is the one current arms control treaty between Russia and the US, was recently reaffirmed
- Limits warheads, ICBMs and SLBMs
- Nuclear Terrorism Convention signed by 115 states with 91 ratifications in 2005
- The most comprehensive anti nuclear weapons treaty is the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons
- It has not been ratified by a single nuclear state

Iran Deal

- Negotiations between the P5 plus 1 and Iran were ongoing for 2 years
- On 14 July 2014, the Iran Deal was created
- 4 core elements
- First is restrictions on Iran's enrichment capabilities
- Iran is restricted to 5,000 enrichment centrifuges and can only enrich U-235 to 3.67%
- Remaining centrifuges are put under IAEA safeguard
- Second is restriction on fissile material
- Iran had 10,000-12,000 kgs of LEU
- Reduced by 98%, and required to stay like that for 15 years
- Arak reactor modified to reduce plutonium output, and the output is shipped out of Iran
- Iran agreed to not reprocess spent fuel for plutonium for 15 years
- Third is verification
- Iran accepted the IAEA additional protocol
- Declares nuclear construction and gives inspectors 24-hour access
- Entire nuclear fuel cycle monitored
- Fourth is sanctions
- UN Security council agreed that if the agreement was violated and not resolved in 30 days, all sanctions against Iran are snapped back
- Iran would have access to $50 billion upon termination of sanctions
- Both sides have met the terms of the Iran Deal
- Its stockpile was neutralized, inspection was increased, and sanctions were lifted
- Critics argued that inspection could be worked around, the timeline was too short, and that Iran still had fissile material reprocessing capacities
- On May 8 2018, the US withdrew from the Iran Deal and resumed sanctions against Iran
- Through 2020 and 2021, Iran resumed higher levels of uranium enrichment
- Biden announced that the US would return to the Iran Deal and would lift sanctions if Iran returned to full compliance
- Iran argued that they would only comply if sanctions were lifted first, and the argument continues
- Iran has added 17.6 kg of uranium to its stockpile and enriched uranium to 20-60%
- This is a clear violation of the deal
- Recently there have been no meetings regarding the deal
- This has been complicated by the fact that Russia demands that it's trade with Iran be exempted from the sanctions in light of the invasion of Ukraine
