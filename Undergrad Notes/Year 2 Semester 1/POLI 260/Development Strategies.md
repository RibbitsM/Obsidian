Liberal Development Model

- The first step of development is promoting trade, so countries can benefit from comparative advantage
- Must attract investment capital into the country by deregulating
- Privatize government owned sectors of the domestic economy
- Accept foreign aid not to the point of dependence, but enough to kickstart economic activity
- This is the general global model, but is only half
- Part two focuses on domestic reform
- This is often ignored because liberals expect results to happen after part one is finished
- Most developing countries have weak regulation, rampant corruption, and are usually authoritarian or oligarchic in nature
- Market and legal reform are needed to ensure rule of law is upheld
- Banks must be reformed to be truly independent
- Policy should be directed to enable entrepeneurship, this means enabling citizens to challenge existing industries
- Education is a big part of this, helps to encourage entrepeneurship
- Reduce military spending to free up capital for other efforts
- Democratic rule is usually suggested, but is not always required
- Many global institutions like the WTO, World Bank and others operate according to these rules and suggestions
- This is a result of embedded liberalism, makes up the global standard for development
- Trade and investment are the engines of growth
- Welfare is not what drives economic development, it is flow of financial capital into developing countries

Governance

- State governments, UN, OECD, IMF and World Bank, and NGOs are all involved
- These actors make up the modern world of development
- One of the major characteristics of economic assistance in development is the existence of conditionality
- Provision of foreign aid, financing, and other assistance are extended under the assumption that the aided countries follows the rules and norms set out by these international actors
- Otherwise, these funds may be abused by corrupt systems and authoritarian rulers
- Of course, this creates the issue of binding the receiving country into the liberal economic model
- Therefore, many countries have little choice but to submit to the liberal world order to gain aid
- The balance of respect and conditionality is one of the main issues facing liberalism today

Millennium Development Goals

- Established in 2000, were the guiding principles of global development
- 8 goals relating to poverty, hunger, education, gender equality, child mortality, maternal health, diseases, environmental sustainability, and partnership
- By 2015, progress was made in each one of these goals, but was highly uneven
- In addition, any progress made was still vulnerable to backsliding
- The original goals were replaced by the Sustainable Development Goals
- 17 goals to be reached by 2030
- The latest major development was in 2011, the formation of the Global Partnership for Effective Development Co-operation
- This is an effort to reform foreign aid to increase its effectiveness
- Signifies a move away from the traditional top-down model and towards a model where countries are consulted first and focus is shifted to results rather than just delivering aid

Foreign Aid

- Funding commitment from donor states is .07% of gross national income
- All countries agreeing to commit foreign aid must commit this much money
- Not a long term solution, just temporary relief
- The goal is to create wealth so proper infrastructure can be built
- The majority of foreign aid comes from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), specifically the Donor Assistance Committee (DAC)
- This money goes to countries and multilateral organizations
- In 2021 official foreign aid totaled $178.9 billion US
- The top 3 donors were the US, Germany, and Japan
- From a percentage perspective, Luxembourg, Norway and Sweden are the top 3
- The vast majority of countries don't donate .07%, only the top 5 percentage donors actually do so
- Foreign aid flows into developing countries are quite small in comparison to other forms of capital
- FDI, portfolio investment, and private donations/grants are the biggest contributors, followed by remittances and private donations
- Private development assistance is a surprisingly high percentage, equivalent to about a quarter of all development assistance

International Monetary Fund and World Bank

- Lender of last resort, the IMFs goal is global financial stability
- Its reserves come from a pool of capital contributed by member states
- When the IMF does give out loans, they always come with conditions
- The world bank group is actually five institutions, with the original world bank being only one of the five
- Wide variety of development NGOs, some major ones are World Vision, Oxfam, Save The Children, Doctors Without Borders
- China is also a significant contributor to foreign aid despite not being part of an official aid group
- China currently ranks about seventh in terms of $US
- Donations made by China also come with conditions, but different from the traditional ones
- Any country receiving aid from China cannot recognize Taiwan as a country, and cannot interact with its government

Intra-Liberal Debate

- Development is dominated by liberal thought, but arguments exist between liberals
- One is the criticism that development is based on the assumption that development is achieved by following in the footsteps of other developed countries
- One example would be the Nova Cidade de Kilamba in Angola
- This is a city built as a top-down development project, and failed miserably
- It is a western-styled city with apartment buildings and schools, and failed due to how expensive the housing was, since it was also based on western pricing
- Integrating countries into the global market leaves them vulnerable to many factors
- Countries may experience fluctuations in their commodities and currencies
- Seeking aid may result in going into debt to other countries and IOs
- Loan conditionality requirements can create domestic tensions
- Multinationals entering the country can create capital flight or make the country dependent on their infrastructure
- To solve these issues, there must be reform of the global market
- This means reform in the IMF, World Bank, and WTO
- Programs for debt forgiveness must be put in place
- Domestic food production must be made possible
- Another criticism is the inefficiency and harm created by foreign aid
- Foreign aid creates dependence
- It facilitates corruption and perpetuates poor governance
- It ties receivers to the donors
- May result in inappropriate infrastructure projects
- Alternatives exist

Alternative Efforts

- No other organizations have been able to enact change in foreign aid, although many have tried
- Includes the NAM, NIEO, and others
- Depends on support from developed countries and on effective governance in developing countries
- The most promising alternative is community development as opposed to state level
- Meeting needs of communities, fair trade rather than free trade systems
- Creating microcredit systems to local entrepreneurs, and focusing on the efforts of women to sustain the community
- Simple applications of technology to make life easier in communities
- Research indicates that the health of the female population in a community is linked with the health of the community as a whole
