- Basic objective of development is raising quality of life
- People should be able to live creative and fulfilling lives

Themes

- First theme is the constant increase in human development over time
- All things considered, the majority of people have felt gains in the past decades
- We are living in the best period in human history
- The next theme is the consequence of the first, rising income inequality
- Those who are not benefitted by development fall further and further behind
- Growing divide between the rich and the poor, both in individual and state terms
- Goes hand in hand with the theme of consistent extreme poverty
- Also a theme of disparate economic growth, while growth is experienced by many, it happens at different rates
- Very large number of people who are considered economically vulnerable
- People who aren't in poverty, but are only one or two misfortunes away from falling back into poverty
- Theme of focus on reducing economic vulnerability
- Covid-19 and the Russia-Ukraine war are the most recent focuses of development, especially their effects on the poor

Terminology

- Developed countries are those doing the best, used to describe industrialized or rich countries
- Developing countries are those who are neither industrialized or rich, sometimes called the third world
- These are assumed to be fundamentally lesser than developed countries
- Some other terms are Less Developed countries, underdeveloped countries, low income countries, the Global South, Least developed countries, poor states, and even the fourth world
- All of these terms create a dichotomy of superiority and inferiority
- Paints the rich countries as the goal
- Casts blame on the poorer countries for their economic state
- Rooted in feelings of cultural bias and racism
- Phrases like "developing countries" and the "Third world" lump together many countries that are in reality very different from eachother
- Countries exist in the state that they do for a vast variety of reasons, and even within the group of developing countries there is a power hierarchy
- This mode of analysis is also very state centric, there are still rich people in poor countries and poor people in rich countries
- Media portrayal of poor countries are often misled and inaccurate
- They tend to overwhelmingly focus on starvation, while in reality malnutrition is the real problem
- Since malnutrition is less visible, the smaller issue of starvation supplants it in the global view
- Depictions of poverty are also often racialized, for a very long time Africa was the global poster child for poverty
- Despite being far from the only place experiencing poverty and being a lot more developed than most people think, Africa is often depicted in media as a wasteland of extreme poverty
- Vulnerability is distinct from poverty, and refers instead to a sense of insecurity and a susceptibility to shocks/stress
- Vulnerability is not poverty, but rather the possibility for poverty
- Individuals, groups, and regions can all be vulnerable
- Vulnerability can be due to geographical location and weather, social standing, ability for growth, etc.
- Recent shocks to vulnerable populations have been food and fuel price rises due to the russia-ukraine conflict, and the covid-19 pandemic

Measuring Poverty

- The World Bank defines extreme poverty as making less than $1.90 US a day, and poverty as less than $3.10
- Income is not the only factor affecting poverty, but it as often used as a stand in
- For example, it is possible to support yourself without money, you may be involved in an agricultural collective and receive food as payment
- Wealth is also an important factor, making no new income means much less when you already have a significant amount of money
- Another measure of poverty is PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)
- Used to equalize purchasing power of different currencies
- Depending on what country you live in, prices are different and so are wages
- PPPs show the ratio of prices in national currency to the same good/service in several countries
- The common denominator is the basket of goods/services used in the equation
- A good example is the Big Mac, which is sold by McDonalds in a wide variety of countries and always contains the same ingredients
- The Big Mac Index determines that since the Big Mac is 2.84 euros in France and 2.20 dollars in the US, 1.29 Euros on the dollar is the ratio
- This way we can calculate how many minutes of minimum wage work it takes to buy a Big Mac
- Comparing income to price lets us effectively see differences in purchasing power
- Another indicator is the HDI (Human Development Index), which is a composite measure of income, education, and health indicators
- This seeks to gain a measure of all aspects of poverty in one index
- Countries are given a score between .000 and 1
- These are divided into very high, high, medium, and low
- The highest countries include Iceland, Switzerland, and Ireland
- Lowest include South Sudan, Chad, and Niger
- HDI has been climbing since 1990, but variations between regions are drastic
- Inequalities between max and min education, income, and life expectancy are dramatic, especially in income
- The next index is the MPI (Global Multidimensional Poverty Index)
- This is similar to the HDI but breaks it down even further into even more dimensions and variables
- Less known than the HDI but is much richer
- According to the 2019 MPI 21.7% of people live in multidimensional poverty, more than other estimates
- About half of those people are children
- More than 67% of them live in middle income countries
- Out of the countries with long term data, almost all of them saw significant reductions in poverty
- The Gini Coefficient measures the deviation of the distribution of income among individuals/households within an economy
- A value of 0 means perfect equality, and 100 means one person has all the money and everyone else has none

Inequality

- In 2021 the average adult earned PPP $23,380 US, and owned $102,600 in wealth
- Richest 10% earns 52% of income, poorest half earned 8.5%
- This gets even more drastic in wealth terms
- Poorest half owns 2% of wealth, richest 10% owns 76%
- With the exception of Europe and South East Asia, the top 10% makes the most income
- Inequality has risen over time, but has actually declined recently
- The top 10% went down, the middle 40% went up and the bottom 50% went up a little
- Income inequality peaked in 1980, where the top 10% made 53x more than the bottom 50%
- There is also extreme concentration of wealth in a very small minority
- The top 1% has captured 38% of global wealth growth
- The middle class in the emerging world captured comparatively more growth than those in rich countries
- In the US the share of wealth captured by the few richest households is even higher today than before the great depression

Why Inequality Matters

- Asymmetric distribution of capital makes recessions and market failures worse, economic recovery is much harder for low income populations
- Economic growth is greater when income is distributed equally, that way more people have more money to spend
- The rich have disproportionate influence over policy and affect decisions on investment, environmental policy, taxes, social programs etc.
- A weak middle class is unable to create accountability for the rich in the political process
- Inequality erodes faith in institutions and democracy and causes human misery, lost potential, and political extremism
- Looking full circle, the inequality created by liberal capitalism leads to the same conditions it sought to eradicate in Weimar Germany
