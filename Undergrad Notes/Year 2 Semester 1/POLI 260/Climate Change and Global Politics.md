- The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change or IPCC was formed in 1988
- It is a synthesis and summary organization, meaning it assesses the work of climate scientists and condenses them into Assessment Reports
- Cornerstone of climate change science because it make this information concise, accessible and readable
- Also discusses possible responses to the challenges of climate change

Nature of Climate Change

- Driven by human greenhouse gas emissions and radiative forcing
- Carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gasses are the main GHGs
- Greenhouse gasses have risen above sustainable levels and have increased enormously since 1750
- At the start of human civilization CO2 concentration was 275 ppm
- The safe upper limit is estimated to be 350 ppm
- The current concentration exceeds 410 ppm (according to Assessment Report 6)

Impacts

- There are five main impacts of climate change, although many more exist
- First is temperature increases, with certain regions more affected
- Rising sea levels, ice melt and ocean temps
- Increased extreme weather
- Ecosystem/habitat destruction and loss of biodiversity
- Invasive species and tropical disease spreading
- Observed temperature increases have been determined to be unnatural and due to human activity
- Oceans are warmer, higher, and more salinized and acidic
- On land, warm days and nights are more frequent than cool ones
- Fewer frost days, snow cover, and degradation of permafrost
- This will cause devastating socio-economic effects including relocation, productivity issues, and widespread temperatures
- Concerns are also raised about resource wars due to overall lower production

Individual

- On the individual level, human consumption and variations in it are one of the largest issues
- As an example, individuals are often asked to reduce their consumption in order to reduce their climate footprint
- Because of our principles of individuality and consumption, it is very difficult to make people sacrifice personal consumption for the collective good
- Of course in response to this is the argument that corporations are mainly to blame, and personal action has little effort on the real issue
- More than half of global industrial emissions since 1988 are because of only 25 companies
- The largest polluters have a vested interest in encouraging individual action
- Education and awareness of the processes of climate change
- Information about climate change is rather varied and can look much different in different places

State

- On the state level we have the responsiveness of the state to climate issues
- The leadership of states and the partisan politics and political parties also contribute heavily to this
- Industry and the market account for many emissions
- The actions of civil society including the media and public opinion
- The role of science, scientific discoveries and their impact on decisions
- What determines government decisions on climate change is not entirely clear
- All of these aspects of the state level play parts in decision making
- It is governments who have the power to enact laws and create incentives and disincentives to change behavior
- No other actor has the power that governments do
- Governments can reduce or eliminate fossil fuel subsidies, create renewable energy subsidies, implement carbon taxes, and introduce GHG cap and trade systems
- Fossil fuels are infamously over subsidized compared to renewable energy
- The problem is that all of these actions are very politically unpopular, especially carbon taxes
- Dependence on fossil fuels makes creating change in energy systems incredibly difficult
- In addition to this, systematic climate change denial campaigns make things harder
- The Bush administration was lobbied by the fossil fuel industry to abandon the Kyoto Protocol
- Efforts were made to undermine scientific progress and ensure that the public was uncertain on the effects of climate change
- The 2000 US National Assessment of Climate Change Impacts on the United States was a comprehensive assessment of the works of many climate scientists
- It was held up in court by lawsuits from US Congress, killing its potential for sales and discourse
- The Greening the Earth Society is an NGO created by the fuel industry to spread misinformation about climate science
- These efforts have been immensely successful, opinions on climate science and policy are drastically different between Canada and the US
- There has been an unprecedented rise in climate activism, groups exist in almost every country
- Interestingly, the growing public consensus and activism around climate change has not translated into political will and action

System

- On the international level, energy demand is a big factor
- The energy sector accounts for an immense amount of greenhouse gas emissions
- Most of this is a result of oil, coal, and gas
- Inequality in climate impacts
- Not only does climate change exacerbate existing inequalities, it also creates new ones
- China, India, the US, and the EU are the main polluters, they account for about half of all emissions
- North America and Europe have historically emitted the most, especially compared to their population
- This has created a tension between the current emitters and historical emitters over who is at fault
- Looking at per capita emissions, North America is overwhelmingly at fault
- In terms of income inequality, the difference in emissions between the top 10% and bottom 50% of earners is staggering both domestically and globally
- The poorest communities are also the most vulnerable to the impacts of climate change
- This also applies to indigenous groups who live close to the land
- Indigenous groups often live in environments at risk from climate change, and rely on the land in order to sustain their lifestyles
- At the same time this gives indigenous people great insight into climate science and policy efforts
- Divergent interests between states
- Negotiation of international agreements
- Environmental conflicts
- Climate refugees
