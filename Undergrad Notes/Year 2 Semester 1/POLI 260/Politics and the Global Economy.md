Global Trade

- Holistically, world trade has succeeded in it's goals
- Comparative advantage has been able to effectively raise the wealth of all countries, albeit unevenly
- Global trade was valued at $28.5 trillion in 2021
- Achieved via global and regional trade agreements
- Progress on these achievements have been uneven and challenging
- Differences between cultures, economies, goods etc. create significant walls to trade
- Whatever agreements that are easy have already been made, the pace of trade agreements has slowed as tension increases
- Politics act on both global and state levels
- Competition increases and free trade agreements have begun to encroach on protected industries
- Domestic politics of free trade must recognize that the benefits have not been spread equally
- A state engaging in free trade does not benefit their entire population, making it more difficult to endorse fully
- Feelings of resentment and frustration towards free trade, led to populist movements
- Globalizations and free flow of goods/capital privilege only the few, leading to resentment from the middle working class
- Widening inequality affects politics
- This force of resentment has been exploited and manipulated by several political parties
- A international phenomena is having effects on domestic politics

Common Features

- There are several commonalities between most free trade agreements
- Elimination/reduction of tariffs
- Liberalization of services trade, includes services like entertainment
- Liberalization of investment, not limited to just government level and corporation investment, but also the ability of citizens to invest in foreign stocks
- Intellectual property protections, standardization of copyright and patent protection across multiple states
- Labour/environmental standards prevent one country from having an advantage and raise standards across the board
- Dispute resolution mechanisms
- E-commerce guidelines, a rising aspect of trade agreements

WTO

- The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) was established on October 30, 1947
- This was the trade end of the Bretton Woods agreement
- Focused on tariff reductions and reducing discrimination between countries
- Consisted of seven rounds of negotiation going into 1995
- Key reason for the explosion in trade in the 1950s and onward
- The GATT was doing so well it was promoted into an organization in 1995, the WTO
- Rather small budget and staff, located in Geneva, but incredibly significant
- Inherited all the results of the GATT
- The world's preeminent forum on world trade and settling trade disputes
- Dr Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala is the current director-general of the WTO
- Global finance expert and former Finance and Foreign Minister of Nigeria
- Widespread agreement that the WTO is in trouble and facing acute political challenges
- The next round of negotiations following 1995 was the Doha negotiations
- This round failed horribly, and no new negotiations have been made since
- Since 1995 protectionism has increased sharply largely due to the two level tensions surrounding global trade
- Geopolitical tension is increasing worldwide, especially between superpowers such as the US and China
- Trade embargos and sanctions are more common than free trade agreements now
- The most recent blow to this has been the American decision to ban microchip trade with China and instead subsidizing domestic production
- Two of the leading countries in microchip development are ceasing to cooperate and help eachother entirely due to political tensions
- Agricultural subsidies are very common, most countries support their domestic industries due to their political importance
- Food, textiles, and fish are Tariff Peak Products, meaning they are especially protected industries
- Another recent trade dispute involved Bombardier, who were accused of being subsidized by the Canadian government by the Brazilian government
- Canadian subsidies were reduced, and also reduced in Brazil who were discovered to be subsidizing their own aircraft company
- Drug patents are the most contentious aspect of intellectual property rights, and only became more relevant during COVID
- Growth hormones are another source of contention, they are allowed in North America but not in Europe
- Despite lack of evidence on the harm of growth hormones, North American meat is not sold in Europe because of this
- Contention raised on whether this is a fake barrier to trade or not
- The same issue emerges with GMOs, that are often labelled as such which may pose a barrier to trade
- Service sectors are also challenging, free flow of banking and insurance information is being opposed for privacy reasons

RTAs

- Most of the recent involvement in trade have been regional agreements instead of global
- These are reciprocal agreement between two or more partners
- Cover more than half of global trade
- Not the same, have varying levels of scale in terms of what products are involved and varying level of depth in terms of what barriers are raised
- The EU is one of the widest and deepest agreements, but is a significant exception
- The Canada US Mexico agreement is also very deep but not quite as broad
- Most agreements are broad, but shallow
- Free trade agreements are the most common forms of agreements
- Customs unions are the next step up, also unify external tariffs
- Common markets share factors of production between members
- In theory this includes labour
- The next is the economic union, with a common currency, tax rates, and monetary and fiscal policy (European Union partially, not all countries use the Euro)
- These regional agreements have raised concerns about the global economy being separated into distinct blocs
- Where world trade fails, islands of preferred economic cooperation emerge
- Not all agreements are created equal, some agreements involve more powerful states than others
- While Mercosur and the AfCFTA each account for about 2-3% of global GDP
- EU consists of 17.9%, CUSMA is 28%, and the upcoming Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) will account for 30.5%

Inequality in the World Economy

- The WTO agreement is less comprehensive compared to the RTAs, therefore countries in a regional agreement have the advantage compared to those on the outside
- Significant trade agreements exist on basically every continent
- When a country is only in the WTO, your product is at a disadvantage compared to countries in RTAs
- Therefore countries outside the system face higher barriers and receive less trade
- This is especially prevalent in the larger agreements
- On top of this, countries not even in the WTO face much larger issues
- This system specifically disadvantages poorer countries that don't have the clout to contribute to trade agreements
- From a critical perspective, this system is unacceptable even though it does increase trade efficiency
- Bilateral agreements are one way for poorer countries to get an advantage, but only if they produce a strategic resource
