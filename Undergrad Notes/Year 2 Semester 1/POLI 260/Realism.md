Origins

- The origins of realism are rooted in the concepts of "power politics" or "Realpolitik"
- Realism is considered the oldest out of all the theories of global politics
- Realists claim that their ideology emerged from works as early as Thucydides and Kautilya
- They also take influence from Sun Tzu, Machiavelli, and Hobbes
- All of these thinkers despite their differences, all expressed similar views on politics and power

Assumptions

- Realists believe that the state is the most important actor in global politics
- They recognize other actors, but overwhelmingly focus on the state in all matters
- States are considered to be rational, meaning they always make decisions by seeking to maximize utility, and deliberate over decisions extensively
- They are also unitary, meaning they make decisions as one unified front, despite any divisions that may exist within that state
- The government represents the wishes of the whole population, it is unitary
- Realists see the world as an assortment of various states, with the power of those states determining their importance
- Realists also believe that the main goal of the state is survival
- The defence of the state and the advancement of it's status on the global scale is the goal of all states and to act otherwise is immoral
- All leaders should primarily seek to secure security for the state
- The third concept realists believe in is that the world of global politics is anarchic, and each state follows the principles of self help
- There is no government present on the world stage comparable to the government of a state
- Because of this, the world is effectively anarchy, with no enforcement of norms, rules, and laws
- Realists recognize that these global laws exist, but there is no system of enforcement
- Because of this, states "self-help", they depend only on themselves to ensure their security
- This mainly means arming themselves

Anarchy

- Anarchy is absolutely core to the realist school of thought and makes up the system of IR
- A system where war is inevitable, and no other state can be truly trusted
- It's possible, but very difficult as there are no consequences for betraying trust
- Realists like to use the model of Jean-Jacques Rousseau's Stag Hunt
- In it, five self interested hunters that all provide for their own communities, group together to hunt a stag, a bigger animal than they could kill on their own
- One hunter has the choice to either go through with the hunt, or kill a rabbit instead and let the stag escape
- Say another hunter also sees the rabbit and has the same choice to make
- Realists state that this hunter would take the easier kill, and let the other hunters go home empty handed
- Similar to the prisoner's dilemma, the point is that people cannot trust others to not act selfishly, and as such they should always act selfishly
- However, this assessment ignores the societal consequences of acting selfishly
- It also ignores possible power dynamics that may exist in the group

Cooperation

- Realists explain the existence of cooperation on a global scale as simply being an outcome of states acting in self interest
- Cooperation only happens if it's in the self interest of all states involved
- This is why alliances and trade unions come and go
- The only reason the UN exists is because the states that make it up think it's in their interest

Characters of Realism

- There are many different faces to realism, and this is important to understand
- Classical realism focuses on the idea that humans are naturally evil and selfish, and this is why the world is how it is
- Structural realists or neorealists believe that structure is the reason for everything on the global scale and is the cause for all decisions
- Neoclassical realism considers the human elements of balance of power and the patterns of mistrust that emerge as a result
- English school realism believes that there is some sort of society of states within the anarchy of global politics
- Most states follow norms, cooperate and trust eachother most of the time, which creates a social layer to the anarchy

Realism and Power

- Realists define power as the ability to get someone to do something they would not otherwise do
- Above all else, realists value hard power, which is military might and economic power
- The more of an advantage you have over other nations in this regard, the more important you are
- When looking at the history of global politics, realists focus on the states with the most hard power, and what happened to them
- Sees history as a cycle of power ebbing and flowing, with one dominant hegemon at any given point
- "King of the Hill" approach to global history
- Realism is a theoretical framework, not a political platform, they're not to be used for directing policy most of the time
- However, realism suggests that your policy should focus on accumulating power and acting independently of other states
- If you have power, watch vigilantly for other states growing in power
- Global politics is a zero-sum game, meaning there are only winners and losers, power is in limited supply
- Realism rejects the influence of globalization on the world
- Despite it's efforts to de-emphasize the state, it was created by states and it will not replace them
- Realists also talk frequently about the decline of the United States and the rise of China
- From the realist perspective, we're about to see the American hegemon dethroned by China
- The growth of China has been incredibly rapid, they have completely revamped their economy in the past 30 years and may soon directly challenge the US
- They are also in the process of unifying East Asia under their control
- However, they are encircled by many other powers in the region
- Their growth rates are still promising but declining
- Despite the many benefits that China offers to it's neighbors, they come with the caveat of submitting to Chinese influence, which many states reject
- Despite their astronomic growth, China is struggling to acquire reliable allies
- However, if an alliance between Russia and China actually happens, this would be solved
