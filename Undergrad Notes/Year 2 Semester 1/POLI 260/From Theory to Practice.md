- LIEO was created in the aftermath of WWII, created to prevent Weimar Germany from happening again
- Guiding principles were Trade and Comparative Advantage, Financial System, and Governance, Institutions, and Rules
- Financial systems would attempt to remedy vast differences between currencies, reduce inflationary pressures, and allow free flow of capitals globally
- US hegemony was recognized and taken into account when creating this new order
- The US dollar was the most robust currency at the time and was established as the global standard

Hegemony

- This period of history is known as the US hegemonic moment
- A hegemony is a states ability to dominate the rules/arrangements of global politics and economy
- This is a part of hegemonic stability theory
- The basic idea is that stability of norms and rules can only be ensured if a preponderate power is supporting your order
- If the hegemon is declining, there may be competition around norms with new groups pushing their own agendas
- The US has been the hegemon championing the LIEO for decades
- The economic chaos between WWI and WWII was the cause of a dominant hegemonic economy
- Of course, if the US's influence truly is declining, than we are looking towards a period of time where the existing norms of global politics may be changed
- The US lends great stability to the existing international institutions
- The presence of the United States as a hegemon was pretty widely accepted, most countries outside of the Soviet Bloc recognized US authority
- In the aftermath of the Cold War, the LIEO was considered to have won as opposed to the forces of communism
- The Soviet Union and its allies were considered as threats to the ideals of western countries
- The LIEO wasn't just about preventing future threats and banding states together, it was also about creating a unified front to contain communism
- Negotiations between states became more and more common with the rise of the LIEO
- Treaties for free trade, peace treaties, cooperation on global issues
- New organizations are being formed to this day

Managing the LIEO

- Promoting confidence in free trade is key, getting countries on board is essential
- By engaging in comparative advantage, states are giving up manufacturing in very valuable fields and trust in free trade is necessary
- The backing of an economy as powerful as the US is essential for building trust
- Barriers to trade must be eliminated, especially tariffs
- Financial stability of currencies is valuable, contracts must be guaranteed over time
- Flow of investment must be directed to appropriate places to maximize innovation
- International trade must be regulated to ensure abuses of power don't occur
- Again, the backing of the hegemon does a lot to enforce international trade laws
- If all the most powerful actors buy in, the rest will follow
- Institutions are needed, organizations for management, forums for discussion, and groups that collect data and present reports are all required

Free Trade in Practice

- First order of business for free trade is reducing tariffs
- This is still the principle focus of many free trade agreements
- Tariffs have become rare in some places, for example US and Canada have barely any tariffs between them, and the same goes for the EU
- Tariffs are usually imposed to prevent foreign goods from competing with the domestic production of that good
- In comparative advantage, if another company is better at producing a good than another, instead of imposing tariffs the prevent cheaper foreign goods, remove them and stop producing that good
- Non-discrimination argues that all countries that join international trade organizations are to be treated equally
- A country cannot shut out a certain countries product from their economy
- Most favoured nation status is applied to every state in the agreement
- This must be reciprocated if all countries are to benefit
- All goods imported into a country must receive the national treatment
- This means that all imported groups will be treated the same as their domestic equivalent
- This applies to all aspects, including advertisement, supply chains, inspection, etc.
- These are called non-tariff barriers and are eliminated by the national treatment
- Most current arguments about tariffs are centred around non-tariff barriers
- Traditional tariffs are limited to states with powerful lobbies or ones that have very important domestic industries
- Subsidies, licenses, customs procedures, quotas, export restrictions, local content requirements , and nationalism are all common NTBs
- A good example of this was the Japanese rice harvest failing, which led the government to campaign to convince locals to still buy Japanese rice by painting foreign products as inferior

Financial Flows

- The goal of free financial flow is to get money from those who have it to those who need it'
- After WWII the US had money, and everyone else needed it
- The Marshall Plan was an enormous donation of American money to rebuild Europe and some places in Asia, namely Japan after WWII
- Foreign countries were encouraged to export goods to America, where valuable US dollars would be spent on foreign goods
- All of this was anchored on the American dollar
- 13 billion US dollars to rebuild the world isn't worth much if it's not stable
- Therefore the Bretton Woods System was established based on fixed exchange rates and the US Dollar
- The goal was to peg all the world's currencies to the most robust and stable currency, the US dollar
- The US dollar would be pegged to gold at a fixed rate of $35 per ounce (in 1944)
- All currencies in the world with the exception of the Soviet ruble and later the Chinese yuan
- The US gold reserve is a remnant of the Bretton Woods system, a physical representation of wealth
- In addition, the Bretton Woods system established the IMF and the World Bank
- Both are still present and incredibly influential
- The Bretton Woods System collapsed
- Initially, demand for dollars exceeded supply
- A central unit of account was incredibly appealing for many state's financial institutions
- As American dollars continued to flow out of the country, the US trade deficit gradually grew
- This began to have negative effects on the American economy, even though it succeeded in rebuilding the rest of the world
- People from elsewhere in the world who got rich in American dollars started buying up American real estate and industry
- The US started printing money in the 1960s to compensate, and inflation followed
- At one point, accountants realized that the ratio of US dollars in circulation to gold was becoming increasingly distorted
- The overhang was so unacceptable high they had to get rid of some of the money in circulation
- In 1971, Richard Nixon went on television to announce that the convertibility of US dollars to gold would be suspended temporarily
- At this time, every person involved in finance was keeping all of their wealth in US dollars, it was the central unit of account
- The hegemon had signaled that they were no longer willing to back the system
- Today the value of currencies aren't based on a fixed peg system
- Currency is based on estimates of the strength/performance of the economy
- Some states still fix their currencies via multilateral agreements
- Some former French colonies peg their currency to the franc
- States can also peg their currency unilaterally, Costa Rica still uses the US dollar for everything, their currency is pegged to the US dollar to the point that you can use physical US dollars in Costa Rica

IFS Today

- Financial flows are encouraged mainly by currency markets
- Three main forms
- Currency transactions, could be trading to speculate on currency or just paying between countries
- Foreign Direct Investment is physical investment in foreign countries, investment in infrastructure
- Portfolio Investment is the stock market
- Due to modern currency markets, you can invest your money in companies worldwide
- At any time of day, there is a financial market open somewhere in the world
- The average daily turnover before Covid was 6.6 trillion US dollars
- 88% of all transactions involved the US dollar
- Despite the system not being tied to the dollar anymore, US dominance is still clear
- Currency markets encourage capital flows and are widely endorsed by liberals
- Exchange rates are political, its best for your currency to be slightly lower than the currency of your trading partner
- That way, your product in cheaper in their market
- China fixes the Yuan artificially low to compete in the American market
- Obviously the American government is unhappy with this

Cryptocurrencies

- Prior to the popularity of cryptocurrency, financial technology companies were already prominent
- Companies like VISA and PayPal were incredibly prominent and valuable
- Digital transfer of capital was the new standard
- Cryptocurrencies are designed to mimic three values: use for payment, store of value, and unit of account
- Cryptocurrencies can be completely decentralized, or can be pegged to existing currencies
- Some central banks are considering creating their own digital currencies
- Crypto is a completely independent way of financial transaction
- Wealth is transferred from wallet to wallet with no intermediary
- This makes the task of monitoring flows of wealth in a country infinitely more difficult
- The ultimate goal of cryptocurrencies is to supplant the existing financial system
- Free, mutually beneficial exchange backed by the blockchain
- In September 2022, all cryptocurrencies across the market crashed drastically
- Things called stablecoins have been created that are attracting the attention of governments and corporations
- Stablecoins are cryptocurrencies linked to another currency (eg. US Dollar), commodity (gold or oil), other cryptocurrencies, or any other financial product (portfolio or stock)
- Gives the efficiencies of crypto without the volatility
- Governments are seeking to regulate cryptocurrencies to ensure that their volatility doesn't cause another financial crisis
- Cryptocurrencies can either be beneficial or incredibly destabilizing
- China has moved quickly to ban the mining and use of all cryptocurrencies
- This was likely due to the immense strain on the Chinese power grid that bitcoin mining had caused
- Crypto also posed a threat the government's ability to control the flow of currency within it's borders
- However, they are now looking at launching a stablecoin version of the renminbi to compete on the international market
- The EU is also looking at creating a similar digital version of the euro
- With more and more countries launching coins, a new field of political economic competition may open up
- Several large Russian companies have been able to evade economic sanctions by funneling money through crypto markets
- Ukraine has issued a cryptocurrency, and raised money through donations to help the war effort
- This money is nearly untraceable, and there is no way to tell what they will do with this money
- A large number of the world's prominent central banks are cooperating to create government digital currencies
- Despite the many advantages of cryptocurrencies, there is the issue of their incredible energy consumption
- Already, crypto networks use a significant amount of electricity and produce tons of carbon emissions
- After being walled out of China, Russian crypto miners moved to Kazakhstan
- This caused electrical issues, inflation, and widespread riots
- Political instability caused by cryptocurrency
