What is Security

- We think of security as three main freedoms
- Freedom from external threats, or the ability to resist these threats
- Security can either be a lack of threats, or the ability to defend against threats
- Freedom from fear both on the individual and state level
- A state should not fear other states, and it's citizens should not live in fear either
- Freedom from want can refer to either concrete needs like food and water, or abstract needs like human rights, or political power
- We look at threats on the different levels of analysis
- Individuals, groups, states, and systems
- Human security refers to focusing on the security of individual people
- A group can be threatened, if a group is at risk of ceasing to exist, it is threatened
- Systematic changes can undermine the rights of certain groups
- Prejudice, attacks on identity, all these things are threats to groups
- A state may feel like they are physically at risk, their borders or territory are in peril
- They can also feel threatened culturally, their way of life or media may be at risk of being reduced
- Arms races and global terrorism can pose threats to states
- States can also pose threats to their own citizens
- The ideal outcome is that all individuals, groups, and states have all three freedoms
- The referent is very important, threat is only perceived, it has no physical reality
- The actors are what determines how threat is felt
- Some groups or individuals within a state may feel threats differently
- Threat and intent are not necessarily linked, threat can be perceived even when there is no intent to harm
- A classic example of this would be climate change, there is no intention to harm yet a threat exists
- Another example would be a global pandemic like COVID-19

Security Agendas

- Over the past few decades, security agendas have changed drastically
- Rather than a focus on an eastern and western bloc, we now discuss the forces of politics between several different powers and regions
- Conflict has gone from high intensity to low intensity, and new forms of warfare have emerged
- Many cold war alliances have fallen apart (barring NATO), and have been replaced by institutions
- The previous focus on hard power is now shifting towards social instability and collapse of culture
- Nuclear deterrence is no longer the focus, we just monitor proliferation
- National liberation of colonized countries has already happened, now we deal with the aftermath that caused at risk states, and civil wars everywhere
- Instead of arms control being bilateral, we now talk about global arms control
- In almost every aspect, the current security agenda has expanded compared to the cold war

Interstate Issues

- A large part of the current security agenda is still armed conflict between states
- Just look at the significance of the Russian invasion of Ukraine
- All over the top of south America, east and west Africa, and in the middle East and southeast Asia, there is armed conflict and fatalities
- There are several tense relationships between great powers currently
- Conflict between great powers has historically caused large scale conflict
- Russia and the US, China and the US
- India and China, China and most of southeast Asia
- The current prediction for the next place war will break out is the south China sea
- There are many overlapping land claims in the region, and the stakes are high
- Under United Nations law any island you own gives you sovereignty over a 200 nautical mile radius around it
- Iran and Israel
- Iran and Saudi Arabia
- Nuclear weapons are sadly still a relevant topic in modern interstate relations
- Currently the US and Russia have the most, in the mid thousands
- The UK, France, and China have a few hundred each, and Israel, Pakistan, India, and North Korea all have a few as well
- World military spending in 2020 was estimated just below 2 trillion US, corresponding to 2.4 percent of global GDP
- In comparison, the UN regular budget for 2020 was just over 3 billion
- 2020 was the highest year ever in terms of military spending
- US is 40% of this, China is about 13%, everyone else is somewhere in the 3% and below range
- India is at 3.7%, Russia is at 3.1%
- Related to this, arms races are a source of concern in interstate security
- The value of the global arms trade industry was estimated at 118 billion US in 2019
- US was the biggest exporter, Saudi Arabia was the biggest importer
- India was close behind
- Russia was also a big exporter, but this has changed
- Energy security is also an increasing source of concern
- Pipelines, SLOCs (sea lines of communication), and maritime trade
- War is changing, cyber warfare and hybrid warfare are on the rise
- Hybrid warfare is a combination of conventional and unconventional warfare
- This can include misinformation, economic warfare, support of local unrest groups, etc.
- Overwhelmingly, most wars since WWII have been intrastate, or intrastate with the support of an outside power
- Some interstate wars have occured, but the vast majority are intrastate
- "Failed" and "Fragile" states are an increasing concern as well
- Fragile is considered the more respectful term
- We classify states as fragile when they have a weak central government, widespread corruption, limited or no public services, refugees, and economic decline
- When states collapse, chaos inevitably ensues, so preventing these states from failure is paramount
- Social instability, separatism, and irredentism are also related outcomes of failure of states

Transnational Concerns

- Terrorism is the number one concern in transnational security
- According to the global terrorism index, deaths from terrorism are on the decline, but are still worryingly high
- The vast majority of terrorist attacks occur in just a few countries
- Terrorism is commonly defined as the use or threat of illegal force by a non-state actor to attain some sort of goal through fear, coercion or intimidation
- Weapons proliferation is also a problem
- Apart from just nuclear weapons, there are many other types of weapons to consider
- CBRWs, Ballistic and Cruise missiles, Conventional weapons, and the biggest killers of all, small arms and light weapons
- Organized crime, especially drug trafficking, is a major concern
- About 5.5% of the world population was reported as taking drugs in 2019
- Cocaine, opioids, and amphetamines are considered the three main problem groups
- A big issue with data collection on drug use is the distinction between pharmaceutical drugs and illegal drugs
- Low income countries are disproportionally affected by drug use
- Most cocaine originates from central America and the top of South America, while opioids originate from central Asia
- Human trafficking is another concern in organized crime
- Global phenomenon, there is flow to and from most continents
- 50% of all victims trafficked are for the purpose of sexual exploitation, 38% for forced labour, and the rest is around 1%
- Piracy is still a genuine concern, especially off the coast of West Africa, and Oceania
- Private security companies are becoming increasingly popular worldwide, especially in light of the invasion of Ukraine
- Forcible displacement is still relevant, around 82.4 million people were displaced in 2020 with 48 million of those being internally displaced
- Diseases
- Environmental security includes climate change, but also resource wars
- How will climate issues destabilize countries?
- Human security/human rights
- Security is becoming increasingly transnational
