Nuclear Fission

- Nuclear technology relies on the concepts of fission and fusion
- Nuclear fission is when stable radioactive elements like Uranium become unstable
- The nucleus will split into several nuclei, releasing energy
- This energy is absorbed by other uranium atoms, creating a chain reactions
- The larger the mass of uranium, the amount of energy released increases exponentially
- There are two main designs for nuclear warheads
- Highly Enriched Uranium (uranium-235) and plutonium are the two materials used
- 2-6 kg of either material is enough for a bomb
- Less than 2kg won't go critical, more than 6 kg is considered too destructive
- Two main methods are the gun-type and the implosion method
- In the gun method, a conventional explosive goes off that forces two pieces of subcritical material to collide
- This method is basically obsolete
- The implosion method has several high explosives arranged around a core of fissile material, same concept
- Both have been used, "Little Boy" was a uranium gun-type, and "Fat Man" was an plutonium implosion type
- First test of nuclear weapons was the Trinity Test on July 16, 1945
- Hiroshima was bombed on August 6, 1945, and Nagasaki was bombed on August 9
- Nuclear warheads are measured in terms of tons of TNT
- A kiloton is equivalent to 1,000 tons of TNT exploding
- As warheads developed, the megaton was coined, which is equal to 1,000,000 tons of TNT
- The largest warhead ever detonated had a yield of 57-62 megatons, the Tsar Bomba
- Little Boy had a yield of 13-15 kilotons, Fat Man had a yield of 20-22 kilotons
- Compared to today's nuclear arsenals, these two bombs are tiny in comparison
- What Russia refers to as tactical nuclear weapons are bombs with yields similar to these

Nuclear Fusion

- Fusing H3 and H2 creates H4
- This free neutron releases an enormous amount of energy
- The theoretical output of a fusion reaction is unlimited, nuclear fusion is the process that occurs in hydrogen that makes up the Sun
- Fusion bombs require a fission warhead
- The fission explosion emits x-rays that irradiate polystyrene foam which turns into plasma
- This energy pushes another core into fusion
- By adding more stages, the yield can be increased infinitely
- Most warheads only have two stages
- Any more than this, and the bomb will be unreasonably large
- The knowledge to create a nuclear bomb is easy to acquire, the means are not

Effects

- Unlike conventional explosives, nuclear bombs also produce radioactive aftereffects
- Immediately after a nuclear explosion, neutrons, gamma rays and x-rays are emitted
- Then comes heat in the range between 3000-4000 degrees
- Next is the blast wave, which can propagate through the air, ground, water etc.
- In the air, this can create winds peaking at 1000 km/h
- A nuclear bomb has two blast waves, the vacuum created by the detonation causes the atmosphere to rush back in to fill the gap
- Then comes the fallout and residual radiation
- Dust and air around the impact become contaminated with radioactive isotopes
- Most of these elements take centuries to decay, and spread worldwide
- Almost everywhere in the world today, we can find radioactive signatures originating from nuclear explosions in the 1940s
- A theory raised by many is the concept of a nuclear winter
- The theory is that in the case of a large nuclear war, the dust kicked up will block out the sun, making the earth inhospitable
- This has never been proved or really confirmed but it is likely
- Conventional explosives just create heat and a blast wave, no radiation

Nuclear Testing

- Nuclear testing was seen as a political tool, and was used as a status symbol
- As a result, 2056 nuclear tests have been run worldwide since 1945
- 528 of these were detonated above ground
- The rest were contained below ground
- US conducted the most tests, followed by the USSR, France, and the UK/China
- The USSR detonated the most aboveground, followed by the US, France, and China
- The underground tests were safer, but weren't free from consequences
- They could cause cave-ins, and had the possibility of contaminating groundwater
- That's why they were tested mainly in Nevada, there was little groundwater and the bedrock was very stable
- The first sign that North Korea was testing nuclear weapons was abnormal radioactive isotopes in rivers near a civilian reactor

Delivery Systems

- Aircraft are mainly used, in Japan gravity bombs were dropped by bombers onto the target
- Today, bombers would use nuclear-equipped cruise missiles
- ICBMs or Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles are missiles that rise into the atmosphere and then fall onto their target within a range of thousands of kilometers
- These are made with the same technology used to launch satellites
- Submarines can launch cruise missiles equipped with nuclear warheads, including from below the surface
- Short, Medium, and Intermediate Missiles are also used
- India and Pakistan have these, since they are neighboring countries
- A missile fires from one of these countries can hit the opposing capital within 7 minutes
- Cruise missiles can also be launched from ground installations and belowground installations
- The hypersonic glide system is a hypothetical missile that launches into the atmosphere and glides at extremely high speeds in the upper atmosphere with a glider before dropping
- The orbital glide release is similar, but is launched from a satellite
- Hypersonic glide systems will be functional in the next ten years
- Both techniques are near undetectable
- Early warning satellites are constantly orbiting the earth and watching for rocket plumes
- NORAD can detect a missile launch from anywhere in the world within 5 minutes
- Satellites can also track the orbiter and the warhead of nuclear missiles
- Ground-based RADAR networks also watch for missiles
- American radar dishes bounce their signal off the atmosphere to detect missiles over the horizon
- Israel also has very sophisticated RADAR networks, part of the Iron Dome
- This kind of high tech RADAR is fairly rare
- Launch platforms for hypersonic glide are undetectable by radar, and are difficult to track in the atmosphere because they don't follow a ballistic trajectory, and move at such high speeds

Deterrence

- Deterrence is defined as a policy of preventing action by an opponent by threatening them with a risk they are unwilling to take
- The goal of deterrence is to stop action before it happens
- As stockpiles increased, the concept of any country actually winning a nuclear war became near impossible
- The destructive power of both sides was so great that any conflict would be an apocalypse
- Therefore, nuclear weapons paradoxically exist to prevent war
- There are two forms of deterrence
- Deterrence of denial is having defenses so strong that the cost necessary to win isn't worth the reward
- This has existed since siege warfare
- Deterrence by punishment became prominent after 1945
- The idea is that you utterly destroy anyone who opposes you
- This became a problem once both sides can do this
- Therefore the concept of Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD) was born
- If either side launched nuclear weapons, the opposing side would launch a counterattack so devastating that the country would be annihilated
- As the cold war continued, each side developed more warheads and delivery systems, even past the point of enough to ensure MAD
- Redundancies on top of redundancies were developed to ensure that no matter what, they would be able to retaliate in the case of a first strike
- Both sides had a "launch on warning" policy, meaning that as soon as satellites detected incoming missiles, the missiles will be launched
- This policy still exists between the US and Russia, India and Pakistan, and soon Iran and Israel
- Deterrence creates what cold war scholars called an "uneasy peace"
- There are many critics of deterrence, saying that deterrence is not guaranteed to work
- Today, Russia is threatening nuclear action if anyone interferes in Ukraine
- Almost the exact same thing occured during the cold war
- There is the idea going around that if a nuclear missile can be deployed without triggering all out war, the fear of nuclear weapons will be heightened so greatly that objectives could be achieved
