- Conflict management in IR focuses on managing conflicts between states, armed groups, and international organizations
- There is a distinction between the study of war versus the study of peace
- Peace is defined as the absence of war, so people tend to study war in order to achieve peace
- Peace studies scholars reject this notion, and argue that true peace is the absence of causes of war, not just war itself
- We should seek to address the root causes, not just the symptoms
- Most of international relations education uses a war studies lens

Objectives

- Preventing or minimizing conflict is the primary objective, and as an extension we also want to prevent war
- In the case that war breaks out, we want to control or contain this war
- Keep the intensity and scope of the conflict as low as possible
- Prevent wars from spreading, and prevent destructive weapons from being used
- If this fails, we seek to terminate them as fast as possible
- How do you stop a war?
- We want to resolve the conflict and ensure it won't flare up in the future
- Remove the root causes of the war in order to prevent it from resuming

Conflict Management Spectrum

- Used by the United Nations
- Demonstrates how the international system as a whole thinks about conflict
- The first tool on the spectrum is conflict prevention
- These are usually diplomatic efforts to avoid escalation, and ensure that a conflict doesn't become an armed conflict or crisis
- Three tiers, conflict, then crisis, then armed conflict
- The next tool is peacemaking/diplomacy
- The UN defines peacemaking as diplomatic efforts to cease violence in the case of an armed conflict
- Usually advocating for ceasefire, or at least a pause
- However, in the US this phrase is more often used to refer to stopping a conflict via military force
- The next tool is peace operations, or peacekeeping
- This is the use of force to deter armed conflict, either with military or police force
- Can also refer to protecting civilians
- Peace enforcement is the next tool, and refers to the use of military force to terminate hostilities, and is usually used as a negotiating tool in peacemaking efforts
- Peace building is a tool related to peace studies, and focuses on preventing root causes
- This can include reconstruction, reconciliation, building institutions and shoring up economies and political systems

Conflict Management Instruments

- The preferred tool of conflict management is diplomacy
- Ambassadors, diplomats, embassies, envoys, all of these things are embedded in the international system and are respected by most countries
- The ideal solution is that conflicts are solved via diplomacy, representatives of each party sit down at a table and discussed
- Most negotiations are done in secret, to avoid political consequences for the parties involved
- Usually third party mediators are used to facilitate communication between the parties
- Mediation is a core part of conflict management
- Switzerland is usually used as the third party, but other countries are also used
- Arbitration is also used, which is when both parties agree to hand over matters to a third party arbitrator, who decides the outcome of the conflict
- Agreements must be made in advance to ensure the decision of the arbitrator is upheld
- This is usually used in trade conflicts, but is sometimes seen in other situations as well
- Diplomacy efforts can occur at any point during a conflict, including before and after
- The Oslo Accord between Israel and Palestine is considered one of the greatest achievements of third party mediation, but has since collapsed
- Arms control and disarmament is another tool of conflict management
- Used mainly to discourage conflicts, or employed in the aftermath of conflicts
- Arms control is reducing the number of weapons, disarmament is banning entire categories of weapons
- There have been many treaties on any number of weapons, whether multilateral or bilateral
- Chemical weapons, biological weapons, and environmental modification weapons have been banned entirely
- Many treaties have attempted to limit nuclear weapons to middling success
- In theory, the consequence of breaking treaties is being attacked by the other countries signing the treaty
- International law is also an important tool that comes in four forms
- Treaties are the main form, legally binding documents involving countries
- Legal precedent is similar to common law, previous international legal decisions are upheld
- Adjudication is different form arbitration, because the decision is made by an international court
- By submitting an issue to a court justice, you are agreeing to whatever decision is made
- Arbitration is third party, adjudication is a court
- A good example of international law is the Geneva Convention(s)
- Outlined the core principles of warfare, detailing protection of wounded, civilians, POWs, etc.
- The International Court of Justice is made up of states, while the International Criminal Court deals with individuals, although it is very difficult to bring someone to trial there
- Representatives of the International Court visit conflict zones to ensure the laws are being upheld, including the Geneva Convention
- International institutions are also key to conflict management
- The UN general assembly and security council are important forums for interaction between countries
- Permanent representatives of the assembly interact with eachother which minimizes misunderstandings between states
- Whether informal or formal, these interactions have weight
- While the General Assembly has no real power, its decisions have social significance
- Meanwhile, the decisions made by the Security Council do have significant weight
- Assuming there is a sufficient number of votes and no P5 members veto, it will pass
- However, even the act of a P5 member being forced to veto a motion has significance
- The UN Security Council determines peacekeeping missions, there are 12 active peacekeeping missions that the security council has agreed on
- Sanctions are another common tool of conflict management
- These are the limitation or outright end of economic relations between countries
- Many have argued that there is little between diplomacy and war in conflict management, and sanctions were created to fill the gap
- Sanctions often don't work, and only end up hurting the innocent civilians of a country
- In most cases where sanctions are required, the government officials in question are too privileged to really be affected
- Targeted sanctions are required in order to properly persuade officials
- Current sanction programs are being levelled against Iran and North Korea to deter them from developing nuclear weapons
- The most relevant system of sanctions currently active is sanctions against Russia
- In order for sanctions to be effective, most or all strong economic powers must participate, or else the country being sanctioned will just go elsewhere for trade
- The final tool is peace operations
- Highest level of conflict management, involves military force
- Peacekeeping, peace enforcement and other missions authorized by the UNSC carried out by third parties all fall under this umbrella
- Includes observer missions that may not even require weapons, or just service pistols
- Used to signify the presence of a third party
- Traditional peacekeeping missions involve light arms and small numbers of soldiers
- In more volatile situations, more complex peacekeeping missions are carried out using vehicles, artillery, heavy weapons etc.
- Peace enforcement actions or stability operations become almost indistinguishable from wars
- In this case the situation is considered an armed conflict and the laws of war apply
- Robust use of military force to end a conflict
- And then peace building efforts, which rarely need any sort of force other than just basic policing services
- The UN and the regional organizations, alliances and coalitions associated with it have conducted 78 multilateral operations in 2021 alone

Peacekeeping

- Modern peace operations are based on a set of principles that are widely recognized but not necessarily enshrined in international law
- There must be a peace to keep
- Consent from both parties is preferred
- Impartiality is also preferred, unless there is a clear aggressor
- It should be lightly armed if possible
- Also should have a UNSC mandate
- And the personnel must be from contributing countries, the UN has no army
