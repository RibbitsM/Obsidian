- A pandemic means global spread and high infection rates
- Epidemics are more regional or affect a smaller range of countries
- Outbreaks are more contained and have a smaller number of cases
- No hard limits to what each term refers to
- The International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses dubbed severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 on February 11 2020
- The WHO adopted the name COVID-19, short for coronavirus disease 2019
- Declared a public health emergency of international concern in January, and a global pandemic on March 11, 2020
- Pandemics and global politics go hand in hand
- The Epidemiology on how to deal with Plague, written in the 1500s, refers to masking
- Main instrument of preventing the 1918 Spanish flu was masking and quarantine
- Some other major pandemics since WWII are Ebola, H1N1 and H2N2, and SARS
- International Sanitary Conferences started in 1851
- Established international cooperation on spread of diseases
- The League of Nations had a health organization as well
- Predecessors to the WHO established in 1948 in Geneva
- Mandate of the WHO is to advocate for universal healthcare, monitor health risks, coordinate responses, and promote health and wellbeing
- The International Health Regulations are the teeth of the WHO and detail the legal obligations of states in terms of response to infectious disease
- Comprise four core elements
- Ability to detect, assess, report, and respond to infectious disease
- Member states must develop all four capabilities up to national standards
- They will also cooperate with other states in these matters
- Focal point, but not the only actor
- Another important actor is Gavi, which deals with vaccines
- The Global Preparedness Monitoring Board (GPMG) ensures preparedness for global health crises
- In its first report, urgent action was called for in a wide variety of sectors
- This was released in 2019, just before the pandemic
- In addition, the Global Health Security Index ranks countries based on response capacity for epidemics
- Average score out of 100 was 40.2
- National health security was fundamentally weak and unprepared for a potential pandemic
- In the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic it is clear that the health emergency system needs major reforms
- Effective leadership makes a huge difference in pandemic response
- Poor leaders were one of the biggest reasons for insufficient responses
- Even if we had the best leadership, the pandemic still would have happened
- However, it was a lot worse than it could have been
- Donald Trump, Xi Jinping, Boris Johnson, Vladimir Putin, and Jair Bolsonaro all caused huge problems for COVID-19 responses
- Much blame for poor global response was shifted to the WHO, although it was responsible for some failings
- This was mainly because the WHO does not have the funding required to carry out its obligations
- This is still a problem today
- In addition, many member states were not actually fulfilling their obligations under the IHR
- The WHO itself also has a weak chain of command, a lack of personnel, and indecisive leadership
- The WHO also does not have infrastructure to track the spread of diseases, and everyone switched over to the John Hopkins tracking mechanism early on
- On top of this, the WHO waited to declare COVID as a pandemic until March 11 2020
- By this time there were more than 100,000 cases in 110 countries
- The WHO also did not recommend travel restrictions right away due to pressure from member states

The G-7

- Failed to meet until early March, well after global spread occurred
- No concrete agreements emerged
- In addition, support was undermined due to disagreements
- The US demanded that the virus be referred to as "the Wuhan coronavirus" in an attempt to level criticism at China
- The G-20 didn't do much better, and met in late March, nearly three months in
- UN security council also had troubles, China blocked all resolutions about the pandemic by arguing that it was outside of the council's mandate
- The US and China were especially important in COVID geopolitics and were constantly in opposition
- The US took a nationalist approach, and framed COVID as a threat against the US, not the global community
- Also took an especially anti-Chinese position, referring to COVID as a "foreign" and "Chinese" virus
- Trump closes borders to foreigners who has recently traveled to China in January, and also suspended air travel from Europe with no notice
- Also threatened to withdraw support from the WHO, which would have fatally undermined its operations but this was reversed by the Biden government
- The Trump administration was also heavily pushing the "lab leak" theory as the de facto explanation for the origin of COVID-19
- The Chinese government didn't fare much better, information about COVID was revealed very slowly
- On the upside, they were able to sequence the genome very quickly and distribute it globally
- Turned down offers from the WHO and CDC for assistance
- The WHO chose not to declare an emergency based on lack of Chinese information
- China did not cooperate with the G-20 and the UNSC and spread misinformation on the origin of the virus
- China also spread a theory that the virus leaked from an American laboratory
- Did not cooperate fully with the WHO when a study on the origins of COVID 19 was held
- The final decision is that COVID was most likely introduced through an intermediary, and that the lab leak theory was possible but very unlikely

WHO Response

- WHO main tool was the ACT Accelerator
- Multilateral approach involving many actors
- Consisted of four pillars
- Diagnostics: tests and detection
- Therapeutics: how to treat patients
- Vaccines: aligned with COVAX facility
- Health Systems Connector: networking component
- The COVAX facility is focused on accelerating vaccine development and guaranteeing equitable distribution of vaccines
- Over 180 countries participated
- Biden brought the US into COVAX in 2021
- Russia is a significant omission from COVAX
- Co-led by GAVI (the Vaccine Alliance), Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness (CEPI), and the WHO
- COVAX is a platform to support research, development, and manufacturing of vaccines
- The COVAX is a buyer of vaccines, and buys vaccines in bulk at a lower negotiated price from manufacturers and distribute those vaccines equally to member nations
- Donor countries pay for the vaccines so developing countries can gain access
- For many countries, COVAX is the only source of vaccines
- Ghana was the first country to receive COVAX vaccines
- COVAX fell very short of the goal of 2 billion doses by the end of 2021, with only 1.4 doses given by March 2022
- 42% of the original pot of money came from Germany, with the US second, followed by Norway, France, the UK and the Netherlands following
- Part of the challenge was the struggle of vaccination campaigns in many states
- This was mainly due to prioritized access based on equity
- Individuals most at risk were given the vaccine sooner
- Price, location, and accessibility of vaccines were also issues
- However the main issue was hesitancy, either in the background or specific hesitancy about COVID vaccines
- As time went on, vaccination rates have risen significantly

Vaccine Nationalism

- Emerged almost immediately in response to vaccination efforts
- Revealed the challenges of collective action during a global emergency
- The US was a preeminent example of vaccine nationalism, no participation in global vaccine efforts was done prior to the Biden administration
- Vaccine nationalism also emerged in Europe, when delays occured in the delivery of the AstraZeneca-Oxford vaccine
- As a result, the EU accused the UK of intentionally withholding vaccines to give to British citizens
- Hoarding vaccine orders also became prominent, with Canada being one of the worst offenders, ordering enough vaccines to vaccinate the population 5 times over
- The governments of India and South Africa requested to the WTO that vaccine-producing countries suspend intellectual property rights to accelerate vaccine production
- This was rejected by the US, UK, and the EU because of the precedent that would be set

Vaccine Inequality

- Infectious diseases have always led to backlash against oppressed groups
- The effects of the pandemic have has disproportionate impacts on people based on occupation, race, gender, sexuality, location, income, etc.
- Wealthy people and wealthy parts of the world rebounded much faster than the poorest
- The pandemic also fuelled racism, mainly anti-asian racism and xenophobia
- Women workers experienced greater unemployment compared to men
- Sectors dominated by women like domestic care, were disproportionately affected
- LGBT, disabled people, and Indigenous people were affected worse too
- Disabled people especially were vulnerable due to pre-existing health conditions raising fatality rates
- Equitable access to vaccines and therapeutic methods was considered paramount
- However, as of early 2021 95% of vaccines had gone to 10 countries, and 130 countries had received zero doses
- Even as vaccine numbers continue to rise, patterns of inequality still pervade

Vaccine Diplomacy

- Usually takes the form of countries signing bilateral deals with a supplier and providing payment/political concession or support on an issue
- Israel, the UAE, Russia, India, and China all participated heavily in vaccine diplomacy
- The motivation was mainly due to rivalry with other states, or a need for support
- For example, recognition of Taiwan, support of Palestine/Iran etc.

COVID and the State

- State power has risen around the world as a result of the pandemic
- Restrictions of travel and migration, limited movement and social restrictions
- Enormous stimulus packages were delivered
- State nationalism also rebounded, states appealed to nationalism and patriotism in order to rally the population and create unity
- However, this created a backlash against groups harmed by nationalism
- The general global consensus is that we need a new way of responding to global pandemic
- This could be a treaty, accord, instrument, process, or something else
