International Relations Theory

- There is no dominant unifying theory in IR
- Several major theories in competition with eachother
- Unlike other disciplines, there is no theory that all scholars agree on
- 5 major theories of International Relations
- Realism, Liberalism, Marxism, Constructivism, and Feminism
- Realism believes in the balance of power and inherent anarchy of IR
- Liberalism believes in an ideal state of democratic interdependence
- Marxism believes in a structure built on exploitation of class and hierarchy
- Constructivism believes that the basic structures of politics are inherently flawed
- Feminism believes in a structure based on gender and dominated by the patriarchy
- These theories are excellent tools, but being too loyal to any one of them is a bad idea
- Use them as interchangeable lenses to examine issues from different perspectives

What is the Problem?

- The main problem that global politics has focused on is ending war
- How do we stop wars that are going on, and prevent nations from going to war in the future?
- Others believe that the main issue is the stability of the global economy
- Are all the needs of people being met? How should we distribute goods and services world wide?
- Another significant issue is climate change
- The world needs to band together and agree and implement a solution to end climate change
- Similar to the economy, another issue is the vast disparity between the richest and poorest people in the world

What can we Learn from the Past?

- There is a general assumption that history will repeat itself and that it indicated future events
- Chamberlain signed the Munich agreement with Hitler, and WWII still broke out
- The lesson people take from this is that we should not appease dictators
- Historical research indicates that Britain and France knew that WWII was coming and the Munich agreement was drafted to buy time for them to prepare

Is the International Community Fragmenting?

- Globalization is an accepted truth of modern politics
- The European Union is considered one of the greatest steps in international cooperation in the past decades
- But in light of Brexit, questions are being raised as to whether integration will continue
- After Covid-19, international trade dropped drastically
- Markets go up and down, they never always rise
- Plane traffic has crashed, airline companies are still trying to recover
- There is a constant steady increase in the number of internet users, with no cliff yet
- Forces of fragmentation are common as well, many mid 19th century countries have been split into smaller and smaller states
- Disruption, Disconnection, Disparity, Destruction, and Discontent are the 5 forces of fragmentation
- All of these pose barriers to international cooperation and integration
- The forces of fragmentation and integration are constantly opposing eachother

What will the Economy look like in the Future?

- Will the global economy continue to grow? Or will it collapse and leave each state to trade in isolation.
- Some of the current main economic powers are the US, the EU, and China
- A common fear is that the tension between these economic blocs may soon become political tension
- Economic alliances are very common worldwide, splitting the globe into several distinct economic areas
- Another theory is that the future of the global economy is Asian
- East Asia has an incredibly overwhelming population advantage compared to the rest of the world
- The RCEP, an east Asian free trade agreement, is on the horizon and approaching rapidly

What will Geopolitics look like in the Future?

- Many people theorize that in the future, the world will be split between the US and China
- For many, many years, the center of power has been in the West, either western Europe or the US
- This may soon change

Is the State in Decline?

- The power of the state has been continuously challenged by the powers of integration and fragmentation
- We may soon see an era where people are no longer primarily loyal to their state
- Corporations, interest groups, races, are all things that people may identify with rather than state

What does "Security" Mean

- What does it mean for a state to be "secure", and how do we achieve it?
- Is security achieved through military might or through a system of treaties and arms control agreements?
- How does a state keep it's citizens safe? What does safe mean?
- How far do the responsibilities of the state go? Do they extend outside of their borders?
