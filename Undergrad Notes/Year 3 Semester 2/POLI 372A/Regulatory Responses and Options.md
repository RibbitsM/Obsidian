**Lecture 1**

- Generally speaking, all kinds of businesses are generally hostile to regulation, and MNCs are not an exception to this
- According to Levy and Prakash, MNCs prefer market enabling regimes at the international level and keep regulation at the national level
- Thanks to the OECD, at the global level MNCs are very well treated
- Regime put in place to encourage investment to spread capital between OECD countries, economic cooperation
- Recall that the Bretton Woods System never put in place an international organization for investment
- The MAI (Multilateral Agreement on Investment) is the only existing example of a regulatory framework for MNCs that was actually supported by the MNCs
- It was created by the OECD and primarily market enabling, although it never manifested
- The fear of "scaring away business" is prevalent, no country wants to regulate because it will end up hurting them economically
- For the state, their primary goal is to preserve the security of their people
- This is not the goal of MNCs, with the exception of security contractors like Blackwater
- However, acting without regard to the social good is generally a good way to get regulated
- Nowadays most MNCs have adapted to this through CSR statements and charity work
- To avoid making an enemy of the state, corporations must adopt a portion of the state's responsibilities
- It's not just states, regulation is closely tied to international organizations
- Historically, the most important of these were the IGOs like the UN although we are now seeing the rise of NGOs like AdBusters in Vancouver
- The general goal of these orgs is not to directly regulate but name and shame MNCs to make them targets of the more powerful states and IGOs
- When we talk about "regimes" in the context of IPE and regulation, this is different than when we use it as a synonym for government
- A regime is defined as the principles, norms, rules, and decision-making procedures around which actor expectations converge in a given issue area of IPE
- These can be trade regimes, monetary regimes, security regimes, sports regimes, etc.
- But what is notably lacking is investment regimes
- As opposed to something like the GATT/WTO for trade, there is no equivalent for investment
- While the WTO is an IGO, it is also a regime and there's an important difference
- The WTO is the manifestation of the global trade regime and is the organization that enshrines and enforces those principles, norms, rules, and decision-making procedures
- You can have a regime without a corresponding IGO, hence we could technically have an investment regime
- Beyond just the WTO there's free trade zones all over the world, regional blocs, bilateral agreements etc.
- The WTO is the symbol and face of the regime but not the whole thing
- Most rules in the OECD and global regimes as a whole are aspirational
- There is very little in the way of consequences for breaking these rules and are instead mainly suggestions
- Some may argue that we do have an investment regime, it's just that the norm in question is to keep the MNCs unregulated hence why no IGOs have formed
- It's telling that the vast majority of proposed international legislation for MNCs were all made with the purpose of making investment easier

**The Multilateral Agreement on Investment**

- The MAI was modelled on the example of the WTO, lowering barriers to investment worldwide
- A key component was that no signatory would be able to treat an MNC differently to their domestic firms, pulled directly from the WTO concept of MFN status
- The main reason the MAI never succeeded was that people realized it was happening, the economic elites who drafted it were pushing it through in the background
- Secrecy was the ultimate reason why it failed, it made people suspicious of its contents
- Not being able to give favourable treatment to domestic industry was also a huge deal and would significantly damage the ability of OECD countries to develop domestically
- In addition at the time there were only 30 countries in the OECD at the time which would have pretty heavily limited any beneficial impact it might have had
- This was also in the mid 90s, any momentum the MAI might have had was completely killed by 9/11 as countries around the world closed borders and retracted from global business

**Lack of Consensus**

- The OECD, especially in the 90s, was representative of only one type of country: the heavily industrialized global north nations
- With the exception of NZ and Australia, basically all OECD countries were in North America or Western Europe
- These were the countries dictating the flow of FDI and were also creating the investment regime
- While FDI and the spread of MNCs were very beneficial for these countries, they were a drain on the global south
- Countries like the Asian tigers were trying to play both sides, allying with the global south politically and with the global north economically
- As more and more countries gained independence and joined the UN, the ratio of developed to developing countries in the UNGA was decreasing rapidly
- The group of 77 was formed in 1964 out of 77 less developed countries to present a list of demands
- These demands were called the NIEO (New International Economic Order)
- This included things like a reformation of voting rights and procedures in the UN and an organization for the regulation of MNCs
- Keep in mind this was long before the prevalence of climate change and environmental initiatives in the UN
- Seeking to put a ban on practices like transfer pricing that were enabling MNCs to thrive as well as mandating technology transfer
- Needless to say the main industrial states basically just ignored these requests
- After all they didn't want to give up their advantages and didn't have the power to force the MNCs to do so in the first place
- One of the minor successes was the creation of the UNCTC or United Nations Center on Transnational Corporations
- It was not all that the group of 77 imagined in the first place, but did provide a resource center and some voluntary guidelines that were primarily ignored
- A normative agenda had been established, but no robust code of conduct could be created
- The UNCTC eventually disbanded and was replaced by the UN Global Compact in the early 2000s
- This divide between the north and the south really came to a head in the 70s
- This was the period where expropriation and nationalization of foreign subsidiaries were at their peak
- Here we have stories like the overthrow of the Allende and Arbenz regimes and the nationalization of Alcan in Guyana and the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company in Iran
- Where the global north saw MNCs as engines of economic production, the south saw them as sources of exploitation
- While the state could push back by keeping the MNCs out, in the end their responsibility is to preserve the security of their citizens, look at what happened to Venezuela
- OPEC is the best example of the states fighting back but oil is a special case
- As the 80s roll around, the strategy most adapt to is working with the MNCs instead of against them

**Lecture 2**

- We will examine four strategies for managing MNCs, the first being the national/unilateral approach
- The most extreme form of this would be expropriation, where the government assumes control of the company's resources and property
- More of a realist approach, most popular from the 50s-60s
- Vernon had something called the "Obsolescing Model"
- At the time, the balance of power was heavily in the favour of the MNCs, but this model states that over time this advantage will fade
- As host countries adapt to the corporations, their ability to push back increases
- The same goes for the MNCs, if the balance shifts in the state's favour they will eventually regain the advantage
- Again, the main issue is with mobility, if the MNC is at a disadvantage they can just leave
- However, this mobility is still limited by sunk costs, especially with extraction MNCs
- Another strategy for pushing back is the seizure of assets
- This was especially popular in Latin America, mostly during the first wave of multinational expansion
- Popularity was a product of the Calvo Doctrine, named after litigator Carlos Calvo who prescribed a clause to be included in all FDI agreements where the MNC must cede to the state in any disputes
- This gives the government free reign to confiscate any assets they want without giving MNCs an avenue to fight back
- Avoids gunboat diplomacy where the corporation can just petition their home country to use their armed forces to retrieve their debts
- US supported this doctrine for a while until the Cold War when it backfired on them
- Recall that MNCs are easy targets for politically unstable regimes
- Peak year for expropriation was 1976, before the postwar economic liberalisation
- By 1990, acts of expropriation had disappeared completely
- Usually doesn't end very well, there's a reason why expropriation disappeared
- Goals of increasing revenue and decreasing dependence on MNCs frequently not met
- Remember these are oligopolistic industries, nationalize one and there will be 4 or 5 more to take their place
- Countries are pushing back not just against one country, but a whole industry
- Some states tried doing it in a friendly way by offering compensation, but that still didn't work
- If the 70s was the decade of confrontation with MNCs, the 80s were the decade of pragmatism

**National Management**

- A softer alternative is simply adding screening mechanisms for FDI, Canada and Mexico are great examples of this
- In Canada it was the Foreign Investment Review Agency in 1974 and the Foreign Investment Law in Mexico in 1973
- Basically just a process to screen out investments that won't ultimately benefit Canada
- Overall this does little to deter investment given that most proposals are waved through
- What is less certain is the number of MNCs who don't even bother to apply since going somewhere with no bureaucracy would be easier
- Mulroney later renamed the agency to Investment Canada (and removed the NEP)
- This is a process that works for Canada and Mexico, but may not for many weaker economies
- Mexico limits investment to 49% equity, favours Mexican companies heavily
- In the years since this law has been liberalized, foreign companies now just need approval to hold more than 49%, and only in certain sectors
- Other more creative controls have been created to target specific countries/industries
- Examples are sanctions against specific countries and legislation allowing individuals or groups to sue for damages (US Alien Tort Claims Act, US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act)
- In Canada, there was a lawsuit in 2017 where protestors demanded compensation from a Vancouver-based silver company for an incident where 7 men were injured by the company's security guards in a shooting in Guatemala
- First time a Canadian appellate court allowed a lawsuit against a Canadian company for human rights violations committed abroad
- Codes of conduct for investment can be created by groups other than the state, for example NGOs or labour organizations
- These can even be implemented by the MNCs themselves, by the 90s most companies have their own codes of conduct

**Lecture 3**

- These codes of conduct aren't really even real codes, more like a pre-emptive action to avoid being subjected to codes of conduct
- The next option for regulation is regional codes, at a higher level compared to national agreements
- One example is the Andean Pact, later the Andean Common Market
- Started in 1969, became the Andean Community in 1996
- Founded by Bolivia, Chile, Columbia, Ecuador, and Peru with Venezuela joining in 1973 and withdrawing under Chavez in 2006 and Chile withdrawing in 1976
- Decision 24 under the pact created a set of regulation on FDI and technology transfer
- Member states are no longer competing for FDI, if all have the same regulations it limits mobility for MNCs since they can't just go next door for a better deal
- Technically the OECD is also a regional agreement because it isn't a global pact, but it also doesn't comprise countries from a specific geographic region
- But remember, the goals of the OECD are very different from the Andean Pact
- Did devise a voluntary code of conduct in 1976, ultimate goal is to enable MNCs but they have to behave somewhat for the OECD to help them
- Created in response to the overthrow of the Allende regime
- Code has been updated several times since, but its still voluntary
- Also came out with another voluntary initiative called the "model tax convention" in 1979 to cut down on transfer pricing
- A looser example is NAFTA, which has investment provisions in chapter 11
- Can be used to make member states behave in terms of cross-border investment, but is also primarily intended to promote investment
- Ex: Canadian oil company punished for selling oil to Florida with an additive that's illegal in the state
- A step above this is international codes for investment and regulation, but with narrow scopes
- Great example is the UN breast milk substitutes code, very specific but applied worldwide
- Finally we have international codes with broad scopes
- The spark here was the ITT-Allende affair
- US conducted a congressional investigation into ITT and a potential bribery scandal, but also didn't approve of any sort of international code of conduct for MNCs
- Group of 77 and other LDCs were pushing for it, Allende affair only fuelled this
- In 1974 a commission on MNCs was created in the UN in response to the requests of the LDCs, but it didn't last forever
- Mandate of the commission was to produce a multilateral agreement on MNCs
- Faced heavy opposition from the OECD countries, decommissioned in 1993
- Seemed not to have any impact, but did increase the UN's capacity to monitor MNCs and paved the way for the Global Compact

**UN Global Compact**

- Not a universal code of conduct, but a way to facilitate companies developing good corporate citizenship
- In exchange for showing people that they are part of the global compact, companies must devise a code of conduct that aligns with UN human rights values
- Defines itself as "a strategic policy initiative for businesses that are committed to aligning their operations and strategies with ten universally accepted principles"
- Age of pragmatism, encouraging MNCs not restricting them
- Tries to impose some of the burden states carry on to companies, should obey similar norms
- Criticized for just being a bluewashing initiative
- Like everything else (with the exception of expropriation), its all voluntary
- Most ambitious CSR initiative to date due to the scale of the program
- Highly visible as well, appeals to the sensitivity of MNCs to optics and perception
- Deeply rooted in constructivist theory, idea is that eventually we will shift to a world where companies are held to the same normative standards as states
