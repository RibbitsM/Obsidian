**Lecture 1**

- Most large MNCs are oligopolistic
- When looking at many major resource extraction industries, there's usually a small number of very large firms that dominate the industry
- Example is the seven sisters of Exxon, Mobil, Texaco, Gulf, Socal, Bp, and Shell (Socal and gulf merged to become chevron, as did exxon and mobil)
- Two main aspects of corporations to consider:
- Amoral objectives: corporations are driven by a responsibility to create profits and maximize shareholder value
- How can we impose moral values onto multinationals
- Lack of accountability: MNCs exist across multiple jurisdictions and are usually quite mobile
- Very difficult to prosecute for their actions, and even then it's limited to tort law
- While corporate social responsibility creates some offset, it doesn't solve the key issue
- Usually just a token gesture adopted in service of further increasing profits

**Forerunners/Predecessors**

- Early mercantile firms were very resource-hungry, especially in terms of land
- They relied on the existence of the colonies to survive, and on the support of their governments
- Once the resources ran dry and they stopped being useful to their hosts, they went extinct
- Were formed as part of charters, very different from a traditional company
- Artificial companies, not spawned by market forces like we might expect
- Modern multinationals came from the capitalist system, not the mercantilist system
- As opposed to mercantilism, capitalism assumes that resources can be most efficiently used through cooperation rather than the harsh state competition of mercantilism
- Restriction of access to resources should never happen, free market
- In the case of the British charters it was the Companies act in 1862 that made it easier to form companies and limited their liabilities
- Capitalism made the world much more friendly for corporations
- In 1886, Santa Clara County took the Southern Pacific Railroad to the Supreme Court to force them to pay for a fence built around their new railroad going through the county
- The result was that Santa Clara had to pay, but more importantly the decision recognized Southern Pacific, and corporations as a whole, as legal persons
- Enshrined the idea of corporate personhood, giving them legal protections without the associated social responsibilities or culpability of a genuine person
- MNCs also acquired a certain degree of independence from the states they were born in, allowing them to move freely between countries
- The American government cannot legally force an MNC to "come back home", they can only apply political pressure
- Early critics of corporations were Lincoln and Hobbes, raising concerns about the destructive potential of corporations to damage the integrity of the state
- Despite these concerns long before the birth of even the first MNCs, backlash didn't happen until at least the 1970s
- MNCs emerged in the Americas and expanded through patterns of colonial influence
- This is why MNCs started off with major influence in Latin America, the "backyard" of the US
- Same goes in Europe, with countries like Spain, UK, Belgium, the Netherlands etc.
- Often multinationals are forces of neocolonialism, even when the metropole withdraws, its influence remains through corporations
- First major wave of MNC expansion was in the 1940s, and they were generally considered to be apolitical, which didn't really change until the overthrow of the Allende regime
- A major change came in the aftermath of WWII with the establishment of the Bretton Woods system
- The one thing that is notably absent from the BW is any kind of control on FDI
- No investment authority, no guidelines, nothing
- The closest thing is the OECD or the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development
- Most corporate investment happens between OECD countries, but it doesn't regulate it
- It's central mandate is to make things easier for multinationals, not harder
- It was initially created to run the Marshall plan as the OEEC, but later changed its name and became more global
- Most member countries are advanced industrial states, but recently states like Chile, Columbia, and Costa Rica have been admitted
- The OECD does have some guidelines, but no enforcement mechanism so its all voluntary

**Lecture 2**

- There was an odd switch in terms of thinking about states in the 70s and 80s
- Started with the rise of MNC power reacted to with claims that the power of states were crumbling
- This was in 1969, by 1984 people were talking about states being powerful again
- Over time both sides of the argument have been constantly overexaggerated
- Swinging of the pendulum, constant cycle of exaggeration and overcorrection
- When thinking of realist perspectives on economy, we can assume they are mercantilists
- Liberal, marxist, and mercantilist perspectives on economics are all distinctly different
- These are all broad theories of political economy
- Each has it's own main referent object, liberals focus on households and firms, marxists focus on classes, and realists focus on states
- Notably, liberals are the only group that view economics as mutually beneficial while realists and marxists view them as conflicting
- For mercantilists there is economic conflict between states and for marxists it's conflict between classes
- Also the difference between absolute and relative gain, mercantilists have a zero sum view where liberals have a positive sum view
- While dependency theory is related to marxism the focus isn't really on class but does focus heavily on wealth inequality
- Duality of bourgeoise and proletariat is replaced by core and periphery
- Extending these attitudes towards MNCs, the liberals like them and the marxists/realists don't
- They also view them as unnatural, either due to government policy or class interest
- The concerns are also different, the harms of MNCs could be exploitation or a weakening of state power and control
