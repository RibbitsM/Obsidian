**Lecture 1**

- In terms of models we have the spontaneous structural forces model
- Discussed this earlier, mutual dependence of states and markets, states create markets and markets reinforce states
- MNCs aren't really on either 'side' of the equation but are definitely more market-based
- New models are including NGOs and IGOs into the international political economy
- Think about the recent media importance of orgs like the UN and the rise of the WHO during covid

**States and Markets**

- By the 80s, MNCs are becoming too powerful to be effectively bullied by most states
- Especially developing economies are prone to becoming dependent on MNCs
- Working with MNCs becomes normalized because it is practical and useful, not necessarily because of some general change of opinion
- As states start to work with corporations they may adopt their habits, making decisions that prioritize budgets and revenue
- On the other hand, concepts like CSR start to leak into the private sector that cause corporations to take responsibility and offset harms
- Overall states start to act more like businesses and businesses start to act more like states
- Global issues are becoming too big for any single state to tackle, and increasingly they need the help of other states or corporations
- For governments to effectively achieve their goals, they need the cooperation of MNCs
- A good example is the search for the COVID-19 vaccine, which was drastically accelerated by partnership with the major pharmaceutical multinationals
- Of course this was not for the good of the world, they made immense amounts of money on it
- US government used to have an autarkic policy for defense spending before the 80s
- Wouldn't let non-American companies into the US military industrial complex
- Another significant event for MNCs was 9/11, a direct attack on American corporate power
- Sparked the creation of the Department of Homeland Security which required unprecedented amounts of cooperation between the US and companies like ISPs and other data collectors
- Global stability cannot be preserved by states alone anymore
- Economic stability means peace, and as a result MNCs have a vested interest in preserving global stability
- Former theories of stability depended on a balance of power between states or one state having hegemon status, but if even the US needs MNCs to keep it's army going this can't be true
- In parts of the Congo where not even the government will go, there are still Belgian MNCs

**Theories of MNCs**

- MNCs are new actors, and as such they demand new theories
- The rise of MNCs caused a shift from portfolio investment to FDI
- Before the late 40s, most investment was in portfolio capital, which focuses on investing to make money, not investment for control
- The key difference is that FDI also comes with an element of influence over the country being invested in
- Another key question to answer is why the number of MNCs skyrocketed so dramatically in the 60s and 70s
- Economists explained this in terms of the market, some condition of the market is enabling the rise of MNCs and making them especially profitable
- Initially thought of as a blip, an incidental affect of the post-war economic boom
- Another phenomenon happening at the same time was an increase in the number of states
- The self-determination movement was chopping up the empires of Europe and MNCs were being formed to invest in these new states
- The Marshall Plan was also happening at this time, American MNCs were pouring money into Western Europe, and later did the same in Eastern Europe in the 90s
- Portfolio investment theory didn't work to explain MNCs, new theories around FDI had to be created
- Three main theories: Kindleburger-Hymer hypothesis, product-cycle model, and the ownership, location, and internalization model also called OLI model or internalization theory

**Necessary vs Sufficient Conditions for Expansion**

- The necessary conditions for MNCs to expand would be the conditions that allow them to function across borders
- A great example would be the reductions in transactions costs like information exchange, transit, and cargo shipping
- Things like standardized shipping containers just did not exist prior to the rise of MNCs
- Developments in organizational structure also enabled this, scaling up businesses to function across entire continents
- Market power is also required, most MNCs function in an oligopolistic environment and most were former monopolies or close to being ones
- These companies have the finance needed to subsidize FDI and the technical and managerial expertise to keep it running
- Outside of the firm itself, it's also important for a potential MNC to be in a beneficial political environment
- A good example is Japan, there was a deliberate shift in Japanese government policy to allow corporations to expand via overseas investment insurance, tax policies etc.
- Pro-business governments will generally create tax incentives to expand overseas and will occasionally shoulder some of the risk
- Some aid policies are also tied to FDI, state assistance can come in the form of private capital
- Another example of governments and MNCs cooperating
- Gilpin specifically mentions tax policy, there is an incentive for MNCs to move jobs elsewhere to save on taxes (hence why so many MNCs are headquartered in Ireland)
- This is called transfer pricing, when MNCs make sure the majority of their profits are accounted for in countries with exceptionally lax tax policies
- These are all only necessary conditions, doesn't explain why domestic corporations actually decide to expand across multiple countries
- We need a theoretical framework to understand the motivations

**Representative Theories**

- Recall our three theories, the product-cycle model, OLI Theory, and Kindleburger-Hymer hypothesis
- Lets start with the product cycle model, created by Raymond Vernon
- Focused on manufacturing firms, specifically in the 60s
- It's a behavioural theory, and focuses mainly on the evolution of industrial technology
- Because of that, this really relies on companies having the cutting edge of technology, before it diffuses into the market
- The product cycle is graphed over time, technology is introduced, it grows and matures and then eventually declines
- For an example, lets use the television
- It was introduced in 1948, originally created by RCA
- At this time, they were the only company capable of creating televisions
- In the 50s, America was the worlds main R&D and manufacturing center and created the vast majority of new technologies
- For RCA, their goal is to keep the television from diffusing so they can keep their monopoly
- They also wanted to drive the price down to make it more affordable
- But once everybody in the US has an RCA TV, what do you do?
- So RCA expanded to Canada and Western Europe and started exporting TVs
- By the late 1950s, the technology is beginning to diffuse
- Not only are companies abroad beginning to reverse engineer the TVs, but domestic competitors were beginning to emerge as well
- In order to get into foreign marketplaces, the country will often demand the rights to the technology in exchange
- This is what happened to IBM, to sell computers in Japan they had to share the technology with them, which then diffused to the _zaibatsus_
- Now there are TV manufacturing facilities in Western Europe, and RCA has to compete
- These domestic European corporations have the advantage of not having to export, they're exempt from tariffs and hidden behind other protectionist policies
- This incentivized RCA to stop exporting, and start production overseas
- Now RCA is a multinational corporation, not because of some motivation by the firm but because of the natural progress of technological advance
- At this point the product cycle curve is at its peak, the RCA TV is as prominent as it will get
- To continue to be competitive, RCA must use their new MNC status to lower their production costs
- In the mid 60s, Japan has started manufacturing TVs, and they're being exported to the states
- They were high quality and cheap, beating out most American domestic producers
- RCA is no longer losing their market share in Europe to domestic firms, but also losing their market share in the US to Japanese firms
- The technology has been fully standardized and has diffused across the world
- To stay competitive, RCA has to keep cutting costs which pushes them to offshore production to Mexico and South-East Asia
- This coincided with the rise of Export Processing Zones, or Special Economic Zones
- These are set up by host economies to bring in manufacturing firms with cheap labour
- At this point in the early 70s, we're at a recognizable arrangement compared to modern MNCs
- This theory also lends credence to the Tariff Hopping Hypothesis
- If a country raises tariffs to protect a domestic industry, as Western Europe did, the exporting firms will just avoid them by expanding production to the tariff country
- A very similar thing was happening in the American automotive industry, where Japanese firms like Mazda were jumping tariffs by producing in the US
- This model is good, but only really applies to manufacturing industries
- Vernon is also a liberal at his core, he believes that MNCs are fundamentally beneficial
- The benefits will trickle down by creating jobs worldwide and enhancing the power of the host
- MNCs are also depicted as emerging naturally in response to market forces
- The motivation is not expansion, but survival and expansion is just the best way to do so
- MNCs are depicted as defensive, acting just to protect their economic interests
- Of course these are manufacturing firms, who are less prone to causing coups compared to resource extraction MNCs

**OLI Model**

- Next is the OLI model, which stands for ownership, location, and internalization
- Theory of the firm itself, as opposed to Vernon's model that focused on technological change
- Created by John Dunning, an Englishman writing around the same time as Vernon
- Theorizing the firm, not the product
- The reason we don't outright call this and other MNC theories "theories" is because their scope is not broad enough, hence the moniker "model" or "hypothesis"
- Emphasizes MNCs access to proprietary knowledge, their intellectual property
- Can't just rely on copyright law which is notoriously weak, have to create their own protections
- MNCs tend to have some sort of technological advantage, hence their size and power
- Key word is internalization, MNCs rely on having sole ownership of their technology, intellectual property, etc and therefore want to internalize markets
- Internalizing the marketplace means preventing diffusion of your technology to your competition by becoming the competition
- Use mergers and acquisitions to absorb challengers and internalize them, keep your property inside the corporation
- Seeking a monopoly is therefore seen as defensive behaviour and a natural response to a firms proprietary technology being threatened
- Technology then diffuses through subsidiaries rather than through the marketplace
- For an MNC to meet this theory they need to meet three requirements
- Ownership: Must own some sort of specialized knowledge that has yet to diffuse
- Location: Going abroad is possible, and must be more advantageous than staying in the home market
- Internalization: Must be incentives to do FDI instead of using export and licensing agreements to sell their product through a foreign firm
- Recall the example of IBM selling their technology to Japanese firms
- Similar assumptions as Vernon, MNCs are created by market forces causing firms to expand defensively, not aggressively
- Complements the PCM and works well for most high-tech oligopolistic firms
- Also challenges FDI-only theories by increasing the variety of investments MNCs can make
- However, it does not explain the occasionally aggressive market-seeking behaviour of many firms
- Liberal economists tend to have a blind spot for this, viewing MNCs as only defensive

**Hymer-Kindleberger Hypothesis**

- Full names Stephen Hymer and Charles Kindleberger, after Hymer's untimely death his PHD supervisor, Kindleberger, took over his work and completed it
- Has other complicated names like "structural market imperfection model"
- Goal was to explain the spread of US MNCs in the 50s
- Three basic questions:
- Why do firms go abroad?
- How do they survive in foreign markets?
- Why do they want to retain control and ownership?
- Hymer's final argument boils down to two incentives
- MNCs have oligopolistic/monopolistic advantages over native firms in the host state
- Nothing new, we've seen this before
- Calls these 'factors of efficiency' that allow these MNCs to overcome the barriers to entry
- Also argues that MNCs seek to remove oligopolistic competition
- This is new, first indication of aggressive MNC behaviour
- Very personal for him, his parent's Quebec textile company was being driven out of business by American competitors
- Understanding of MNCs as strategic as opposed to passively responding to market forces
- Hymer was never an outright Marxist, but many have labeled him as a quasi-Marxist
- Again this is a theory of the firm, not the product, but it's not just an economic theory because it delves into the strategy and decision-making of these firms
- Treats MNCs as they have some level of autonomy in the face of market forces, they are not helpless in the market as they can act to change it
- This is where Hymer's realist tendencies come in, neither liberals or Marxists tend to view actors as autonomous
- Also states that MNCs aren't just motivated by short-term profits but long-term survival

**Other Critical Perspectives**

- The global reach perspective came from a 1974 book by Ronald Muller and Richard Barnet
- Harsh critique of MNCs, depicts them as aggressively trying to exert control over the world
- Created the idea of the international corporate elite, MNCs trying to rule the world
- Studied mostly in the national context, why do MNCs arise where they do
- Paved the way towards the idea of a global network of elites or a transnational capitalist class
- Leslie Sklair and Robert Cox both produced Neo-Gramscian and Marxist analyses of the global economy, with MNCs being a major part of that
- Gramsci's fundamental idea about power is that it is not strictly material, it can be immaterial
- Power can be exercised through physical force, but is more often exercised through ideas
- Especially relevant for MNCs because of the power of branding and the corporate identity

**Dependency Theory**

- Deeply connected to Latin America, also called core-periphery analysis
- The world is made up of different classes a la Marx, but divided on the state level
- The northern states, the OECD countries, are the rich core where the majority of capital originates
- Canada is strange in the sense that it is generally considered a wealthy country but it functions as a periphery country in dependency theory
- Created originally by Raúl Prebisch who was an Argentine economist working for the UN in the 50s
- Assigned to figure out why the southern states weren't developing, and concluded that the elites in the core states would collaborate with the elites in the periphery states
- Elites in places like Argentina have little loyalty to their state, and instead cooperate with their fellow elites in other countries
- Wealth does spread to the periphery countries, but only to their elites
- Created a neo-colonial system where the periphery states became the primary producers where the core states reaped the economic benefits
- The development of these poorer states is **dependent** on the richer states, hence the name
- In most cases, the tool of control is the MNCs, they are the chain linking the core and periphery

**Realism**

- Our main realist is Robert Gilpin, who was the first realist to really tackle the topic of MNCs
- They weren't really seen as political actors since they don't have an army and therefore lack any sort of hard power, the currency of realism
- Gilpin depicts MNCs as a threat, the US government risks replacement from it's own MNCs
