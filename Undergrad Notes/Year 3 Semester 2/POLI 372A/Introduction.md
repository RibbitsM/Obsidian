**Week 1**

- "Parallel existence and mutual interaction of states and markets in the modern world" - Giltman's definition of the international political economy
- Giltman is a realist, so this conception of the IPE is a method of discussing non-state actors without compromising a realist view
- For this course, we will date the emergence of MNCs to the early 20th century
- Brand new actor that grew exponentially, especially in the 60s
- Prominent examples of MNCs affecting politics are the United Fruit Company in Guatemala, ITT in Chile, and BP in Iran
- All occurred around the early 70s, 1973 major turning point for the prominence of MNCs
- Key innovation is the expansion of supply lines, and moving manufacturing to many different countries where costs are lower
- Not all countries accept investment from multinationals, but suffer the loss of potential money
- Corporate social responsibility didn't used to be the standard of corporations
- The CEO of the Dow Chemical Company was quoted in 1974 as wanting to purchase his own island to headquarter the company on to avoid government restrictions
- By definition, a multinational country only needs to own and operate in 2 or more countries
- Usually are headquartered in a "home" country and operates subsidiaries or units in "host" countries who report to central headquarters
- Despite the negative connotations of the term "host", oftentimes this is a willing arrangement since foreign direct investment is often one of the only ways for a country to develop
- Canada is a great example of this, most of Canada's major industries are controlled by American branch plants

**What do MNCs Do?**

- The key term here is foreign direct investment or FDI
- Direct means that the company is physically present in the host country
- Distinctly different from trade, instead of trading goods a company will establish itself in another country directly, and the benefit is the GDP created by the subsidiary/branch plant
- Involves a direct stake and foreign control in a productive enterprise, much more politicized
- As opposed to portfolio investment, FDI has the ability to control the policy and future of a country
- The benefit is not just wealth creation and cost savings, but control
- You don't even need 100% control of your subsidiary, can even be done with less than 50% ownership
- In most cases the host government will be given equity in the subsidiary, but not enough to control it
- Joint ventures do exist, but direct investment is much more popular
- FDI is also an invention unique to the 20th century
- Infrastructure investment as in settler colonialism did not have the objective of control, but the production of wealth
- Investors did not control the Canadian railway system, the company did
