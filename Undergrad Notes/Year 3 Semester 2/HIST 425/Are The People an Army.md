- In 1789, King Louis XVI summoned the estates of France so that they could approve his new powers and taxes
- The estates did not take kindly to this, and began talking about reforming the social order in France and began working to reduce the power of the monarchy
- This went on until they eventually imprisoned the king, effectively taking control of France and dubbing themselves the "National Assembly"
- In response, the kings of Austria and Prussia joined forces in 1791 to support King Louis and preserve the power of the sovereigns of Europe
- A faction within the new French government containing thinkers like Robespierre were against the war, and doubted France would survive such a conflict
- In order to spread their revolution, France must not be antagonistic to the rest of Europe
- On the other hand, the opposing Girondin faction believed France should go to war as they would surely win and solidify their newfound liberty
- The matter was put to a vote in 1792, with the result being France launching a preemptive war against Austria
- Ironically, only the king could declare war so the National Assembly had to get Louis XVI to declare war on his monarchist allies
- They began by invading the Austrian netherlands, where their invasion was very unpopular and fell apart quite quickly
- Monarchists in the French army were constantly defecting which caused significant problems
- On the other hand, the Austrian counteroffensive had even more issues, barely being able to field half their full manpower
- The general in charge, Brunswick, was extremely methodical but also quite slow
- This gave the French plenty of time to prepare a defense mostly without any officers who were mostly unsympathetic to the National Assembly
- The forces of the French army were quite different to the _kabinettskrieg_ forces, they were volunteers, very loyal to the cause with elected officers
- But against the very well organized and armed Prussian hussars, they crumbled immediately
- Compared to professional soldiers, they were unmanageable
- The answer was to entrench them, and force the Prussians to meet them
- With some effective artillery and a better position, Kellermann was able to use his volunteers to repel the Prussian forces
- While the French incurred more casualties, the careful nature of the _kabinettskrieg_-accustomed Brunswick led him to believe the war was unsustainable, and withdrew
- While the bravery of the _federes_ volunteers probably did play a role, this victory likely had more to do with the effectiveness of the French military machine and Brunswick's pragmatism
- However, the French did not see it this way, venerating the bravery of the volunteer infantry

**How did War Affect the French Revolution?**

- The war of the first coalition would set the stage for a French obsession with the valor of men, and may have impacted their society going forward
- Not only preserved the revolution, but radicalised it
- Instead of fighting for money and to expand territory, this was a war for liberty fought by ordinary people, not by professional soldiers
- Fought with less polish and discipline, but more aggression and boldness
- The reason Louis had to raise taxes in the first place was because France was going to go bankrupt from spending on war
- The state was terribly in debt, but the people should have been able to pay for it if taxes were applied correctly
- But all tax reform efforts were being shut down by the aristocrats
- The estates that Louis had to appeal to were mostly made up of aristocrats, but the third estate was actually made of commoners
- The notion of having a "nation" was usually only applied to the upper class, the nobles of an area defined the nation
- The _Natio Polonica_ referred to the nobles of Poland
- But in 1694, dictionary definitions referred to all the people living in a state, given they had a common language and laws
- Ironically, at the time only about a fifth of France actually spoke French

**The French Revolution**

- When the revolution begun, the priority was to gain arms
- Most commoners were unarmed, so they raided military outposts and guard towers to arm themselves
- Especially after the storming of the bastile, the National Assembly now had access to an army with quality weapons
- At this point they were not actually seeking to form a republic, most members of the Assembly sought a reformed constitutional monarchy
- Almost all feudal privileges were abolished, usually by aristocrats volunteering up the privileges of other aristocrats
- In the end, anyone who paid a certain amount of taxes could vote, with the bar being quite low, and the king remained in power
- They had achieved all their goals, but the revolution wasn't finished
- The constitutional monarchy didn't last very long and sparked a constant rotation of different forms of government through Paris up until the end of the 1700s
- Some think that this was because of the initial radical nature of the Revolution that it spiralled so far out of control
- Another way of thinking is that it was the events of the revolution that radicalised the people of France, the conditions they were put in changed them
- From the beginning, the assembly was divided as most of the participants had no idea how to perform in an open assembly
- They were used to court intrigue and manipulating the political structure to their will
- There was very little structure or organization, they just voted on whatever was popular at the time
- The only controls in place was the royal veto, where the king could prevent a topic from being discussed for five years
- While this was a good idea in concept, it just created more division between king and assembly
- There was no house of lords or senate, just the assembly and King Louis
- At the beginning, most right wings and moderates left due to fear of the future of the government and a general sense of antagonism
- All that remained from a fairly balanced legislature was varying different flavours of left wing which were divided into different clubs/groups
- The war with Austria in 1792 only made this worse, there was an uprising during the war led by the most radical factions in the assembly that occupied the Tuileries palace
- The day after the battle of Valmy and Brunswick's retreat, the Republic of France was established, Louis XIV deposed, and the assembly was renamed to the 'National Convention'
- King Louis was put on trial for treason among other things, found guilty, and guillotined

**Every Citizen a Soldier**

- Now a republic, the army of France had to be completely reformed to remove its association with the monarchy
- Names of regiments and uniforms alongside the officer-soldier dynamic were all purged
- As a new measure, the right to vote was now extended to all soldiers, no matter their income
- In addition, soldiers were allowed and encouraged to join political clubs and participate politically
- Just as all soldiers were now citizens, public participation in war was now widespread
- The _levee en masse_ decree put into place in 1793 allowed the mass conscription of all French citizens
- Between 1793 and 1794 the French army almost doubled in size, going from 645,000 to 1.5 million
- War was no longer just the provision of princes and mercenaries, but of every citizen in the state
- Women were also included in the war effort, as parts of the supply chain but also petitions were raised for the creation of women's regiments of the army
- If women were citizens, it would make sense that they would have to fight as well
- The one advantage this new French army had was numbers and determination
- As a result, battle tactics relied on buying victories with casualties, using aggressive charges and deep infantry columns supported by artillery
- Instead of relying on inexperienced troops outshooting trained musketeers, they would simply rush them down with sheer numbers and bayonet them
- This strategy actually worked fairly often, allowing French generals to quickly and decisively beat professional armies albeit at the cost of significant casualties
- Of course this relied on the French significantly outnumbering their opponents, so later on this strategy was modified to include a cavalry pinch and skirmishing forces
- Lazare Carnot was one of the first French generals to really pioneer these techniques, a product of the French military education system
- He adapted to the conscription system and created a system for effective training and arming of so many soldiers
- Given that most soldiers were volunteers meant that a lot less effort needed to be put into keeping them from deserting, allowing them to employ tactics unavailable to other forces
- If you didn't need officers to monitor all their men you could have sneak attacks, skirmish forces and more
- This was not just a war to liberate France, but all of Europe
- The National Convention agreed to grant assistance to anti-monarchists all across Europe seeking to gain their liberty as well
- Even when not asked, the French would invade to "free" them
- This marked a strong resurgence of ideological warfare the likes of which had not been seen since the Crusades
