**Seven Years War**

- This was considered to be the first "world war", and was also responsible for the French loss of New France in modern day Canada
- Not the first transcontinental war, the Ottomans had fought several as had the Austrian and Spanish Succession wars
- The Ottomans had actually travelled surprisingly far, even to Newfoundland and Iceland at some point
- However, the Seven Years War had a much larger geographical extent and was centrally financed and directed
- Strategy came from the metropole and spread across the world
- Resulted in huge colonial losses for France, and led to huge British expansion
- Britain's Prussian allies managed to fight their many enemies to a standstill even gaining some land
- Spain and Austria had been thoroughly beaten, losing many of their most profitable colonies
- Britain and Prussia were not at all the expected winners, had much smaller forces compared to their enemies

**New Warfare**

- This expansion of warfare was not just a result of expanding armies, but expanding states as well
- 1648-1789 was seen as the era of _Kabinettskriege_ or 'cabinet wars'
- These wars were fought almost exclusively between princes/monarchs, not states or religions
- These wars were fought by professional armies led by aristocratic officers and directed by the princes cabinet of advisors
- Fought to annex territory, so collateral damage was kept to a minimum, defined by calculations of material advantage
- Wars fought for resources not ideology, so alliances had a tendency to shift very quickly
- The main cultural shift was in the goals of war, they were heavily economic/territorial and caused by balance of power
- Not fought for ideology or sociopolitical reasons
- Even with the Ottomans, Europeans had little reason to fight them despite religious differences, and made frequent deals with them
- Stark shift from the many wars sparked by Catholic-Protestant divides that preceded the era
- Tactics of the era focused on limiting casualties, trained professional soldiers were an incredibly expensive resource
- When battles were fought all-out they were devastating, but this was avoided at all costs
- A prominent French general named Maurice de Saxe wrote in 1757 that the skill of a general should be measured in his ability to avoid war
- This is a complete 180 from what we will see Napoleon talk about soon
- War was generally accepted as normal and necessary in aristocratic society, and was constrained by etiquette and shared values
- Being a soldier was seen as a job like anything else, you just fought for your employer to gain money and nothing else
- Military rank was an investment that was bought, it was not merit-based
- Changing alliance as individual generals and soldiers was also permitted, since it was just a job no loyalty was expected or necessary
- Similar to the highly controlled culture of duelling, but on a much larger scale
- Compared to the Thirty Years War and the Napoleonic Wars, the period in between from 1618 to 1815 had drastically less war deaths
- In addition, barely any of these deaths were civilians, most were soldiers

**The Military Enlightenment**

- This was also the period of the Enlightenment, which was a very broad movement but had significant effect on military affairs
- Sought to improve all aspects of life, including warfare
- Most important development was prioritizing individual happiness in this life rather than the afterlife
- Strong belief in the importance of reason and experience meant that common customs of warfare came under critique
- Previous wars were inconclusive and expensive, and must become more efficient to avoid causing unnecessary unhappiness
- During the enlightenment, books and articles on war were written frequently at an unprecedented rate
- These writers believed in a universal characteristic of war, despite differences in technology you could apply ancient principles to modern war
- Generals were encouraged to read about Polybius, Livy, etc.
- This did not always work, arranging musketeers in a greek-style phalanx was horribly inefficient
- Also prompted a focus on applying science to warfare, using scientific principles to make military decisions
- Simultaneously generals were encouraged to look to the past and to scientific reasoning
- Military engineers wrote many works on both constructing and besieging fortresses using geometric principles
- Cunning and courage doesn't matter, what matter is how well you know the relevant mathematical calculations
- More and more war was a mathematical function solvable through calculations of resources, manpower, force, and mass
- A Prussian general named Bulow coined the term 'Base of Operations' and attempted to solve the ideal geometrical orientation of your objective compared to your frontier
- Naturally this emphasis on knowledge led to importance being placed on education
- Many military academies were formed to teach officers in the ways of war and to learn from the failures of previous wars
- France in particular had a fairly rich history of military education
- Especially military engineers were trained extensively alongside artillerymen and naval officers
- Before the 1700s, most "military academies" were just boys schools, but schools for technical training and military theory really expanded from 1717 onward
- The _Ecole royale militarie_ in Paris founded in 1750 is especially notable as where Napoleon was trained as well as many of his officers
- Another evolution was in standardisation, many different foundrys were creating fairly different muskets, cannons, and cannonballs
- Especially with artillery, having a cannonball/cannon that was to large or small was a huge issue
- Lt Gen Jean Baptiste Vaquette de Gribeauval pioneered the Gribeauval system that made French artillery the best in the world
- First of all, cannons were made out of a lighter copper-tin alloy and made shorter and thinner on a lighter carriage
- This made them easier to move, aim, and manufacture
- In addition, instead of casting cannons in different pieces and fusing them, the cannon was cast in one piece and had its hole bored by a lathe
- This meant that as long as you kept the lathe the same, all cannons would have the same calibre
- These cannons also had interchangeable parts allowing for easy repairs
- L'Ordre Mince was also pioneered which abandoned the classic deep formation for a longer thinner arrangement
- Similar to Ottoman techniques, this maximized firepower by making sure every man and cannon could fire simultaneously
- As for naval developments, ships had been modified to be able to bombard land targets with much lighter cannons
- This allowed for a lot more firepower, making galleons basically mobile artillery
- This allowed for real amphibious warfare, instead of the classic ramming and boarding method used since the time of the Phoenicians
- Ships could land their troops and then supported them with artillery

**Challenges to the State**

- The biggest obstacle to European rulers was actually internal
- In the 1600s, barely any state in Europe had what we expect from a modern state, including centralized control and monopoly on violence
- There were local armies, elites, and even legal systems everywhere
- Monarchs were not the ruler of their whole state, but rather rulers of a set of local municipalities inside their state
- For example, the Hohenzollern dynasty had territory all across Europe but were contested in most areas and barely any were actually connected
- In their territory in Prussia they had to defer to the King of Poland, but in Brandenburg they paid tax to the Holy Roman Emperor and so on
- This created immense amounts of red tape if they wanted to anything anywhere as they had to get many people's approval
- This was not an exceptional circumstance and was very common in Europe
- Since their territories had no natural coherence (physical barriers, common ancestry etc.), they sought a standing army to defend them
- Friedrich Wilhelm Hohenzollern struck a deal with the local parliament to allow him to form an army which was carried on by his descendants
- His grandson, Friedrich Wilhelm I, was incredibly frugal with a strict Calvinist work ethic, and devoted almost the entirety of state resources to the army
- He also divided his land into cantons of 5,000 households each where every able-bodied man had to serve in the regiment of that canton for at least a small period of time
- Basically turned almost the whole of Prussia into an army, the state only existed for the purpose of fuelling the military
- Also had a weird obsession with tall soldiers, even doing eugenics to breed tall Prussian children
- The Prussian civil service was also trans-territorial, your birthplace had little to do with where you would serve
- It was also highly centralized and staffed by many foreigners as he did not trust the nobility
- Bureaucrats were highly scrutinized, and any errors in their accounts would be punished severely for corruption
- Ironically, Friedrich who loved his army so much, never used it but his son Friedrich II was a different story
- He almost immediately invaded Silesia, declaring war on the massive Habsburg empire
- Over the following years with the start of the Seven Years War, Prussia was invaded by army after army
- France, Austria, Russia, Sweden and a variety of German states all attacked him and almost destroyed Prussia
- However the military machine crafted by his father allowed Friedrich II to persist despite all odds stacked against him
- This sent a message to the other European states, proving the effectiveness of a centralized state with the aim of making the military as strong as possible
