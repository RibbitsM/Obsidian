- At the time of the greatest expanse of the Ottomans, Vienna was considered the main seat of power in the Holy Roman Empire
- It also happened to be right on the border of Ottoman Hungary, and became a frequent target from Buda
- After Suleiman had failed to siege it in the 1500s, his successor was determined to take it
- He mustered a massive army to attack Vienna, with the siege force outnumbering the entire Habsburg army
- The Sultan at the time was Mehmed IV, a spoiled and weak ruler that was propped up heavily by his Grand Viziers while he spent all his time hunting rather than ruling
- The Grand Vizier leading this specific siege was Kara Mustafa Pasa, delivering an ultimatum to the peoples of Vienna
- At the time, the Habsburg army was mostly on the other side of Europe around the Rhine
- The Holy Roman Emperor at the time, Leopold I was similar to Mehmed IV, but with a passion for music rather than hunting
- Him and most of the nobility immediately abandoned Vienna, and left it in the hands of the remaining nobles and the head of the Vienna garrison
- The Habsburgs did manage to assemble an impressive alliance against the Ottomans including the Polish commonwealth, the Russian Tsars, and the Republic of Venice
- However this was going to take a lot of time to put to use
- Vienna did have impressive fortifications that withstood Ottoman artillery very well
- The Turks were forced to resort to sapping efforts through a complex series of trenches and tunnels
- The siege went on for 60 days with the usual disease and starvation harming the citizens
- Eventually Turkish sappers managed to plant an especially large mine under the inner wall, blowing a huge hole in Vienna's defenses
- But on Sept 11, when it looked like Vienna would be lost, a relief force of Austrian and German forces alongside the Polish commonwealth army led by Jan Sobieski
- The Vizier made an error by dividing his forces with his best men continuing the siege and the others meeting the relief forces
- Despite being a relic of the cavalry age, the Polish winged hussars were incredibly effective in this battle
- In one of the largest cavalry charges in European history, 18,000 polish cavalry smashed the Ottoman lines
- This caused a retreat, and the Habsburgs and their armies pursued them back into their own territory
- In the resulting Treaty of Karlowitz in 1699, the Ottomans were forced to make major territorial concessions
- This was the first major Ottoman loss in 400 years, and was disastrous for the empire
- Most of Hungary, Slovenia, Ukraine, and Transylvania was lost permanently, and led to a significant reduction in raids
- Also gave up the Crimean peninsula and the rest of it's northern Black Sea territory
- A society based so much on expansion being forced to give up expansion was a huge psychological blow to most Ottomans
- Also created tension with many groups that were forced to stop slave raiding
- From the 1560s onwards, the Ottomans started to decline in comparison to their golden era
- But why did this happen?

**Ottoman Decline**

- One possible explanation was change in the centralization of the govt
- Once admired as strong statesmen, the Sultans were beginning to get less competent
- Ever since Suleyman, the quality of Sultans had declined drastically
- The succession system involved princes being given their own principalities to rule, and when the current Sultan died a battle royale was sparked between all of their sons
- Through trial by fire, any prospective Sultan would be forced to defeat and kill all of their brothers to get there which resulted in very competent rulers (and was definitely against Sharia Law)
- As for Mehmed II, he had 19 siblings to go through to get there
- But when Ahmed I came to power at age 13 in 1598, he refused to kill his brother Mustafa
- From then on, all future princes would be raised in a single building and succession would be decided by seniority
- This resulted in a series of much worse rulers who were often underaged, insane, or both
- Because of this, the government was basically ruled by the viziers or other powerful government servants like the _kapikullari_
- Infamously, there was a period where the government was mostly controlled by the state concubines and mothers of the underaged sultans lasting from 1533-1656

**Military Revolution Cont.**

- In terms of military trends, the military revolution was still going strong in Europe
- Armies were increasing in size and firepower constantly
- Arquebuses gave way for muskets, and new ignition methods were pioneered
- Older firearms were matchlocks, which required the user to light a rope to fire their weapons
- There were rudimentary trigger mechanisms used, but still required a "slow match" or lit rope
- Next was the wheellock invented in the 1500s that used a piece of pyrite and a high friction wheel
- This made it work in all weather, but were expensive and difficult to build and were never employed en masse in armies
- Finally in the 1600s the flintlock came around using a flint and steel mechanism which effectively made wheel and matchlocks obsolete
- The popularization of smaller carbines and pistols now allowed cavalry to use gunpowder weapons from horseback
- Accuracy was still very poor, and reloading on horseback was near impossible
- New units were created, Cuirassiers with helmets and breastplates armed with sabres and pistols
- Harquebusiers were lightly armored cuirassiers, and dragoons were infantry that arrived to the battle on horseback but fought on foot
- In addition, pike and shot was replaced with bayonets that allowed the rifle to function at both close and far range
- Again, these new inventions were very effective and very expensive
- For example, from the 1550s to 1600s Spain's territory increased drastically, but their military expenditure also doubled every year
- Even with Moctezuma's gold gained from their colonization of the Mexica, the Spanish crown went bankrupt almost every decade
- Expenditure was accelerating faster than even the largest states could keep up with
- At this point, warfare was something that could only be paid for by the combined wealth of the state, not just individuals, even the crown
- Monarchs could no longer rely on their private reserves to fund warfare, and needed more cash
- Schumpeter outlined the 3 Cs for a state to wage war
- Cash economy, Consent to taxation, and Collection by the state
- Somehow states must raise immense amounts of cash without citizens revolting in protest
- This took a while to get right, and caused many civil wars sparked by taxation
- The 1600s could be broadly defined by a rift between society and the state

**Ottoman Military Trends**

- The Ottomans continued to expand their firepower
- Also expanded the number of troops paid in cash who were mostly palace troops like janissaries
- The most significant difference was the loss of the sipahis that had once made up so much of the Ottoman army
- Across Europe countries were expanding their armies and navies and the Ottomans had to keep up
- The exception of course was Spain with their constant overspending
- The Ottomans were also reluctant to accept new technology, not only in fortifications but with bayonets, cuirassiers, or even small things like bandoliers
- Also resisted change in tactics, preserved the tradition of large units without distinct uniforms
- Likely based on the idea that one-on-one, Ottoman soldiers were superior and as such these innovations weren't required
- From the perspective of the Hungarians that fought them frequently, there were 3 advantages
- The first was fortification, specifically their lack of the _trace italienne_
- Next was a lack of proficiency in artillery, most Ottoman gunners had little math knowledge
- Most important was a lack of efficient organization, Western forces regularly outperformed Ottoman forces through drilling and effective division of command
- Like the Western states, the Ottomans had to meet the three Cs to keep running
- The main cashflow option for the Ottomans was plunder, but they had already defeated all their weaker enemies and had only gunpowder empires left
- Taxes were raised and new taxes added, all being paid in cash
- What was more difficult was turning non-cash taxes in to cash
- Of course this was dangerous for the state, as raising taxes caused unrest
- To help remedy this the state regularly debased the currency, basically just printing more money to fund their wars rather than getting it from taxes
- In the long term this of course caused massive inflation
- A major disadvantage was that the original system relied on a lack of cash, _sipahis_ were always paid in land they were allowed to collect tax in and personal plunder
- This was what pushed the _sipahis_ out of the army, the state would repossess their _timar_ land allotments and sold them to local elites for cash
- As for consent, the Ottomans had strong executive powers and usually just pushed any new changes through unilaterally and then put down any resulting protests
- The Ottomans were already effective at collecting tax, but expanded their bureaucracy even further
- This also was a new source of cash, bureaucrats were allowed to simply buy their positions and so when cash was short the Ottomans would fire some and make money replacing them
- Of course this led to an immense amount of corruption as these new bureaucrats would try to get their investment back by embezzling money
- This was all happening while the rampant inflation was happening
- Despite all these issues, the Ottomans were keeping up
- But as the 1600s went on, military expense rose astronomically
- By 1930, 77% of the state budget went into the military, meaning almost every cent gained from taxes went straight to the army
- It only got worse from here, with the Ottomans struggling to maintain the same state while their Western neighbours were exploding in terms of profits
- The insane levels of corruption meant that only about a quarter to a third of tax revenue collected actually made it to Istanbul
- The janissary system also changed, new janissaries were now more often Muslim or children of existing janissaries rather than the _devsirme_ system
- This led to a lower level of control, and a shorter training period and less professionalism
- The era of strict discipline and full-time soldiers had passed
- For example, out of an artillery regiment of 1000 men, almost all of them would be retired or reassigned but were still on the books collecting their wages and meals
- To compensate, the state resorted to arming commoners (_raeya_) to fight who would be demobilised when not needed
- This violated the structure of Ottoman society, destroying the boundary between tax collectors and tax payers as well as creating animosity with janissaries
- Also created a large population of angry armed ex-soldiers in Anatolia which naturally led to tons of banditry and revolts
- During this time, non-Muslims became more and more marginalized as military changes and taxation reforms often targeted non-Muslims first
- The benefits that had made Christians accept Ottoman rule had disappeared
- This increased distrust towards "infidel" innovations which drastically slowed the adoption of new technology being employed by the Europeans
- Any and all reforms had to be justified in a religious context

**Conclusion**

- The Ottomans lost their advantage in several areas
- Weakening of the state, stagnation of military innovation and internal division were the main factors
- Also struggled with the three Cs of Cash, Consent, and Collection
