- Three key questions about war in the course
- What is war?
- Is war inherently social?
- Is society inherently warlike?

**What is War?**

- Not just the act of killing, more complex and involved
- Goes beyond expression of aggression and violence, several wars with no casualties

**Working Definition of War**

- Must be deliberate, not started by accident
- Usually persistent, last longer than at least a day
- Must be started by a cohesive group, that has the authority or claims the authority of any sort of community
- Each side is defined, and they engage in violence against each other which is organized and socially acceptable
- Warfare and warcraft refer to things related to the conduct of war like organizations, technologies, skills, etc.
- Organization is key in the process of war, good social cohesion is usually required
- Often waged on behalf of a whole group or order, not because of a single person

**Is War Inherent to Society?**

- Some people say that specific societies are especially warlike
- Common belief across many cultures that war is an inherent part of society
- Thomas Hobbes was a notable advocate of this opinion, wrote about it in Leviathan
- Ideologies, political systems, and religions have all been described as prone to war
- Evidence of "war" happening in nature, groups of chimpanzees have been observed working together to kill other
- Called coalitionary killing
- War between groups of chimpanzees observed in Gombe in the 70s and recently in Gabon between chimps and gorillas
- Targeted killing of infant gorillas by the chimpanzees, very organized
- But what is the evolutionary advantage of war?
- Instead of just competing individually, why kill in a group?
- There is so much coordination required, similar to hunting but without the reward
- No immediate payoff, just the notion of some vague future gain
- Chimps are more likely to start war when there are many males in one group and few in another group
- More of a case of opportunity, only happens when the target is weak
- Reduces the risk inherent to war
- In the case of the chimps, the eventual payoff is more territory and more offspring
- However, this same logic can't really be applied to humans

**Historical Evidence**

- Walls are usually a good indicator that warfare occurred in a society
- Evidence of walls potentially used for defense dating back to the Neolithic era
- Of course it's difficult to tell what they were actually used for, and debate continues about the earliest evidence of war
- The same goes for ancient arrowheads found that could have been used for war or hunting
- One good example is the walls of Hacilar in Turkey that archeological evidence indicated that they were burnt to ash and then rebuilt thicker than before
- In Tel Hamoukar in Syria, the city walls were seen to be pockmarked by rocks thrown from a sling, alongside rocks that seem to be shaped to fit into a sling
- Mass burial sites are also good evidence of wars in the early eras
- Especially if there is evidence of defensive wounds on the bodies
- A burial site in Sudan had several of these bodies, but could have been deposited there over time
- However in Austria, a site was found with many bodies that were similar ages and were exposed for a length of time before being buried
- This could have been an execution site or prisoners of war, but could have also been a sacrificial site
- Evidence of war in early human history is actually pretty sparse, these examples here are a majority of what has been found
- If war is truly inherent to humanity, shouldn't there be more sites like these?
- Lawrence Keely argued in his book _War Before Civilization_ argues that almost 90% of known hunter-gatherer societies engaged in warfare
- In addition, he argues that many of these people died due to warfare, as many as 1 in 5 or even 1 in 4
- The ability to effectively wage war would have been crucial to these societies
- The combination of good will towards those in the group and violent hostility to those outside the group may have been extremely beneficial

**States of War**

- Bellicist theory cites war as the main motivation for the creation of the state
- Many thinkers in social science have voiced this opinion, that war is completely or at least mostly responsible for the birth of the state
- Philosophical and religious approaches usually condemn warfare, and it is seen as a violation of either morality or some sort of divine will
- War was opposed by the church for many years, soldiers used to be excommunicated from the church is they killed in war as it was a violation of the 10 commandments
- Thinkers like Rousseau believed war to be against human nature and coerced by their environments
- The prevailing view among anthropologists was that war was not a common occurrence among hunter-gatherers
- Man is a peaceful animal, and in the absence of the state war would not happen, therefore war is a product of the state
- In the case of chimpanzees, some primatologists argue that this is exclusive to chimps, and no other primates are seen to act like this
- Chimps are the exception among primates, not the rule
- And even among chimps, many bands never engage in warfare
- The cause of coalitionary killing may be pressures in the environment instead
- UNESCO and the American Anthropological Association state that it is incorrect to say that humans inherited warfare from primates

**Societies Without Wars**

- There is an assumption that hunter-gatherer societies today are similar to hunter gatherers in the past, but there is no real evidence for this
- The prevalence of war in modern hunter gatherer groups may be a product of the environment, similar to the argument about chimps
- In addition there are several modern hunter gatherer groups that are pacifists or simply do not engage in warfare
- For example, the Moriori were almost wiped out by the Maori on Chatham Island due to refusing to fight back
- Other examples are the Jains, or some Christian sects like the Amish, Quakers, and Hutterites
- Overall, it is likely that war is not an inherent trait, but is learned
- The Marshall Study examined soldiers that refused to engage in violence and concluded that less than 25% of soldiers would fire towards the enemy even if they were in danger
- Many soldiers are reluctant to kill, this is why firing lines exist because if diffuses the responsibility of killing
- However, with proper training you could solve this "problem" and cultivate an environment where people are more willing to kill
- Using this information, the US army was able to significantly reduce the percentage of soldiers who refused to kill
- Therefore aversion to killing is an inherent trait, but can be broken down through training and conditioning

**Conclusions**

- War is deliberate and violent confrontation started by a cohesive group that has the authority of a larger community
- War is inherently social
- Societies clearly have the capacity for warfare, but in practice it is not universal, it varies between societies
- War emerges from society, but also works against it
