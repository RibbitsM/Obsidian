- Two case studies of conflict on the northwest coast of North America

**Sheet'k****á**

- First is in Sheet'ká in the Alaskan panhandle, which was a territory of Russia at the time
- They were there to trade for otter pelts, usually with guns
- In fact, they would often only trade for guns, and high quality arms as well including powder and shot
- There was a large fortified village named Noow Tlein in modern day Sitka
- However, the Russians wanted to use this area to build a trading post
- The Tlingit were not thrilled, but allowed them to build Fort St Michael in the area, about 11 kilometers north of Sitka
- Almost immediately, relations between the Tlingit and Russians began to worsen as the Russians began to mistreat the locals
- Basically bullying them into slave labour and assaulting local women
- The Tlingit knew that there would be consequences if they expressed their dissatisfaction, so they organized in secret
- Two chieftains amassed an alliance of many Tlingit and Haida clans armed not just by the Russians but American traders as well
- Attack was carried out on June 18, 1802
- When the Russians went out to fish, the alliance performed a two pronged attack on the fort from both the land and sea sides
- The defenses of the fort were breached easily, and the defenders slaughtered or captured and the walls burned
- Simultaneously, another group found the Russian hunting party and killed them
- One Russian who was outside the fort at the time witnessed the destruction of the fort in hiding
- In October of 1804, the Russians attempted to retake Sitka, but in the meantime the Tlingit had built a new fort that was resistant to cannons
- They specifically made the walls out of flexible saplings that would bend to absorb the impact of the cannonballs
- The Russians were forced to storm the fortress on foot, but were repelled and driven back to their ships
- However, the Tlingit canoe carrying their entire supply of gunpowder was blown up, forcing them to withdraw
- Even with the fort occupied, the Russians couldn't really do anything since they could not win in the forest or get more supplies
- In the end, both sides agreed neither could win and made peace

**Yuquot**

- While it is not very populated today, the Nootka Sound was a key residence for the Mowachaht in the late 18th century
- This was also the intended location of the first permanent residence on the Northwest coast
- Maquinna was the chieftain of the Mowachaht here
- In 1803 a ship named _The Boston_ came into the sound and was received by Maquinna
- Upon leaving, the captain of the Boston gave Maquinna a gun for shooting ducks
- The next day, Maquinna came back with the gun saying that the gun was damaged, and requested for it to be repaired
- The captain was upset, and tossed it to his blacksmith saying that "this savage doesn't know how to handle a gun"
- However, Maquinna knew what he was saying, and was highly insulted
- Later that night, the Mowachaht overran the ship and killed the crew, except for John Jewitt, the ships's blacksmith
- He was kept as a slave for years in the Nootka Sound, where Jewitt kept a journal
- At some point he was freed or escaped, and published the journal
- Judging by both these examples, the native people of the northwest were not only accustomed to war, they were proficient at it
- They were also eager to improve at it and gain new arms, unlike many other native groups encountered in North America

**Hunter/Fisher-Gatherer Warfare**

- One thing that distinguishes the people of the Pacific Northwest is their way of life, and how they get their food
- These societies are reliant on wild animals, plants and marine life, no agriculture or domestication involved
- These groups would encourage the growth of local populations, but never directly
- The size of their communities were usually quite small, and moved around frequently to follow food sources
- Social structures were relatively simple without much disparity between individuals
- Very egalitarian groups, often only made up of a family or extended family
- Many forager societies have been observed to have individual violence, but rarely engaging in organised warfare
- Various forager groups have been described as peaceful, or non-violent, but clearly this is not true of all hunter gatherer societies
- What distinguishes groups like the Semai or Penan from the Mowachaht?
- Archaeological evidence suggests that war has been present in the PNW since at least 1000 BCE
- There is a little evidence before this, but only individual injuries or weapons
- In the time between 1000 BCE and 1000 CE the evidence of violence increases dramatically
- We begin to find clubs that are clearly made to kill people as opposed to animals, alongside fortifications and mass burials of bodies with violent trauma
- Many skeletons were found with wounds closely matching the clubs also commonly found
- There also seemed to be more of these instances in the North than in the South

**War as a Solution**

- Violence is present in pretty much all communities, but at various levels of organization
- The usual answer is that war provided some sort of material reward or was the answer to some sort of societal problem
- One explanation is that war was the solution to human overpopulation
- Warfare would emphasize have male children rather than female children
- As a result, men would die through war, and women would die through infanticide
- This explanation was popularized by Marvin Harris
- However, this entire argument hinged on the idea that women did not fight in older societies
- This has been debunked slightly by recent archaeological evidence
- Another common assumption is competition for resources, either because of greed or scarcity
- The idea of resource hoarding is especially important in the context of the PNW coast where resources are plentiful
- Raymond Kelly argues that wars are rare among hunter gatherers, but only as long as they are small family groups
- But with the development of more complex societies with larger groups, we get war
- A good example of this is a clan, which is technically a lineage group, but they are much larger than even an extended family
- Once organized into groups like this, the idea of who is connected to who expands
- Say someone in another clan kills someone from your clan
- Not only are you connected to the person who was killed, but not only is that one person responsible for their death, but their whole clan too
- Without that social structure, resulting conflict would be limited to only a few people

**War in Hunter Gatherer Societies**

- Hunter gatherers are also people accustomed to killing
- Even though complexity is higher, these groups are still small
- Due to the social structure, wars are usually fought only by the willing
- Difficult for one person to impose their will on a group
- Not lengthy, complex wars, usually only a few days for a simple purpose
- Little permanent structures means no siege warfare
- Wars usually sparked by limited resources, and resolved peacefully
- The tools and strategies used for hunting large animals are easily applicable to killing humans
- Common strategies include scouts, silent signalling, and ambushes
- The vast majority of hunter gatherer warfare involve deception, either luring the enemy into a certain area or attacking unexpectedly
- In Western warfare a lot of these strategies are frowned upon in war
- Also hunter gatherers tend to attack with superior numbers

**First Nations of BC**

- There is diversity in all First Nations, but especially in BC
- In terms of language families, there are 6 in BC alone
- Crazy linguistic differences in groups not very far apart
- Although all are hunter gatherers, some are classic nomadic foragers like the Kaska and Dane-Zaa
- But those living by the coast are much different, still hunter gatherers but with permanent settlements, rich natural resources, complex societies, and advanced food storage procedures
- There are hereditary chiefs and clear social strata as well
- Wars could be started over territory of course, but also for non-material reasons
- Often war wasn't waged over property and were instead lent out, shared, etc, but a strong sense of owning territory was felt
- But much more often wars were fought over honour and prestige
- For example, Maquinna slaughtering the crew of the Boston over an insult
- In some communities if an important figure dies, this is a cause for war
- Essentially "spreading the grief" by making nearby communities experience loss as well
- Masks, ritual regalia, dances, and songs were passed around as property in most societies, and could be acquired either by buying or trading, but also by killing the current owner and taking them
- They were seen as vessels of spiritual power
- There was also widespread practice of slavery on the northwest coast, with population of slaves being up to 20% in some areas
- Owning slaves was a sign of status, and wars were fought expressly for the purpose of capturing slaves
- Wars were usually organized by tribal chiefs, and there may have been specific "war chiefs" that earned authority as commanders during war
- Also private warfare fought by smaller groups of friends and family for the purpose of gaining material goods or slaves
- War was fought by trained warriors for whom war was their chosen vocation, who were often disliked in their community in some groups
- Since being a warrior requires being deceitful and disregarding the wider rules of society, they were generally regarded as antisocial people
- In addition, some societies, prevented warriors from having families and acquiring wealth
- Warriors were usually men, but evidence of women warriors exist
- Set battles were unusual, and surprise attacks were most common
- Fighting units seemed to not exist, warfare was much more individual
- Even in larger conflicts with big alliances, they usually did not last for more than one battle
- These warriors rode in large war canoes containing up to 50 people with room for any loot they may gather
- We have evidence of raids being carried about from many miles away, the Haida Gwaii were seen raiding groups as far south as Oregon
- Very similar to the Vikings, they would appear suddenly from the sea and raid villages
- Specific anti-canoe weapons were built to sink canoes by tying large rocks to stones
- In addition, some larger canoes were equipped with sails and even swivel guns acquired through trade
- Coastal communities often built fortified villages much like in Sitka
- These came in a variety of forms from underground houses to lookouts to trench embankments and palisades
- A trench embankment is when a deep ditch is dug on a peninsula, separating the landmass from the mainland that the population of a village could retreat to
- Surprise attacks were so common that many villages had multiple night watch towers
- Some warriors even had metal daggers and short swords pre-contact
- These were usually made from copper or iron
- Armour was also a common feature far before european contact, which actually went away post-contact presumably since wooden armour was ineffective against more powerful firearms (although they stopped musket balls)
- These were similar to samurai armour and involved multiple layers
