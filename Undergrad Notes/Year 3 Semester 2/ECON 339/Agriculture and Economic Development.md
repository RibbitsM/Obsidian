- Marxist section of the course
- Argued that different "phases" of human history were defined by the prominent technology of the time
- Technology is not necessarily a bad thing, has the capability for incredible levels of production
- Problem is that technology leads to the alienation of workers
- If mediated properly technology can be a force for good, but currently comes at the expense of valuing pure production over the well-being of the worker
- Marx cites this alienation as being a result of a loss of a meaningful connection to their work
- Argues that work should be a fulfilling and creative process, not just a means to acquire capital
- Capitalism not only separates the worker from the work, but the products they produce and their fellow human beings
- Especially focused on technological change, believes human history is defined not by ideology but by pragmatism
- The material circumstances which include available technology are the principal root of change
- The economic makes up the foundation of society and dictates the superstructure of society which is made up of creative pursuits like science, philosophy, art, etc.
- Generally flow goes from the base to the superstructure, but can conceivably move in the other direction
- The modes of production determine the methods for creating the necessities of life
- These are things like health, food and shelter but could also extend to education, science, etc.
- Each of these modes of production is also characterized by its division of labour
- Represented by not only the economic base, but also the social superstructure
- Base shapes the superstructure, and the superstructure maintains the base

**Economic Base**

- Two core components of the base
- The forces of production consist of labour plus the means of production
- This includes the equipment needed for production but also land and raw resources
- Modern equivalent would be the factors of production
- The social relations of production determine the organization of society
- Structured based on their relation to the means of production, example would be like a feudal system where social status determines your role in the production system
- Also can think of this as the structures of property ownership and labour organization
- The natural extension of this idea is to consider that people are indelibly shaped by the production structure they are born into
- It's not just your role that is determined but the way you think as well

**Marx's Stages of History**

- Six stages are named, beginning with primitive communism
- According to Marx this process is not yet over, the next stage of history is yet to come and will be defined by communism
- Since capitalism is inherently flawed, the alienation caused by the system will eventually spark a global social revolution that will transform the global system
- These "contradictions" are inherent to an improper form of production and can be seen in past modes of production like slavery
- This idea was pioneered by Hegel who created the concepts of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis
- Every thesis has it's contradiction, the antithesis which leads to synthesis, the creation of a new and improved thesis
- Evolutionary process on a societal scale
- Marx does credit capitalism for increasing the extent of the global market to an unprecedented scale across the globe
- This prodigious expansion led the former feudal system to fall apart to be replaced by division of labour and manufacturing
- The guildmaster and apprentice system made way for specialized assembly line positions and mass industrial production
- New technologies made this possible, handmade products were insufficient and had to be replaced by machines to meet the new demand
- The event that really pushed this over the edge was the "discovery" of the New World
- As industrialization progresses, communication and transport technology develops, which grows the capital of the bourgeoise and so on
- The former aristocratic power structure was replaced by the new industrial class
- This is all depicted as positive, but there are downsides that Marx mentions
- As capitalism spreads it also erodes national lines and the world becomes more cosmopolitan
- This is disastrous for domestic culture and industry as the world becomes homogenous
- Not only in terms of product, but in terms of culture and media as well
- He also cites talking points that we now associate with commercial liberals, national divides are weakened as elites across the world are united on class lines
- The downside here is in the exploitation of wage labour
- At the time, manufacturing occurred at home, each household had it's own production implements and all households were in constant competition for production
- The introduction of the factory changes this, bringing the workers together
- Marx argues that bringing these labourers together will lead to the inevitable revolution
- He also mentions the occasional commercial crises experienced by the business world which seem to increase in severity
- Since constant expansion is unsustainable, society must occasionally either expand to new markets or destroy the old ones through warfare
- In addition, supply and demand of labour dictates that when unemployment increases, those labourers still employed are incentivized to work harder
   

- Here we have a classical and neoclassical labour market
- In the classical market the labour supply curve is perfectly elastic, there is an infinite supply of labour at a subsistence wage
- Wsub represents the subsistence wage
- Only in the neoclassical model does labour have some sort of scarcity and bargaining power
- There's actually a lot of discourse if this was true or not, how were wages affected by the societal environment
- Many scholars suggest that the labour supply was not infinite but was probably fairly abundant
- The reason for this difference is explained by Marx to be because of the increase in surplus labour caused by the accumulation of capital
- As capital replaces labour, then unemployment increases as well as competition for jobs
- Agriculture used to be carried out communally, villages would be surrounded by fields and the citizens would work those fields to gain their individual sustenance
- This land was generally leased from the landlord, but just received a portion of the product as payment
- Private ownership came with the fencing off of fields where independent farmers would own their own land which came with high status
- This was the driving force of the agricultural revolution, fencing off fields increased productivity and decreased reliance on labour
- New technologies increased the use of capital in agriculture and increased the wealth of landowners
- Reflected on the graph, the surplus of labour is so great that wages never rise above subsistence levels
- For Marx, the forces of supply and demand are tools of capitalism and should be opposed by unionization of labour (wage shouldn't be set by the market)
- In this situation, labourers are dependent on the capitalist class

**Alienation** ^5e67c0

- This process of creating the surplus of labour effectively forces labourers to work whatever job they can, not the job they are passionate about
- This alienates the worker from their labour, it becomes something detrimental to the spirit rather than reinforcing it
- Marx believes that it is this process that will eventually lead to the uprising of the proletariat
- Marx and Engels imagined a communist society of involving abolition of private property rights, graduated income tax, abolition of inheritance rights, and centralized banking/communication
- Also prescribed free education of children and extension of factories and agriculture
- Ironically, most of this has come to pass in many modern states

**Development**

- At the most basic level, agriculture supports development by allowing sedentary society
- Also creates a home market and grows labour pool of other sectors even if it is subsistence agriculture
- If there is a surplus, encourages exporting and importing goods
- Difference between subsistence and value-added agriculture
- Rising productivity is key to all of these factors
- Important question is how this growth relates to institutions
- The industrial revolution in Britain is important, but it was only possible because of the previous agricultural revolution
- Technological change in agriculture increases total factor productivity, but also can increase partial factor productivity
- Agriculture also creates a home market for goods in other sectors, since agriculture creates surplus income and tools and other supplies are needed for agriculture
- Even subsistence agriculture leads to farmers occasionally selling surplus or buying tools to make farming more efficient
- Subsistence agriculture can support the labour pool for other sectors since agriculture is often seasonal in nature
- Also facilitates trade between rural and urban environments, farmers go to urban centers to sell produce and buy tools
- As agriculture becomes more efficient, surplus grows and can be used for exports
- Surplus also functions as a form of savings, less perishable goods can be stockpiled or pickled or can be sold for currency savings
- Agriculture also facilitated other kinds of trade, notably the slave trade was motivated by a need for labour on plantations in the New World
- Productivity increases in labour free it up for other industries, and productivity increases in land free up land as well
- Also can reduce food prices, which raises standard of living across the board
