- Harley's paper exists as an example of technological change, but also has a counterpoint to the standard view of technology popularized by Schumpeter and Mansfield
- The content is only applicable to a very specific topic, bulk ocean shipping, but can be seen as an alternate view of technological change
- Three stages of technological change: invention, innovation, and diffusion
- In the example of steam ships, we are watching their diffusion
- Inventions can either be basic inventions, totally new devices, or improvements on existing tech
- The steam ship would be an example of the latter, an improvement on sailing technology
- When looking at the process of invention one possible path is that invention first arises from science, usually some discovery or theory
- This idea is translated into a tangible product through experimentation and research, usually in a private firm or public laboratory
- Innovation is the application of an invention to some sort of purpose, usually commercial
- In the example of the steam engine it had many applications, not just in maritime transport
- Used in mills, trains, etc.
- Also used heavily in the mining industry, steam pumps were used to get water out of mines
- This also doesn't have to be physical, in the tech economy we're seeing more and more applications of data analysis and other software developments
- Diffusion is then how the new technology is adopted, and is generally where the economic value of the invention is determined
- Most studies of technological change are related to diffusion, focusing on adoption speed and why some inventions are adopted and not others
- Diffusion is generally measured as rate of adoption
- One especially hotly debated topic is why some communities and firms are slower to adopt compared to others
- Universal adoption can often take a long time, it's rare for a new technology to have an absolute advantage over what it is replacing

**Economic Framework**

- Competitive equilibrium is characterized by minimizing average total cost and setting prices equal to marginal revenue and cost
- Harley argues that the shipping industry is almost perfectly competitive, fairly low BTEs mean that the market was made up of many different companies owning a few ships each
- Very little market power, the largest firms had about 100 ships which was a fraction of the total
- Depending on which is more profitable, rational firms would presumably buy either sailing or steam vessels
- Common theory is that new markets or markets exposed to drastic change exist in a temporary state of disequilibrium before adjusting
