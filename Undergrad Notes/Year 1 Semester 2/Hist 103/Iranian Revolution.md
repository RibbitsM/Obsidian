The 1953 Coup

- Dynastic system in Iran, the Qajar Dynasty lasted from 1789 to 1921
- Iran subject to British incursion and was losing land to Russia
- Gave economic concessions to Britain on oil and other things
- After a coup in 1921 the New Shah is established by the UK and gives more major oil concessions
- The Anglo Persian Oil Company was a British oil company created in the colonial economic system
- Constitutional monarchy, both a Shah and elected politicians
- In the 1950s the reformist elected officials the government nationalized oil which challenged the rights of the Anglo-Persian oil company
- After the 1953 coup, gave more power to the UK controlled Shah
- US and UK organized the coup, and the outcome benefited them greatly
- Mohammad Mosaddegh was the reformist who sought to nationalize the oil industry based on nationalist and anti-imperialist ideals
- Many policy parallels to South America, created unemployment insurance, sick leave, and rural housing
- Most of these reforms were undone by the coup
- Progressive reformer, saw removing British influence from Iran as a way to gain economic power to create reform
- US intervened because of concerns over cold war and spread of communism
- Nationalizing oil and reformist policies looked like communism, because Iran was on the Soviet border
- Britain was losing influence worldwide and was interested in asserting their authority in the middle east after the Suez crisis

The White Revolution

- Over the course of the 1960s the Shah becomes more and more reliant on the US
- Get assistance from the CIA to establish intelligence services and get US weapons
- The white revolution emerges as a method of staving off a communist revolution by maintaining central authority
- Land reforms, modernization of farming industry, and some govt corporations are privatized
- Also established female suffrage, education reforms, literacy programs
- Ironically very similar to revolutions put down by the US in other places
- Growing consumer culture in Iran, very westernized and urbanized
- Creates huge socioeconomic disparity and alienates most peasants
- Students marginalized by growing undemocratic practices
- Power becomes more and more concentrated on the Shah and their close circle
- Heavy military spending for no reason also sparks discontent
- The OPEC oil crisis creates a huge increase in revenue for Iranian oil
- However, this also created inflation and the increased wealth was not shared equally at all

The Iranian Revolution

- Discontent and economic inequality increasing
- Urban poor suffering, farmers destroyed by cheap imports
- Concerns about relationship between the Shah and the US
- Religious Iranians raise concerns about moral degeneracy and dangers of western values
- Protests on these topics occur over the summer of 1978
- State represses these protests heavily, but eventually steps back
- Diverse set of voices all calling for the Shah to step down for very different reasons
- With so many competing interests who are only united in opposition to the Shah, there is bound to be trouble
- Shah never truly abdicates, but leaves the country
- An important leader, Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini returns from exile in Iraq
- He was heavily opposed to secularization and westernization
- Supports the Islamic revival and has very conservative views
- Quickly gains lots of support from the religious masses and asserts control over the protestors
- By 1979 there is a new constitution and Khomeini is established as the supreme leader of the Islamic Republic
- Over the next three years millions of Iranians emigrate due to his policies
- In the immediate aftermath, there is a breakdown of US connections with a hostage crisis at the US embassy and sanctions imposed on Iran
- The US supports Iraq in the Iran-Iraq war afterwards
