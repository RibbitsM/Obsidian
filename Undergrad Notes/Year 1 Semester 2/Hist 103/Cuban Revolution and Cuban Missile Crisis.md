- What makes a revolution?
- There was a previous revolution in Cuba in 1933
- Led by Batista, was in control for 10 years after
- In the aftermath, Batista allied with the Communist party and made populist reforms
- Women's suffrage, progressive constitution
- Very in line with other populist movements
- US starts to see the world through anti-communists lenses
- Batista stages coup upon return in 1952
- US-backed regime, Batista goes back on his previous policies
- More oppression, more US military, bans Communist party from Cuba
- All progressive reforms rolled back
- Set the stage for Castro's revolution
- Made a trip back from exile in Mexico in 1956, accumulates popular support
- Batista's regime was immensely unpopular and Castro had much support for his "moral campaign"
- As the advance continues, the US withdraws support in 1958
- Batista falls, and the US recognizes the Castro government in 1959
- Part of long history of US influence and support in Cuba
- Less disruptive than most communist revolutions because of the enormous public support
- Instead of unseating an established leader, Castro drove out a foreign power
- Key aspect is the anti-imperialist nature of Castro's campaign
- Big draw of his policy was "Cuba first"

Revolutionary Policies

- Many takes on the exact timeframe of the revolution
- Period of intense mobilisation in 1960s
- Mass organization, state committed to improving education and health care
- Spirit of volunteerism key support
- Education reform sought to eliminate illiteracy
- Law of universal education finally established in 1960
- Part of a common trend during Cold War of poorer communist countries increasing education
- "Spelling brigades" were squads of teachers sent out to rural communities to spread literacy
- Mass support from the public
- Established agrarian reform, very typical of communist states
- Bans foreign ownership of land in Cuba, lots of estates expropriated

Creates huge conflict with US interests

- Seeks a trade agreement with the USSR in 1960
- Provided the USSR with sugar, in exchange for industrial equipment
- Nationalized oil refineries in Cuba, most of which were US-owned
- Seemed like a clear communist revolution from US eyes, but could also be seen as an anti-imperialist movement
- Americans often confused anti-US movements for pro-communist movements due to obsession over the Cold War

US Response

- US escalates by stopping sugar imports from Cuba
- Inadvertently encourages Cuban relations with the USSR
- As a response, Cuba nationalizes all other US companies
- US had a much larger presence in Cuba than in other countries, this was a large blow
- US sets a general trade embargo on Cuba, further worsening their relationship
- This creates a full break of diplomatic relations with Cuba
- US organizes an attack on Cuba of armed Cuban expatriates and planned by the CIA
- Called the Bay of Pigs incident and is a huge disaster
- The assumption of the US was that the Cuban population was unhappy with the failings of the government, and would side with the US forces
- This turned out to not be true, and the US forces found little support from the people and were swiftly arrested
- Followed up by several assassination attempts on Castro plotted by the CIA
- US expels Cuba from the Organization of American States in 1962, and broadens trade embargo
- Cuban immigrants were given special privilege and were encouraged to immigrate to the US in a time where the US was closing it's borders

Cuban Missile Crisis

- Cuban government seeks military support from the USSR
- The US could launch nukes at the USSR, but not the other way around
- Reasonable to say that US nukes in Turkey were a contributing factor to the Cuban Missile Crisis
- However this somewhat discredits the relationship between Castro and the Soviets
- With Cuba's relationship with the USSR, this was no longer true
- If nuclear missiles were established in Cuba, almost all major American cities could be targeted
- Changed the balance of power between the two powers completely
- Once the US discovers the installations, contacts Khrushchev immediately
- Major outcome is Kennedy's promise to not invade Cuba, solidifying Castro's power
- The USSR points out the double standard, and as a result the US secretly removes nukes from Turkey
- The nukes in Turkey were US's only real bargaining chip in the situation and they had no choice but to back down, but this was never revealed publicly
- Even though Cuba and the USSR win pretty big, the USSR standing down and withdrawing was an embarrassment
- Castro was mostly sidelined and put aside for this issue
- First clear example of MAD, first time both countries could conceivably destroy eachother
- Cold War flashpoint, closest the US and USSR ever came to war
- Threat of nuclear war and global destruction very high
- Symbol of the danger posed to the entire globe by US-Soviet rivalry
- From a Latin American perspective, it's a symbol of successful resistance of US imperialism

Conclusion

- Cuban Revolution was driven by anti-imperialist, Cuban nationalist movement
- Widespread education and economic reforms
- Affected US foreign policy heavily
- Cuban Missile Crisis was caused by this revolution
