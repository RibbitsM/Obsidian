- An empire can be defined as a territory greater than a kingdom that is led by an emperor
- Has supreme power, sometimes over several territories
- Related to the idea of imperialism, which is basically the ideology that forms empires
- Extending the authority of a nation over foreign countries and imposing the countries will on those territories
- Also closely related to colonization
- US international relations had many imperialist tendencies
- Pursued their self interest and economic advantages
- Uses force to attain it's goals and invests in national defense
- Violates the sovereignty of other countries to force them into submission
- However, is the US truly an empire?
- This foreign policy is a stark contrast to the US's national values
- Their actions against foreign nations violates it's domestic values
- Interventionist posture, claims democratic reasons for violating sovereignty
- Americans believe that the US has a special role in history and must spread their free market capitalist ideas
- Related to ideas of colonialism and manifest destiny
- Grandin said that the US acted as a new kind of empire in Latin America
- Also argues that the capabilities of the modern US army were trained in combat with Latin America
- Many of it's current imperialist actions in the Middle East and Asia had their origins in Latin America

Government and Economic Foreign Policy in Latin America

- US foreign policy had 2 main goals
- The first was to maintain stability for US companies, this did not necessarily mean stability for the country
- Local nationalism, labour and land reforms were contrary to US interests
- The second was to maintain US supremacy
- The US consistently blocked any and all European powers from gaining influence in the region
- In the 1890s, US companies established presences for the first time outside the US
- Initially mining, rail, and sugar companies, but later became electricity, oil and agriculture
- In 1900, Mexico received one quarter of all US foreign investment, and Cuba was the US's third largest trading partner
- In 1898 the US made the Philippines and Puerto Rico territories and Cuba a protectorate
- After their war with Spain they seized these territories
- Different from land acquired in mainland US, imperial relationship
- People from these territories were denied citizenship unlike Hawaiians
- Skin colour, economics and social relations clashed with US ideals of their nation
- Had much larger local population than Hawaii or the US west, harder to assimilate or destroy
- In 1903 the US incited revolution in Columbia and received major concessions from the independent Panama government to build the Panama Canal
- Other clear examples of imperialism were several invasions of the Caribbean (34 times)
- Also occupied Honduras, Mexico, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Haiti, Cuba, Nicaragua, Panama, and the Dominican Republic
- In the 1930s the US army was sent to Nicaragua to destroy the leftist revolution led by Augusto Sandino
- First time the US had been involved in a small international conflict
- The UK denounced this action as imperialistic

Good Neighbor Policy

- In 1933 Franklin Roosevelt established the Good Neighbor Policy
- Recognized US's imperialist actions and renounced it's right to intervene in foreign governments
- Changes the tone of US-Latin American relations
- Shifts to "soft power" to increase US influence in Latin America without military intervention
- Roosevelt removed several occupation forces in the Caribbean islands, removed the US's ability to intervene in affairs from the Cuban constitution
- The US negotiates trade treaties with almost every country in Latin America and increases exports to Latin America
- Influences how the US operates in western Europe in 1945 onwards

Conclusion

- The US behaved imperialistically towards Latin America from 1835 to 1933
- US Imperialism softens in 30s due to FDR Good Neighbor Policy
- US actions in Latin America shape US foreign policy in western Europe and onwards
