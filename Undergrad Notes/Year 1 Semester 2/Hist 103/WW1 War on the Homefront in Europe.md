- Rations, production rerouted to war efforts
- Loss of life very visible
- More civilians experienced war than soldiers
- Mobilization of all members of society to support war
- Sense of enthusiasm and euphoria
- Shift in gender of workforce, more female representation
- Xenophobia against immigrants from opposing countries

Scarcity

- Scarcity mainly experienced in European countries, less in North America
- Huge disruption of labour, loss of factory workers and farmers
- Must ramp up to wartime production without workers
- Russia lost a lot of migrant labourers
- Women and unemployed filled this lack of labour
- Britain filled void with trade with US and colonial production
- Gave workers longer hours and encouraged lower consumption levels
- Food and gas rations put into place
- Scarcity worsens as war goes on
- Coal consumption changes across countries
- UK only begins to ration coal in 1917, France has many more issues
- Distribution crippled from 1915 onward, affected much more than UK
- Germany has little coal scarcity until 1916
- Distribution as important as production
- Civilian mortality levels much higher than average for various reasons
- Widespread affect across all European countries
- Malnutrition, cold, work, disruption of life all factors
- Female mortality rate raises dramatically across 5 years
- In Germany female death rate goes from 13 per 1000 to 19 per 1000 in 5 years
- In 1914 Germany imported a quarter of it's food
- Imported mainly grain from Russia, lost Russian trade and US trade
- Britain blocked off German supply routes

Labour and Political Unrest

- WW1 financed by borrowing using war bonds
- Taxes also raised to pay for war production
- Leads to near hyperinflation in many European countries
- More pronounced in Germany
- Purchasing power of workers drops dramatically
- Labour unrest in many countries, turn to unions
- Factor in the 1917 Russian revolution
- Unrest also affects the abdication of leaders in Germany and Austria
- Many more protests and strikes than usual
- Average of 100,000 workers striking per month in 1918
- Theme of demands was an end to the war, peace without annexations
- UK had decline in labour activism and strikes
- 59% decline in amount of strike days, huge contrast to Germany
- However, the end of the war leads to a huge peak in strikes and labour action in the UK
- Pent up frustration about scarcity and inflation held until the end of the war

War dead

- 65 million soldiers enlist worldwide
- Paris has a much higher enlistment rate than other major cities
- Berlin has the highest death rate
- Many women were single mothers with 2 kids
- 55% of men went to war, many injured or killed
- 1/3 of women worked in heavy industry
- Costs of flour raised while price controls put on bread
- Most foreign nationals put in internment camps, others were unemployed
- Constant fear that loved ones would die
- Smaller meals, less nutrition
- 1.7 million Germans died
- Most under 30, half left unrecognizable
- 4.1 million wounded
- 600,000 left with severe mental trauma like paralysis, muteness, blindness, anxiety

Women in Work

- Huge growth in female involvement in the workplace
- 1/3 of workers female by 1918
- Most male dominated industries like metalworking and chemicals
- Exceptions to the rule, in some industries men still were still preferred over women
- Not linear process
- Only by necessity, was resisted by government and factory owners

Conclusion

- Difficult living situation for all
- Food scarcity
- Less purchasing power, more labour unrest
- New family burdens, emotional strain
- More women in wage labour
- Many soldiers suffered death and disability
