- Non-aligned movement
- Movement among powerful southern countries like India and Indonesia to not take sides
- Closely related with the idea of the "Third World" meaning any country not affiliated with the US or the USSR
- Has nothing to do with developing vs industrial countries but has a different meaning now
- Latin America did not have the luxury of joining this non alignment movement
- All of Latin America was solidly under the US's sphere of influence
- Referred to as "our backyard", saw it as their property
- Very much forced Latin America to take the side of the US
- Obviously Cuba is a special exception
- As part of the New World Order after WWII, many Pan-American institutions are formed like the Organization of American States
- Under US control, used to discuss economic issues
- Cuba is expelled from the OAS in 1968
- Most of Latin America signs onto the Rio Treaty in 1947, meaning they must assist eachother in the case of foreign invasion
- Used to justify the US blockade of Cuba
- However, the US did not intervene when Argentina went to war with the UK under a dictatorial government
- Clear example of how the Rio Treaty really only serves to benefit the US

American Anti-Communism

- School of the Americas was a military training school for Latin Americans in Georgia
- Specifically created to train soldiers to combat communism in Latin America
- Designed to discourage guerilla revolutions like the Cuban Revolution
- Theme of the US siding with militaries instead of governments in Latin America
- Trained soldiers in torture tactics and used them to overthrow left wing democracies in Latin America
- US viewed all issues through Cold War goggles
- Policies related to national development were suddenly seen as communist actions
- Not a huge change from how America acted before the Good Neighbor Policy
- That policy was effectively over, and returned to high levels of interventionism
- A new strategy was destabilizing countries by facilitating military coups in countries that established leftists policies

Guatemala, 1954

- One of the first examples of the US clearly causing a foreign coup
- US didn't take issue until the Cold War began
- Juan Jose Arevalo comes to power in 1944 and implements leftist policy
- New labour code to counter awful business practices from the US companies
- US doesn't care because it's still WWII
- Called it "Spiritual Socialism"
- Jacobo Arbenz comes to power in 1951
- Redistributes uncultivated land from the United Fruit Company to citizens
- Compensates the company appropriately, but angers the United Fruit Company
- Secretary of State AND CIA Chief both has close ties to the company
- Wealthy elites in Guatemala and US business owners labelled him as a communist
- Arbenz government bought arms from Czechoslovakia, sealing the deal for the US
- First violation of the Good Neighbor Policy
- CIA starts to destabilize the Guatemalan government by funding local terrorist groups, training mercenaries in the Honduras, and bomb key pieces of infrastructure
- In 1954, the Guatemalan military chooses the side of the US over the destabilized government
- Leads to a military coup in 1954
- Che Guevara witnesses these events firsthand, and recognizes the US role in the events
- Flees to Mexico and meets Fidel Castro and shares his experience
- Similar strategies to those used in the Bay of Pigs invasion

Nicaragua, 1979-1990

- Leftist revolutionaries called the Sandinistas take power from the existing power dynasty named the Somozas, who has been in power since 1937
- Somoza was closely connected to the US and had been trained in the School of the Americas
- Sandinistas acted very similar to the early Cuban Revolution, pushing literacy projects and health care
- Coincided with Ronald Reagan's rise to power in 1980, who pushed US policy much further right
- US starts supplying support to the Nicaraguan National Guard, called the Contras
- Contras repeatedly launch raids into Nicaragua, to destabilize the government
- Reagan spearheads a propaganda project deeming the Contras "Freedom Fighters"
- Somewhat related to how Reagan later deemed the mujihadeen Freedom Fighters as well
- Attempts to cut off Nicaraguan trade and cripple their economy while continuing to fund the Contras

Agency of the Latin American Right

- These movements were not only the responsibility of the US
- Most revolutions were caused by the US supporting local far right groups
- Latin American generals were adamant supporters of anti-communism, and were enticed by US offers of power
- US support generally led to increased military power so most generals were willing to cooperate
- Pretty much every coup took advantage of this local military authoritarianism
- Elites took advantage of US foreign policy to advance their own agendas
- US left leaning presidents did lessen the pressure on Latin America but nothing was rolled back until Carter

Conclusion

- Increased US intervention in Latin America during the Cold War, ignoring the Good Neighbor Policy
- Harsher intervention to establish authoritarian states and overthrowing leftist democracies
- Stifled efforts for economic and social improvement
- Local militaries were active participants in the repression of these ideals
