Solidification after Revolution

- After elimination of the Dual Power, Bolsheviks make peace
- Civil war breaks out in Russia from 1918-1922
- Hostile forces backed by 100,000 foreign troops
- Bolsheviks vs socialists, liberals and tsarists
- As outcome of the civil war, areas on the margin of the Russian empire maintain independence
- Bolsheviks maintained only a small area of territory
- Surrounded by enemies on all sides
- During the civil war food scarcity continues even after coup
- Quality of life is even worse than it was during WW1
- Huge spike in mortality and emigration in cities
- Population of Moscow and Petrograd decrease by over half due to death and emigration
- Once the dust settles, renamed as the Soviet Union
- Struck a balance between socialism and capitalism
- State socialist management, but local capitalism
- Agricultural producers given partial autonomy, contributed quota to the state and sold the rest
- One party of 500,000 members controlled the whole country
- Small elite controlled a large amount of territory
- Repressive practices used to eliminate enemies of the state
- Espionage, purges and establishment of forced labour camps
- Direct attack on the influence of the Russian Orthodox Church
- Removed their authority from schools
- Instead of dual church and state, only state remains
- Widespread censorship of political opponents
- Tangible improvements to quality of life for majority of citizens
- More food, better housing and healthcare, improved education

Stalinism

- Lenin dies, power vacuum in the government
- 3 leading figures in the revolution
- One dies, Trotsky and Stalin compete to replace Lenin
- Stalin wins, Trotsky is sent into exile and is killed on Stalin's orders later
- General Secretary of the Communist Party from 1922-1952
- Very authoritarian, used secret police to enforce authority frequently
- Constantly invokes harsh and violent means to enforce obedience
- Crucial in establishing the nationalities policies
- Jockeys for power in the government from 1924-1927
- Pushes out the more educated and academic "old Bolsheviks" and replaces them with working class radical communists
- Expels Trotsky in 1929
- New Bolsheviks control the government
- Architecture of Stalinist rule is constant competition for influence in government
- Uses purges frequently under Lenin already
- The Red Terror, approximately 100,000 political opponents executed
- Before Stalin rose to power, violence was already a key tool of the USSR
- Establishes precedent for Stalin to purge his personal enemies in 1924
- Small purges at the local level becomes the Great Purge
- Stalin constantly fears dissent and sees enemies everywhere in his government
- Purge lasted from 1936-1938
- Soviet secret police kill a million people and send another million to work camps
- Most are actually loyal to Stalin, purged because of paranoia
- Nikolay Yezhov, the head of the secret police is eventually purged himself and replaced
- Loses in a Stalinist power struggle with Lavrentiy Beria, tried in secret trial and executed in 1940
- Key example of the political environment during Stalin's rule
- Political violence hinges on the secret police, called the Cheka until 1922, then the NKVD
- After the second world war, Stalin's control on the USSR is even tighter
- After Stalin's death in 1953, tone shifts away from surveillance and purges
- Key feature of the state were the five year plans
- Key part of the Soviet economy, set a new goal every five years
- First five year plan had 2 goals, mass industrialization and collectivization of agriculture
- Assertion of authority in two key industries
- Bringing soviet ideals to agriculture
- Expands economic capabilities and spreads the revolution
- Idea that by revolutionizing the economic sector, the ideology would leak into society
- Undoing the mixed economy compromises of the early USSR
- Assertion of central planning authority

Class, Ethnicity and Deportation

- Key part of collectivization was constant increase in quotas
- Forces farmers to keep increasing production
- Ukraine had more of a mixed economy than other areas
- Middle class of farmers called "Kulaks"
- Mass famine in many parts of USSR, mainly Ukraine
- All of the farmers production is seized by the central government
- 3.3 million people die of famine because of collectivization of agriculture
- Labelled a genocide, based on class rather than race, called the Holodomor
- Famine intentional to punish the Kulaks
- In addition, forced migration was very prevalent
- 5 million citizens forced to move to central Asia
- Idea was to increase production in underdeveloped areas
- Strategy to maintain power as well
- 1.7 million Kulaks deported to Siberia and Kazakhstan
- Specific ethnic groups also targeted in the deportation
- Poles and Finns also deported to Kazakhstan
- 175,000 Koreans deported to central Asia as well
- Political and economic rationale, groups that tended to opposed to Soviet ideals were disproportionately affected by forced migration
- Takes on genocidal tone in WW2
- Soviet union invades Poland in 1939, deports 1.5 million poles
- Occupies Poland, half of the deportees die
- Once Germany goes to war against USSR, Germans living in Russia are deported east
- Constant population management to punish disloyal groups

Conclusion

- Bolsheviks win the civil war, consolidate power
- War with Germany ends
- Tangible improvement in life of citizens
- Lenin establishes mixed economy and authoritarian rule
- Stalin establishes system of jockeying for power
- Also introduces violent repression of political opponents and surveillance
- Economy switches to five year plans
- Central planning focuses on mass industrialization and collectivization of agriculture
- Discrimination against Kulaks, mass famine and genocide in Ukraine
- Targeted class and ethnic groups deported to Central Asia and Siberia
- Authoritarianism lifts with Stalin's death
