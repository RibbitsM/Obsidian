Ways We Look at Languages

- Can be viewed with a synchronic or diachronic approach
- Synchronic is looking at how languages are at a certain point in time
- This is good for comparing different dialects
- Diachronic approaches look at the progress of a language over time
- Good for looking at origins of languages or development of a language family
- Synchronic studies also study language differences between different populations
- Can look at language over race, class, gender, location etc
- Diachronic often compares past language to current language
- For example Shakespearean english to current english
- Language is a form of communication through icons and symbols
- Iconic representation is when a word represents something directly like onomatopoeia

Properties of Language

- Communication is not exclusive to humans, but language is
- Hockett identifies 16 design features of human languages
- Animal communication shares some of these properties but not all
- For example one requirement is a vocal/auditory channel
- Pigs have this, but they don't have human language
- However, this also rules out some human languages such as ASL
- ASL is only one of many sign languages
- We focus on 6 properties
- Discreteness, arbitrariness, displacement, productivity, duality of patterning, traditional/cultural transmission

Arbitrariness

- Means that words and symbols bear no resemblance to their meaning
- The word "sheep" has no meaningful connection to an actual sheep
- Unlike the word "baa" which mimics the noise a sheep makes
- This is the difference between iconic and symbolic representation
- Some animals are thought to use arbitrariness
- For example, bird songs can express the presence of a predator but do not mimic that predator

Displacement

- Allows you to discuss something that is not immediately present
- Can talk about past events, objects not in the room, can express future wishes
- Can express hypotheticals, which most animals can't
- However, bees can communicate displacement through their dances
- Can communicate distance and direction

Discreteness

- The feature of smaller units making up communication
- EX: letters make up sounds, make up words, make up sentences, make up conversations
- All these small units are discrete
- Saying a word a little differently doesn't change the meaning
- Bee communication is continuous, any change to the dance changes the meaning
- Basically think of a gradient vs a colour palette
- Continuous is like a red to green gradient, Discrete is like one block of green and one of red
- There is no in-between with discrete communication, no matter how you say the sounds, the same combination of sounds will have the same meaning
- Keyword: productivity

Recursivity/Productivity

- Reminder, Hockett's features are not a hard rule
- Productivity means having a finite set of words that can be combined in infinite new ways
- Can assign new meanings to old words and use existing words in new ways
- EX: Google (noun) becomes a verb (Googled)
- Syntax or sentence structure determines what things can be combined and how
- Recursion is adding parts of a sentence within a sentence
- Build up sentences out of smaller parts
- Songbirds can learn recursive patterns

Duality of Patterning

- Words are made of morphemes and morphemes are made of phonemes
- Bagels is made of Bagel and s, with s implying plurality
- Phonemes are the sounds that make up bagel and s
- Meaning arises from the combination of these phonemes and morphemes
- Duality of patterning is two levels of language, having meaningful levels and distinctive levels
- The morphemes have meaning on their own, but phonemes are only meaningful in combination but are distinctive
- Sounds of a language are meaningless, their only purpose is to form words
- This is thought to be unique to human language

Cultural Transmission

- Human language is not transmitted genetically but culturally
- Language ability develops with experience and input
- Children are much better at learning languages than adults, but their learning is influenced heavily by their surroundings

Where Does Language Come From?

- Unable to definitively answer because physical evidence of spoken languages is basically impossible to find
- Written language developed after spoken language
- One of the earliest linguistic experiments was described by Herodotus in the 4th century
- An Egyptian king had two children raised without hearing any language to see what the "original" language was
- We now know that language is developed mainly through your surroundings so there is no real "original" language
- There is a natural element to language too, children are much more able to learn language than animals
- They can't learn as well or reach the same fluency of human children
- Children who are not exposed to any kind of language have been shown to develop signed communication systems
- Children who aren't exposed to language early in life struggle to reach proficiency in adulthood
- Because of the negative effects of this, experiments involving depriving children of language are now banned for ethics reasons
- Zebra finches learned a level of language where they could use affixes to change meanings of words
- A chimpanzee was taught to communicate in ASL but never reached fluency
- There are many theories about how humans are specially suited to language
- A main one is evolution, our specific kind of vocal apparatus and manual dexterity allows for our kind of communication
- The drawback is that we choke more easily
- Our brain structure also differs from other primates
- Some specific genes that assist communication
- Broca's area is a part of the brain that handles motor ability related to articulation and speech production
- We also have mirror neurons, that activate in response to seeing another person do something, which in humans assists communication
- Also present in monkeys who use them for recognition
- We also have strong pathways connecting language parts of the brain
- Some other theories are related to cognitive patterns and the social/cultural aspects of language
- Language emerged alongside arts and was useful in group survival
- Recursion is key in development of language and distinguishing human language from animals

Language Change

- Languages are constantly in a state of change
- How did we end up with so many languages?
- Important theme of linguistics
- Languages are constantly evolving constructs
- Language change is a normal part of language
- Small generational changes compound over time and lead to large changes and divergence of language
- Languages change through transmission and diffusion
- Transmission is when language is passed from adults to children
- Diffusion is adapting how you speak depending on who you're talking to
- Can be a change to become similar or different depending on the context
- The balance between these two forces in controlled by incrementation and generational identity
- Incrementation is the process of embracing changes at a gradual pace to prevent the language from changing too much
- Generational identity is the process of how new slang develops in isolation in a generation, language develops within a certain group and those in that group are best at using it
- While Broca's area is unique to humans, animals have similar structures
- Some morphemes are made of only one phoneme, so they can exist on both levels at once
- Recursion also allows for use of the same structure infinite times
