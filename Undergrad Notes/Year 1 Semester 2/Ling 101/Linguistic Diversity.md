Languages of the World

- English is talked about a lot in lower level linguistics courses
- This is because it's the language of instruction and is good for examples
- Doesn't represent the variety of linguistic concepts, like how English lacks certain sounds
- An example of reduplication in Hindi is the phrase "and the like"
- Mez-vez means "tables and the like", aam-vaam means "mangoes and the like"
- Different meaning of reduplication
- Reduplication in mandarin can mean "a little"
- Jiao jiao means "teach a little"
- In Austronesian language, reduplication can emphasize a concept
- Salish uses reduplication in a way where you can duplicate more than once for new meanings
- Linguists are interested in investigating all languages, not just English
- All languages have grammar, and all are equally complex
- Linguistic diversity is a measure of how different languages are in a geographical region
- For sure learning a language as a second language can be harder or easier, but the first language you learn is always easy
- Papua New Guinea has enormous linguistic diversity with 800 plus languages in a small area and population
- Places like Japan and Sweden have much lower linguistic diversity
- National Geographic measures linguistic diversity between 0 and 1, with 1 meaning everyone speaks the same language, and 0 meaning no one speaks the same language
- Useful for determining cultural diversity, and useful in linguistics as a science
- Provides an empirical measure of diversity
- Used for theories, building typologies and predictions

Linguistic Relativism vs Determinism

- The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis is that language directly shapes the way a person sees the world and how they think
- An example would be how masculine and feminine nouns shape how people think about gender
- The strong version of this hypothesis is linguistic determinism
- Hypothesises that language determines thought and limits cognition
- The weak version is linguistic relativism
- Hypothesises that language influences cognition and worldview but does not determine it
- Linguistic relativism is much more widely accepted
- An example of why linguistic determinism is untrue would be how colours are seen in the Shuswap language
- There is only a single word for both green and yellow, but Shuswap speakers can still recognize the difference between these colours
- Linguistic determinism is considered too limiting of a theory
