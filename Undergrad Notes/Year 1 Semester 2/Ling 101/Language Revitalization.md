- Language revitalization is a concentrated effort to restore the transmission of a language that is falling out of use in a community
- Needed when speech community is declining, or transmission between adults and children has halted
- Hebrew and some indigenous languages have been the subject of revitalization efforts
- A language having a small number of speakers doesn't mean that a language needs to be revitalized
- On the other hand, a language with many speakers may need revitalization
- Numerical measures can't appropriately represent the state of the language
- Some languages have historically had small speaking communities so there is no inherent need to increase that amount
- The number of people learning the language is just as important as the number of speakers
- Can indicate the future of a speech community
- Increasing the amount of learners and the level of access to the language is key to revitalization, and the usefulness of the language
- A language program is fairly useless if there is no benefit associated with learning the language
- Even if the learning is mainly in the classroom, it should be applied to situations too
- Don't just learn the language, use it
- Even though speakers may be dwindling, this does not mean that the language is decaying, the speakers usually have fairly intact grammar
- Small communities can have the same natural language change seen in larger communities

Language Vitality

- In a multilingual community, languages are associated with different levels of vitality
- Languages with more vitality are used more, and are associated with more prestige
- Choosing which language to speak in which setting is a socially significant choice
- As the name implies, language revitalization is increasing the vitality of a language, and encouraging speakers to choose the language in social settings
- Invisible languages have low vitality and are usually absent in things like formal and legal settings, written records, etc.
- Reduces exposure of the language, an issue with many sign languages
- The term vitality emerges constantly in discussions of language policy
- Measures not just the social strength but the institutional strength of a language
- How often is it spoken and how well is it supported?
- Vitality is different from prestige, which is based only on the social perception of a language, while vitality also includes transmission, robustness etc.
- Prestige can be part of determining vitality but does not define it
- Number of speakers, transmission, attitudes towards own language, documentation, government policies, availability of materials, shifts in language use are all factors that determine vitality
- Vitality exists inside and outside of the community, attitudes from both speakers and non-speakers have significant impact
- Ethnolinguistic vitality is another kind of vitality made of three pillars
- Status can include economic, educational status etc.
- Support can be formal or informal, from the government or from other communities
- Demography covers both the geographical distribution and the number and proportion of speakers combined with inflow and outflow
- Language revitalization can restore language lost to colonization and cultural genocide
- Evidence that language revitalization efforts can lower suicide rates in indigenous communities
- Endonyms are the word within a language that refers to that language (Español)
- Exonyms are the words used to refer to a language by other languages (Spanish)
- Can be multiple endonyms and exonyms referring to dialects
- Halkomelem refers to a group of three dialects
- Usually prefer endonyms, but exonyms are sometimes used if there is no endonym for all dialects

Language Teaching and Learning

- The first language you ever learn is called L1
- This is the initial stage of language acquisition and is very important
- If you are deprived of acquisition as a child, it's harder to learn any language later on
- Second language acquired is called L2
- Usually not present during infancy, age of exposure is after L1
- Acquisition is usually much slower
- If you were exposed to multiple languages from birth, those would all be considered L1s
- Related to stages in brain development, more suited for language acquisition at earlier ages
- In L1 you learn how to speak first and learn reading and writing later, but in L2 speaking and writing are usually taught simultaneously

Critical Period

- Language acquisition must occur before puberty
- After this point, plasticity of the brain decreases and acquisition slows
- Study in second language acquisition over age range from 3-39
- People who arrived earlier had much better acquisition
- Little difference from the 3-7 age groups and L1 speakers
- Linear relationship between age of exposure and performance later on
- After age 15, more variation in performance
- Arriving at 18 vs 35 made less difference than 7 vs 9
- However, taking language classes at an earlier age doesn't have any change on performance
- Length and amount of classes, motivation to learn and other factors also had little impact
- Exposure is much more important than instruction
- People who associated with America more positively also had better performance
- Self-consciousness rose with age of arrival which worsened performance
- Age of arrival was overall the most important factor
- For the later groups the hardest concepts were plurals and determiners, and the easiest ones were word order and present progressive
- Starting early is the most important thing for learning a language, but past puberty there's no rush to start earlier rather than later
- Formal education is less valuable than exposure to a language
- Mindset and identity is key, self consciousness and association with culture have significant effects

Linguistic Fieldwork

- Cooperation between a fieldworker and a speaker or speech community
- Goals can be documenting the language, collecting recordings, or documenting the language
- Should respect the needs and wishes of the community
- Use special terminology to respect speakers by not calling them participants or subjects
- Methodologies may vary between speakers
- Sessions are called elicitations because the researcher is seeking to elicit the aspects they want to investigate
- Lots of trial and error, can lead to unexpected results
- After a session, we transcribe it and check out our results
- Revise elicitation methods accordingly and prepare for the next session
- Many different kinds of data that can be collected
- Data can be volunteered or suggested, meaning was a sentence volunteered spontaneously or was there a suggestion from the researcher
- A sentence can be more grammatically constructed or constructed in terms of acceptability, is it grammatically sound and if not, is that acceptable in the context?
- Data can be positive or negative, meaning did the speaker indicate something that is possible in the language (positive), or did they indicate something is inappropriate in the language? (negative)
- The goal is to acquire positive data by eliciting the speaker into talking about what you want them to, but to not prompt them so much that your results are affected
- Negative data is fine, but it's less constructive and requires prepared sentences with potential ungrammaticality
- Similar to how we made grammatical judgements during the prescriptive vs descriptive unit
- Mark ungrammatical sentences with * and unacceptable sentences with #
- Ungrammatical means that it violates perceptions of grammar, and unacceptable means it doesn't make sense in the context
- An example would be someone saying "look at the apples" when there is only one apple in the room
- There's many common pitfalls with preparing these sentences
- Translating word by word from English often will mess up word order and pattern
- Sometimes instructions are too vague, or include linguistic jargon
- Sentences may lack context which should always be provided
- Some examples of prompts would be asking to translate something from English, creating storyboards or participating in games, having a conversation, or asking about stories or recipes in the speaker's language
