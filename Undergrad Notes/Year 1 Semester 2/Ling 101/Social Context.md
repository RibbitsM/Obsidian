- Nicaraguan Sign language is a creole
- Children who are deaf or hard of hearing sometimes create sign languages specific to their household
- However, when many of these children interacted a pidgin emerged
- New children started learning the signs from the older children and created a creole
- Shows how a creole can be created by many different languages instead of just 2
- Another difference between a pidgin and a creole is that a pidgin has no first language speakers
- Usually pidgins are created for a specific purpose like trade, is inflexible
- This process is called creolisation
- Pidgins come from two languages that aren't mutually intelligible
- A Koine is a mix of two mutually intelligible languages
- Koine Greek was mix of different Greek dialects that was used for business
- Dano-Norwegian mixes written Danish with spoken Norwegian
- Mainly used by richer Nords, and was the base for the current written Norwegian system
- Rural Norway sounds less Danish than urban Norway
- The Transatlantic accent was an accent created by actors and politicians that mixed British and Atlantic dialects
- Unique to actors and aristocrats, required expensive training to learn
- Speaking in Transatlantic English was a status symbol

What counts as a variety of language?

- We have regional dialects with continuums that are based on geography and politics
- However, Transatlantic English is not a dialect because it's not associated with geography but rather social context
- Languages are tied to social contexts
- There are times when it's okay and not okay to talk and you are expected to speak differently
- People are able to predict where a person is from by how they talk
- Everyone is tied to a speech community
- Can be regional, economic status, age, race, sexuality, gender etc.
- Sociolinguistics are the study of social meaning of individual variation
- Study differences in speech in different contexts
- The term "variety" can refer to either a language or dialect, neutral term
- Certain dialects can carry more prestige than others

Mutual Intelligibility

- Mutual intelligibility is usually symmetrical but not always
- Asymmetrical intelligibility is when speakers of one language can understand the other but not the other way around
- An example of this is Swedish and Danish
- Danish speakers have a pretty easy time understanding Swedish, but it's much harder for Swedish speakers to understand Danish
- Meanwhile Swedish and Norwegian speakers can understand eachother quite well

Language Attitudes

- It's important to not just consider one's role as a speaker but also a listener of language
- People make many assumptions based on how we perceive language
- Usually based on previous experience
- Language attitudes can be positive or negative
- Negative attitudes can be declining a job application because the applicant speaks with an accent
- Positive attitudes are when we hear a nice voice, like a radio host or customer service
- These attitudes are all responses to different language varieties
- Accents can be perceived negatively often
- A study found that comparing a US, French, and Colombian accent showed that the French accent was often ranked below the US accent
- This can lead to people seeking accent reduction or trying to change their accent
- The focus of linguistics is actually eliminating the biases people have about accents
- Social, political, and historical sources create accents with prestige
- Usually linked to accents associated with media or high status communities
- Prestige is community dependant, different accents are valued more in different contexts
- For example, the BC English accent is associated with good things, but speakers can't identify what is different about their accent
- Some amount of prestige associated with BC English
- Canadian accents were rated for correctness, pleasantness, and similarity
- BC English ranked highly in all categories by respondents from Alberta and Ontario
- Ontarians ranked their variety slightly higher than BC English but still highly
- Prestige can be overt or covert
- Overt prestige can be because of shibboleths or stereotypes
- A shibboleth is a pronunciation of a certain word or sound that is used to identify someone
- Important aspect is being able to identify where someone is from
- An example would be how people pronounce Toronto without the first O if they live there
- Covert prestige is when listeners are less aware, like with BC English
- Stereotypes are widely recognizable linguistic features and are usually mimicked in impersonations

Prescriptivism and Descriptivism

- Linguistics uses the scientific method, similar to how psychological research looks
- Collect data, use methodologies, make and test hypotheses, test them
- Results are only valid if methodology is sound
- Science is descriptive, and value neutral
- Descriptive approach to language means describing how people speak, not how they should speak
- Grammar is discussed in terms of how people actually speak
- Grammar is a set of rules and constraints that reflects how people form sentences and what sentences are acceptable
- With prescriptive grammar, the sentence "But I think it's raining today" is incorrect because you should not start sentences with a conjunction
- However, in normal conversation no one would question this sentence
- Common theme of studying grammar, there is a divide between what is allowed by the rules of grammar and what is allowed by society
- This also creates differences between what is correct in spoken vs written language
- Grammar also changes over time, like how "abso-fucking-lutely" is more generally accepted even though it's not grammatically correct
- But this still adheres to grammar rules, changing the phrase to "absolute-fucking-ly" sounds incorrect
- It's merely a modification of grammar, not ignoring it
- Main goals of lingustics are achieving empirical and explanatory adequacy
- Empirical adequacy is describing how people speak
- Explanatory adequacy is explaining why people use specific constructions
