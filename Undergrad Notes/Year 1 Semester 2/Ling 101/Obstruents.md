- While vowels are more sonorous than consonants, it's not a binary system
- The actual spectrum goes from vowels, to sonorant consonants, to obstruent consonants
- Plosives, fricatives, and affricates are examples of especially sonorous sounds
- Plosives are also called stops, and are a closure and release of air
- An example would be /p/which is a bilabial stop, while /t/ is an alveolar stop
- Stops are on the top row of the IPA chart
- These rules only apply to oral stops, nasal stops are a separate concept
- Fricatives can be articulated at any place in the mouth
- Many of the possible fricatives are not in English, mostly because we do not use uvular or pharyngeal articulation
- Wherever you restrict airflow, you will create a fricative
- Affricates are combinations of features of a fricative and a plosive
- Usually written with a tie bar above them to distinguish them
- These are dynamic like diphthongs, and have multiple targets, usually 2
- For example a ts with a tie bar over it would be an affricate
- Within obstruents, fricatives are more sonorous and plosives are less sonorous
- This further subdivides the sonary hierarchy

Sonority Hierarchy

- Mainly focus on nasal stops in 101
- Examples in English would be /m/ and /n/
- /m/ is bilabial and /n/ is alveolar
- They appear in a separate section under plosives but are also plosives
- To find a phoneme inventory, multiply place by manner, and then by voice to find the number of phonemes
- Then find phonemes that fit into this description
- Important parts of IPA chart are all consonants, all vowels, ejectives and some others
- Sonorant consonants are more sonorous than obstruents, which are the plosives, fricatives, and affricates
- Approximants are sounds that are smoother than obstruents but are oral, not like the nasal stops
- These are sounds like /r/ and /l/
- Created by a constriction of airflow, but not as much as fricatives or stops
- Treated as consonants even through they have qualities of vowels
- Two kinds: glides and liquids
- Glides are the most sonorant form of consonant, and include /j/ and /w/
- Liquids are slightly more closed and include /l/
- Some of the hardest sounds for kids to learn
- Most to least sonorant goes glides, liquids, nasals, fricatives, and plosives
- Don't need to distinguish between glides and liquids, just know approximants

Secondary Articulations

- Descriptions of the sound systems of languages are used to compare and contrast languages and phoneme inventories
- Some of these include new symbols called diacritics
- The devoiced diacritic appears as a little circle under a nasal or voiced sound and makes it voiceless
- The ejective diacritic is a ' that appears next to a phoneme to signify that it is spoken with an ejective airflow, not a pulmonic airflow
- A new one is the glottalized diacritic which is not on the midterm and signifies an alternate form of voice where the glottal folds are used
- It appears as a small question mark after a consonant
- The aspiration diacritic is not on the midterm and are produced with a stringer voiceless airflow
- Appears as a small h and is written as aspirated in places of voiced/voiceless
- The last new one is the labialization diacritic which means a sound is pronounced with additional rounding and is marked with a little w
- Doesn't replace anything and adds the phrase "labialized" to the beginning of the description
- Palatalization diacritics create additional palatal sound in a consonant and appears as a small j next to the phoneme, and are added like labialization
- Another symbol looks kinda like : and signifies a longer vowel
- So describe this we just add "long" to the description

Typology

- Can classify languages by phoneme inventory size
- For example, Southern Michigan English has 11 monopthongs (vowels), while Central Arrernte has only 4 monopthongs
- Can also examine the number and kind of stops, some use pulmonic stops only vs ejective/nasal stops
- Typological comparisons are possibly with any languages because you are examining universal features
- The WALS is an important tool because it allows you to see what languages have or don't have a certain phoneme
- Can also see what phonemes are rarer vs more common by examining which appear in less or more languages
- For example, pulmonic airflow is much more common than non-pulmonic
- Velars and nasals are common, but a velar nasal sound is rare
- Some more rare sounds are pharyngeal sounds, interdentals, and clicks
