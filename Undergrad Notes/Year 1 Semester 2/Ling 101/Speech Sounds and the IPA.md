- Speech sounds are discussed in terms of allophones and phonemes
- The phonemes are the discrete units, allophones are what the phoneme actually sounds like
- The phoneme is the target sound while allophone is the specific sound
- Phonemes also have meaning on two levels
- Broad vs narrow transcription
- Phonemes are discrete and distinctive
- For example, the phonemes for "bagel" are always the same, but the allophones vary based on dialect
- Phonemes are written in the international phonetic alphabet (IPA)
- We use this instead of English because english letters can't represent all languages
- Plus, english letters don't even represent English phonetically
- An example would be that you can pronounce ghoti and fish the same way in English pronounciation
- Take the gh from enough, o from women, and ti from nation
- IPA is the solution to this issue of weird spelling
- Every possible sound has a symbol equivalent
- Spelling is also changed between language families
- The same sound that's represented by a ? in one Salish language is a 7 in another
- On the chart, velar vs uvular represents place
- Right letter is unvoiced, left letter is voiced
- An idiolect is how a specific person speaks, more narrow than a dialect

Phonemes

- So how do we describe and discuss these phonemes?
- Name the phoneme, describe the voice, manner, and place, and then state the language it is from
- You can also talk about the airflow in addition to these three
- Also remember to say what language family the language is from
- The basic template goes airflow, voice, place, manner but airflow is optional if not relevant
- Symbols on the right is voiced, left is voiceless
- Top column shows place
- Manner is on the side
- Phonemes can be voiced in a range of places depending on language
- This chart only shows pulmonic consonants
- There is a variety of non-pulmonic consonants expressed with ' (for ejectives) and other symbols
- Don't have to specify that it's non pulmonic
- /t'/ would be an ejective voiceless alveolar stop, while /t/ is a pulmonic voiceless alveolar stop
- The -s ending in English can appear as a [s] or a [z]
- [s] follows a voiceless consonant and [z] follows a voiced consonant
- But which one of these is the norm and which is the variation?
- One possibility is that the underlying phoneme is /z/ and changes to become devoiced when next to voiceless consonants, becoming [s]

Identifying Phonemes

- Two strategies for identifying phonemes are looking for minimal pairs and finding complementary distribution
- This means finding a pair of words distinguished by a single sound, proving the sounds belong to different phonemes, because phonemes are contrastive
- Big and bag is a minimal pair because there is a difference in vowel height that creates a different meaning
- [p] and [ph] are not a contrasting pair because they don't change the meaning of the word pat
- However, this is the case for t, p, and k
- There is no phoneme /ph/, that is an allophone
- The phoneme /p/ creates both the phoneme [p] and it's aspirational version
- Allophones all 'belong' to a certain phoneme, and any phoneme can have many allophones
- Remember, allophones are the sound you hear, and the phoneme is it's abstract representation
- Phonemes are not constant across languages
- The aspirated p is the same phoneme as p in English, but they are discrete sounds in Hindi
- You can find minimal pairs for /ph/ and /p/ in Hindi, but not English
- Another distinction between sounds is nasal or oral sounds
- This refers to where the air exits, in /p/ and /b/ it exits through the mouth, but exits through the nose in /m/ and /n/
- Vowels are produced with much less airflow construction
- Because of this they can be lengthened and are much stronger
- Vowels are described with height, backness, and lip position
- This refers to the position of the tongue and lips

Writing Speech Sounds

- With the English ending -s the underlying phoneme is /z/ and can appear as [z] or [s] depending of if the preceding allophone is voiced or not
- However, this was based on a heavily simplified data set
- If we expand the dataset there is a third version of the ending, [-iz]
- This appears in fixes and sixes
- The new question that emerges with this is what are the different forms of the morphemes?
- In addition, what are the underlying forms of these morphemes?
- Words are made out of morphemes which are made out of phonemes
- Part of the idea of duality of patterning, where there are two levels that are meaningful and distinctive
- Morphemes are meaningful, and phonemes are distinctive and appear as allophones
- Three different endings are -s, -z, and -iz
- How many allomorphs is this? It could be 1, 2, or 3

Vowels

- The IPA chart for vowels divides them into three heights
- It also divides tongue position into front, central, and back
- The vowel equivalent of voiced or unvoiced is lip position, meaning rounded or unrounded
- Phonemes on the right are rounded, and the left side is unrounded
- Many of these rounded vowels are not in English and sound unfamiliar
- The /u/ in boot is an example of an English rounded vowel
- All of these vowels are monophthongs meaning the tongue doesn't move
- Vowels with two targets are called diphthongs, an example would be the /oa/ in boas
- Triphthongs are also possible, an example would be in the middle of liar
