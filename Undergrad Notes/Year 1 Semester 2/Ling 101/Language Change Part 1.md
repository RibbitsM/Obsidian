Languages Over Time

- Historical linguistics is the study of language change and evolution, and exists as a sub-field of linguistics
- Study is based on current languages and examining how they are related in order to form hypotheses about how they came to be
- Little physical evidence of languages that existed thousands of years ago
- Two main approaches, comparative and diachronic
- Diachronic is comparing the same language at different points in time
- The comparative method is comparing modern languages to try and understand if they have common roots by seeing what features they share or lack
- "reconstructing" an earlier form of languages via the comparative method
- The romance branch of the Indo-European tree is split into Portuguese, Spanish, French, and Italian
- Italian split off first, French second, and Spanish and Portuguese are the most closely related
- But all of these were once one language called "Proto-Romance"
- A daughter language is the direct descendant of a mother language
- For example, Ibero-Romance is the mother language of Spanish and Western Ibero-Romance
- Therefore these two languages are sister languages to eachother, and the daughter languages of Ibero-Romance
- Can also consider Portuguese a sister language of Spanish under the Ibero-Romance branch
- Basically language trees are traced through common ancestors

Cognates

- Cognates are words that appear in multiple languages, or have similar forms in other languages
- Cognate sets are sets of these words that emerged from the same origin or are similar across many languages
- By looking at cognate sets comparatively we can try and reconstruct what a word would have looked like in the original language based on the cognates
- A reflex is a sound in a daughter language that is the same as a sound in the parent language
- When we have a set of these reflexes, it is called a correspondence set
- Finding the reflexes in a cognate set is key to the contrastive method
- For the example, the reflexes are the velar k in italian, spanish, and portuguese and the fricative ch in french
- Therefore, the correspondence set would be Spanish: k Italian: k Portuguese: k French: ch
- Which is more likely? Which is more frequent?
- K is more frequent, so we would use /k/ when reconstructing Proto-Romance
- Can also do this for the middle of the words, r is present in the middle of all the words, so it would also be reconstructed in Proto-Romance
- Italian: p Spanish and Portuguese: b French: v
- Based on the frequency approach, we would reconstruct b

Reconstruction and Proto-Sounds

- When reconstructing a phoneme inventory, mark the sounds with * to signify that they are theoretical only
- Some examples of evidence used for historical linguistics are basic vocabulary, glottochronology, and comparison by inspection
- Basic vocabulary is looking at words that are common in many languages, and tend not to be affected by language change
- Glottochronology also focuses on these core words but uses a statistical approach
- Inspection is a more flimsy method where you try to associate languages by looking at similarities between them
- This is a problem because almost any two languages will be similar in some way
- While these methods can be useful, they are based mostly on sound correspondence, which is affected by many factors like language contact and social context
- For example, a loanword created from interaction between two separate languages can lead someone to believe that two languages are related when they aren't
- This is especially challenging when examining a multilingual population
- However, this can be avoided by making sure that you also examine the historical context of a language
- In addition, languages tend to change to favour sounds that are easier to pronounce or perceive, creating false similarities between unrelated languages
- Some sounds are just more common than others, and sharing a common sound means very little
- Also geographical proximity can create similar sounds and create false similarities when languages are geographically close but unrelated
- Reconstructing Proto-World is a futile effort, there is simply not enough information to do so
- While English shares many characteristics with Scandinavian languages, we have enough historical evidence to know that it is a Germanic language
- But without this information, it would be much harder to know
- Also theories that environment can affect language, like how high altitude areas tend to have languages with more ejectives, but these are also explained through language contact
