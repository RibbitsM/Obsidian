- Globalisation is the contact of many people of different backgrounds worldwide
- English has been effectively globalised and is a lingua franca
- English has also become more and more homogeneous
- Called dialect levelling, where features of certain dialects are lost over time
- This is usually caused by mobility and increased global contact
- Regional dialects are less important
- Being multilingual means speaking more than one language
- Multilingual communities also emerge
- Bilingual means speaking two languages, but multilingual is a better blanket term
- In 2016 about 19% of Canadian households spoke more than one language at home
- In the case of multilingual communities, one language may be used more or less often
- For example, in Norway most people can speak both Norwegian and English fluently, but generally use Norwegian
- Languages are associated with different levels of vitality, meaning how much it is used
- In the Norway example English is usually used in institutions while Norwegian is used in social contexts

Language Policies

- Policies can be implied or overt
- Implicit policies are usually social pressures to speak a certain language in a particular setting
- Overt policies can be like national languages or official languages
- National language is a language variety associated with a nation, while an official language is the language for official/government business
- An example of the complications of language policy would be South Africa
- After the end of apartheid there was a push for equality under the South African Constitution
- There are now 11 official languages of South Africa, all with equal status
- This is an example of a linguistic plurality, one of the first in the world
- A problem is ambiguity with education
- Schools get to decide what language to teach in, which may cut off education for many people who can't commute to the school of their preferred language
- In Vanuatu, there are an estimated 114 languages spoken
- However, the only official languages are Bislama, English, and French
- Education is done in English and French

Code Switching

- Code switching is how speakers of more than one language switch between languages
- Can have a number of motivations to reflect the situation, or the person you are talking to
- Usually pretty unique to the individual and has less formal rules
- Represents significant linguistic skill
- An example would be a Ukrainian tv show where one host switched between Ukrainian and Russian depending on the guest they were interviewing
- Arabic speakers sometimes change their dialect depending on what topic they were discussing
- Code mixing is the act of integrating words from other languages into a sentence
- Some see code mixing as negative, but signifies a marker of fluency in multiple languages
- Very common in multilingual environments, part of teaching a language
- An example of a community that uses a lot of code mixing is Cantonese-English bilinguals in Toronto
- Associated with a specific social group
