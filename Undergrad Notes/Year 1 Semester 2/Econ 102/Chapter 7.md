GDP and Circular Flow of Income

- This use to not be considered important before economies were in direct competition
- Came from the idea that the economy was self regulating
- Over the 20th century economists became aware of recessions and how they diverged from classical assumptions
- Discovered that investment will not always equal savings
- Russian economist invented national income accounting
- Great depression was a turning point for the measurement of GDP
- Congress asked economists things like what level of economic activity is there currently and how much it had dropped
- Economists realized they had no way to measure these kind of things in order to start addressing the idea
- As an answer, GDP and national income accounting was created
- Allowed economists to study standard of living over time and to compare standards of living

How to Measure GDP

- Goal is to measure the size of economic activity within an economy
- Also measure how close to maximum potential the economy is operating
- Measure the market value of all final G & S produced within the economy in a specific time period
- Important terms to note are market value, final G & S, economy, and period of time
- Need to add market value because markets sell so many different goods it is hard to determine quantities
- Address the problem by measuring in dollar value of goods sold instead of measuring in quantity
- Use the market price of the good, found in microeconomics
- In a market at equilibrium, the market value of a good sold equals the benefit bestowed on the consumers
- Final goods and services means eliminating intermediate goods and services or any inputs used to make these goods
- The problem is that it is difficult to determine what is and isn't a final good
- Usually purchases made by a household are final purchases
- If a household buys bread from Costco, it is a final good, but if a household buys bread to use for their sandwich shop, it is no longer a final good
- Inside an economy is what separates GDP from GNP
- GDP includes anything within the economy, but GNP includes goods coming from outside the economy
- Say BMW opens a plant in Canada, a German company making profit in Canada
- This would count for Canadian GDP, but not German GDP
- However, it would qualify for German GNP because it is German owned operating in the Canadian economy
- Looking over a given time period means GDP is usually reported yearly or quarterly
- Most common is a quarterly report, and is used to extrapolate the next three quarters
- This also marks the difference between a stock and flow variable
- Imagine a running faucet where water falls into a bathtub
- After shutting off the faucet the water stays in the bathtub and we measure it, giving a definite quantity
- But at any given point when the faucet is running there would be different values
- This is a stock variable, like factors of production or population
- At any given point these values are fixed
- The flow variable is the running water from the faucet
- To measure it we need a time period, say measuring how much water comes out over one minute
- This lets us determine a flow rate
- GDP is an example of a flow variable because it is measured over time
- Preferably measured monthly but is more cost effective to measure quarterly

Different Ways to Measure

- Three approaches, value added, income approach, and total expenditure
- Value added approach is adding the values of all final goods and services produced in the economy, eliminating intermediate goods and services
- Only info needed is values gathered from each individual firm, this is how the government measures GDP
- From the difference of costs and revenues of firms we determine value
- Income approach is by measuring the income of households
- Hypothetically if you add up the income of all households this will equal to wages, rent, interest and profits
- Total expenditure approach is adding up all money spent in an economy
- The idea is that as long as foreign goods are omitted, total expenditure will be equal to GDP
- Most institutions use a combination of all three of these methods
- Usually no substantial difference between these three methods
- If all three methods produce similar results, then you know you collected your data appropriately
- This is because of the circular flow diagram

Circular Flow Diagram

- Comes in different levels of complexity from least realistic to most realistic
- Assume the economy of a country is closed, and there is no government
- Our circular flow diagram would have two sectors, the household and the firm
- We also need the market, we have the factor market and the goods market
- Factor market is where factors of production are sold from the households to the firms (labour)
- The goods market is where households buy products from the firms  
- The households do not just offer labour but can also offer land by renting it to firms or offering entrepreneurship
- Firms make money by selling goods that are produced via factors of production that they pay the households for
- Households spend the money they make from the firms on goods from those firms
- So by calculating how much spending is going on here, we would find the GDP
- Now if the government is present, it adds another factor to the equation
- This creates a leakage where there is money unaccounted for by calculating spending alone
- Taxes paid by households are not included, GDP will fall short
- In addition to household consumption, you must also calculate government expenditure
- Now, let's assume the households save
- The amount of money saved creates another leakage in the equation
- This money is stored in the financial market
- Money from the financial market is used for investment in the economy in firms, and the firms spend these funds on goods and services
- Adding household expenditure, govt expenditure, and firm expenditure we can get a clear picture of total expenditure
- The final sector to be added is the rest of the world
- Now the country is an open economy and can run a trade surplus or deficit
- In reality, many goods and services in the goods market come from outside of the domestic economy
- Creates a large leakage if not accounted for
- Local firms also make profit in foreign economies
- Could solve this by dividing household and firm expenditures into domestic and foreign spending
- Much easier is calculating the net market value of all goods imported into the economy and all goods exported
- Add this to all other spending and calculate
- This diagram is not entirely realistic and serves for more of a conceptual framework
- Actual GDP calculation has further complications
- Reason for the three different approaches

Value Added Approach

- What are the advantages of this approach?
- Say you buy a notebook that is produced like this
- Wood chips > Paper rolls > Office supply firm > Staples
- Values are $2.00 > $5.00 > $10.00 > $15.00
- In reality we don't have this kind of information
- Don't include value of first three, only measure the final value which is $15 here
- In this situation the only final good is the notebook
- Statistics Canada calculates the value of the final good by calculating all of intermediaries
- 2 + (5-2) + (10-5) + (15-10) = $15
- Adds together the amount of value added to the good at each stage
- Value added is the payments to the firm's factors of production
- In a competitive market, a firm pays the owners of the factors of production equal to how much value those factors have provided
- If this is true, GDP calculated this way will be the same as GDP calculated through expenditure
- This is not true in reality for several reasons

Expenditure Approach

- Measures GDP as expenditure on domestically produced goods and services
- Affected by four variables, consumption, investment and government expenditure, and net export
- Government transfer payments are not included in government expenditure
- Net export is calculated by subtracting total value of imports from total value of exports
- Household expenditure is affected by purchase of real estate since it is exchange of existing value
- If a new housing unit is purchased, the value of the unit itself is not included if it was not built in the current year
- Just one example of a complication in the expenditure approach
- Market transaction is calculated when rent is paid, but not when the owner lives there
- Another consumption activity that is not calculated is foods consumed by farms, based on their own production
- If a farmer eats the own corn he produced, it was produced in the economy and consumed but never went onto the market
- Calculated by finding the market value of food consumed and how much was consumed
- Another leakage is in investment expenditure
- Newly constructed housing units bought by households are included in investment expenditure
- In addition, goods and services purchased by firms are included
- A major component is inventory adjustment
- If there is a surge in demand, you can sell from your inventory
- Adjustments in output inventory are added to investment expenditure
- Not referring to the value of the output to avoid double counting
- Add if inventory increased, subtract if inventory decreased
- Output from the inventory would not normally be calculated if we do not do this
- Say a car company has 10 cars in it's inventory and finishes the year with 10 more unsold cars
- This is an increase in inventory and should be added to investment expenditure
- If this is unaccounted for, it creates an underestimation of economic activity
- Tough to find market value of government goods because there are no markets for them
- For example, tough to quantify the value of a firefighting service
- Calculated by adding together government spending on wages/equipment for firefighters
- Transfers from the government to households are not included because this money is accounted for in household expenditure
- Net export is important because it excludes the value of goods from foreign economies and includes value of domestic goods consumed in foreign countries
- This is also called net trade outflow
- Calculated almost exactly like value added approach by adding consumer plus investment plus government plus trade

Income Approach

- GDP measured by adding up all of the income earned by all households in the economy
- This includes wages, interest on savings, rent, and dividends for shareholders
- Circular flow is more useful for expenditure than income approach
- Also include non factor payments which is indirect tax and capital depreciation
- The rest of the income in considered factor payments because they are based on the factors of production provided by households
- Called gross factor payments because we do not calculate the income tax associated with rent or wages
- Non factor payments are a concept unique to the income method
- Capital depreciation is associated with the productive process and is also called the capital consumption allotment as it represents money that firms set aside to maintain their factor of production
- This is money that doesn't go from factory owners to firms and as such would not be included otherwise
- The indirect tax is the cost added to a good by the government like GST or PST and is not included in factor payments
- This accounts for all other kinds of government tax that aren't accounted for by using gross factor payments
- Indirect tax is net tax, and we subtract any government subsidies given back to the economy
- The way Canada calculates GDP through income approach comes in 4 categories
- Compensation of employees, gross operating surplus, gross mixed income, and taxes minus subsidy
- Compensation of employees is the most important component here and represents gross wage and salaries of workers
- Also includes social contribution by employers which is things like money contributed to CPP, health insurance premium, and employment insurance premium
- Second is gross operating surplus which includes returns to capital and the capital consumption allowance
- Third is gross mixed income which includes income of small businesses where returns to labour and capital are difficult to separate
- The final category is net taxes which is government taxes like sales tax and excise tax minus government subsidies
- Use all three because they keep eachother in check to increase accuracy
- Equal it by subtracting half of the difference and adding to the other approach
- Usually accurate to eachother within 500 million or less than 1% of total GDP

Real GDP vs Nominal GDP

- Measuring GDP at current price is called nominal GDP
- Over time nominal GDP increases even if quantity didn't change because of inflation
- Real GDP is used to overcome this downside
- Can be misleading because if inflation is high enough GDP can grow even if quantity produced drops
- Calculating by setting a base year, can be any year without hyperinflation or any other irregularities
- Total value of final goods and services calculated using prices of the base year
- Real always refers to quantity, not price
- Real GDP is important for comparisons over time and between two countries
- Can also use GDP to calculate growth rate
- Calculated with the real GDP of final year minus real GDP of base year divided by real GDP of base year times 100
- Real GDP ignores changes in price and measures changes in quantity
- Calculating growth rate depends heavily on the base year
- This is because you use future prices to determine real GDP
- When 2007 is the base year, growth rate is 20.6%
- But if 2017 is the base year, growth rate is 22.8%
- Which year you choose depends on politics and context
- Statistics Canada calculates chain dollar real GDP to remedy this problem
- Need consecutive years for this method
- Step 1 is to value production of adjacent years at prices of both years
- Say in 2018 GDP was $7000 and 2019 GDP was $7450 if 2018 was base year
- Then, in 2018 GDP was $7000 and 2019 GDP was $7500 if 2019 was base year
- Step 2 is treat each as the base year and find the average of the two growth rates
- Step 3 is to choose an arbitrary base year and calculate it's nominal GDP
- Say we choose 2018, it would be $7000
- Step 4 is calculate real GDP for all other years based on average growth rate from step 2
- Point of real GDP is to analyze standard of living
- Economic activities generally grow with population
- As long as population/immigration grows, the economy grows
- Therefore an increase in real GDP still doesn't truly represent better standard of living
- This is why we calculate real GDP per capita
- If real GDP grows faster than the population, it is a true increase in standard of living
- Real GDP per capita is simply real GDP divided by population
- Not always accurate, but generally a good indicator

GDP and Life Satisfaction

- There are some correlations with life satisfaction and GDP per capita
- Life satisfaction is measured by random sample survey
- Generally richer countries have higher life satisfaction
- As GDP rises, each marginal increase in GDP increases satisfaction less and less
- There are multiple outliers where middle income nations have higher satisfaction than rich nations
- For example, Finland has much more life satisfaction than the US but the US has a much higher GDP per capita

Aggregate Price Level

- This is calculated to determine whether income has kept up with cost of living or not
- Also used to determine real interest rate by calculating nominal interest rate with inflation
- The central bank has differing power in every country, but is always responsible for calculating inflation
- In Canada the inflation rate is kept between 1 and 2%
- To stay healthy there must be moderate inflation in an economy
- In the 70s, the US decided to adopt contractionary monetary policy focused on reducing monetary supply and increase interest rate
- This curbed the US's rampant inflation problem
- Jimmy Carter actually lost the election due to this decision
- Called Paul Walker Disinflation named after the policymaker who pioneered it
- Different from deflation, which is falling price levels rather than curbing inflation
- CPI is the consumer price index, which is a measure of cost of living
- By having a fixed bundle of goods with fixed quantities, we can calculate CPI by multiplying by price
- Called the market basket and can be adjusted to represent different households
- Hardest part is determining the quantities of each good
- Market basket is consistent over time, just need prices to determine inflation over time
- Inflation is published every month in Canada
- Determined through monthly price surveys
- Usually represents the average urban household
- Every four years a consumer survey is held to determine the market basket, taking into account sales taxes and subsidies
- The price index is the cost of market basket in a given year divided by the cost of market basket in the base year and then multiply by 100
- It is also normalized, meaning the value of the price index in the base year is always 100
- To calculate inflation we use consecutive years, not base years
- Calculate inflation by comparing the difference in CPI between adjacent years
- Inflation is ALWAYS measured in consecutive years
- Equation is price index year 2 minus price index year 1 divided by price index year one times 100
- The current CPI in Canada is made mostly of housing, food, and transportation with medical care also taking a fairly large slice
- Recently CPI has been increasing slower and slower each year
- When depicting CPI on a graph we use the log because the base is increasing over time
- This flattening of CPI shows the effectiveness of central bank policy
- Other similar price measures are PPI (producer price index), and GDP deflator
- PPI measures changes in costs of inputs purchased by producers, which usually predicts an change in CPI
- GDP deflator represents the ratio of nominal GDP to real GDP and also measures inflation
- PPI has a much different basket of goods including aluminum, steel, energy etc.
- The difference between calculating inflation through CPI vs GDP is that GDP excludes imported goods
- These three measures move together but in different degrees
- GDP deflator is usually less exaggerated and PPI is more exaggerated
- CPI has a direct effect on Canadian citizens as it determines how much the government allocates to CPP and OAS plans year to year
