Savings and Investment Identities

- Derived from income and expenditure approach to calculating GDP
- The amount people in an economy are able to invest depends on how much they save
- This is true for a closed economy, but more complicated in an open economy
- The trade deficit/surplus depends on which is larger
- Assuming closed economy, with no government
- Using the expenditure approach, add together consumer spending and investment
- Investment in economics is different from investment as we usually know it
- Investment in this context refers to buying physical capital, not buying financial assets
- Using the income approach, income goes only to household
- Also no financial market
- If households get income, it's either spend on goods and services or saved
- If the government is present, add G to expenditure calculation
- As for income, part of it will now go to the government
- The government acts as a household and has income and spending
- Income is taxes, and spending is transfer payments and spending on goods/services
- T-(TR + G) = government budget/public savings
- If this number is positive, there is a budget surplus, and if not it's a deficit
- For national savings, add private savings to the equation
- Private savings is found by subtracting taxes and consumption spending from GDP, and adding transfer payments GDP-(C+TR-T)
- Basically all money left over after household spending plus govt intervention
- National savings is determined by adding the sum of all private savings to the government budget
- In expenditure approach, Y minus C minus G signifies national savings while I represents investment
- All money included in GDP can be considered either savings or investment
- Therefore since GDP minus G and C is national savings, national savings is equal to I in a closed economy
- If national savings is equal to investment expenditure, then savings equals investment
- This is the basic fact of savings and investment identities

S and I in an Open Economy

- Foreign trading is mainly in the goods market and the asset market
- Net foreign inflow or NFI is the difference between funds flowing outside the economy and funds coming it
- If NFI is negative, there are more imports than exports (trade deficit), if it is positive then there are more exports
- The portion of investment funded from within the economy is represented as Id Dom
- Id For is the portion of investment outside the economy funded from inside the economy
- If Dom is investment in the economy funded from outside the economy
- National savings is found by adding I and NFI
- The second letter represents the source of the money and the third word represents where it was spent
- The full equation for NFI is Id for minus If dom
- Outflow minus inflow
- Because of current account, calculate NFI by subtracting imports from exports
- If an economy has a deficit in trades (meaning net foreign investment is <0), then it must have a surplus in assets meaning it has surplus foreign currency
- And vice versa if an economy has a trade surplus, it has a deficit of foreign currency
- If a countries investment does not match it's savings, the NFI will adjust investment to match savings
- This is because investment is fuelled by savings, so if there isn't enough savings in a country to finance it's investment spending, money must be borrowed from elsewhere

Loanable Funds Market

- The loanable funds market depends on cooperation between borrowers and lenders of money
- Made up of the savers, who lend their money to investors via the market, and receive return from the investors efforts
- The market is not a literal market, but a hypothetical model to explain how supply and demand is generated by borrowers and lenders
- The nominal interest rate is determined by the loanable funds market
- Similar model to determining supply and demand of a good in micro, but the good is money
- There are two reasons why the loanable funds market is theoretical only
- In any given financial system there are many different ways that funds are exchanged like stocks, treasury bonds, etc.
- All of these different forms of exchange have their own markets
- Even within bonds, there are private and government bonds which have separate markets with different nominal interest rates
- However, for the hypothetical model we ignore these other markets and just focus on the equilibrium interest rate for all loaned funds
- Even though there are different interest rates for these assets, they cannot truly be individually determined because of opportunity cost
- All interest rates are based on the OC of alternative investments
- We look at nominal interest rate, not real interest rate for the loanable funds market
- It doesn't matter much, it just makes things easier
- In the loanable funds market there are two factors, demand and supply
                           

Demand

- When the nominal interest rate is high, there is lower demand and a low interest rate creates higher demand
- This means there is a negative relationship between interest rate and demand
- The interest rate earned on savings is the opportunity cost of borrowing since you have to put up some of your own money in order to borrow usually
- Therefore the higher the interest rate in the economy, the less you will want to borrow because the OC is higher
- To determine the OC of borrowing, calculate the money you would have made by saving that money
- Say you want to borrow/invest 100 dollars, and the interest rate is 5%
- After a year, that 100 dollars would be 105
- If this project has a return of 105 dollars, 100 dollars is the max amount worth borrowing
- If the interest rate was 10%, the max amount you should borrow is 95.45
- The equation is return divided by 1 plus the interest rate to the power of time
- From this example we can see how as the interest rate rises, you want to borrow less

Supply

- There is a positive relationship with interest rate and supply, so as interest rate rises, more people want to save
- Therefore, more funds become available in the economy
- Saving is an intertemporal choice meaning it is dependant on future events
- By reducing your consumption in the short run, you will ensure consumption in the long term
- When people see that they can earn more by saving (high interest rate) they will save more
- Looking at a closed economy, the intersection of supply and demand will be at the ideal interest rate for the economy
- If the interest rate is too high, there will be excess supply of loanable funds meaning noone is willing to borrow it
- This is why interest rates are usually lowered to discourage saving during a recession
- If the interest rate is below equilibrium there is excess demand, meaning more people want funds than there is funds to lend

Changes

- Changes in perceived profitability can affect levels of demand for funds
- Out of all the factors in the GDP expenditure approach, investment expenditure is the most volatile because it is based on people's personal outlook
- This is implemented in the graph via shift of the demand curve
- However, changes in quantity demanded or anything related to interest rate moves along the curve
- Say there is economic trouble approaching, demand would shift to the left
- Another factor is government policy, which can strongly affect investment spending
- Right now, governments are trying to promote investment in renewable energy
- This would create a rightward shift in demand for lending
- Government budget also affects demand for loanable funds
- The formula is (T - TR) - G < 0
- When the budget deficit increases, the government needs to borrow more money from the market, and demand shifts to the right
- Disposable income of households, wealth effect, and expected change in disposable income are also factors in shifts
- When households Have paid taxes, bills etc, the money left over is called disposable income
- The proportion of disposable income spent/saved for any given class is fairly fixed
- So in macro analysis, we can predict the change in savings/investment when disposable income increases or decreases
- A reason for a widespread increase in disposable income could be economic growth
- A predicted change can also affect consumer behavior, which leads to changes in savings and investment
- Price of assets changing in the economy can create a wealth effect
- Say you own apple stock, and apple stock is currently going up
- This leads to stockholders feeling wealthier, and increasing consumption vs saving
- All else hold constant, an increase in budget surplus would increase available loanable funds, causing a rightwards shift in supply
- In an open economy, funds can also flow in from foreign markets and also flow out
- This flow is also assumed to be frictionless
- For simplicity, we will assume the world only has two economies to stabilize the nominal interest rate
- Only two markets, domestic and foreign, each with their own interest rates
- If the foreign interest rate is higher then the domestic rate capital will flow out, and if the foreign rate is lower then capital will in
- However, this is also dependent on differences in value between domestic and foreign currency but we assume this isn't a factor
- This flow of capita equalizes interest rates, as capital flowing into a country with high interest rates will decrease the interest rate as the amount of money in the economy increases (supply curve shifts to the right)

Inflation

- All graphs depicting nominal interest rate are for a given expected inflation rate
- Affects both supply and demand and raises the nominal interest rate
- If expected inflation increases by a certain percentage, nominal interest rate increases by the same amount
- This is because of the fisher equation, where real inflation equals interest rate minus expected inflation
- Real interest rate does not change in response to expected inflation, nominal interest rate does
- Say real interest is 2% and expected inflation is 3%
- Therefore nominal interest rate will be 5%
- If expected inflation is 5%, nominal rate is 7%
- Real interest rate stays constant
- Over time, nominal interest rates increase due to inflation
- An increase in nominal interest rate shifts both supply and demand up, keeping the quantity the same but increasing the equilibrium rate
- Looking at graphs, the interest rate of a country tends to follow the inflation rate

Financial System

- Several duties within the economy
- Main ones are reducing transaction costs, reducing risks on investment, and providing liquidity for funds
- When investing on a large scale, firms will need access to money from thousands of households in order to access their savings
- Instead of talking with every individual household, the financial system enables this transaction to happen quickly and easily
- Can get money by taking out loans or issuing bonds
- Reduces risk by spreading it out between firm and shareholders
- Say a firm makes a risky investment, it only puts up a certain amount of it's own money, and raises the rest by selling stocks
- The risk is divided between the firm and the shareholders, reducing risk
- Liquidity is also important because loans can give access to large amounts of money on short notice, say if you need to pay employees but don't have the money yet
- Several different types of assets
- Stocks, bonds, loans, and loan backed securities
- Consumers can also purchase physical assets like buildings, cars etc. that they can make profit on by renting
- Putting money into assets is important as they increase in value with inflation better than funds in savings accounts
- Stocks are issued by firms in order to get access to more money, give owners a share in the firm
- Liability for the firm, and an asset to the buyer
- Meanwhile, a loan is an asset to the bank, and a liability to you
- Think of stocks and bonds as a firm getting a loan from the public, with some extra nuance
- Bonds are essentially IOUs, they don't give ownership of the company but guarantee a certain return on the investment
- The face value of a bond is the price you pay when buying the bond
- All bonds have maturity periods, which is a deadline for when the bond will be payed back
- Firms often offer coupons based on the interest rate on the bond
- The value of the coupon or yield is the profit you make on the bond
- A 100$ bond with a 3 year maturity and 6% interest gives three 6 dollar coupons
- These coupons are guaranteed, and are independent from the interest rate
- If the interest rate in the economy changes, the coupons are unaffected
- Therefore if the interest rate increases, the bond is worth comparatively less than current bonds
- On the other hand, the bond would be worth more comparatively if the interest rate decreases
- These are also liquidable, so you can sell them before their maturity periods depending on the nominal interest rate in the economy
- Calculate expected value of a bond by adding the face value and coupons divided by the interest rate to the power of number of years
- A default risk is the risk of an issuer not going through with a payment
- Bonds have a variety of default risks, and the higher the default the higher the interest rate generally
- Firms issue stocks if it is willing to give ownership, and issues bonds if they are not willing
- Loan backed securities are a new asset that only emerged in the last 40 or so years
- Asset created by pooling together loans and selling shares of that pool
- Examples would be mortgages, student loans, credit card loans, etc.
- When a bank gives a mortgage, they expect the borrowers to pay it back
- When you take a share of a mortgage pool, you get your return when the borrowers pay back their loan
- Mortgage backed securities are the most popular securities, became popular in the 2000s and caused the Great Recession
- Basically the bank lends its money to the person taking out the mortgage, then it gets money from someone who is buying an MBS, and that buyer receives returns once the person taking out the mortgage pays it back
