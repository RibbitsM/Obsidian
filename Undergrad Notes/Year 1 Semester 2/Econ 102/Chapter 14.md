Money

- Money is defined as an asset that can be used to purchase goods and services
- Can simplify this as cash in circulation, plus chequable deposits
- Even though money in a chequing account is not backed by cash, it can still be used to buy goods and services
- The monetary base is the total of all cash and coins minted alongside all cash in bank vaults controlled by the Bank of Canada
- Central bank has the ability to control this monetary base
- Banks hold some currency in reserve for the purpose of meeting demands for withdrawal
- Assume that all banks hold a fixed proportion of their total deposits in reserve
- Monetary base and the money in supply are different things, but both include the amount of cash in circulation
- Reserve currency in banks are not used to purchase goods and services and only exist for withdrawal
- Therefore they are not included in money supply
- Only the Bank of Canada can print money, but other banks can create money and affect the money supply
- Creating money through loans does not increase the monetary base, only the money supply
- Monetary base is bank reserves plus currency in circulation
- Money supply is currency in circulation plus chequable deposits
- Money supply is much larger since assuming a reserve ratio of 10%, each dollar in the bank reserves is backing 10 dollars of chequable deposits
- Therefore money supply is roughly 10 times larger than the monetary base
- This means a money multiplier of 10
- Say all banks seek to keep 20% of deposits in currency
- Borrowers keep their money in chequable deposits
- When a deposit is made in the bank, it keeps 20% of it and uses the remaining 80% to give out loans
- If a bank gets a deposit of 100 dollars, it must loan out 80 dollars to maintain the balance
- This loan will be deposited in another bank, which will make it's own loan in turn
- This continues until the original deposit of 100 dollars is in the reserve
- This "duplicates" the money
- The person who deposited the original 100 dollars has that money in the form of a chequable deposit
- But the person who received the loan of 80 dollars also has that money at the same time
- Money in circulation went from 100 to 180

Money Multiplier

- A deposit of 100 dollars leads to creation of many more deposits
- Deposits increase money supply
- 100 plus 100 times 1 minus reserve ration plus 100 times 1 minus reserve ratio to the power of 2
- Geometric series, functions similar to other multipliers
- 100 divided by the reserve ratio
- If ration is 0.2, 100 dollars deposited will increase money supply by $500
- Money multiplier is 5
- In reality this is not always true, people may hold onto parts of a loan in cash
- Leakage that reduces the money multiplier
- Can also calculate money multiplier by dividing money supply by money base
- In a scenario where leakages don't exist, the ratio of money in banks vs money in circulation is the reserve ratio
