Micro vs Macro

- Micro is about analyzing the decisions of individuals and individual markets
- Macroeconomics deals with the outcomes of micro decisions
- Dealing with all individuals and all markets
- Seek to define unemployment and GDP
- Not sector wise GDP
- The whole is greater than the sum of it's parts
- This divides micro from macro
- Cannot use analysis techniques from micro to understand macro
- Multiplier effect changes outcomes
- Even when every individual is acting rationally the outcome may not be
- Keynes contributed the multiplier effect
- A household decides to spend less because they are in a bad situation
- The multiplier effect takes effect because households spending less decreases the income of other households
- Those households will reduce spending in turn and so on
- Reducing your spending by 100 dollars can reduce market income by much more
- People tend to copy other's decisions
- This is called "hard behaviour"
- This is part of how recessions occur
- Overall drop in spending and income
- The paradox of thrift is similar, and occurs when households and firms cut spending to prepare for an economic downturn
- This causes a self fulfilling prophecy, and even if a downturn wasn't going to happen, cutting spending will cause one

Inflation and Deflation

- Average wages have risen since 1997
- But so has cost of living, so much that it outpaces wages
- Wages grew faster than cost of food and shelter, but slower than things like tuition and gas
- All things considered, living conditions have barely improved
- Inflation is the rise in the overall level in prices and deflation means a fall
- Inflation is a macro issue, cannot be solved by adding up supply and demand curves
- This is because S and D curves examine price relative to other goods
- Inflation examines why a price rises over time despite changes in production and other factors
- The causes of inflation in the short run are linked to the business cycle, troughs reduce inflation and booms increase it
- In the long run however, money supply has a greater influence on inflation, and an excess of money in a market can lead to hyperinflation
- Inflation and deflation can both have negative impacts on an economy
- Inflation discourages people from saving because their money becomes less and less valuable over time
- In situations of hyperinflation, this can lead to reverting to the barter system
- Deflation has the opposite effect, where saving money is highly incentivized and can create a recession because of reduced spending
- Price stability is the desired outcome, where price levels only change slowly

International Imbalances

- Canada is an example of an open economy, meaning it trades goods and services with other countries
- Almost all modern economies are open economies
- An example of exported services is a Japanese firm shipping products via DHL, a German firm
- Or when American firms do their finances through Canadian banks like TD or Chase
- Canada trades so much that they are at a trade deficit, meaning they import more goods than they export
- The opposite of this is a trade surplus, where you export more than import
- Germany and China are examples of countries that have trade surpluses
- This doesn't indicate the health of an economy, healthy economies can have trade deficits and unhealthy economies can also run trade surpluses
- Deficits can allow for maximized production possibilities
- Old economic theory from the 18th century used to argue that all economies should run surpluses
- This is because of opportunity cost, countries export goods that they are good at producing and import goods that they are bad at producing
- Whether countries run a deficit or surplus depends on how much they spend on investment vs savings
- This basically means how the government acts in the asset market
- This means trading things like foreign treasury bonds or even investment in companies
- Controls flow of foreign currency in the market, if many other countries are investing in the country that's an additional flow of currency in that will pay off import costs-
- High investment relative to savings leads to a trade deficit, while low investment leads to trade surpluses
- When Greece switched to the euro, it began running a large trade deficit, before shrinking and nearly becoming a surplus
- This may look like a positive thing, but the deficit shrinking was really because foreign investors stopped buying exports, sending the economy into a severe recession
- Comparative advantage or opportunity cost can explain why countries import some goods and export others, but does not explain why countries run deficits or surpluses
- Deficits and surpluses are determined by saving and investment decisions
- Government restrictions on import/export are macro issues, even though they impact micro decision making
- Can use demand supply models to predict the effect of these restrictions
- Determining whether debt is too high involves comparing debt to GDP, which is a macro variable
- Investment vs savings is based on citizens actions
- Whether the citizens invest more or save more determines surplus or deficit
- This is domestic investment, every year Canada gains money from investment, and if that amount is more than people saved, there will be a deficit
- The reason does not matter, just the values of investment vs savings
- Investment includes both Canadians investing in Canada and foreign investors investing in Canada
