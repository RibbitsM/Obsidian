- Goes back to the short run approach
- Assume that producers are willing to supply any amount of output at a fixed price as long as demand exists
- Therefore the aggregate supply curve is flat
- Want to concentrate only on aggregate demand, not supply limitations
- Usually this does not happen in reality, as marginal costs of production exist
- Main focus is how to changes in aggregate demand affects real GDP
- If Amazon invest 100 million dollars in building a storage facility in Richmond, the real GDP of BC will increase by 100 million dollars as investment expenditure increased by that amount
- However, there is a multiplier effect as this money will increase income of households and increase consumption as well

Marginal Propensity to Consume/Save

- If income increases, not all of it will be spent

There is assumed to be a fixed percentage of income spent

- MPC is the increase in consumer spending for each additional dollar of disposable income
- The fixed percentage is between 77% and 80%
- Calculates by dividing the change in consumer spending by the change in disposable income
- So if income increases by 5 dollars and spending increases by 4 dollars, the MPC is 0.8
- On the other hand there is also marginal propensity to save, which is the opposite
- If MPC is 0.8 then MPS is 0.2
- MPS is 1 minus MPC
- Disposable income increases and decreases alongside total income
- Along the same line, consumption expenditure also follows this trend
- If total income increases by 100 million, consumption spending increases by 80 million and saving increases by 20 million
- But what is the total effect?
- If the first round was an increase in 100 million, the second round is 100 million times MPC
- The third round is MPC squared times 100 million and so on
- Therefore the equation is (initial increase) divided by 1 minus MPC
- Or, initial increase divided by MPS
- Therefore if the initial is 100 million and MPC is 0.8, the final value is 500 million
- The initial change is called the autonomous change
- The multiplier is the amount this autonomous change is multiplied by
- Can be found by setting 1 as the autonomous value
- Yd is disposable income, y is total income

Consumer Spending

- Accounts for 60% of total spending
- The goal of the aggregate consumption function is to determine the relationship between income and consumption
- The consumption function is autonomous consumption plus MPC times change in disposable income
- Autonomous consumption is a base level of consumption that households have when there is no income
- This functions as the y intercept in the graph
- The slope of the graph is the MPC
- Change in consumption spending is rise, change in disposable income is run
- Say when you have no income, you spend 8000 dollars, this is the intercept
- If you spend $12000 after your income increases by $10000, your MPC is 0.4
- This is all you need to construct the aggregate consumption function
- To determine the aggregate, add all values together
- Remember that disposable income and real GDP are equal

Shifts

- If future disposable income is predicted to increase or decrease this can shift the curve
- If expected income increases, current consumption increases
- Households with lower income tend to save less than those with higher income
- People expect that over time income increasing would create more saving, but this isn't true
- No strong relationship between increased income and increased savings
- When the economy is in a growth period, households believe that it will continue to increase, and they save less
- This is the permanent income hypothesis
- The life cycle hypothesis describes how people try to smooth their consumption over time
- When you have high income you save more and spend less, and when you have low income you save less and spend more
- Creates a constant average consumption
- An increase in wealth, or increase in the value of assets can cause people to save less and spend more, as they rely on their increased assets

Investment Spending

- Most volatile factor, but determines less than consumption spending
- Investment expenditure often drives booms and busts in the economy
- Most recessions caused by drops in investment spending
- Believed that drops in consumer spending are caused by drops in investment
- Investment spending is planned, firms look at factors in the economy to determine how much they will invest over a period
- Main three factors that affect investment spending are interest rate, expected real GDP, and production capacity
- Interest rate has the greatest affect on investment in real estate
- Builders prefer to build homes when the interest rate is low
- Mortgages are much cheaper, so demand is higher
- Also when interest rates are higher firms tend to lend their money to earn interest on it rather than borrowing money at a high rate to fund some investment project
- Planned investment is negatively related to interest rate
- Investment is required to expand production capacity
- If a firm predicts that sales will rise enough that more capacity is required, they will invest
- In addition if there is excess capacity, the firm will likely not invest
- Therefore, if real GDP is expected to grow, meaning increased sales, investment spending increases
- This is part of the accelerator principle
- Increasing inventory is considered investment spending
- Inventory investment can be negative if inventory decreases
- Sales are unpredictable, so this kind of investment is often unplanned
- Macroeconomists examine inventory levels to make predictions
- Rising inventories indicate a reduction in growth and falling inventories represent an increase
- Ideally, planned expenditure should equal real GDP
- Planned expenditure is equal to planned investment expenditure plus consumption expenditure
- We assume that planned investment expenditure is fixed, so it's just the AC curve shifted up a bit
- If it does not, you are at disequilibrium
- For brief periods planned expenditure will deviate even at equilibrium because of unplanned investment expenditure from inventory management
- By finding the difference between real GDP and planned aggregate expenditure, we can find unplanned expenditure
- Therefore when planned expenditure exceeds real GDP, the economy is growing and vice versa
- Firms change output to adjust, moving towards equilibrium
- This equilibrium point is where planned AE intersects a 45 degree line on a graph
- Examined the effect of the interest rate in chapter 10
- Real GDP is self explanatory, invest more when the economy is growing
- If interest rate and real GDP are the same the level of production is also a determinant
- Only way the planned expenditure curve can shift is if planned investment expenditure or aggregate consumption change
- A change in interest would affect planned investment, while a change in disposable income would affect consumption
- This creates a multiplier effect
- If planned expenditure shifts up, GDP will rise by an even greater amount to match it
- The slope of aggregate expenditure is the MPC
- If there is a negative inventory investment, then firms increase production which starts the chain reaction
- Equation is still 1/1-MPC
- This is why the paradox of thrift is so dangerous
- A slump in spending causes a slump in GDP which lowers spending more via the multiplier

Imports and Exports

- Exports are sources of spending just like any other and act in the same way
- With imports the multiplier is slightly weakened as not all of the increased income is spent domestically
- How much this falls depends on the marginal propensity to import
- The more trade, the bigger the reduction
- Also with increased trade dependency, business cycles can become international

Price Level in Open Economy

- Discard assumptions that the price level is fixed
- The AD curve is derived from the income-expenditure model
- Measure price level with GDP deflator (nominal GDP over real GDP)
- Remember that planned expenditure has a positive relationship with GDP
- Here the total expenditure decreases due to an increase in GDP deflator/price level
- Price index and real GDP have a negative relationship, price level rising will drop real GDP
- When price level increases, your assets become worth less and the wealth effect reduces planned expenditure
- Price index also affects interest rate, when price level rises people save less which increases the interest rate
- In turn this reduces consumption and shifts the planned expenditure down
- A change in the price level always results in a vertical shift of the income-expenditure model
- This is the AD curve, meaning it is distinct from the normal demand curve
