Measuring Unemployment Rate

- Measuring employment rate is much easier
- The employment rate is the number of people employed divided by the number of the working age population times 100
- These numbers are determined by a monthly survey by statistics Canada
- The number of people of working age does not equal the amount of employed plus unemployed people, there is nuance
- You need to decide who is in the labour force, because not everybody wants a job
- The labour force consists of people with a part time or full time job, unemployed people who are looking for work in the last month, and are available for work meaning they don't have a disability preventing them form working
- This is where the trouble emerges because someone may be working a part time job but are actively looking for a full time job
- The labour force participation rate is calculated by having the number of people in the labour force divided by the number of people in the working age group times 100
- This statistic is important to give context to unemployment rate
- Therefore, the unemployment rate is calculated by the number of unemployed people divided by the number of people in the labour force times 100
- What are the advantages and disadvantages of this approach?
- Can possibly overestimate, but underestimation is a bigger issue
- If someone is declining job offers to wait for a better offer, they are voluntarily unemployed, this can cause overestimation
- A factor that can cause underestimation is discouraged workers, who have given up hope and no longer apply for jobs, therefore are not considered in the labour force
- During times of depression, this is very frequent
- Another way this can happen is when the economy of a small town breaks down and it's inhabitants have not yet moved away
- Marginally attached workers also create underestimation, who are workers that have been laid off but are not applying for jobs in hopes of being rehired at the firm that laid them off
- Someone who has a part time job but wants a full time job is called visibly underemployed
- Someone who is invisibly underemployed is someone who has high skills but can only find a lower paying job
- An example would be an immigrant who is a trained doctor or engineer, but must work a menial job because their certification is worthless in Canada
- There are different measures of unemployment rate including some or all of these factors
- Discouraged workers have the least effect, followed by marginally attached workers who have a slightly larger effect, and finally there are the underemployed workers, who increase the unemployment rate drastically, sometimes by around 1/3

Growth Rate and Unemployment Rate

- The idea is that real GDP growth leads firms to require more labour to increase output, reducing unemployment
- There is generally a connection between higher growth rate and reduced unemployment rate
- If real GDP is rising, but is rising less than average there is usually still a rise in unemployment
- However, if growth is above average then unemployment almost always drops
- When GDP is rising but unemployment is also rising it is called a jobless recovery or growth recession
- At the average GDP growth rate, unemployment doesn't change or barely drops
- There are always exceptions, in 2016 the GDP was falling significantly, but unemployment was still dropping
- Labour force is constantly in a flow state where workers move in and out of employment
- The number of people in the labour force at any given time is also variable
- This is due to people being hired and fired, but also can be caused by workplace accidents, people recovering and coming back to work etc
- The labour force usually has new people joining it constantly by either becoming old enough to qualify or applying for jobs, while also losing people who give up on applying for jobs
- 3 kinds of unemployment, frictional, structural, and cyclical
- Frictional or voluntary unemployment accounts for the people who are unemployed and searching for jobs
- It takes time to get a new job after leaving a previous job, and frictional unemployment refers to people in this waiting stage
- Caused by a lack of perfect information between employer and employee
- Even if there is an employee with the perfect skills for a certain employer, the employee has to apply for that job and go through the entire application process before they are hired
- If there is no recession, there is low unemployment and there is a short waiting period for those unemployed, it's usually frictional unemployment
- If someone is long-run unemployed, this means they have been unemployed for 27 weeks or more
- This indicates that there is a reason for them being unemployed other than frictional unemployment

Structural Unemployment

- When the number of job seekers is more than the number of jobs available, it can be because of cyclical or structural unemployment
- This creates surplus labour
- A couple reasons for this can be due to minimum wage, unions, and efficiency wages
- A minimum wage creates a price floor above the equilibrium leading to a surplus in supply
- Since the wages are higher, the firm will hire less workers and creates a labour surplus
- Unions can create the same problem by negotiating for higher wages
- Efficiency wages seek to overcome moral hazards and adverse selection problems
- By offering higher wages to workers, it increases motivation and productivity
- Any kind of unemployment aid from the government may also create structural unemployment
- In Canada this takes the form of unemployment insurance and Canada emergency response benefit
- Unemployment aid is valuable because it manages frictional unemployment, but can create structural unemployment if it is too generous
- Unemployment aid in Canada has been slashed since the 90s
- Another factor in structural unemployment is an unskilled workforce, where there is a mismatch between workers skills and the skills required by firms
- This can be overcome by retraining workers
- Because of Shumpeter's theory of creative destruction, the job market is constantly changing with older firms being overcome by modern ideas

Cyclical Unemployment

- This refers to unemployment caused by downturns in the business cycle
- In a recession the FAPs in an economy are underutilized due to a drop in demand
- This leads to production under capacity, meaning much less workers are needed
- The natural rate of unemployment is the frictional unemployment plus structural, but the actual rate includes cyclical unemployment as well
- Having some frictional unemployment is good, and structural unemployment means that workers are getting better wages and a better working environment
- Therefore, a certain level of unemployment is considered acceptable
- The natural rate of unemployment is affected by changes in the labour force, institutions, and changes in govt policy
- Frictional unemployment can be reduced by making job searching more efficient, but can never be eliminated

Inflation

- Unemployment and inflation are the two main issues that economists seek to avoid in their management of the economy
- An increase in the price level is not the same as an increase in the rate of change in price levels
- The price level has increased continuously over time, but inflation is variable
- In 2009 there was even deflation, where the inflation rate turned negative
- Inflation measures the rate of increase in the price level, so even if inflation decreases the price level will still increase unless there is deflation
- If inflation decreases but is still positive, it is disinflation not deflation
- Whether or not inflation negatively affects someone depends on real wage and real income
- The term real meaning calculating an increase while accounting for change in price level
- Real wage is the nominal wage divided by the price level, and real income is the nominal income divided by the price level
- High rates of inflation have costs on the economy
- Some examples are the cost of changing listed prices to account for inflation, making calculation more difficult, and increased costs of transactions due to people trying to spend money
- Money can be a unit of value but also a unit of account, so when inflation occurs it disrupts accounting procedures
- These are called menu costs, unit-of-account costs, and shoe-leather costs respectively
- These are all unnecessary costs that emerge from a breakdown in how currency functions
- Only apply in cases of hyperinflation like in Brazil, Venezuela and Zimbabwe
- Another thing affected by inflation is the interest rate, as it can lead to much higher nominal returns
- The interest rate is defined as the return a lender receives from their savings for one year
- The nominal interest rate is the rate in dollar terms, but the real interest rate is the nominal inflation rate minus the rate of inflation
- Disinflation is the reduction of the inflation rate which is very difficult to achieve
- Disinflation requires a large temporary decrease in the unemployment rate which can have drastic consequences for real GDP
- There were two major periods of disinflation in the 70s and 80s which were caused by spikes in the unemployment rate
- Real interest rate is the interest rate in terms of quantities
- If nominal interest rate equals inflation, there is no change
- If they are unequal, calculate the inflation of one unit of a good and calculate the cost of buying the same quantity of the good after inflation
- Then find the real interest rate from the new total
- Say you could buy 8 goods for 120 dollars
- This costs 126 dollars the next year
- With a 10% nominal interest rate you can buy 4.76% more of the good, so that is your new real interest rate
- However there is a simpler method called the Fisher Equation
- The equation is real interest rate equals nominal interest rate minus equation
- This does not give the same results but is roughly close enough
- Therefore, we only use this equation for very small values of inflation
