Law and Politics

- Politics is intended to happen under the law, no member of the government is above the law
- Law is above all things, every person within the political system is bound by the law
- The law is the highest power in the country, comes from Martin Luther and the Protestant church
- Replace god with the law, and you can see the relationship
- We are all equal before the law, even our leaders, unlike monarchs appointed by god
- Not only a Western concept, adopted by many other states worldwide
- Law comes from power
- Creates an odd paradox where law is enforced by power, but at the same time those with power are subject to the law
- Kim Davis was a clerk who refused to give out marriage licenses to gay couples even after the US legalized gay marriage
- She did this on religious grounds, stating that God's authority holds more power over her than the authority of the US Supreme Court
- The US Constitution is based on freedom of religion, and therefore Kim Davis has the right to freely express her religion
- She almost immediately became a prominent public figure and was allowed to meet with the Pope
- She lost her case, was sued, and was no longer involved in issuing marriage licenses
- Law comes from power, and this inevitably leads to the law benefitting those who hold power

Legal Positivism

- Present in almost all countries in the world besides theocracies and absolute monarchies
- Means that the state has the monopoly on law, only the state can create the law
- Even in these countries like the Vatican, the process is still based on legal positivism even though law comes from religion
- Even though the state is the middleman, the law is channeled through the state by God
- The group that controls the state, controls the law
- Core of the Marxist argument, the upper class controls the law and the lower class must rise up and seize it
- Liberals believe that their rights and freedoms protect them from the state and the law
- Most of the time the state is the only source of law
- Law can also come from religion, family, non-state institutions, and even from other states
- An example would be honor killings, where people violate the law of the state on family principles
- In legal positivist countries, we codify decisions made by the court
- A decision made on a single issue, can be applied across the entire legal system
- Laws cannot be made that violate constitutional principles
- Law creators are therefore bound by the law because of the Supreme Court

Rule of Law

- Law applies to everyone equally and all citizens are expected to obey it
- Laws are supposed to have specific characteristics to be considered "good laws"
- The law should be general, public, clear, consistent, and possible to obey
- Laws also should be prospective not retrospective, meaning they only apply once they are established, you cannot punish crimes that happened before the law was made
- In most governments there is a separate judiciary branch that is responsible for upkeeping the law and making sure it is followed
- Made up of several legal institutions
- Courts and judges are independent from the rest of the government to ensure the neutrality of the law
- An example of this was in Canada during the SNC-Lavalin affair where the former attorney general of Canada claimed that Justin Trudeau tried to convince her to make a legal decision that would benefit his re-election
- The reason this happened was because in Canada, the attorney general is also the justice minister
- This mixes the judicial and executive branches, meaning the attorney general has a vested interest in getting their party re-elected

Purpose of Laws

- Law comes from power and is an effect of power
- No matter if this comes from god or a government, it comes from some source of power
- Laws are tools of the state, all states make laws and all states enforce laws (or try to)
- Since laws are made from power, those with power can avoid the law
- For example, fines essentially are a price you can pay in order to break the law
- The purpose of laws is to regulate behavior
- Institutionalized, systemic, exercise of control
- Institutionalized means they are enshrined in institutions and upheld by them
- Systemic means that it is everywhere in society and controls every aspect of life
- Of course with power, you can sidestep this control to some extent
- Those with the least power in society are always those most punished by the law
- In liberal democracies, rights and freedoms exist as a shield against this state exercise of power

Constitutions

- Almost all countries have constitutions
- Detail the principles of a country, not necessarily the laws
- All constitutions are different
- Also indicate how a state characterizes itself
- Secular or religious, federal or unitary
- A federal state is a state that is divided into smaller provinces that have a level of autonomy
- States choose to be federal usually because of the size of the country, 8 out of the 10 largest countries by territory are federal
- These countries usually also have very diverse populations, and as such are sometimes divided on cultural grounds
- Being federal or unitary has little effect on the stability and efficiency of a government
- Power of the state is distributed between the local authorities and the central authority
- For example, in Canada provinces make most decisions on education, not the central govt
- Federal govt still distributes resources but decision making is distributed
- Every federal govt has multiple layers of representation, can be as few as 2 and as many as 6
- In Canada we also have territories, which have less power than the provinces
- Some alternatives are confessionalism/consociationalism (used by the Netherlands and Lebanon)
- This is where the power is constitutionally shared between interest groups to prevent conflicts
- Used in small states with deep divisions along ethnic or religious lines
- Proportional representation, even with free elections
- For example in Lebanon, the army chief must always be Sunni Muslim
- Has existed for centuries, comparable to modern governments where there must be an equal number of men and women in government
- Minority veto powers are also important for these governments
- Builds trust between hostile groups
- Asymmetric decentralization is a hybrid system between federalism and consociationalism
- Grants differing amounts of power to different regions
- Examples are Canada with territories and Spain, where some regions had been devastated by dictatorial rule and hold very different values and even different languages
