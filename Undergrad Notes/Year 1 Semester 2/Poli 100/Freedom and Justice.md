Freedom and Economy

- Can an individual truly be free if they are restricted by economic class?
- It is clear that richer people have much more freedom than poor people even though they are equal under the law
- One of the foundational ideas behind the welfare state, by giving access to essential services the state increases the freedom of it's citizens
- Welfare state advocates argue that it is the state's duty to provide freedom to it's citizens, and should supply services since it creates a net increase in freedom in a society
- Freedom is usually discussed as a net good because it unavoidably removes some freedom from others when it is given

Negative and Positive Freedoms

- Negative freedoms are the absence of constraints, in the context of the state, negative freedoms determine where the state cannot interfere
- Associated with John Locke who believed that humans are truly free in the state of nature, and the state constrains these freedoms for the benefit of society
- Locke argued that the state should impose the least obstacles possible, basically advocated for a night watchman state and a free market
- Rousseau argued for positive freedoms and stated that the duty of the state is to provide a good standard of living for all citizens
- Stated that negative freedoms are only freedoms to those with the means to overcome the requirements for those freedoms
- Think about freedom to eat the dumpling
- The US is a more liberal country that tends to favour negative freedoms as positive freedoms are a burden on the state and harm the capitalist process
- Socialist states tend to favour positive freedoms based on the state's perceived responsibility for the well being of it's people
- A good example is how the US vs Canada handle healthcare, where it is guaranteed in Canada but dependent on economic status in the US
- In Canada, Pierre Trudeau established free healthcare across Canada which is a positive freedom, and also removed prohibition of same-sex acts which is a negative freedom
- Both of these decisions are considered increases in net freedom, just in two different ways
- JS Mill believed that greater freedom leads to greater happiness, a simple outlook
- Western philosophy is heavily steeped in this assumption to the extent where freedom is automatically associated with happiness for most people
- Most discussions about freedoms is the methods of maximizing freedom whether it be through positive or negative freedoms
- Despite being on opposite ends of the ideological perspective, the US and the USSR during the Cold War both sought to maximize freedom for their people
- Mill also proposed the harm principle, where only actions that would harm others should be prevented
- Apart from this one rule, no other freedoms should be restricted

Justice

- Justice promotes freedom, in the sense that in a fair society freedom would be maximized and justice is also a tool used to facilitate freedom in the sense that it acts as a consequence for violating freedoms
- Power is a key connecting force between freedom and justice, because without consequences there is no reason to respect freedoms
- The basic definition of justice is giving people what they are entitled to
- The question is who is entitles to what and how it is decided?
- Hobbs argued that anyone who has power is able to dispense justice
- The question of who holds power is key to understanding freedom and justice
- A main concern of justice is how resources should be distributed
- Resources can be wealth, income, education, and freedoms

How does justice work?

- Can also say how do we operationalize justice?
- We need systems in order to organize justice in society
- It's been proven that judges give out much harsher sentences for the same crime if the hearing is before lunch vs after
- Example of the flawed interpretation of justice by people
- Justice is very different across cultures, for example how elderly people are treated in Asia vs North America
- Moral luck is when two people make the same decision such as choosing to drive drunk, and one receives consequences and the other doesn't
- For example, a rich person will incur less consequences for an offense than a poor person
- Procedural justice is the fairness of the process, in this situation everything is procedurally just
- Meanwhile social/distributive justice is when the outcome for committing a crime is the same
- Taxes are an example of social justice, where money is taken from rich communities and used to fund poorer communities
- Another form of justice used by socialist countries is need based justice
- This is when resources are distributed according to the needs of people, "from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs"
- The final kind is merit based justice, where resources are distributed according to what will benefit the progression of society the most
- Basically more resources are given to smarter people
- Procedural justice is found in every state with a legal or educational system
- Rawls pioneered the liberal theory of justice with the concept of the veil of ignorance
- This is a state where one does not know their gender, race, sexuality, location of birth, class etc.
- Basically how would we create a justice system if we had no biases
- Rawls argued that in this case, everyone should have equal access to the most extensive system of rights possible
- He also argued that inequalities would be arranged so that disadvantaged people would be supported as much as possible and that all people should have equal chance of success

Other Theories of Justice

- Argues that in a globalized world, country's theories of justice should be applied to their international relations too
- Justice is a concept shared by all of humanity and should be applied globally
- Communitarian justice states the opposite, that justice is based on the sociocultural values in their communities and cannot be universalized
- To say otherwise is to impose your will unfairly on other cultures
- Green theory of justice argues that concepts of justice should be applied to animals and plants as well
- Also called ecological justice and inter-generational justice
