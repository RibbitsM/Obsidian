- International Relations is a discipline, without the capitals it refers to the actual relations
- Studies and seeks to explain how nations interact
- The Thirty Years war involved over 20 states in Europe and wiped out about 20% of the population, was worse than the Black Plague in some places
- The wakeup call that created the earliest form of the modern state
- The Treaty of Westphalia established territorial boundaries that no other country can claim, a novel concept at the time
- Separated religious authority and the state authority, religion of the public had no influence on the government
- Divided people along territorial boundaries instead of religious boundaries
- Rulers could set a state religion, but not force the citizens to convert
- States had complete authority within their territory, but no authority in the territory of other states
- There are three main approaches to international relations
- Of course relations between groups has existed almost as long as people have, but the discipline was invented at the end of WWI
- The initial purpose of IR was to figure out how to end war, and prevent anything like WWI from happening again
- The first people to examine IR were the liberals, and were followed by the realists
- Liberals sought to end war altogether, the realists believed this was impossible but sought to have longer periods of peace
- A third approach called constructivism emerged in the late 20th century and was immensely popular
- Kind of replaced liberals in the field by incorporating and improving on the liberal ideas
- Before WWI, a group of British scholars sought to use similar approaches for the means of maintaining British hegemony
- Looked at diplomatic history and international law years before Woodrow Wilson and the liberals

Three Approaches

- Founded on the philosophy of John Locke and Immanuel Kant
- Believed in the capability of humanity to ascend beyond their current state and achieve things like ending war
- Locke and Kant were the main names that agreed with this
- The realists followed the philosophy of Hobbes and Machiavelli
- Believed that IR should be amoral, and morals had no place in relations between states
- Constructivism was based around Alexander Wendt
- Argued that society was created by our beliefs, whatever we believe will come true
- Therefore believing that all people are naturally asocial and violent was inherently harmful
- Provided a challenge to Hobbes and the realist theories
- The order of society is socially constructed, and believes that the liberal theory is possible

Liberal

- Assumes all people are naturally cooperative, and form society naturally
- Saw people who are not naturally cooperative as unnatural exceptions
- Problem is how to create trust and secure peace
- The answer is an international authority that can ensure cooperation and security
- This initially took the form of the League of Nations, but became the UN after WWII
- Kant's Perpetual Peace is a fundamental text for liberals
- Described a federation of free states/republics like the league of nations
- Reason is that a free state cannot justify invading another free state
- Therefore two democracies will never go to war with eachother, which has proven true

Realism

- Believe that human nature is to seek power
- The main actors in the international system are the states, not the central authority
- The international system is "anarchical"
- States are driven by self-interest and desire for power over others
- The problem is national security, how can a country avoid war?
- The objective IR from the realist perspective is to have the two strongest states and a balance of power between them
- The strength of each state will prevent wars from breaking out
- Very similar to the scenario in the Cold War

Constructivism

- Recognize that society is whatever we make it
- Emancipating theory, any goal is within grasp
- Anarchy is whatever we make of it
- The way we think about the world changes how we respond to simple things
- A weapon has different meaning in your hand vs an enemies hand
- Actions shape the world and determine what beliefs/values are reality
- Institutions are social constructs and only have authority because people believe they do
- Humans shape the world around them, and in turn people are affected by their environment

Security

- All of these perspectives have beliefs around security
- Realists believe that security is the most important thing and is all that matters
- Liberals believe that security is only available through a central authority
- Constructivists believe that security is a social construction and you only feel secure
