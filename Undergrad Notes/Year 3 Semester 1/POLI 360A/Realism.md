- Mainly preoccupied with the state, most important unit on the international stage
- Persists since it is rather effective at explaining armed conflict
- Most realists agree that relations between states have not changed much, behaviour of states is predictable and consistent
- The international system of anarchy will ensure a constant state of war, pessimistic and prudent view
- Six variants, classical, neo, rise and fall, neoclassical, and offensive and defensive structural realism

**Classical Realism**

- Famously long history, dates back to Thucydides and Sun Tzu
- Also other classic political theorists like Machiavelli and Hobbes are keystones of realist tradition
- Emerged in 1939, response to the dominant liberal theories of international relations at the time
- Various other key works, one of the most important being Hans Morgenthau's work in 1948
- Mainly obsolete now, but Morgenthau's _Politics Among Nations_ used to be the bible of IR
- He listed six main principles of realism
- The first is that politics is governed by objective laws, one of them being the permanence of war
- These are not laws made by people, but unchanging laws of nature
- Second, international politics can be understood through interest in terms of power
- To examine a state's behaviour, ask what are their interests, and do they have the power to achieve those interests
- The interests of states should match their power, otherwise they are pointless
- Third, power can change but the interests of states remain
- Fourth, universal morals have no impact on the actions of states
- International politics is amoral, a key aspect of future realist thought
- Fifth, there is no universally agreed moral framework, building off the previous rule
- Goes directly against the now common view of organizations such as the UN that there are universal human rights and morals
- Finally, politics is autonomous
- Realism believes international relations is a zero sum game
- In classical realism, there is no difference between domestic and foreign policy, the actions of states are a result of flaws in human nature
- Classical realism began to decline in the wake of the Cold War as arms control treaties began to provide a more optimistic future

**Neorealism**

- In 1979 Waltz's theory replaced Morgenthau as the gold standard of realism
- Argued that systems have a structure and interacting units
- The first element of political structure is the ordering principle
- In this case it is anarchy, and shapes the decisions of states
- The second element is the character of units
- Our units are states, and Waltz argues all states a similar in the sense that they share the goal of survival
- The final element is distribution of capabilities, i.e. power
- The first two elements remain the same, but balance of power changes
- There will always be the ordering principle of anarchy and states will always seek survival, but their capabilities to do so may differ
- Neorealism disregards the internal dynamics of states, and suggests instead that the system shapes international relations
- War doesn't happen because people are evil, but because the system demands it
- This is why history tends to repeat itself, the structure creates the same outcomes over and over
- Tragic and extremely pessimistic view of international politics, offers basically no room for improvement or escape from the cycle
- In classical realism, the end goal of politics is to accrue power, but in neorealism the goal is simply to survive, and power is just a means to that end
- However, depending on the situation this tragedy can change, bipolar systems were seen to be more stable than multipolar scenarios, giving an avenue for change
- As global stability increased, this view began to decline significantly

**Defensive Structural Realism**

- Proponents are Kenneth Waltz, Stephen Walt, and Robert Jervis
- Shares neorealist assumption, states seek security in anarchy and are threatened by other states
- Three main differences
- First, Neorealism allows multiple factors, but defensive structural realism only relies on rational choice to explain state behaviour
- Second, it adds the offensive-defensive balance as a variable
- This is the measure of a power's offensive ability compared to it's defensive ability
- Finally, defensive structural realists believe that states should support the status quo to survive
- States estimate threat through a combination of relative power, proximity, perceived intentions, and offensive-defensive balance
- Believe that revisionist behaviour only ends in disaster for the state pursuing it
- Balancing will always result in the revisionist state being put down
- Fails to examine where revisionist behaviour originates
- By this logic, no state would ever try to change the status quo
- Good for looking at responses to revisionism, but not the causes
- International politics is a tragedy, not an evil
- Another problem is with the security dilemma
- According to defensive structural realists, this is the source of war
- If states signal benign intentions and maintain a defense dominant balance, there's no uncertainty
- Conclude that states seek an "appropriate" amount of power and signal they mean no harm when that condition is met

**Offensive Structural Realism**

- States in an anarchic environment are in constant danger of being harmed by other states
- Therefore relative capabilities are the most important thing
- To be secure is to have the highest possible relative power
- Mearsheimer sets out 5 assumptions
- International system is anarchic
- Great powers have offensive military capability
- States cannot confidently ascertain other states intentions
- Primary goal of great powers is survival
- Great powers are rational actors
- Because of these assumptions, aiming for regional hegemony is a rational idea
- States are skilled at relative power maximization, and succeed through brains and brawn
- Ultimate safety comes from being the most powerful in the system, but limitations prevent world domination from being viable
- Preferable to attain just regional hegemony
- Failing that, maximize wealth and ability to wage war on land
- Mearsheimer has created a theory of great power war, important factors are the number of powers and their relative abilities

**Rise and Fall Realism**

- Emerged as alternative to balance of power theories
- Based around the idea that hegemony creates peace, while balancing and multipolarity creates war
- Theorizes that war is less likely when the system is dominated by one state which has no viable challengers
- Since no state has the ability to change the world order, stability reigns
- Eventually, a challenger will rise and challenge the hegemon resulting in war
- In comparison to other realist theories, rise and fall examines internal factors governing growth carefully
- Considerations include industrialization, social system, innovation developments, politics, and type of economic system
- Attempts to explain why states "rise", or grow, at different rates by examining internal dynamics
- Still leaves other questions, like what time scale this can be implemented on and who initiates these wars between hegemons and challengers?

**Neoclassical Realism**

- Also examines lots of domestic factors, believes that state actions are heavily influenced by domestic factors
- Uses the idea of a "transmission belt", there isn't a clear flow of information from the international system to policy makers
- Threats and opportunities are not clear at all, and decision makers have many different options to choose from
- How states react to systemic imperatives depends on its leadership, society, military, bureaucratic structure, resource extraction, etc.
- Different states have differing levels of efficiency in working towards foreign policy goals, and different priorities in setting those goals
- Behaviour of states is still driven by the international balance of power, but how this information is transmitted through the state structure changes
- States with strong connections between society and elites are better and seeing and responding to threats
- Overall, a main contention of realist thought is that human and environmental security have no place in security studies
- By moving focus away from the state, ISS loses it's theoretical coherence
- Refuse to broaden or deepen the focus of security studies
