- Always referred to in reference to realism, emerged as a criticism of realism like most theories in IR
- History dating back to Adam Smith, Jeremy Bentham, John Locke, Immanuel Kant
- Influences from moral philosophy (utilitarianism), economics and perceptions of threat
- Agrees with the anarchic character of international relations set up by realists
- However, this state of anarchy is not seen as permanent, and can be remedied by international institutions
- Main reference object is still the state, and institutions are made up of states
- Conflict is not inevitable, and our goal should be to limit inter-state conflict as much as possible
- Conflict can be mitigated through intermediaries, treaties, and security communities
- Fundamentally optimistic outlook compared to realism
- Compared to realism, liberals are much better at explaining change in the international system
- Belief in the influence of domestic factors on the international stage
- Explains how great powers can change with a difference in domestic politics
- Various branches of liberal ideology
- Commercial Liberalism, Liberal Idealism, Liberal Peace Theory, and Neoliberal Institutionalism

**Commercial Liberalism**

- Believes in linking the world through economics, creating interdependence with cross border trade
- Economic penalties for war, when states depend on each other for goods, they are less likely to make war on each other to avoid cutting ties
- Response to the prevalence of mercantilism, the belief that international economics is zero sum
- Voltaire said that a country cannot gain unless another loses
- Commercial liberals connect values of free market economies with other liberal values like rule of law
- Promoting liberalism through economic philosophy
- The liberal trade doctrine holds that trade is always mutually beneficial, and free market policies should be applied worldwide
- A good example of the success of commercial liberalism is the EU, as a group of states that are economically connected and have lived in peace for decades

**Liberal Idealism**

- Earlier form on liberalism, originated from the rhetoric of Woodrow Wilson in the interwar period
- Belief in the essentially good nature of human nature
- Led to the creation of the League of Nations as a mechanism to prevent the repeat of WWI, which ultimately failed
- Wilson believed in a form of world government that would enforce universal laws on the international community
- In today's age, liberal idealism focuses on collective security, democracy, and self determination

**Liberal Peace Theory**

- Originates with Kant's Perpetual Peace, where he states that republican governments are less likely to start or join conflicts
- Believes that the test of a good law is it's universalizability
- Constitutional republics are theoretically more prone to peace since they are held accountable by their citizens
- Since citizens are negatively affected by war, they will act as a restraint on the state
- Simply being republican does not ensure peace, therefore republican states have a responsibility to uphold international laws
- Key component of the essay was Kant's criticism of the balance of power
- Two parts to Kant's peace program
- First part is the initial conditions a republic must make within it's borders
- Involves the abolition of standing armies, non interference with other states, outlawing of espionage, treason, and assassination, ending imperialism, etc.
- Clearly, not all of these principles have been adopted by liberals
- The second part is the three principles for global peace
- Every state should be a constitutional republic
- International law will be upheld by an international coalition of free states
- International law will be founded on the idea of universal civic hospitality
- This has been developed by contemporary theorists into the democratic peace thesis
- States that liberal democracies do not go to war with other liberal democracies
- Kant's initial concept of the liberal peace has been distorted over time, as the main conclusion being drawn was simply that democracy is equivalent with peace
- Therefore, the goal of democratic states should be to spread democracy to as many other states as possible
- This has resulted in situations where liberal foreign policy actually causes wars, with Iraq being a notable example

**Neoliberal Institutionalism**

- Response to the neorealist perspective, part of the back and forth between traditionalist ideologies
- Argues that international institutions are essential for changing the character of the inherent anarchy of international relations
- Institutions like the UN act as intermediaries and balancing forces to arbitrate disputes
- Reject the permanence of anarchy in the international system, inter-state conflict can be mitigated by an overarching authority
- The perpetual state of conflict is not real, it can be changed by escaping the dilemmas of anarchy
- If this isn't permanent, what needs to be done to change the character of the system?
- Axelrod asked "under what conditions will cooperation emerge in a world of egoists without central authority"
- The security dilemma can be mitigated through mutual benefit, if one state gives another a boon, they are obliged to reciprocate
- These positive interactions build trust over time, and develops the "shadow of the future"
- Basically as trust grows, it becomes less beneficial to break it in the future
- Especially if it is institutionalized, the benefits of trust should outweigh the drawbacks
- Reduces uncertainty and prevents security dilemma situations from arising
- Transaction costs are essential to neoliberal institutionalism
- Information has a cost, without an official channel like an international institution, information is gained through costly methods like espionage
- Institutions reduce transaction costs for everything, not just information but protecting rights, enforcing agreements, etc.
- The burden is lifted off of individual states and shared among the community
- Institutions are enduring, and are very difficult to create or reconstruct
- Legislatures are key to the institutional approach
- Some argue that the goals of international institutions shouldn't be those of state interests, but global interests
- Topics that surpass the scope of individual states like starvation, disease, climate change, and other global catastrophes
- The main difference between liberal and realist views on international organizations is their views on their purposes
- Realists believe they exist to increase or preserve the power of member states, where liberal believe they exist to maximize the shared interests of those states

**Security Dilemma**

- Realists believe the security dilemma is a permanent part of the system, but liberals reject this
- States are not prisoners of the system, but have the capability to change the reality of it
- Collective security efforts can overcome the security dilemma since it is only an issue of perception
- All traditionalist approaches are criticized heavily by other security approaches, especially on their focus on the state
- Critical approaches seek a deepening of the object of analysis and a broadening of the definition of security
- However, the study of inter-state conflict remains the primary issue in global politics, and the state remains the primary referent object￼
