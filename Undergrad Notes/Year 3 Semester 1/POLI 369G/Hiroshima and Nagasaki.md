**Strategic Bombing**

- Can also be called carpet bombing or area bombardment
- The idea of strategic bombing was around long before WWI
- Can be seen in media like The War in the Air by H.G. Wells
- Some bombing attacks were carried out in WWI, but planes were extremely limited
- Fear around aerial attacks increased in the wake of WWI
- Felt that bombing would be disastrous for civilian morale and could cause whole countries to revolt if done properly
- One event that fuelled this fear was the bombing of Guernica in the Spanish Civil War, inspiring the Picasso painting of the same name
- The second example was the bombings of Nanjing and Guangzhou by the Japanese imperial navy
- When World War II broke out, aircraft had much larger ranges, higher speeds, and greater capacities
- The bombing campaigns of WWII were carried out to destroy industry and infrastructure, halt economic activity, and damage morale
- Many such examples, Rotterdam, Leningrad, and Shanghai to name a few
- However, the most severe targets were the Germans, especially Hamburg and Dresden
- In 1943, a large bombing raid created a firestorm that burned most of the city to a crisp
- Many casualties were due to asphyxiation due to the fires consuming all available oxygen
- More than 3900 tons of bombs and incendiary devices were dropped on Dresden again in 1945
- These bombing campaigns killed almost exclusively civilians, although soldiers were also targeted
- The destruction was wanton and completely uncontrolled, fires ravaging the entire city
- There is an ongoing debate about whether these bombings are considered war crimes
- Well before the development of the atomic bomb, strategic bombing was going strong with conventional weapons alone

**War Crimes**

- War crimes are violations of international law that govern warfare
- The main international laws addressing war crimes are the Geneva Conventions and it's additional protocols
- The 1949 Geneva Conventions are split into four categories
- One for the armed forces, one for those at sea, one for prisoners of war, an one for civilians
- These laws do not expressly forbid strategic bombing, although they could be interpreted as such
- The 1977 additional protocols more specifically address strategic bombing
- 2 protocols, one for international conflict and one for non international
- These set out the concepts of distinction and proportionality
- The principle of distinction states that there must be a distinction from civilians and civilian targets and their military counterparts
- This specifically bans indiscriminate attacks, especially strategic bombardment
- A city cannot be a target, only specific military buildings
- The principle of proportionality states that the collateral damage caused by an attack cannot be in excess of the military advantage gained by the attack
- Does not ban the taking of civilian lives, only in proportion to the expected results of the attack
- Also includes damage to civilian buildings and infrastructure
- Of course there are no guidelines on how to weigh civilian lives against military objectives
- Since the bombings during WWII are only war crimes in retrospect (additional protocols added in 77), they can only really be seen as moral guidelines

**Strategic Bombing of Japan**

- The B-29 bomber was the workhorse behind the bombing of Japan
- Chosen because of it's reliability and ability to carry incendiaries
- Motives were similar to Dresden, disrupting morale, economy, and infrastructure etc.
- Masterminded by Curtis Lemay, commanded the 305th Bomb Group
- Lemay pioneered a new low altitude bombing strategy using a chemical called napalm manufactured by DuPont which would become famous later in Vietnam
- The mostly wooden cities of Southeast Asia were especially vulnerable to napalm
- He would eventually become the head of Strategic Air Command in the US
- In 1945, the air force was dropping 34,000 tons of bombs per month
- Bombings were carried out on basically all cities, anything that had standing infrastructure
- The most significant was the Tokyo Raid, carried out by 334 B-29s creating a massive firestorm that killed 100,000 and injured a million, destroying 41 square kilometers of Tokyo
- Clearly even before the nuclear bomb was dropped, the US air force was capable of causing mass destruction
- Significant portions of Tokyo, Kobe, Osaka, and Nagoya were all burnt to the ground by the time the war had concluded
- The combination of the philosophy of strategic bombing alongside the growing technology of fission bombs created the perfect environment for Hiroshima and Nagasaki
- Curtis Lemay believed that by making Japan yield through air power alone, the US could avoid incurring casualties in an invasion of Japan
