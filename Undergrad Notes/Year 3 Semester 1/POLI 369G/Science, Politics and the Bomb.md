- Nuclear fission discovered in the 30s
- Szilard was born in Hungary and worked on the Manhattan project
- Patented the nuclear reactor with Fermi and discovered the nuclear chain reaction
- Fermi was an Italian Nobel Prize winner that emigrated to the US
- One of the 2 fathers of the atomic bomb
- Fission was discovered by four German scientists: Hahne, Meitner, Frisch, and Straussmann that had fled persecution in Germany
- In nuclear fission, enriched uranium is destabilized by neutrons, releasing energy and splitting into two or more elements
- This also releases more neutrons that can destabilize more uranium atoms
- The Frisch-Peierls memorandum expressed concern that Germany would develop a fission bomb
- Advised that the US must construct a similar bomb to deter the use of a German bomb
- The letter that would lead to the Manhattan project was the Einstein-Szilard letter which was mostly written by Einstein
- Was sent to the president and echoed many of the concerns of Frisch and Peierls
- Einstein later regretted endorsing this letter and leading to the creation of the Manhattan project

**Ethics and the Bomb**

- Two ethical theories, Consequentialism and Deontologism
- Consequentialism looks at the consequences of acts, ends justify means
- Often used to justify the building of the bomb
- Deontology looks at the morality of the actions and ignores the consequences
- Act morally, regardless of the consequences
- Associated with the golden rule
- Usually used in arguments against the building of the bomb

**The Manhattan Project**

- Initiated by FDR in 1939 in response to the Einstein-Szilard letter
- Codenamed Advisory Committee on Uranium
- The S1 Uranium committee controlled the US Army Corps of Engineers division Development of Substitute Materials that was renamed to the Manhattan District
- This is where the project got it's name from initially
- The project was directed by Leslie R Groves, an Army engineer who presided over the construction of the Pentagon
- This was a military project, directed by military men
- Also a political decision, under the command of the president, secretary of war, and General Groves
- The scientific director was J Robert Oppenheimer, a relatively unproven Physics professor at UC Berkeley
- He also had little political connections, and was mainly chosen for his research
- There were a variety of installations in the project, over 50 all over the US including Los Alamos
- Cost about 26 billion in today's money
- Another important site was Oak Ridge, the Manhattan district HQ
- There were also sites in Canada and the UK
- The culmination of the project was the Trinity Test on July 16 1945

**Ethics**

- While Oppenheimer is representative of the deontological perspective, Edward Teller was on the other side of the coin
- Teller believed that the consequences greatly outweighed the means, and later went on to build the hydrogen bomb
- First use policies are pledges from governments to their public promising they would not be the first to use a nuclear bomb in a conflict
- Another ethical issue is the question of giving nuclear technology and materials to other countries
- When Canada gave nuclear reactor technology to India, are they responsible for India creating a nuclear bomb?
- Military pre-emption is another question, is it ethical to perform a strategic "first strike" in the event that a dangerous country gains nuclear weapons?
