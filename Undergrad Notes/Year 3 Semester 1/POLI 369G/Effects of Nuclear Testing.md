**The "Downwinders"**

- "Downwinders" are people and communities who were exposed to radiation due to nuclear testing and accidents
- The name comes from the fact that most tests at the time were atmospheric, and radioactive fallout would be carried by the wind to nearby communities
- Most nuclear testing in the US was done in Nevada, and prevailing winds tended to carry fallout to the northern midwest
- The test that exposed the most residents to fallout was the George test in 1952 that exposed around 7% of the population to radioactive fallout
- A cultural representation of this is a photo taken in Las Vegas in 1951 where you can clearly see a mushroom cloud in the distance
- Those exposed saw an increase in rates of thyroid cancer, leukemia, and certain tumors just to name a few
- However, people living by testing sites were told that there would be no adverse effects
- There were signs in the livestock, animals burned from radioactive grass, mutated livestock, etc.
- An example is the community of St George in Utah which was quite close to the Nevada test site and in the path of prevailing winds
- On May 19, 1953 the Harry test was detonated at the Nevada test site, later nicknamed "Dirty Harry" from the unusual amount of fallout it created
- At the time, a movie called "The Conqueror" was being filmed in the area, and had an unusual rate of cancer among its cast
- Out of the 220 cast members, over 30 died from cancer in the following years
- Notably, John Wayne and Susan Hayward were two of these victims
- Cancer rates were unusually high in the area as well
- Government continued to deny that this was happening, distributed leaflets to communities living nearby
- Leaflet concedes inconveniences, and claims that noone outside the test sites has been hurt from testing
- In 1990, the evidence of contamination came to light and the Radiation Exposure Compensation Act or Downwinders Act was created
- Downwinders were given $50,000, onsite personnel got $75,000, and uranium miners and transporters got $100,000
- As of 2010, $1.5 billion has been approved for 22,716 victims and their families

**Gender, Race, and Indigenous Peoples**

- On the gender front, recent research has confirmed that women are at a higher risk of radiation induced cancer compared to men
- This likely because of reception of radiation in the thyroid, breast, and ovaries
- A study concluded that women have a 48% higher radionuclide cancer mortality risk compared to men
- A more recent report concluded that women's risk from radiation exposure was 37.5% higher than men's
- On average, female biology is worse affected than men's from radiation
- Early opposition to testing was fuelled by mothers with concerns about increased radiation being found in breast milk
- For racial and indigenous perspectives, a good example is the Navajo uranium miners
- These miners suffered disproportionately high rates of fatal lung cancer, and have also had a disproportionately difficult time receiving compensation
- Poor record collection and discrimination against Navajo employees makes it harder to get proof these miners worked in the uranium mines
- In addition, the wives of most of these miners were married in unofficial ceremonies making it difficult for them to prove they qualify for compensation as well
- Outside of the continental US there are many other regions where testing occurred, especially the indigenous people of the Marshall Islands
- The Marshall Islands were a territory of the US from 1944-1979
- 66 tests were conducted at the "Pacific Proving Grounds" in the islands between 1946 and 1958
- The largest ever US nuclear test, Castle Bravo, happened here on March 1, 1954
- Highest yield achieved by the US nuclear program, 15 megatons
- Generated an unexpected amount of nuclear fallout, and an unexpected change in wind direction spread it in a direction that hadn't been cleared beforehand
- Radioactive cloud flew across several of the Marshall Islands
- Necessitated the evacuation of many islanders, and those who were able to return suffered radiation poisoning from contaminated coconut milk
- The islands were basically rendered uninhabitable, with none of the native people being warned this may happen beforehand
- A Japanese fishing vessel called the Lucky Dragon was also contaminated, killing one crew member from radiation sickness
- Scientists were quoted as using the native Rongelap people of the islands as basically human test subjects to study human uptake of radioactive contamination
