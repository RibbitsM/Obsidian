**Opposition to Testing**

- Nuclear testing is in many ways central to the discussion of nuclear weapons
- Connects to radiation impacts, arms control, proliferation, etc.
- First major public opposition to nuclear testing appeared in 1954
- Motive was to stop nuclear testing in light of mounting evidence of the effects of radioactive fallout on humans and the environment
- The Castle Bravo test was kind of the flashpoint as a case where a nuclear test was an international incident
- Provided an impetus for an anti nuclear weapons movement not just in the US, but worldwide
- Peace movements were especially prevalent in Japan, which united at the 1955 First World Conference Against Atomic and Hydrogen Bombs in Hiroshima
- The Japanese were widely opposed to nuclear testing in the Pacific and signed many petitions demanding ceasing of testing
- The book On the Beach by Nevil Shute created in 1957 was the first glimpse of the rising genre of post-nuclear apocalyptic fiction
- Chronicles the lives of US submarine officers who come ashore on the beaches of Australia and their experience watching the end of the world via massive radioactive cloud
- A turning point in the peace movement occurred in 1958, mostly thanks to Linus Pauling
- Pauling was a member of the Emergency Committee of Atomic Scientists and was a distinguished chemist, winning Nobel prizes for chemistry (1954) and peace (1962)
- Along with his wife, Ava Helen, Pauling wrote a petition to the UN in 1958 advocating for a worldwide ban on nuclear testing
- Evoked a poisoning of the entire world via nuclear testing
- Another significant event was the publication of the "Baby Tooth Survey" in 1961
- Demonstrated that nuclear testing had significant impacts on humans
- Children born in St Louis prior to 1963 had 50x higher levels of Strontium-90 in their teeth compared to children born in 1950
- Dr Louise Reiss was the cofounder of the St Louis Committee that carried out the survey
- Basically sent letters to parents asking for them to send in their children's teeth
- There was also widespread opposition to French nuclear testing, and organizations such as Greenpeace sailed vessels into test zones to force pauses
- There were boycotts of French products, strikes on shipping vessels, protests in Australia
- The Nevada Desert Experience was a series of demonstrations held at the Nevada Test Site which resulted in many arrests across many years
- The International Day Against Nuclear Tests is on August 29 and was established on December 2, 2009 by the UNGA
- This movement was evidently successful, the only nuclear tests in recent memory have been courtesy of North Korea

**Opposition to Nuclear War**

- There was much overlap between this and the testing movement and the anti-war movement in general, they all interacted and mixed heavily
- Opposition grew especially after the Cuban Missile Crisis, although there were movements advocating for disarmament before this
- Several anti-nuclear and peace organizations created, alongside ongoing information/protest campaigns about the dangers of nuclear war
- This was all wrapped up in a counter-culture movement of ideas, media, culture, etc.
- The idea of maintaining peace for the military was rooted in strength, peace through strength and deterrence was a very prevalent
- Military officers often did not see themselves as agents of war, but peace
- This was a huge hurdle that anti-nuclear war advocates had to overcome
- The objective is to raise awareness and change peoples minds about nuclear war
- Many of these efforts came in the form of visualizations of the consequences of nuclear war
- The Bulletin of Atomic Scientists came up with the Doomsday Clock, basically a representation of how close the world was to "midnight" or destruction
- The peace hand sign was also an icon of the movement, but of course not unique to the nuclear movement
- The current sign probably emerged in Belgium during WWII, popularized by Churchill and his V for Victory campaign
- Other media, many songs were written, and films created like Dr Strangelove
- Other examples are If You Love This Planet and The Day After (highest rated TV movie in history)
- Also an expanding body of literature on how language around nuclear weapons desensitized us to the reality of nuclear war

**Peace Movements**

- What we mean by peace movements are organized groups of concerned citizens coming together to protest government decisions on nuclear war and testing
- To start with we have the Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament, founded in the UK in 1957 and advocated for arms control treaties
- At the forefront of the UK peace movement since then
- The peace symbol, or the CND symbol was commissioned by this movement and was created by Gerald Holtom
- Made up of the semaphore signals for C and D surrounded by a circle, and is now synonymous with peace
- Women Strike for Peace was created in the US in 1961, marched in 60 cities against nuclear weapons and was the largest womens march in the 20th century
- The Nuclear Freeze Movement advocated for a mutual freeze on testing, deployment, production etc of all nuclear weapons and delivery systems
- Created in 1980 by peace researcher Randall Caroline Forsberg, and was a huge issue in the 1984 Presidential election, prompted by a massive rally in Central Park
- Eventually, the freeze never came to pass and the main candidate who supported it, Walter Mondale, lost to Reagan partly due to not supporting the freeze enough
- Greenpeace was founded in Kitsilano, Vancouver in 1969/70 and began life in opposition to nuclear testing off the course of the Aleutians
- International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War was founded in 1980 by physicians in both the US and USSR
- The organization was devoted to informing people on the effects of nuclear weapons on the grounds that doctors should prevent what they cannot treat
- Nuclear war would be the ultimate epidemic that would wipe out humanity
- There was also the Nuclear Weapons Free Zone Movement that basically established geographical areas where nuclear weapons are not allowed in
- For the most part, most of these zones have remained unviolated, including outer space and Antarctica
- A newer group is the International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons, formed by the IPPNW in 2007 and seek to negotiate a treaty banning nuclear weapons entirely
- There is also the Nuclear Security Project formed also in 2007 by Henry Kissinger, George Schulz, Sam Nunn, and William Perry
- These men were all former key parts of the US government and military, but are now together working to create a world free of nuclear weapons
- Global Zero is another new org that was formed in 2008 and calls for a phased reduction in nuclear arsenals that would ideally get to zero by 2030
