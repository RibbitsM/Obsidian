**First Arms Control Efforts**

- There was a period of time where it was possible that nuclear weapons would be banned entirely
- Understanding how this happened may help us replicate it today
- The UNAEC was established on January 24, 1946
- Created by the UN General Assembly
- Goal was to deal with the consequences of atomic energy being discovered
- Covers both nuclear weapons and nuclear power
- Included both US and USSR but was disbanded due to Cold War tensions
- Big part of why no significant gains were made
- First big effort was the Acheson-Lilienthal Report in 1946, formally known as the Report on the International Control of Atomic Energy
- Named after Secretary of State Dean Acheson and David Lilienthal, the chairman of the US atomic energy commission
- Driving force behind the plan was none other than Robert Oppenheimer
- Proposed that all fissile material would be held by a single international organization
- This agency would distribute material to countries that needed it
- Also suggested that the US should share info with the Soviets in exchange for a ban on atomic bomb development
- Revolutionary idea for the time, arguing the government should give up their nuclear monopoly
- The Truman administration was against giving up the monopoly, and also argued they should be able to inspect countries receiving fissile materials
- The Baruch Plan was a 1946 proposal based on the Acheson-Lilienthal report
- Named after Bernard Baruch, a financier and consultant who was extremely anti-Soviet who was in charge of revising the original report
- Proposed establishing a UN-run organization called the International Atomic Development Authority
- Allowed exchange of peaceful information exchange and control of nuclear power
- Sought to eliminate atomic weapons and weapons that could be adapted to WMDs, and establish regular inspections
- The US was willing to give up its weapons, but only if all other countries agree to the Baruch plan first
- Gromyko looked at the plan and opposed it immediately over concerns over US trying to hold onto a nuclear monopoly
- The Gromyko proposed that the US destroy it's weapons first, to which the US disagreed, killing the Baruch plan
- After this, the job of banning weapons fell to scientists
- Einstein and Szilard formed the Emergency Committee of Atomic Scientists to educate people about the dangers of atomic weapons
- Beginning of the anti-nuclear movement in the scientific community
- Many scientists involved with building the bomb were now working to ban it

**Nuclear Testing**

- An issue with counting nuclear tests comes with failed vs successful tests
- Does a test only count if a nuclear explosion happens?
- Basically does intent count or results
- Can also do subcritical tests where you stop right before an actual nuclear explosion
- We usually only count explosions
- Since 1945, there's been about 2056 nuclear explosions
- Since 1998 there's been 10, and since 2000 there's been 6 (all NK)
- About 25% were in the atmosphere
- Between 1945 and 1996 there was a nuclear test every 9 days on average
- Breaking it down, that’s 55 a year
- There were 178 tests in 1962, 96 by the US and 79 by the USSR
- Insane volume of nuclear testing
- Largest bomb ever tested was the Tsar Bomba on October 30, 1961 and was measured at 50+ megatons
- Over 60 different locations were used for testing
- Most countries stopped testing in the early 90s, with India and Pakistan being exceptions in 1998, and of course NK in 2017
- 4 different ways to test, on the ground, underground, atmospheric, and at sea
- After the Partial Test Ban treaty, the prevalence of atmospheric testing ended and underground tests became more common
- France continued to test aboveground for a bit, but then switched
- All Korean tests have been underground
- There was an unknown incident in 1979 called the Vela Incident
- A US Vela satellite picked up a bright flash on the Prince Edward Islands off the coast of South Africa
- This is believed to be a joint Israeli-South Africa test
- Raises concerns about policing test bans

**Why Test?**

- What are the motives behind nuclear testing?
- Some are technical, but most are political
- For technical reasons, you want to test no matter how crude or advanced your design
- Sometimes you just need to know if your design works, or to test it's efficiency so it can be improved
- Although simulations have improved, testing is the best way
- This goes double for countries testing new warhead designs
- States may also want to test the effect of weapons on different targets including buildings or military installations
- Tests can also be for peaceful reasons, several nuclear tests have been defined as "peaceful"
- One example is the Sedan test to see how much earth could be excavated with a nuclear device
- The bomb did create a very large crater, but radioactive side effects make nuclear excavation impractical
- For political reasons, a test is a public demonstration of a countries possession of nuclear weapons
- It also sends a signal, can remind other states of your deterrent capability
- Can also demonstrate an advancement in nuclear technology
- Testing supports negotiations, and some theorize this is the motive behind North Korea's tests
- Tests can be done in competition with another country, as with the US and Soviet Union and today with India and Pakistan
- Nuclear capability is also a status symbol and is associated with being considered a great power
- Can emphasize security autonomy, not needing to rely on extended deterrence
- An overlooked factor is domestic politics, a good example is India where military and scientific groups pushed hard domestically to test
