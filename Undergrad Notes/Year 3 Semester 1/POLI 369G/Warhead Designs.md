**Fission Bomb Designs**

- Three main designs
- Gun, implosion, and boosted fission
- Two main types of fissile material
- First is weapons grade uranium, meaning at least 90% uranium-235
- Natural uranium is less than 1% 235
- Reactor-grade uranium is enriched to about 3-4% 235
- This is very important since bombs can theoretically be made from enriched reactor grade uranium
- The next kind is plutonium-239 which has a much lower critical mass
- Critical mass is the amount of material you need for fission
- A tamper is used to reflect atoms back into your mass, which greatly reduces the critical mass amount
- With a thick tamper, you need about 15 kg of uranium and 5 kg of plutonium
- The gun assembly is the simplest design
- Two pieces of fissile material are placed at opposite ends of a tube
- An explosive is placed behind one, and an open ended tamper behind the other
- When the explosive fires, it launches the uranium into the second mass in the tamper, triggering criticality
- A beryllium disc is usually used as the source of atoms
- Little Boy was a gun type assembly
- This is a crude design, but simple and reliable
- The trinity test was an implosion device, the scientists were confident in the success of the gun type without even testing it
- Rarely used by the main powers, but easy and relatively inexpensive to build
- The implosion type uses a ball of material encased in a sphere of high explosives
- When the explosives are triggered, it squeezes and compresses the material into a critical mass which is triggered by a beryllium disc
- More difficult to build than gun types, but are more efficient
- They require less material, and result in a higher yield
- It is hypothesised that this is the kind of bomb that North Korea has
- Most of what we use today are boosted fission designs
- A small amount of fusion capable material is placed at the core of the device, surrounded by an implosion type fission bomb
- As the chain reaction happens, the gas (usually tritium or deuterium) undergoes fusion, creating helium
- This creates a large amount of neutrons that inundate the fissile material
- By doing this, we use up a lot more of the material in the bomb than usual
- Little Boy only used about 1% of the fissile material, Fat Man used 16%
- This results in a much higher yield and higher efficiency

**Nuclear Fusion**

- Most common nuclear weapon currently in use
- Exponentially more powerful than fission bombs
- Fusion occurs when two atoms fuse to create a single heavier element and huge amounts of energy
- This happens naturally in stars, where hydrogen and helium are fused
- On earth we usually use deuterium and tritium, two hydrogen isotopes
- When deuterium and tritium fuse, helium-4 is formed along with a free neutron and a significant amount of energy
- We don't know the exact details of how a fusion bomb is built since it is top secret information and not publically available
- First H bombs were built on the Teller-Ulam design
- Leading proponent was Edward Teller himself, often called the father of the hydrogen bomb, or as he called it, the Super
- Stanislaw Ulam was the other half of the team that designed the bomb
- The calculations required to make the bomb were ridiculous, and Ulam contributed significantly to simplifying the calculations by hand
- There are two 'stages' in the Teller-Ulam design, with the primary stage being an boosted or implosion fission bomb
- The secondary stage is physically separated in the casing and is a plutonium rod inside a depleted uranium tamper with a lithium-deuterium fuel surrounding the rod
- Both stages are suspended inside a kind of styrofoam designed to liquidate when heated
- When the fission device is detonated, it creates x rays that turn the styrofoam into plasma, exerting pressure on the fuel and rod
- The shockwave and pressure initiate fission in the plutonium rod, emanating radiation, heat, and neutrons into the fuel
- These neutrons fuse with the deuterium to create tritium atoms, which then fuse with the remaining deuterium atoms
- When these new fusions send out even more neutrons, they trigger fission in the depleted uranium casing, despite it not being U-235
- There are actually three processes going on, the initial fission, then the fusion, and finally the fission of the depleted uranium
- Hypothetically more fusion stages could be added to the bomb ad infinitum for a theoretically infinite explosive yield, but this is constrained by size
- The largest bomb ever detonated with a 50 megaton yield, the Tsar Bomba, was likely a triple stage fusion bomb
- The first test of an H bomb was on Nov 1, 1952
- It was codenamed Ivy Mike, and had a yield of 10.4 megatons
- The fireball was 4.8 kilometers in diameter

**Fusion and Testing**

- Many terms for the hydrogen bomb, also called the super, or thermonuclear weapons/bombs
- The development of the fusion bomb was fuelled by the context of the early cold war, and the threat posed by the Soviet Union
- The first Soviet nuclear test was on Aug 29, 1949, marking the end of the nuclear monopoly
- Very few people were aware of the existence of the hydrogen bomb
- Dean Acheson and Louis Johnson were two of the most prominent proponents of the hydrogen bomb
- The motive was a fear that the Soviets would build the bomb first and a desire to maintain containment and US nuclear superiority
- The main scientific proponent of the hydrogen bomb was of course Edward Teller, but it was widely opposed among scientists
- It was considered by many to be a weapon of genocide
- Fermi did advocate for it, but only to be a catalyst for arms control efforts
- Teller argues that it is the duty of science to do things that can be done, and scientists should not have such influence on public affairs
- Ivy Mike was detonated on Elugelab Island on the Enewatok Atoll
- The project required over 11,000 military and civilian personnel to achieve the test
- The blast effectively demolished the entire island of Elugelab, reducing it to a massive crater
- The Soviets acquired the hydrogen bomb in 1953, UK in 57, China in 67, France in 68, and Israel and India both potentially have hydrogen bombs
