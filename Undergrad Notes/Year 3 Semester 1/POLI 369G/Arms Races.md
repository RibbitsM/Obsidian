**Security Dilemma**

- Fundamental concept of global politics, defined by John Herz
- Happens when states attempt to increase their security and this is perceived as aggression from others
- This leads the other states to build up arms as well, repeating the cycle
- No real threat, just perceived
- Each state defines own measures as defensive and measures of others as offensive
- Rooted in realist assumptions about global anarchy and self-help
- In a case with two states, one builds up arms for defense
- The other responds by matching the buildup
- This leads to both sides trying to gain the arms advantage to ensure their own security, creating an arms race
- In the end, both states end up less secure since both sides have spent so much to increase their armies
- Basically, attempts to increase your own security are futile
- Also, an arms race between two states can spread through a whole internationals system

**Cold War Arms Race**

- In terms of numbers, the US started with more warheads than the USSR, given they discovered it first
- Over time, the Soviets accelerated warhead production as the US rationalized their arsenal and started to remove some of their older warheads
- By the 1980s the Soviets had substantially more than the US
- At the end of the cold war they had 37,000 warheads, almost twice as many as the US who had almost 22,000
- In the aftermath, these numbers decreased dramatically as bilateral disarmament took place
- A similar trend exists when measuring in terms of megatonnage, with the US fluctuating in numbers and the USSR constantly growing
- The final results were 7,100 for the US and 13,000 for the USSR
- For delivery systems, by the time the 70s and 80s rolled around, both countries had settled on the amount they wanted
- ICBMs were around 1000 for the US and 1300 for USSR
- About 600 subs for US, 900 for USSR
- 350 US bombers, 130 USSR bombers
- Domestic politics had a lot of influence on the arms race
- In the US and USSR, propaganda campaigns emphasized the evil of the other side, and fear of falling behind was present
- This is how politicians were able to lobby so much support for buildup
- Especially notable in the US, with the Missile Gap being a standout example
- This was a talking point about how the USSR had more ICBMs
- Used to justify more investment in ICBMs and was based on the US National Intelligence Estimate made in 1957
- This predicted that the USSR had very large numbers of ICBMs, 700 by the most ambitious estimate and 200/400 on the lower ends
- This was a huge overestimation, and the US knew this because of their U2 spy planes not finding many ICBM silos in the 60s
- Khrushchev talked about ICBMs as if they had hundreds, but in reality they probably had about 4
- Exaggeration of the threat was a huge theme of the time
- A publication called Soviet Military Power was a great example
- Always presented the worst-case scenario as most likely

**Military Industrial Complex**

- This was potentially the work of the military industrial complex
- The idea of the MIC was pioneered by Eisenhower
- He coined the term in a speech warning about the rise of the arms industry and concern for a misplaced rise of power
- Worried that it may end up driving foreign policy
- Something called the Iron Triangle of the MIC
- Congress, the military, and industry are the three cooperative points
- The theory goes that these three elements work together to keep the gears of war turning and defense spending rising
- Industry gets the money and grants, congress gets the public support they want, and the military gets the equipment they want

**Arms Races Today**

- The main nuclear arms race going on today is in South Asia
- India and Pakistan are at the center, but China is a player too
- India and Pakistan have a long history of fighting since WWII, and have fought three wars since then
- China is a staunch ally of Pakistan, and has an ongoing rivalry with India
- Both countries have been building up their warheads and delivery devices consistently
- Currently India has about 54 mid range missiles, 7 air cruise missiles, and one submarine with four SLBMs
- They also have more missiles in development including more cruise missiles and ICBM-range missiles
- Currently most Indian ballistic missiles reach Pakistan, and most of China
- Their workhorse, the Agni III, is road mobile and can reach up to 5000 km away
- Pakistan has about 165 warheads and no ranges exceeding 5500 km, but mostly mid range missiles alongside some strike aircraft
- SLCMs and GLCMs in development
- Arsenal reaches all of India and China
- Mainstay is the Ghauri, range up to 1800 km, and again it is road mobile
- Pakistan is also developing an MIRV
- India also is looking at MIRV tech, and are developing cruise missiles with Russia
- India may have "canisterization" technology which reduces launch times to mere minutes by permanently mating missile with warhead
- This is done by storing and transporting missiles in climate controlled containers
- The most likely reason for a nuclear war breaking out is escalation
- Both have fought conventional wars several times before, so it is reasonable to think that it may happen again
- In this event, a traditional escalation situation may occur
- Remember, flight times are extremely short, only about 7 minutes
- This leaves very little room for error in a crisis
