- The targeting committee had six options
- The first was to use it as a tactical weapon to assist the invasion
- Second was to use it as a demonstration, inviting Japanese officials to witness the bomb being detonated in a safe location
- The third and fourth were to use it on a military target, which were in short supply either with or without warning
- The fifth was to drop it on a city but give a warning first via leaflets, and the sixth was what was eventually chosen, dropping it on a city without warning
- This option was chosen for maximum spectacle, was not just related to the surrender of Japan, but also what would be signalled to the rest of the world
- The recommendation was that the target would be a war plant, but populated by civilians and that no prior warning should be given
- This violates the basic principles of the Geneva Convention, but of course this was before it came into force
- There was a conscious decision to target civilians rather than soldiers
- The science panel eventually came to the decision that a demonstration would not be sufficient, and the bomb should be used in a military context
- One argument against the demonstration was that the US only had two atomic bombs
- If one was used for the demonstration, then they would only have one remaining for tactical use
    
    - There was opposition to dropping the bombs on Japan
    - July 1945, signed by 155 Manhattan Project scientists
    - Asked President Truman to show a demonstration of the bomb’s power first BEFORE using it against people
        
        - The mere threat of using such weapons would force Japan to surrender

- Use as a tactical weapon to assist in the invasion of Japan
- Use as a demonstration before observers (would include Japanese government)
- Use as a demonstration against a military target
- Use against a military target
- Use against a city with warning
- Use against a city without warning

The Interim Committee (June 1, 1945)

- War plant surrounded by workers homes
- Would violate basic principles of Geneva Protocols (discrimination and proportionality)

The Scientific Panel:

- No acceptable alternative to direct military use
- Informed the targeting committee was not as nuanced and agreed that a demonstration could not bring an end to the war

**Initial Radiation**

- Radiation injuries were caused by instantaneous penetrating radiation from the nuclear explosions. All of these effective radiations occurred during the first minute of the explosions and nearly all occurred during the first second of the explosions.
- Gamma rays, x-rays

Heat (Thermal Radiation)

- Temperatures at the hypocenter of the Hiroshima explosion reached 2,982 degrees C (5,400 F
- Temperatures at the hypocenter of the Nagasaki explosion reached 3,900 degrees C (7,050 F)
    
    - The heat is at the centre of the survivor narrative (can exceed temperatures on surface of the sun)
    - The heat created massive and horrific burns
 
Blast

- The Hiroshima bomb blast moved at 3200 metres per second out to a distance of several hundred metres, then slowed to the speed of sound, 342 metres per second 
- The Nagasaki bomb blast generated winds estimated at 1,005 km/h (624 mph)
    
    - A vacuum is created at the centre of the explosion (double blast)
 ![Exported image](Exported%20image%2020250409135211-5.png)  

Residual Radiation (Fallout)

- “Fallout” is the residual radioactive material propelled into the atmosphere by a nuclear blast, which falls back to earth in the form of irradiated particles of ash and debris

Death and Destruction

- At Hiroshima, total deaths are estimated at between 90,000-140,000 people
    
    - Over a 5 year period, the death toll is estimated at 200,000 people
- At Nagasaki, casualty estimates for immediate deaths ranges from 40,000-75,000 with total deaths by the end of 1945 reaching 80,000

Ionizing Radiation and Cell Damage

- Ionized molecules are unstable and quickly undergo chemical changes
- DNA is sensitive to ionizing radiation. Ionizing radiation can lead to a mutation in cell DNA, which can kill the cell. The death of a sufficient number of cells will seriously damage an organ, which may cause death
- If mutated cells survive and are not repaired, the resulting mutations will be passed on to further cells through DNA replication and may eventually lead to cancer
- Although the process of cellular changes from radiation takes a fraction of a second, changes such as the development of cancer may take years
- Exposure to nuclear explosion related radiation can also come from contact with residual radiation from fallout, through consumption of contaminated, vegetation, water
