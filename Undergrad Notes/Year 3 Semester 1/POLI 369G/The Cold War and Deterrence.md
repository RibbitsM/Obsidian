**The Soviet Bomb**

- The detonation of the soviet bomb was one of the "two shocks" of the early cold war
- The other being the communist revolution in China
- The US had accepted that keeping the bomb a secret forever was not viable
- Through science or espionage, it would eventually spread
- The estimate was about 5-10 years for the soviets to build the bomb
- Soviet nuclear science was much more advanced than many thought
- In addition, there were several soviet spies in the Manhattan Project
- Spies were in multiple aspects of the project, including the science and engineering sides
- The most famous were the Rosenbergs who were later executed for passing secrets to the Soviets
- The Soviets had not conducted any research early on due to the war with Germany
- George Flyorov noticed a lack of articles on nuclear fission in Western journals, suggesting a secret fission bomb
- Again, the main motivator was that the other side would build the bomb first
- Igor Kurchatov was the head of the project, alongside Andrei Sakurov
- After the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the project was accelerated
- This evolved the project's urgency to match the US's new power
- Prevent atomic diplomacy and abuse of the atomic monopoly
- The first test was the RDS-1 on August 29, 1949 in modern Kazakhstan

**Origins of Deterrence**

- Initially, nuclear weapons were seen as just more powerful conventional explosives
- By 1946 the US had realized that nuclear weapons command should be separated from the traditional air force
- This is how the Strategic Air Command was created
- It was recognized that nuclear weapons would change the face of war
- An early form of deterrence was Massive Retaliation
- A conventional attack could be responded to with a devastating nuclear strike
- Therefore the conventional army was basically going to be obsolete
- In future of war militaries should be restructured to be less vulnerable to tactical nuclear weapons
- Curtis Lemay was one of the "nuclear paladins" whose military careers in WWII expanded into the Cold War
- He was placed as the head of the Strategic Air Command
- General Thomas Power was another of these provocative proponents of unrestrained use of nuclear weapons
- A lot of his colleagues were concerned about him, especially the fact that he had so much access to weapons systems
- By the mid 60s, the yield of nuclear weapons grew alongside warhead numbers
- It became increasingly clear that there would be no "winning" a nuclear war
- Both countries had the capability to raze every major city within their territory
- This was to the extent that planning for protecting civilians was futile, and only done for appearances
- It became clear that nuclear weapons were not to be used militarily, but rather as a political tool to avoid being nuked yourself
- There were later conversations around using them militarily in Korea and Vietnam, but deterrence still dominated the culture

**Deterrence Theory**

- Simply put, deterrence is the policy of discouraging an action by presenting an opponent with massive risk
- Stops unwanted actions before they occur
- Several types of deterrence, the first being deterrence by denial
- In this type, an actor is deterred because it is convinced it cannot achieve it's objectives
- This appears in things like fortification, if a defense is strong enough then most attackers won't even bother trying to crack it
- Nuclear deterrence is deterrence by punishment
- This is where the actor is deterred due to the threat of "unacceptable damage"
- Two main strategies, direct and extended
- This just means whether you expand this threat to other states or not
- For example, the US extended deterrence to Western Europe during the Cold War
- Conceptually, deterrence is very easy to achieve, since by the mid 60s, both sides realized that both countries would be obliterated in a nuclear war
- Britain struggled with this however, many doubted that the British arsenal was threatening enough to deter the Soviets
- Not enough to destroy the USSR completely, but maybe enough for "unacceptable damage"
- Another component is not the cost incurred, but the risk that this cost will be incurred
- Basically, you need to have an arsenal that can 100% ensure unacceptable damage, not just the minimum amount
- This idea that deterrence is actually difficult to achieve is what drove the US and Soviet Union to build their arsenals so large
- In practice, a nuclear deterrent will only work if a country has assured retaliatory capability
- No matter what happens, the country will always be able to respond with devastating force

**Deterrence in Practice**

- How did the theory of deterrence become embedded into political and military systems?
- In addition, deterrence did not go away with the end of the Cold War
- Question for how to get Nuclear Force Posture is how a country can achieve assured retaliatory capability
- In this situation we have two nuclear states, country A and country B, and their only delivery system is bombers
- These are not neighbouring countries
- The force posture is that both have bombers, and lets say A goes to attack B
- First of all, the air defences of country B will try to shoot down the bombers
- At the same time, country B will retaliate by sending it's bombers to country A
- Results in an aerial nuclear war where each side is hoping that their bombers will be able to inflict unacceptable damage
- Not a very stable situation, air defense means that either country may think that they can get away with a first strike
- Let's try this example again, but add ICBM silos
- Country A goes to attack again, this time with it's ICBMs
- If these ICBMs connect, country B could lose all of it's silos in the blast, leaving them with only their bombers
- The, deterrence is reliant on country B's ability to penetrate country A's air defence
- Deterrence is more stable since even if all land based missiles are destroyed, the bombers are still there
- But there is still the possibility that one country could get away with a first strike
- In addition, with good enough RADAR country B can launch on warning, meaning if any missiles are seen incoming, ICBMs are launched
- However this is prone to error and very dangerous
- Finally, lets examine this example with SLBMs added
- Here each country has a nuclear triad and can attack from land air or sea
- In this situation each country can be confident in their retaliatory capability
- If country A launches it's ICBMs, instead of launching on warning, country B can launch it's SLBMs or bombers
- This would cause unacceptable damage, between remaining ICBMs and surviving bombers, alongside the almost guaranteed SLBMs
- If both countries have assured retaliatory capability, meaning a nuclear triad or just SLBMs, both will receive unacceptable damage
- This situation was dubbed Mutually Assured Destruction, or MAD
- Today, the US and Russia are still in this situation, if the US launched ICBMs today, Russia could still retaliate with unacceptable damage
- Another more concerning example is India and Pakistan
- Not only is the political relationship between the two fragile, but deterrence between the two is not as developed as in the US-Russia relationship

**Deterrence Today**

- China significantly building up nuclear weapons
- Ongoing tensions between US, Russia, and China
- Deterrence between India and Pakistan, particularly volatile relationship
- Many conventional wars fought and multiple crises
- Especially worrying is their close proximity, only 7 minutes of flight time between launch and detonating on the neighbouring countries capital city
- Temptation of a first strike is great
- Buildup of nuclear weapons in UK and France
- In the middle east, Israel possesses nuclear weapons which could be opposed by a nuclear-armed Saudi Arabia or even Iran
- Especially given the fact that Hezbollah recently joined the Israel-Palestine conflict, the possibility of Iran possessing nuclear weapons is very relevant
- Of course, North Korea is always a concern and will only grow their arsenal as time goes on
- Deterrence could potentially be destabilized by new technological advances like hypersonic glide
- Faster and stealthier delivery systems increase the temptation of a first strike
