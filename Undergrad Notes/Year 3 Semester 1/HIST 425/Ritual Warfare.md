- There is an entire kind of warfare that seems to appear in several societies where little killing is actually done, and seems to serve no purpose
- Some have argued that this is simply an early stage of warfare, and later develops into full war
- Yet others argue that in the absence of strategy and tactics and given the restraints and rules of ritual war, this should not even be defined as war
- There is also the idea that all warfare is ritualistic, and there is no distinction
- Definitions of ritual warfare and very difficult to come across
- One of the best is by Nathaniel Levtow, "the orchestration of strategic violence in ritualised social environments"
- There is real violence happening, but it is not wanton destruction
- Also given advanced warning and happens at a set location or time
- More as a signal of domination than anything
- Can also be seen as an alternative to full scale warfare, or preceding it
- There is an overarching environment for these conflicts, rules, symbols and acts agreed on by both sides

**What is Ritual?**

- Sequences of formal words and acts that have some sort of performative function
- Repetitive, choreographed, and governed by rules
- Rituals are not subjective, there is a proper way to do them
- Very symbolic, and unify social structures
- Examples in the military are bugle calls, blessing weapons, saluting, and marching
- Ritualized aggression is not exclusive to humans at all, warnings are a common signal in the animal kingdom
- A good example is the Maori Haka, which was initially a show of dominance before combat, but has evolved to be more an act of respect
- Another example is the Q'ero Tupay Tuqtu, a festival in Peru where the population goes to an agreed location and fights
- Occasionally people have died, and shedding blood is actually encouraged
- An example in Rome would be the gladiators
- The word for gladiator in Rome was Munera, which translates to something like duty, funeral games were held when a family member died
- A potential modern equivalent is the Korea Joint Security Area with it's many symbols, rules, and competitions

**War as Sacrifice**

- What matters is not the results of the war but the act of killing itself
- Can either be war as self-sacrifice, or sacrificing enemies
- War has been associated with sacrifice for thousands of years, as early as 1600 BCE
- "great affairs of the kingdom lie in sacrifice and warfare. In sacrifices one receives meat from the temple"
- Quote from ancient chinese manuscript
- What we call the Aztec were actually called the Mexihcah
- Migrated from a potentially mythical place called Aztlan to central Mexico
- Settled around Chapultepec as a client of a largest group called the Tepanec
- Soon they were expelled and took refuge at Lake Texcoco
- What would become the island city of Tenochtitlan began as nothing but swampland and a few islands
- The Mexihcah pioneered the process of building islands called chinampas
- The city of Tenochtitlan was built on top of these chinampas, with canals filling in for roads
- Extraordinary feat of engineering, surpassed the glory of Venice
- Formed an alliance with two neighbouring city states, Tetzcoco and Tlacopan
- Together, the triple alliance conquered the Tepanec and formed a system of allied tribute states
- There was a clear class structure, with an emperor (Tlatoani), hereditary nobility (Tecuhtli), lesser nobility who earned their positions (Pilli), the merchants, and a large commoner class plus many slaves
- The emperor also had a right hand, the Snake Woman (Cihuacoati) who was actually a man dressed as a woman who carried out foreign policy duties
- Cities were divided into Calpulli's or great houses, which were essentially neighbourhoods

**Mexica Religion**

- Large pantheon of gods with many diverse aspects
- Complex religious system, priests had to go through a complicated education process and become literate
- Cyclical view of history, believed this world to be the fifth iteration
- New worlds were created or revived through the gods self sacrifice
- Gods had created the world through immense suffering, and they owed their lives to them
- Therefore the gods needed constant nourishment to keep the world running
- Usually this nourishment took the form of blood
- Many mesoamerican cultures believed in the importance of blood
- Everyone was expected to make sacrifices, big and small
- Some sacrifices were volunteers, some were forced, but all were treated with immense respect
- Usually dressed as gods and treated as such until the moment of their deaths
- Huitzilopochtli was one of the most important gods, representation of the Mexihcah people and god of war and the sun
- In Mexihcah religion, he was a bastard child and his mother was to be killed by his half-sister to preserve the family's honour, to which Huitzilopochtli leapt from her womb fully grown and slaughtered his half sister
- War was foundational to Mexihcah society, and the overall purpose of the state
- No permanent standing army but all males received military training, and for some their main occupation (not only) was war
- When war broke out, all calpulli's were responsible for raising the largest army possible
- Economy of Tenochtitlan supported by an empire of conquered tribute states
- War appears frequently in Mexihcah art and literature, either being glorified or accepted as a given part of life
- Mobility within classes was almost entirely reliant on success in war
- You had the option of not fighting in war, but then you would have no chance to improve your position
- Worth of warriors was measured in captives captured
- The greatest afterlife was reserved for sacrificial victims, casualties of war, and women dying in childbirth
- This was because childbirth was seen as being similar to war, in fact the bodies of childbirth victims would be looted for war tokens and had to be guarded
- Nobles were divided into different tiers of warriors with their own vows and armour
- For example, the Jaguar warriors had to pledge to never take a step back in battle
- Traditional equipment for war was a sword-club with obsidian edges, a wicker shield, and cotton armour
- This armour was designed to prevent slashing attacks from obsidian clubs, but was effectively worthless against steel and muskets

**Flowery Wars**

- Within Mexihcah territory there were several isolated states that were surrounded by the Mexihcah but not tributes
- The Mexihcah were capable of conquering these states, but kept them around for the purpose of the "Flowery Wars"
- These were a series of small scale battles where the only focus was capturing prisoners
- These wars were routine and scheduled, and were sources of prestige
- Allegedly the origin was an event where the Mexihcah required a steady supply of many human sacrifices
- Therefore they decided to designate a battle in a specific time and place with no territorial goals, just for sacrifice
- The victims of these regular battles were captured, carried to the home state, sacrificed and flayed
- In 1476, the leader of the Mexihcah wanted to create a new Sun Stone, an ornate carving with religious purposes
- To consecrate it, human sacrifices were required so they proposed a flower war with the Tlliluhquitepec
- Met at a preset location that was prepared with images of the gods
- No projectiles were used, and was fought hand to hand by all volunteers
- In the end the Mexihcah captured 700, and the Tliluhquitepec captured 420
- Afterwards, the leader came over to congratulate the Mexihcah and wished them safe passage home
- Very strange for a "war"
- One suggested reason for this was to satiate the incredible need for human sacrificial victims
- Some estimates put this as up to 250,000 sacrifices per year
- This could not be satisfied from volunteers alone, captives from other groups were needed
- While sacrifices were acquired from traditional conquests, there were advantages from getting them from the flowery wars
- First of all, the victims were supposed to be in good shape, which was hard when they had to walk all the way from a battlefield on the fringes of Mexihcah territory
- In addition there was no territory at risk, the worst that could happen was losing a few men
- Others argue that the flower wars existed for the purpose of military training and a place to send restless warriors
- It has also been suggested that the Mexihcah actually could not conquer these territories at will and the flower wars were for purposes of attrition
- For example, the Tlaxcala were firmly entrenched in their mountain territories, and had significant fortifications
- A less desirable school of thought was that these were a method of gaining protein, since there was little protein in the Mexihcah diet
- In addition, cannibalism was a common practice in rituals relating to the flower wars
- However, most have debunked this as being merely for "communion" since the amounts of flesh eaten were very small and infrequent
- It is also possible that there was genuinely no rational reason for the flower wars, or at least not that we would understand
- Despite no clear goals existing by our standards, the Mesoamericans saw the flower wars as real warfare with real consequences through sacrifice
