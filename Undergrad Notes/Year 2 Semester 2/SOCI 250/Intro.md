- Criminology is the scientific study of criminal behaviour, prevention, and punishment/rehabilitation
- Crime is a fully socially constructed concept that refers to behaviours that need to be controlled or corrected
- They are relative (different in time and space) and evolutive (constantly changing)
- The study of criminology seeks to understand the reasons why acts are defined as deviant or criminal, and to understand the effects of these changes
- In Canada, all infractions of the law are called crimes and the term offence is specific
- In other places, offenses are less serious violations of morals while crimes violate laws
- Laws focus on conventional crime mainly (break ins, robbery, etc.) and less on non conventional crimes like organized crime, terrorism, and white collar crime
- Laws evolve, criminal acts are not always criminal
- People cannot be predisposed to crime if the definition of crime is relative and variable
- Anything that has to do with deviance and its consequences
- Scientific study means that we follow the scientific method and take a hypothesis and experiment based approach to crime
- Strict procedure, appropriate sample and controlled variables
- The purpose is to understand how and why crime occurs
- Huge field that can be divided into many different subfields
- Statistics is a big one, collecting data about crime is a fast growing field
- Another one is sociology of law, which is popular with people in politics and law
- Examines the effects of policy change on society
- A good example is how chinatowns were created across Canada and the US as a result of the Chinese Exclusion Act
- Constructing theories of crime is another subfield, called etiology
- Etiology is when you study the source or causes of phenomena
- Seeks to understand why crime happens
- The three main branches of etiology are the biological, psychological, and sociological aspects of crime
- The biology branch includes discussions about the genetics of criminal behaviour, does heredity cause deviance?
- The psychology branch examines how cognitive processes can lead to crime
- Mental issues are often cited as the reasons for crime, especially mass killings
- A big concern of criminology is creating psychological profiles for criminals
- In terms of sociology, we examine the social structures that create environments for crime
- What role does environment and the arrangement of society have in creating crime?
- Economics is also considered in the causes of crime, making connections between unemployment rates/recessions and crime rates
- The environment and geography of crime are also considered, and are useful for finding prevention methods
- Penology is another subfield that examines the penal system, prisons and other punishments for crime
- Statistics play a big part in penology and so does finance, Canada doesn't have privatized prisons and therefore sends less criminals to jail since its so expensive
- Alternatives such as restorative justice is growing, especially in places like Canada
- Victimology is closely associated, and examines the victims of crime and how to integrate them back into society
- Criminology is integrated and interdisciplinary, it spans many aspects that combine together to create one whole
- Some scholars have attempted to break down crime into categories of behaviour
- Today we group crimes based on the act committed, but previous efforts would divide into types of criminals, based on motive

**How to Define Crime**

- The definition of what is and is not criminal is often variable
- Even when a crime is committed, the punishment is also frequently debated
- There are three main approaches to defining crime
- The legal approach is the most simple approach, and states that an act is either criminal or not
- Binary approach based on criminal law, if you violate a law you have committed a crime
- Depends on age and severity in Canada, the criminal code only applies if you're over 18, and involves indictable and summary offenses
- 12-17 year olds are covered under the Youth Criminal Justice Act
- Different consequences, involves the parents and much more counselling
- Under 12 there are no laws, because of this some groups use children to commit crimes since they cannot be punished
- Not all bad acts are covered legally, prior to 9/11 terrorism was not mentioned in the Criminal Code despite clearly being criminal
- The statistical approach to crime is based on the statistic distribution of good/bad acts
- Extremely good acts draw attention, as do bad acts
- The vast majority of good acts are considered normal, and draw no attention
- Below a certain point, acts are considered bad enough to be a violation of the law
- When we evaluate acts, those that are out of the norm are considered deviant
- This depends on social norms, which are dictated by culture
- Has nothing to do with the laws of society, except when laws influence culture
- The multi-dimensional approach is a combination of the legal and statistical approaches

**Hagan's Pyramid**

- Under the multidimensional approach there are three main dimensions, like a pyramid
- The first is social consensus, what percentage of people agree that an act is wrong
- The second is perceived harmfulness, the perception of the damage an act does to society
- Actual harmfulness is less relevant, perception has more of an effect e.g. Cigarettes being harmful but not perceived as such
- The final dimension is the severity of the response
- With consensus crimes, most people agree that they are wrong, they are perceived as very harmful, and call for an extreme response
- These make up the tip of the pyramid, and include things in the criminal code like murder and kidnapping
- Conflict crime makes up the next step down and have less consensus, are still largely seen as harmful, and have disagreements about punishment
- An example of conflict crime would be prostitution, vandalism or drug possession
- Consensus and conflict crimes are criminal, meaning they are regulated and enforced by the govt
- When you move acts up the pyramid you are criminalizing them, moving down is decriminalizing
- The second step from the bottom is called social deviation
- Mesh of statistical and legal approach, not necessarily illegal but definitely unusual
- No full consensus, somewhat harmful, and there are debates about whether to focus on punishment or rehabilitation
- A good example of social deviation is swearing, it's not really illegal but has social consequences
- A&F used to exclusively hire fit white people as employees and were sued over discrimination
- Advertisers still hire based on racial categories to fit an image, but it isn't illegal, just strange from an outside perspective
- The bottom of the pyramid is social diversion
- An example of this is having tattoos, they were originally associated with religion (mostly polytheistic ones)
- With the rise of Christianity, stigma against tattoos rose because it was considered a mark that you were not Christian
- Tattoos are still considered social diversion, but not as much historically, tattoos are still a barrier to employment in many sectors for optics reasons
- Social diversion isn't really harmful, there are many disagreements on whether its wrong, and the punishments are often social and informal
- Crimes are legally regulated, deviances are socially regulated and the multidimensional approach combines them both
- Consensus and conflict crimes are covered by the CJS, social deviation is covered by other laws (anti-discrimination laws), social diversion is covered by social consequences and institutions
- Hagan's pyramid also helps us see definitions change over time as the same acts move up and down the pyramid, for example having tattoos used to be incredibly serious, violates 10 commandments

**Limitations**

- Hagan's pyramid assumes all three dimensions are connected and agree with each other
- There are crimes that are very harmful but aren't punished, like killing someone during a war
- Also crimes that are not very harmful but are punished severely
- There is also the assumption that democracy is present and the laws are determined by public will
- The perception of harm is dependent on the visibility of the harm, public awareness is necessary
- Harms may exist, but they don't have a visible effect people are aware of
- Advocacy increases perceived harmfulness, can lead to acts being moved up the pyramid
- The extent of victimization is also an issue, the numbers of people harmed is important
- Isolated cases may not draw attention, but a pattern of similar cases might (ex. #MeToo movement)

**Perspectives**

- There are a couple main perspectives around social control
- The first is the functionalist perspective, which prioritizes the secure integration of people into society
- The goals are to maintain social order and promote social stability for the benefit of society
- Social control has the function of maintaining stability and order
- Social control also has economic benefits, a good example is the issue of homelessness in cities
- Homelessness is a humanitarian problem, but also an economic one
- The presence of homeless people in a commercial area lowers business taxes, income taxes, and property taxes in the surrounding area
- This motivates the government to deal with it, in the same way that maintaining order does
- This is called the conflict perspective, dictates that the purpose of social control is to control the powerless and preserve social hierarchy
- Other examples of this are trespassing and loitering laws and inflated luxury condo prices
- The conflict perspective sees laws as a way for the powerful to exert control

**Textbook**

- Three main conditions to deter criminal offenders
- Certainty of punishment, swiftness of justice, and proportionate penalties
- Cesare Beccaria also defined four general principles
- Equality: all offenders treated equally, no individual punishment, judge by societal harm
- Liberty: Only the law can punish criminals, the state can't abuse power
- Utilitarianism: The goal is happiness for the most people, laws should instill fear in potential offenders, not specifically punish those who offend
- Humanitarianism: fair and humane punishment, nothing cruel
- Legal punishment has many different effects, apart from potential deprivation of liberty, it also hurts image and future opportunities
- Positivism is the attempt to create rational and scientific answers to social questions
- Cesare Lombroso attempted to connect physiological differences with criminal activity, similar to phrenology
- However, the more likely explanation is that those with unusual features were socially excluded and committed crimes due to the exclusion
- Chicken or the egg type problem
- Determinist approaches often encounter several issues
- They usually focus only on conventional crime, their applications can be totalitarian, and the methodology is often weak

**Schools of Thought**

- The classical school emerged in Europe, as a response to the harsh treatment of criminals
- Fuelled by the processes of the enlightenment to make criminal justice more humane
- Cesare Beccaria was the leading figure of this movement
- Embraced free will, set out the three conditions and four principles
- Influenced the American constitution and thinkers like Jeremy Bentham (utilitarianism)
- Basis for many North American legal systems
- The main idea of the classical school is that if fair and reasonable punishments are established for offences and delivered equally and swiftly, the fear of punishment will deter offenders.
- Positivism emerged in Italy in the nineteenth century, coined by Augustus Comte
- Seeks to create a scientific explanation of criminal behaviour
- Pioneered mainly by Cesare Lombroso
