**Ways of Knowing**

- Rationalism is truth through reasoning or logic
- Follows classical logic statements like all blank are blank and if-then statements
- Empiricism is truth through observation
- Not necessarily evidence based, but more truth through lived experience and perception
- When thinking about whether a place has higher crime, you can reason through it logically, or observe it and make a decision that way
- Authority can inform our perceptions of crime, as can the decisions and actions of people around us (conformity)

**Agents of Socialization**

- Socialization is the process by which you are integrated into a culture
- Your family is the first agent of socialization, teaches simple things like greetings
- The next agent is education, through school and non-fiction sources like textbooks
- Eventually education and your peers becomes the primary agent of socialization, and the influence of your parents wanes
- Mass media is an agent of socialization, and so is the state
- Mass media is different because it is self-selective, it is chosen by you
- The feedback loop of media consumption creates your identity through what you consume
- The state is referred to as an "invisible agent", meaning it doesn't really have a direct effect, but it controls other agents of socialization like the media and education
- These agents of socialization shape our definitions of what is deviant, and also shape how we perceive crime and deviance
- Socialization creates the structure, the framework of what is acceptable and we have free will to choose where we are within that framework
- However, we do not truly have free will since we are still constrained by what is acceptable

**Media Effect on Crime Perception**

- People's perceptions of the world are greatly influenced by mass media
- Especially in terms of crime, perceptions of crime are heavily influenced by media
- Since reporting on crime is rather difficult for non-official or government sources, it is more homogeneous than other forms of mass media
- When looking at mainstream news, the majority of articles are crime related
- Crime news triggers emotional reactions, more than other kinds of news
- People tend to remember more details of crime news due to these emotional attachments
- While coverage of crime in the news is increasing, actual crime rates are decreasing
- Related to this, people who watch the news often perceive crime rates as increasing
- Audience effect, related to the Chiricos article
- Most crime news is reported in progress, it rarely reports on the results and final verdict
- Crime news is created to provide entertainment and to emphasize that the CJS is functioning
- The police and the news have a special relationship, the news needs information on crime stories to remain popular, and the police get publicity and a positive image
- The Vancouver media depicts VPD much more favourably than the RCMP
- This is because the VPD is much more media friendly and flexible than the RCMP which is country wide and has a more military structure
- Crime news also functions to create moral panics, or overblown reactions towards specific actions and groups, especially in terms of norms violations
- Not every crime makes it into the news, what determines what is newsworthy?
- The media has a specific bias in how crime is reported
- Jack Katz did a content analysis of crime news from 1974-78 and discovered four common themes
- The first was personal competency and sensibility
- Competency is ability to carry out a crime, and sensibility is related to moral values
- Many news stories describe criminals as being very competent, and/or very insensitive in terms of moral values
- The second theme is collective integrity
- News stories focus on crimes that are perceived as an attack on a collective identity
- Crimes that break the social order, usually the victim/offender is a high profile individual or has high profile characteristics
- The target can also be an institution or object, the key is contrasts or subversion
- Eg. High profile athletes committing crimes, kids toys used to smuggle drugs
- The third theme is moral political conflicts
- This is news where the focus is on an oppositional group, or any group that is outside of the norm
- In terms of crime, this is used as evidence that a group is considered "undesirable"
- Usually focuses on a specific characteristic, this can be race, religion, political affiliation, etc.
- An example of this was reporting after 9/11 highlighting crimes by Muslim immigrants
- The final theme is white collar crimes
- These always emphasize the power and status of the offender, inspires themes of the corruption of wealth and power
- A good example of this was Conrad Black
- Crimes can demonstrate more than one of these themes simultaneously, they are not mutually exclusive
- When we do content analysis without consulting the people who made the content, the results are only theoretical
- However, it can expose interesting patterns
- The theoretical reason these themes emerge is for entertainment, to inject moral issues and dilemmas for readers to contemplate through the lens of the news
- People don't read the news for the content, they do it for the controversy and conversation
