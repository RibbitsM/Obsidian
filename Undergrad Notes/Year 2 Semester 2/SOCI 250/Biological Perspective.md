**Trait Theory**

- Refers to the approach of looking for common "traits" among criminals
- Implies that there is a tangible factor about someone that creates deviant behaviour
- This can be physical or mental, like a genetic mutation or missing a certain receptor
- Modern trait theory looks at a bio/psych combination, and how it interacts with the environment
- Looking at traits is often an easy explanation, and dates back many years
- One of the first examples of this would be biological determinism, like palm reading
- Idea that examining your body and its makeup can help predict your future (genome reading)
- Physiognomy is the study of facial features to determine personality (Laveter)
- In the 18th century, Laveter attempted to figure out if criminals had a certain eye shape
- Phrenology was a similar study, but it looked at skull size and shape instead (Gall)
- By measuring criminals skulls, they would attempt to find correlations between skull shapes
- Of course, your skull shape has much more to do with how you sleep as a baby than anything else
- Somatotyping looked at body types to determine personality (Sheldon)
- Three body types: endomorph, mesomorph, ectomorph, mesomorph was most likely deviant

**Biology and Crime**

- The Italian positivist school tried to take this to the natural extreme and focus on all of these traits
- Noone was really trying to disprove these theories, just find more traits to look at
- Favoured nature over nurture, people are born to be criminals and need to be regulated
- This led to a lot of discrimination, in fact concepts like phrenology were used to justify slavery
- Lombroso, the father of criminology went to Italian prisons and measured and examined different physical traits of criminals
- Coined the term Atavism to refer to physical traits that represent an under-evolution of a person
- Pictured criminals as an unevolved form of humans who can be identified via certain traits
- Linked with common scientific theories at the time of evolution and race differences
- Observed certain facial features that he associated with criminals, like fat lips
- Politics at the time loved these findings, it justified a much higher level of state control
- This methodology had a few flaws, especially that he only looked at prisoners in his experiment
- An effective research would have looked at prisoners and non-prisoners, to get a control
- There was also a lack of real scientific evidence, there was no mechanism of causality
- Used evolutionary framework, but failed to mention what exactly was inherited

**Modern Biology**

- Four main sections in modern biology
- Biochemical, Neurophysiological, Genetic, and Evolutionary
- The biochemical aspect mainly refers to hormones and neurotransmitters
- Examines the effects of surpluses and deficits of certain chemicals in the body
- What chemical imbalances lead to deviant behaviour?
- The main one we look at is the genetic factors
- People especially like looking at twins, since they have the same genetics they're perfect for nature vs nurture experiments
- If genes fully determine your actions, we would expect twins to act in the exact same way
- We assume that genes have some power of determination, but we don't know how much
- Therefore the more often twins act similarly, the more genes determine your actions
- This is called the concordance rate, meaning how often genetically related people act similarly
- Identical twins have higher concordance rates than fraternal twins, who have higher rates than siblings
- DiLalla and Gorresman did an analysis of studies using twins, and found that the average rate among fraternal twins was 22%, while it was 51% among identical twins
- Genetic similarity matters, there is definitely some aspect of biological determinism
- Not exactly full determinism, but there is a significant nudge
- There were no differences in male or female identical twins
- However not everything is traceable, not all criminals have traceable heredity
- The concordance rates rarely, if ever, exceed 70% meaning there's something else at play
- In twins studies, they're usually raised in the same environment
- For siblings the family environment could change between births, but twins are born simultaneously
- Cloninger studies children whose parents were criminals
- Found that the crime rate for children whose biological parents were criminals was 4 times higher than kids whose adopted parents were criminals
- Limited in ways you can observe biology, you can really only determine correlation not causation
- The definition of what is criminal changes over time, the social definitions may change between generations
- Genome research rarely include interaction with the environment, small sample sizes and don't look at birth order, class, occupational status etc.
- Many variables are left unexamined and aren't crossed with genome research
- We have yet to see a real study properly integrate genetic research with sociological imagination

**Theft**

- As an example, we'll look at the different perspectives and theories on just theft
- In Canada, theft is the unlawful taking of property from the possession of others
- Theft is either over or under 5000$ (not motor vehicles)
- People don't always just steal because they're poor and needy, rich people steal too
- A good example is Winona Ryder, who was taken in for shoplifting despite being rich and famous
- Kleptomania is an illness that prevents people from resisting the impulse to steal things
- This is especially connected to stealing things you don't really want or need
- Mostly diagnosed in females, and may be related to a lack of white matter in frontal regions
- Biological explanation for theft, physical evidence that people might be predisposed to theft
- Following in Lombroso's footsteps, Adrian Raine attempted to find commonalities in brain scans of violent criminals like serial killers
- Goal is to try and determine what people are more likely to commit crimes in the future
- Identified multiple identifiers like low resting heart rate and birth complications
- Only possible through an intersectional approach between psychology, criminology, psychiatry, and medical research

**Hormones**

- Two kinds: excitatory and inhibitory
- Dopamine and acetylcholine are the main excitatory hormones
- Serotonin and norepinephrine are the main inhibitory hormones
- Generally a lack of serotonin is associated with criminal behaviour

**Biosocial Theories**

- Mednick believed that all behaviour was controlled by the autonomic nervous system
- We learn to not deviate through the social system of rewards and punishments
- Some people's nervous systems adapt slower than others, and these people have a harder time not deviating because they haven't learned yet
- In addition, amount of exposure to punishment and reward is also indicative of deviance
- Eysenck built on this theory by considering how introverts and extroverts respond to punishment and reward
- He argued that being an introvert or extrovert is an inherited characteristic
- Extroverts respond less to punishment and reward, and therefore are more prone to deviance
- Moffitt believed that deviance was usually a product of congenital issues, or the environment in early childhood
- Some children are born predisposed to deviance, while others become predisposed through early childhood due to environmental factors
