- Ten different ways of explaining from the sociological viewpoint
- First six are rooted in a structural functionalist perspective, looks at the social structure
- The next three are rooted in social process, focuses on micro level interactions
- The final three are based on social conflict, how power is exercised in society
- Looking at social structure theory today, all theories are linked together and interrelated
- Not really mutually exclusive

**Strain Theory**

- Argues that in society everyone generally has a common goal, and therefore common values
- In modern society, this is generally agreed to be the pursuit of wealth
- To get money, we use institutional means, which in this case is getting a job
- To get a job, you have to go to school
- Schools and jobs are the institutional means to acquire the common goal
- However, not everyone has the same opportunity in this process
- There is unequal access to these institutional means so it is harder or easier to acquire the goal
- Merton critiqued the American ideal of rags to riches through strain theory
- Argues that social structure puts strain on cultural values by making it easy for some people to achieve these values and more difficult for others in 1968
- This critique was focused on two main groups seeking the rags to riches story which were black people and italian people
- Both groups widely stereotyped as criminals and being involved with organized crime
- Both groups were seen as being prone to becoming criminal due to their culture
- Merton discredits this concept and says that social structure is the reason why these groups are failing
- When you disadvantage a group socially, they will turn to other means to acquire the goal like organized crime
- Legitimate means to acquiring the goal are blocked behind class and status, this creates "anomie"
- Also known as the theory of anomie
- Durkheim's original theory of anomie named two types of criminals created by anomie, common and altruistic
- Altruistic criminals violate the law to change society, while common criminals do it for personal gain or other selfish motives

**Merton's Typology of Deviance**

- 5 different means of adaptation
- Depicted in a square where the y axis is acceptance of goals and x axis is acceptance of institutionalized means
- If you accept goals and means you are a conformist, which makes up the majority of people
- "I need to raise a family and have money, to do this I need to go to school and get a job"
- If you accept goals but reject the means you are an innovator, this is "get rich or die trying"
- "I need to raise a family and have money, to do this I will use any means"
- An example of this is mainly criminals like robbers and drug dealers
- If you reject the goals but accept the means then you are a ritualist, more neglected
- "Don't care about having a family or money but I will go to school and get a job anyway"
- The main examples of these are artists or people who have ulterior motives, usually creatives
- If you reject the goals and reject the means you are a retreatist, and withdraw from society
- "Don't care about having a family or money, and I don't care about school or work"
- A good example of these are hermits and people who are intentionally homeless
- The final one is outside the main quadrant
- If you create new goals and create new means, you are a rebel, you have different ideas
- "I don't care about society, I only care about the rule of God" example:
- Religious fanatics are a good example, or people who attempt to establish small self contained new societies like commune leaders or cultists
- Can also be hermits and people who just live isolated from society
- This typology suggests that people aren't inherently evil, and are just responding to the structure of society
- Crime occurs when people are unable to achieve their goals through legitimate means
- People are frustrated/strained and resolve this via unconventional means

**Limitations**

- A problem with this is that this typology primarily focuses on crime committed by the lower class
- This ignores things like white collar crime, as well as crimes that aren't financially motivated
- From a statistical analysis of theft, people in the lower percentages of income are much more likely to steal vs the higher percentages of income
- This is good evidence for strain theory, but it's just for one crime
- Merton says that money is the only motive for crime and is the end goal of society
- Most of the time, money is just a tool to achieve goals
- Often times people immigrate to get opportunity, but also safety and peace of mind
- There are many deviant behaviours that have no monetary incentives, and there are many recorded cases where people commit crimes just for fun
- Car theft can be for money by stripping it for parts, but also people just like to go joyriding
- In the example of joyriding, there is basically no distribution in terms of class
- In addition, not all poor people commit crime and rich people sometimes commit crime which is unexplained by strain theory
- The term "strain" is very vague and never clearly defined as a psychological or sociological concept
- Also most of Merton's data came from official sources, meaning he didn't use self reported data
- Yes, there is high drug use among low income communities but what doesn't show up in official data is high rates of drug use among high income communities like Hollywood and Wall Street

**General Strain Theory**

- Pioneered by Agnew
- Argued that strain is not purely structural/interpersonal, but emotional
- Interpretation of the environment, not actual environment leads to strong emotions
- Created to explain higher levels than expected in middle and upper class groups
- Strong negative emotions are tied to criminal behaviour
- Negative emotions are the primary mechanism that motivates people to commit crimes
- Strain creates negative emotions which creates rule breaking
- Four main sources of strain identified
- One is the failure to achieve goals of society like gaining money
- The next is disparity between expectations and achievement, like seeing someone outperform you with less effort
- The next is a loss of positive stimuli or influence, like losing a family member or home
- The final one is the presence of negative stimuli like a bad working or living condition
- In his research, Agnew identified Negative Life Events, Life Hassles, Negative Relations with Adults, and Parental Fighting as being correlated with increased criminal behaviour
- The same occured with drug use, but Neighborhood Problems also had a strong correlation
- Also includes a measure of the psychological factor
- Argues that stress is cumulative and repetitive, and criminal acts only happen after a long period of repetitive strain
- Those subjected to constant strain become more likely to commit crime over time
- Negative emotions are affected by external factors, and can be enhanced or minimized
- In modern therapy, this emphasis on negative emotions is widely accepted, especially how they can either facilitate or inhibit delinquent behaviour
- One of the main weaknesses of this theory is that stress/strain is measured in absolute terms
- Strain is relative, not absolute, different people have different tolerances and life experiences
- Even if you're in a good position, you can still feel relative stress

**Relative Deprivation Theory**

- This theory asserts that strain is not absolute and is instead relative
- Even the poorest people in Canada are still better off than many people in other countries, but they still experience lots of stress
- Anomie is institutionalized, there is an established and widely agreed-upon measure of normlessness, which is generally a lack of material success
- Companies benefit from this sense of institutionalized anomie, since it encourages people to improve their condition through consumption
- People with the same social standing can have drastically different senses of deprivation
- This explains a key issue in many theories, it explains why rich people may commit crimes
- Research done in 2004 discovered that GDP per capita is negatively correlated with many crimes, but income inequality is positively correlated with crime
- Yes, having absolute strain matters for crime but so does relative strain
- The effect of income inequality on stress is also affected by environment
- In a place like Vancouver, often the rich and the poor people live very close to one another
- Meanwhile in Toronto, relative stress would be much lower since rich and poor communities are much more segregated
- Relative deprivation is the economic gap between rich and poor people in close proximity
- Middle class people feel relative deprivation when they witness lifestyles they can't achieve
- When people feel this way, they question their place in society and the ensuing sense of injustice and entitlement can lead to white collar crime
- More sociological than a psychological theory, offers prescriptions for urban planning
