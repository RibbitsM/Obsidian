- Focuses mainly on individual factors, not societal or group factors
- Three main branches of theory
- Psychodynamic theory, or the "layers of the self" focuses on the experiences that make up your psychology
- Childhood, family, etc.
- Cognitive theories focus on how you interact with and perceive the world, how you process information
- Behavioural theory is how you learn and are conditioned by your education and your experience growing up
- These three theories make up the bulk of models for explaining criminal deviance

**Psychodynamic**

- Mainly pioneered by Sigmund Freud and his model of psychoanalysis in the 1900s
- Places great importance on childhood and early experiences with social interaction
- Incredibly popular psychologist, depicted in media frequently
- Practiced dream analysis
- Difficult to test many of his theories
- Laid the groundwork, but never really contributed directly to criminology
- Three components, the Id, Ego, and Superego
- The Id is your unconscious urges, biological needs
- Instant gratification of instinct, like food, sex, and survival
- Avoids pain and seeks pleasure
- You are born with your Id, it persists for your entire life
- Your ego and superego are developed over time during your childhood as you learn
- The ego weighs the pros and cons of acting on the Id's desires and acts as an intermediary between desire and action
- The superego also arises through socialization and includes your moral values acquired from your family and society
- Therefore, if your childhood doesn't teach your superego the correct values, you may permit yourself to commit criminal acts
- This leads to a "delinquent ego" that rationalizes criminal acts as acceptable
- The three parts of your psyche are in constant conflict
- You conscious superego is constantly suppressing the Id and the ego intermediates
- If your id is not properly repressed, issues arise
- Repression is one of several defense mechanisms against your hidden desires
- Repressed or denied traumatic memories can cause imbalances between the three forces, leading to acting out and criminal activity
- Criminal behaviour can be explained as a product of an uncontrolled or unsatisfied Id
- Another potential explanation is an unconscious desire for punishment that is created by unconscious guilt or an overdeveloped superego
- According to Freud, humans are naturally selfish, amoral and hedonistic but society creates the ego and superego which controls these desires
- Ego and superego also produce guilt, which can override the Id and cause criminals to confess
- Very difficult to test these theories since how can you measure the Id or Ego
- The core idea is that your past affects your future, especially childhood
- The idea of repression is also useful, having internal conflicts you are entirely unaware of
- An example of this would be research at the University of Montreal linking shoplifting to anxiety and depression
- The three variables they used were significant losses, anxiety, and depression
- Losses could be deaths in the family, lost relationships, unemployment etc.
- Only 35% of shoplifters had not experienced a significant loss in the last 6 months
- About 75% did not have symptoms of severe anxiety, which isn't a clear link but is still less than in the general population
- On the depression scale, only 40% had no symptoms for depression
- This indicates that there may be some linkage between anxiety, depression, and loss and shoplifting

**Behavioural**

- How we learn, focuses on classical and operant conditioning etc.
- Main idea is what conditioning leads to criminal behaviour, and what lack of conditioning may do
- Behaviour theory believes that humans learn through social interaction, conditioning is not always required
- By modelling certain actions, you can learn by imitation
- Bobo doll experiment investigated if aggression was learned through observation
- Argues that learning is not trial or error, but imitation
- Children observe an adult beating up a clown doll, and are then left alone in a room with the doll
- They also had children who observed adults treating the doll well
- One limitation was the argument that the children were just copying the adult like they were following instructions
- Hard to expand this to the real world
- In addition, the children were aware that the doll wasn't real and they were not causing any real harm
- Many of these talking points have been reiterated in the arguments around violent video games
- There was also no opposition to their behaviour, since noone tried to stop them the child assumes it's fine
- Opposition would teach them that they should not be aggressive
- It's difficult to measure the effects of multiple models simultaneously
- Are some models more effective than others?
- Even if you have many models representing one thing, one model representing the opposite can change things
- Also attachment to the model can affect their influence

**Cognitive**

- Cognition is the process of thinking, or the way in which we intake information from our environment and make decisions based on that information
- Information comes from our environment, is processed in our minds, and its outputted as action
- From this perspective, criminal behaviour is the result of a flaw in this cognitive processing
- One link to deviant behaviour is frustration, where inability to achieve ones goals results in irrational and violent behaviour
- Road rage is a good example of frustration leading to anger
- Five outcomes from a frustration-anger study in 1939
- Aggression is always a consequence of frustration
- Chance of aggression increases with interference, frequency of experience, and intensity of frustration
- The higher the perceived risk, the lower the likelihood for aggressive acts
- If aggression towards the source is risky, anger was instead redirected
- Once frustration is vented, a sense of catharsis is felt
- This model is still regularly used in anger management programs
- Berkowitz updated this theory by defining two distinct personalities
- The first is the individual offender, who is a product of constant intense episodes of frustration
- The other is the socialized offender, who is a product of conditioning and modelling by their environment
- Again, this theory runs into the issue of being unable to directly observe the mind
- The moral development theory argues that children are born without morals, and learn them through their environment as they grow and mature
- Therefore, criminality is caused by improper learning or a faulty childhood environment
- Piaget argues that there are four different stages of moral development, going from birth to age 15
- Kohlberg ran studies and discovered that many offenders have weaker moral reasoning
- This theory has its uses, but fails to offer explanations for why moral development may be stunted
