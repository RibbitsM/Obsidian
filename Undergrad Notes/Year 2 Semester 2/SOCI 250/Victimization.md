- Crime is often viewed in terms of a relationship between a perpetrator and an victim
- However, we usually only focus on the perpetrator
- Victimology looks at the nature and causes for victimization, consequences of victimization and the characteristics of victims
- This can help explain why crimes happen, and help develop prevention strategies
- Also gives a voice to victims who are often overlooked in the CJS

**Victim Behaviour**

- There is correlation between victimization and substance use, youth exposed to abuse are more likely to use
- Crime victims are also more likely to commit crimes themselves
- Crime propagates itself, called the cycle of violence
- If young children are exposed to violent behaviour from adults, they will be more likely to become violent as they age
- The cycle of violence is intergenerational, those abused as a child will be more likely to become abusers
- Timing matters, abuse experienced during adolescence has the largest effect

**Victimization Surveys**

- Early victimization groups used the 3 Rs: Rights, Reparation, and Representation
- Victimization surveys used to count the dark figure of crime
- Surveys give information on the aftermath of victimization
- Assesses CJS response to victims and levels of fear on crime
- Separate from CJS reports, collects the victims who do not report victimization

**Victim Characteristics**

- Overall, the chance of victimization decreases with age, with 20-24 years olds most likely to be victims of violent crime
- Overall, male children are more likely to be abused, and are much more likely to be physically abused
- Female children are less likely to abused, but more likely to be sexually abused
- Victimization for personal property crimes does increase with income, but at a fairly low rate
- Victimization for violent crimes is much higher for lower income, but falls off fairly quickly as income increases
- Interestingly, people who are married are less likely to be victimized across the board, from both strangers and acquaintances
- Having more social connections protects you from crime, older people are also less likely to be victimized
- Your partner is a permanent protector/witness, makes getting away with a crime more difficult
- Older people have many more shields from victimization from young people, they tend to be married and live in quieter, high social control neighbourhoods
- Age does not inherently reduce victimization, but older people have accumulated more protections

**Victim Precipitation Theory**

- Study in 1948, wanted to look at the telltale signs of victimization
- How can we distinguish easy targets for crime compared to harder targets
- Typology of different common classes of victims, highlighting key variables like gender and age
- Analysis updated in 1978, tying lifestyle to victimization
- Connected to science of setting insurance premiums, analyzing the 'risk' of people
- People who are more active and social have higher risk, so are unemployed people
- Three main hypotheses based on this model
- The first is the equivalent group hypothesis, the offender and the victim will similar characteristics related to race, gender, or socio economic status
- The second is the proximity hypothesis, the offender and victim will be from the same social space
- The final one is the deviant place hypothesis, the victim lives or works in a place where crime is high and social control is low

**Routine Activities Theory**

- Looks at things we do every day and how our daily routines predispose us to being a victim
- Three main elements to this theory
- Motivated offender, the offender actively wants to commit a crime against a target
- The victim must be suitable, how susceptible are they to interaction
- And an absence of a protective influence, can be environment, society, or a person
