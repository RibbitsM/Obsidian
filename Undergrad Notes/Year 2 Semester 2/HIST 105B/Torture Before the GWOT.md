- Long history of torture beginning in the middle ages
- First and second class citizens vulnerable to torture to different extents
- Still relevant to today
- Torture vs enhanced interrogation
- Rationalization of torture by the state
- Spanish inquisition good example of this
- Geneva convention established to ban torture
- Two main categories of torture, clean and psychological
- Imported from history, not new methods invented by the CIA
- Psychological torture is supposed to not physically harm the subject
- Second degree torture involves threatening the subject's loved ones
- Technically not a threat on the prisoner himself
- Playing loud music, preventing the prisoner from sleeping
- Waterboarding is probably the most discussed torture method used by the US
- Very long history, appeared in the spanish inquisition
- Called 'simulated drowning' by the press
- However this is actual drowning, and can definitely kill the prisoner
- Clean torture is physical torture that does not leave marks
- When officials come to examine the prisons, they will not know that the prisoners are tortured
- Ice water curing, ice cold showers used on criminals, and has been used against slaves, conscientious objectors, and prisoners of war historically
- Put prisoners in cells with constant air conditioning, came from Vietnam War
- Waterboarding is both clean and psychological torture
- Hot temperatures were also used like sweatboxes, used today in Iran, Turkey, Latin America
- Standing cells, sometimes combined with other torture
- High cuffing was an old punishment for slaves, modified to leave less marks
- Rationalized as being not as bad as the torture implemented by other countries
- Occasionally the US would outsource prisoners to other regimes that would employ torture
- Similar to the phrase 'simulated drowning', 'Forcible Cell Extraction' was basically beating prisoners but phrased in a different way
- Media often silent on when torture used against Muslim prisoners, but covers torture used on Europeans
- No discussion of systemic torture, framed as being isolated incidents

**Colonial Torture**

- Colonial countries like France and Britain have also done this, justifying torture used against colonized people
- France in Algeria, and Britain in Kenya especially brutal examples
- A huge stash of British documents were uncovered detailing torture used by the British to suppress a rebellion in Kenya
- Torture was revealed as being a key part of British policy, not just the result of a few bad actors
- Caroline Elkins, the researcher who discovered this, received immense amounts of backlash
- The ongoing narrative was that Britain was more of a benevolent empire compared to others like the French, which was challenged by these documents
- Not only was the narrative destroyed by this, but opened the door for many countries to pursue legal action from Britain and collect reparations

**Main Points**

- Long history of torture, many techniques used have existed for a very long time
- Terms surrounding torture created to sanitize and justify it
- Almost all states use torture despite it being outlawed
- Especially think about who can be tortured, and how identities affect how prisoners are treated
- Laws ineffective or ignored, regimes simply destroy the evidence

**Torture Memos**

- Created new category of illegal enemy combatants
- Rationales of revenge and need for justification
- FBI using tactics of non-violent interrogation, gaining the trust of the prisoners
- Eventually CIA gets sole authority of detainees, uses force
- Extraordinary rendition, used to outsource torture
- Ibn al-Sheikh al-Libi target of the CIA, effectively executed since they knew he would die in Egypt
- In desperation, al-Libi claims to the Egyptians that he is a senior terrorist officer and was responsible for an al-Qaeda training camp
- He also suggests that Saddam Hussein offered to train them in the use of biological weapons
- This led Powell to go the United Nations and provide this as evidence to invade Iraq
- Made no sense, he only said it because he wanted to escape torture
- Extrajudicial detention and CIA black sites carried out in several allied countries including several European countries
- Planes going tp multiple definitions to cover up paper trails leading to secret CIA black sites
- Many suspects held based on faulty intelligence derived from torture
- Sunk costs, often these prisoners would be secretly tortured, flown around the world, only to find out they are innocent
- Instead of being released which would reveal US violations of the Geneva convention, these innocent prisoners are simply held without warrant indefinitely
- The trial of Salim Hamdan changed this, a lot of black site prisoners were transferred to Guantanamo Bay, which was barely more humane
- Red Cross officials are allowed to visit black sites, but cannot talk about it
- Going back before 9/11, extraordinary rendition was originated under Reagan and expanded significantly under Clinton and later Bush Jr.
- In this case extraordinary rendition was less of a torture tactic and more to just get rid of prisoners
- This was done to a Canadian citizen who was arrested and sent to Syria
- Overarching dilemma of what to do with these illegally detained prisoners

**Evasion of Accusations**

- 'Enhanced interrogation techniques' used as a euphemism for torture
- Not just soldiers and US officials at these sites, but also academics, psychologists, doctors, who are complicit in these acts
- Media asking the wrong questions, reduces the discussion to be just about waterboarding
- Evading the real issue gives the government the green light to do this with no consequences
- While they have received criticism, no legal action has been taken against any high level officials who were involved in these decisions
- Only legal action was taken against soldiers carrying out torture who were labelled as bad apples and solo actors
- Much of the evidence has been hidden or destroyed
- Even the individuals who are released suffer consequences to their mental health and are often unfit to give testimony in court

**Abu Ghraib**

- Maximum security prison in Iraq
- Used for torture by Saddam Hussein and his brother, notorious even before the war
- After the invasion, it was taken over by the United States and used for further torture
- Photographs of violent and sexualized torture released from Abu Ghraib, creating huge backlash that was simply blamed on a few bad actors as usual
- Torture via sexual humiliation, likely rooted in Orientalist thinking
- Torture memos invoked even though the Bush administration claimed to not be responsible
- Basically arguing that American soldiers are allowed to torture people if they are overseas
