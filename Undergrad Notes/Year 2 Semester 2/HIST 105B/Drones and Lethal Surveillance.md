**The British in Iraq**

- In the 1920s, the Ottoman Empire was falling and the territories seized by the British were combined into one mandate and labelled 'Iraq'
- Generally the Iraqi public was distrustful or outright hostile to the British government
- Britain was stretched to its limit dealing with the aftermath of WWI, and wanted to maintain control of Iraq with the minimum possible money and casualties
- The answer was to use air power since it was relatively cheap compared to establishing a full police force
- Air power was appealing not in its financial aspects, but also in the potential to gather knowledge about the people of Iraq and track their movements
- Ground troops were confused and bewildered by the land, and did not trust to populace
- Problems were solved easily with few British casualties with plane mounted machine guns and bombs
- By terrifying the populace into obedience, Iraq could be controlled with a small amount of planes
- Panopticon effect, if a plane was overhead the people below felt like they were being watched at all times
- Even if you couldn't see the plane, it could still be there
- Although the government attempted to cover up the bombings in Iraq, they also stated that it didn't matter if anyone found out, since Iraqi lives don't matter that much
- This sentiment can be felt in policies on who is and isn't a civilian during the Obama era as well
- The British described all men in Iraq as 'potential fighters', very similar to how Obama would talk about Afghanistan
- Drone attacks are described as being precise and causing no unwanted casualties
- The British explicitly stated that they were doing this to cause terror, today it is still being done implicitly

- Confirmation bias assumes that the targets being tracked are terrorists, and the goal is to find information to confirm this
- In this case, intelligence suggested that a terrorist attack on an airport will involve a white Toyota
