**Comparative Advantage**

- Comparative advantage is essentially a comparison of opportunity cost between countries
- A production possibilities frontier (PPF) depicts all possible combinations of quantities of two goods that you can produce  
- Points outside the curve are impossible, and points inside are inefficient
- The slope of any given point on the line represents the opportunity cost of A in units of B
- In the Ricardian model our only factor of production is labour
- If you send 5 workers to grow apples, that's 5 workers not growing bananas
- Say the US can grow 10 million apples or 30,000 bananas
- Colombia can also grow 10 million apples but only 10,000 bananas
- Following comparative advantage, the US should grow bananas and Columbia grows apples
- The ratio of apples to bananas is lower in Columbia, the opportunity cost is larger
- When countries specialize, total production increases

**Assumptions**

- The Ricardian model assumes 2 countries, two goods, and labour as the only input
- Total labour productivity varies between countries due to technology but labour productivity is constant
- Supply of labour is also constant
- Workers are paid equal to the value of what they produce and labour markets are competitive
- In a competitive market price equals marginal cost, therefore wages are equal to marginal cost
 
- Since labour productivity is constant, the production function is linear and doesn't experience diminishing returns
- The slope of the line represents marginal product of labour
- The unit labour requirement is the number of hours needed to create one unit of output
- You can find it by dividing 1 by the marginal productivity of labour
- This means it's the inverse of productivity, the more of a good is produced per hour, the lower the unit labour requirement, lower is better
- So if aLW > aLC then cheese production is more efficient (assuming same prices)
- aL is the unit labour requirement in country a
- Labour supply is L and is fixed
- aLC is the labour required for one pound of cheese
- aLW is the labour requirement for wine
- All workers are employed in either industry so L = LW + LC
- To find the total quantity of any good, multiply the amount of workers in an industry by the unit labour requirement
- The PPF is aLCQC + aLWQW less than or equal to L

- Maximum amount of goods that can be produced with limited resources
- Since L = LW + LC, aLCQC is equal to LC and the same goes for wine

- Since MPL is constant, the PPF is linear
- Vertical intercept is L/aLW, horizontal intercept is the same but with C
- Represents production when fully specialized
- The slope ends up as -aLC/aLW
- Calculate rise over run, the Ls cancel out and the fraction flips
- Represents the opportunity cost of cheese measured in units of wine
- To find comparative advantage from a graph, compare the slopes
- If aLC is 1 and aLW is 2, then the absolute value of the slope is 1/2
- The opportunity cost of 1 pound of cheese is half a gallon of wine
- Or, the cost of the good on the y axis in terms of the x axis good

**Relative Prices and Supply**

- The wage paid to workers is equal to the price of the good divided by the unit labour requirement
- A competitive wage equals the value of additional output produces by an additional worker
- Therefore the price is equal to the wage times the unit labour requirement
- This is how you get different labour costs in different countries, when production is less efficient wages are lower
- If cheese costs 4 dollars and aLC is 2, wC is 8, if aLC is 1, wC is 4
- Price is equal to marginal cost, competitive market
- Fixed labour supply in each country, creates linear PPF
- The value of any given point on a graph given price is PC x QC + PW x QW
- Multiple points on the graph can have the same value, linear equation that creates isovalue lines
- Infinite number of isovalue lines for different values
- The slope of the equation is -PC/PW
- Slope of the PPF is the ratio of unit labour requirements, the slope of the isovalue line is price ratio
- If the isovalue line is shallower than the PPF, the relative price of cheese is less than the opportunity cost of cheese
- Say the price ratio is 4/7 and OC ratio is 1/2
- For the isovalue line that overlaps with the PPF, wages will be the same for both industries
- Workers are willing to make both      
      

- Steeper isovalue means focus on cheese, shallower means focus on wine
- Without trade, relative prices should equal opportunity cost

**With International Trade**

- Labour capacity doesn't matter, only relative production
- That's the difference between comparative and absolute advantage
- If one country can produce one unit of a good with less labor than another country, you have absolute advantage
- Lower opportunity cost (aLC/aLW) means a comparative advantage in C, shows up as a flatter PPF        
- Here, home has an absolute advantage in both, but a comparative advantage in cheese
- This means that aLC/aLW < aLC*/aLW*
- Autarky means no trading
- PCAUT means the price of cheese at home when not trading
- Without trade, relative price equals relative opportunity cost and both countries produce both goods
- With trade, countries can specialize in the relatively cheaper good and export the excess and import the goods they don't produce
- Production doesn't have to match up with consumption

- High productivity can give a comparative advantage, but so can low wages

- Competitive advantage depends on both relative productivity and relative wage rate

**Relative Demand**

- As the relative price of one good increases, consumers will tend to substitute towards the cheaper good
- Therefore relative demand is downward sloping, just like normal demand
- When we add individual demand curves together, the combined demand curve shifts right
- To find global relative demand since relative demand isn't additive, instead the global relative demand lands somewhere in the middle
- To find equilibrium, look where global RD and global RS intersect
- Most of the time, the equilibrium is on the vertical segment of the RS curve but not always
- If it does land on a horizontal section, one country will not be fully specialized
- Even in this situation, the non-specialized country should still make gains from trade
- The idea is that you trade can convert units of one good into another
- Instead of producing limited amounts of both goods, produce a large number of one good and trade it for another        
- Free trade effectively allows two countries to combine their PPFs
- Expands the consumption possibilities of both countries
- Consumption possibilities are not the same between countries, even with free trade  
- Here, the black lines represent possible consumption possibilities
- They can be any line that isn't steeper than PPF* or flatter than PPF

**Relative Wages**

- Differences in productivity determine wage differences
- How many times more productive is a worker in one country compared to a worker in another country in terms of a certain good
- Ratio of home marginal product of labour and foreign marginal product of labour
- Say home is 6 times more productive in good A compared to foreign
- Therefore the home wage would be 6 times higher for good A
- If home has a ULR of 2 for good B and foreign has a ULR of 3, then we do aLB*/aLB which gives us 3/2 as our relative productivity
- A country will specialize in the good where it has the greatest productivity advantage
- In free trade, the relative wage will fall between the two relative home productivity values
- When a country has an absolute advantage in all goods, the unit labour requirement is lower for all goods in that country compared to its competitors
- When a country has a lower unit labour requirement, the wages for those workers are higher
- Inverse relationship between unit labour requirement and wages
- When each country has an absolute advantage in at least one good, the relative wage can be greater than, equal to, or less than one depending on demand
- If the export of home's specialty is in higher demand, home wages will rise
- In our example where home has an absolute advantage in both goods, w/w* will be greater than one
- Say cheese and wine both sell for 12$
- In Home, the wage is 12$ since ULR is 1 for cheese, home's specialty
- In Foreign, the wage is 4$ since the ULR is 3 for wine, foreign's specialty
- 12/4=3, above 1
- Our relative productivity was 3/2, wage ratio is 3/1
- High wages offset by high productivity, low wages offset by low productivity
- The nominal wage is the wage paid to workers in $ per hour
- The real wage is the wage paid to workers in terms of consumption units
- Basically, how much can you buy with your money
- In our example our only goods are cheese and wine, so real wage is measured in units of cheese/wine
- w/PC = real wage in terms of units of cheese
- **In autarky, relative price is equivalent to the ratio of unit labour requirements**
- If both goods are being produced in autarky, the wages are equal in both industries
- In autarky, real wages are equal Pgood x MPLgood
- Therefore, real wages are equal to the marginal productivity of labour in autarky
- Wgood/Pgood = MPLgood
- If home's price ratio (in autarky) is 2/5 for cheese and it specializes in wine, then foreign's price ratio for cheese must be even less
- In addition, the free trade price ratio must also be lower than 2/5 to create an advantage, but also higher than the foreign autarky rate
- Home's wages in the wine industry is equal to the free trade price of wine minus MPLW, and the real wage is equal to MPLW
- Same as autarky, just different price
- Although nominal wages may change, real wages remain constant (in terms of specialized good)
- In terms of the other good, the real wage improves and same goes for foreign
- Since workers likely consume a varied basket of goods, real wages always increase in free trade

- The opportunity cost of one apple is 1.5 bananas
- In autarky, the relative price of apples to bananas is 3/2     

- Here, home has a comparative advantage in apples and foreign has a comparative advantage in bananas
- Relative supply is 1/2
- Here relative demand for apples is equal to the relative price of bananas
- Basically, as bananas increase in price, relative demand for apples rises and vice versa
- At RD1, the free trade relative price of apples falls between 3/2 and 5, and relative supply is 1/2
- Therefore, RD1 is also equal to 1/2
- Since RD is equal to the relative price of bananas, then the free trade relative price of apples is 2
- If the calculated free trade relative price was not between 3/2 and 5, we would know that the equilibrium is in one of the other sections of the graph

**Free Trade Relative Wage**

- Home workers are 5/3 times more productive in apples, foreign is 1/2 times more productive in bananas
- Therefore relative wage can be greater than or less than 1
- In home, all workers are producing apples
- In foreign, all workers are producing bananas
- Relative free trade wage = free trade wage ratio times foreign ULR/home ULR
- Relative free trade wage is equal to 2 times 1/3 = 2/3
- Here foreign workers make more, but if the demand for apples was higher then home would make more

**Comparative Advantage with Many Goods**

- In our model we can now have an infinite number of possible goods, but only one factor of production: labour
- The ULR of a good is represented as aLi with i being the good
- Goods will be produced wherever it is cheapest to do so
- The cost of producing a good is determined by multiplying the wage in a country by the ULR of the good
- Alternatively, we can say that if the relative productivity of a good is greater than the relative wage in the home country, the good will be produced there, same for foreign
- If a*Li/aLi > w/w*, then the good will be produced at home
- Home productivity advantage is a*Li/aLi, foreign productivity advantage is aLi/a*Li
- The country the advantage is referring to is in the denominator
- Begin by calculating productivity advantage for every good, then get the relative wage
- Say if home has a productivity advantage of 4 in a good, but home wages are 3 times higher than in foreign, that good will still be produced in home
- But if home only has a productivity advantage of 2, than it would be produced in foreign
- Remember that with ULR, the lower the number the better
- A ULR of 5 is 8 times better than a ULR of 40
- Make sure to order goods in terms of relative productivity advantage, makes determining who produces what much easier
- Relative wage is determined not by relative supply and demand of goods, but the relative supply and demand of labour
- As wages at home become lower relative to foreign, demand for it increases and vice versa

**Relative Demand for Labour (Home relative to Foreign)**

- When w/w* = 3, home produces 3 goods, and foreign produces 2
- World demand for 3 goods must be met by home, other 2 must be met by foreign
- By comparing amount of labour utilized in home and foreign, we can get the LD/LD*
- Say the relative wage increases by a small amount, say going from 3 to 3.1
- By looking at the productivity advantages, we can see that the set of goods produced would not change, specializations stay the same
- However, an increase in relative wage still increases the relative cost of production slightly
- Goods produced by home will be consumed less due to higher price, so production is scaled back
- If w/w* increases but doesn't hit a breakpoint, LD/LD* decreases proportionately
- If relative wage changes enough that it hits a breakpoint, meaning the pattern of specialization changes, then a drastic shift will occur
- Home has a relative productivity advantage of 4 in Good C, but if w/w* exceeds 4, them home will no longer produce that good
- Relative supply of labor is fixed, and determined by the amount of labour available in each country
- Drawn as a vertical line at L/L*, the relative size of homes workforce compared to foreign
- The labour force equilibrium is found at the intersection of RD and RS, and determines w/w*
- From here we can find the relative prices of all goods
- For example PE/PC can be found by multiplying w*/w with aLE*/aLC which gives us 1
- You only need the relative wages if the goods are produced in different countries

**Transportation Costs and Non-Traded Goods**

- Only apply to goods moving between countries
- If a transport cost is a fixed percentage of the cost of production and z is the percent, the cost of the good is now multiplied by 1 + z
- A good will no longer be traded if the cost including transport is greater than the benefit received via trade
- If z = 50, the cost of a good will increase by 50 when traded
- When a country is importing a good they must consider w x aLC (1+z)/ w* x aLC* (for foreign)
- In this case, aLC is 3 and aLC* is 12, and w/w* is 3, so it is more expensive to import from home than to produce it in foreign
- Therefore each country produces C on their own and it becomes a non-traded good
