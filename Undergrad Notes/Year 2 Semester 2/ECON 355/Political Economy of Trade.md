**Optimal Tariffs**

- For subsidies on both imports and exports, total surplus will always decrease even for a large country
- For tariffs, the gains from terms of trade may offset the distortionary losses in the case of a large country
- When we have a tariff that increases total surplus, it is an optimal tariff
- We can graph the effect of tariffs and determine the optimum point
- When the tariff is 0, there is no difference from world trade

- The surplus created by the tariff rises to to, the optimal tariff, and then starts to fall
- It falls until you reach the prohibitive tariff rate where trade is 0, and you revert to autarky conditions
- This can also be done to find the optimal amount of revenue generated by the government from a tariff
- Government revenue is represented by area g, and the goal is to create the largest rectangle possible, as the tariff points move up and down the line it changes in total area and shape
- Object of economists is to maximize total surplus, which is represented by a + g
- However, this ignores the possibility of other countries countering with their own trade policy since you are taking surplus from other countries
- To avoid a trade war, it is sometimes better to remain in free trade
- It is also possible to set an optimal export tax, which is effectively a negative export subsidy

**Market Failure**

- An argument against free trade is the potential for market failure to harm trade
- This is when we see a divergence between the cost to firms and the cost to society
- Pollution is a good example, since it has a societal cost that isn't considered in the firms cost
- Other failures of the market can be consumers lacking information to make the best consumption decisions, or technology being unequally utilized by different groups
- These are called market externalities, any costs or benefits that aren't accounted for in the market and can be negative or positive
- Leads to a difference between private marginal cost/benefit and social marginal cost/benefit
- Pollution would be a negative production externality since it's associated with production, and has a negative effect outside of the market
- In this case, social marginal cost would be greater than private marginal cost
- Technological spillover would be a positive consumption externality, since it gives unintended benefits to society not accounted for in the market              
- In this example, marginal social cost is marginal private cost plus marginal external cost
- The ideal equilibrium point for society is lower than the actual equilibrium
- Competitive market gives us too much production and consumption
- Ps Qs shows us where total surplus is maximized for everyone inside and outside the market
- This equilibrium can be achieved using a tax, an example is the carbon tax that attempts to account for social costs of pollution in the market
- At the market output, the total surplus is the green area minus the red area
- At the socially optimal output, it's only the green area
- This example assumes we have a negative production externality
- But market failures are not always negative, it just means that social benefit/cost is not properly represented by surplus calculations  
- C represents external benefits, the difference to society achieved by producing more
- Domestic market failure is part of a larger argument called theory of the second best
- Government intervention can address market failures, for example tariffs and subsidies distort the market but they can also cause external gains
- Intervention is the 'second best' option in the case where fixing market failures is unfeasible
- If a tariff was applied to the above example, a and b would be deadweight losses that are offset by area c, representing social benefit

**Direct Policy**

- Import tariff raises price from Pw to Pt, which is the new amount that home producers receive and the amount consumers pay
- The side effect is reduced consumption, which didn't need to happen
- Therefore, we need a policy that would raise production but without affecting consumption
- One option is a general production subsidy to home producers
    
- Here, price remains unchanged but home production increases by Sp
- Provides the same additional external benefit but with less deadweight loss
- Instead of both consumption and production distortions, we only have a production distortion

**Negotiation of Trade Policy**

- US tariff rate has generally declined over the past century with a big drop in the 20s and a big rise in the 30s
- These reductions in tariffs are mostly due to the GATT and later the WTO
- When negotiating trade policy, usually multiple nations are involved and supports exporter groups lobbying for free trade
- Multilateral approach counteracts pressure from import competing groups
- Having multilateral negotiations as opposed to bilateral or unilateral helps to avoid trade wars
- By eliminating incentives to adopt protectionist policy, we avoid a prisoner's dilemma situation
- Since protecting is a strictly dominant strategy, both sides will ultimately end up worse off
- Even if both agree to pursue free trade, one can switch up and reap the benefits
- However, with multilateral agreements there are layers of obligation and accountability that lock countries into free trade
- WTO negotiations focus on reducing tariff rates and then binding them so they cannot be raised in the future
- Also looks at non tariff barriers to trade like quotas and subsidies
- WTO is made up of several agreements, not just the GATT
- Disputes among WTO members are settled by an impartial panel of experts
- The last successful WTO negotiations took place in Uruguay in 1994
- Further efforts going forward have been blocked because remaining protected industries are very organized and powerful
- The Most Favoured Nation Treatment is a key concept of the GATT which states that nations must treat all trading partners equally, meaning tariffs are extended to all countries or none
- All trade partners should be treated as your most favoured nation, hence the name
- This does have exceptions
- The National Treatment article seeks to eliminate non tariff barriers, meaning imported products must be subject to the same inspection and regulation as domestically produced goods
- Anti-Dumping and Countervailing Duties is the exception to MFN, allowing tariffs in the case where unfair trade practices are being carried out
- Dumping is when a lower price is charged for exports compared to domestic goods
- Can also be done through export subsidies or domestic subsidies, called countervailing
- Dumping is price discrimination, which requires some amount of market power meaning this is in imperfect competition
- There also must be segmented markets, meaning goods cannot be bought in one market and sold in another

**Dumping Model**

- Say home has a monopolistic market, but the free trade market is competitive
- Competitive market price is Pw   
   

- In this case, Ph is much higher than Pw, and may cause home to be accused of dumping
- We're assuming here that foreign demand is perfectly elastic
- But what if home demand and foreign import demand are both downward sloping?
- In the case where the monopolist has constant marginal cost and two distinct markets, the monopolist will produce until MC matches MR in both markets
                           

- The monopolist has market power in both markets
- Therefore to maximize profits they set different prices in different countries
- Say we have another case where MC is not constant, it's upward sloping in home
- Home demand is still independent of foreign import demand but MC creates a connection
- Profit maximization is Qh + Qf when MRh(Qh_) = MC(Qh + Qf), and same goes for foreign
- Can't find one equation without the other
                         
- To calculate this, we're given home demand, foreign import demand, and MC
- First find MRh and MRf
- Then take horizontal sum of both by rearranging to price is function of quantity and adding
- To find this from demand functions, take inverse demand and double the slope, then rearrange the MR functions so Q = MR, then do the addition
- Then find Q where MC matches the sum
- We know that MRh(Qh) = MC(Qh + Qf)
- So find MC(Qh + Qf) and look for the quantity where MRh = MC
- Do the same for MRf and you have home and foreign prices from the demand functions
- Elimination of Quantitative Restrictions seeks to avoid quotas and get countries to move towards tariffs
- This is why countries mainly switch to Tariff Rate Quotas which achieve the same effect but with tariffs
- Emergency Action allows countries to temporarily protect at risk industries
- Regional trade agreements are permitted, which is another exception to the most favoured nation rule
- Allows countries to remove further barriers among their neighbours without extending it to the rest of the WTO
- Examples of these are NAFTA (now CUSMA) and the EU
- These also usually set the tariff rate at zero, which is an exception to the rule
- The EU is also a customs union, which unifies trade policy among all members
- These trade agreements are not always good for national welfare
- If new trade is created, meaning high cost products are replaced by low cost imports, the country benefits
- However, it can also replace low cost imports with higher cost imports from member countries

**Example**

- Say we have 3 countries with 3 prices
- A = 8, B = 6, and C = 4
- Now say A sets an import tariff of $5
- B = 11 and C = 9, trade is prohibited
- If A sets a tariff of $3, then trade is only blocked with B
- A will still import from C
- But if A forms a trade agreement with B, then A will mainly import from B instead of C
- Despite the fact that in the base case C is the lowest cost exporter, tariffs and trade agreements create a situation where A mainly imports from B, reducing welfare
- In the case of the $5 tariff, the trade agreement creates trade with B, raising welfare
- Difference between creating trade vs diverting trade

**Offshoring/Outsourcing**

- Outsourcing is when a firm takes an activity being done within the firm, and moves it outside of the firm
- Handing over operations of some aspect to another firm or some other third party
- A good example of commonly outsourced operations is IT support
- Offshoring can be combined with outsourcing and is related to the location of activities/operations
- Offshoring is geographic, it means moving operations to another country
- Doesn't need a third party, but the two are often combined
- Before now we looked at the production of a good mainly in terms of inputs and outputs
- But production has several steps that require different kinds of inputs
- For example, R&D would need high skilled labour, where assembly requires low skill labour
- Say we have two countries, with differences in wage rates for high and low skill labour
- In our example, foreign has lower wages across the board, and Wh*/Wl* < Wh/Wl
- Moving operations offshore has transportation costs, and additional costs of capital
- There will be a threshold where it is no longer worth the lower wages to offshore
- In our example we would offshore lower skilled processes, and keep high skill processes at home
- Threshold is determined by additional costs, but what if they change over time
- If additional costs fall, you'll want to offshore more, and if they rise you'll want to offshore less
- When you increase offshoring, demand for low skill workers falls in Home, raising Wh/Wl
- In foreign we have an increase in high skill workers, also increasing demand for high skill workers and raising Wh*/Wl*
- Demand for skilled labour increases in both countries
