- Export subsidy in a small country standard trade model
- Say cloth is the export good and food is the import good
- An export subsidy is imposed by a small country
- The internal price ratio of Pc/Pf increases since relative price of cloth increases
- Since producers would prefer to sell on the world market due to the subsidy, the price on the domestic market increases
- There is an increase in the relative supply of cloth since the price increased, and a decrease in relative demand
- In the case of a large country this would create a change in terms of trade, lowering terms of trade
- In a small country, your internal relative price increases but terms of trade stay the same
- RS and RD shift within the country, but no change to world RS and RD occur  
- Here you can see on the graph that the PPF does not change
- Consumption also strictly decreases since the terms of trade did not change
- For a large country, the slope of the consumption line would change when the subsidy is imposed
- At the new production point, Home shifts more resources into cloth production

- In this example the new terms of trade line will be even flatter than the original
- This is because according to our RS RD graph, Pc/Pf world decreases
 
- Say we have a case of dumping with a monopoly
- At home the monopolist faces downward sloping demand
- Foreign import demand is perfectly elastic at Pw

- Say our home demand is 200 -3p
- Inverted that is 66 and 2/3 - 1/3q
- Marginal revenue would be 66 and 2/3 -2/3q
- We double the slope of inverse demand to find marginal revenue
- If you're only selling in home, find MR = MC
- Since we're exporting we find MR = Pw
- The next case we look at here is where import demand is downward sloping
- MC will be constant
- For home, we sell where MRh = MC
- For exports we sell where MRf = MC

- This is also an example of dumping since the price in foreign is lower than the home price
