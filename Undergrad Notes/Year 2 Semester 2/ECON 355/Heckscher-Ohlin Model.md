- Under the HO model, countries have differences in all factors of production, and different relative abundances
- This means that countries have unequal relative resources and factors of production have unequal relative intensity
- Trade occurs due to differences in labor productivity and differences in resources
- No specific factors, just varying levels of intensity
- We have two goods, two countries, and two factors of production
- The amount of labour and capital used varies between countries
- In production, you can vary both factors of production, nothing is fixed
- However, the overall supply of labour and capital is fixed for each country
- In the long run, labour and capital can move between industries, so wage and rental rate are equalized between cloth and food industries
- Opportunity cost is not constant, and the PPF is not straight
- If we have fixed marginal productivity of labour and capital, we can find unit labour requirements and unit capital requirements
- This assumes that factors are not substitutable, can't substitute labour for capital and vice versa
- Our production possibilities are determined by both capital and labour
- We have limited amounts of labour and capital, can't exceed total amount
- Say our labour is 2000, aLC is 2 and aLF is 1
- Capital is 3000, aKC is 2 and aKF is 3
          
- Here we have our ricardian style PPFs for labour and capital
- We can't produce at a point if we don't have enough capital or labour
- We can't make 3000 units of food because we don't have enough of either, but you also can't make 2000 units of food since you lack the capital
- However, you can make 1000 units of food, or 1000 units of cloth
- We take the inner boundary to find the real PPF
- As you can see, the OC is not constant, at the top the slope is -2/3 and at the bottom its -2
- Gets steeper as you move along the curve
- If producers could substitute inputs, then the PPF would be curved (concave)
- The economy wants to maximize value of production
- The slope of the isovalue line is -PC/PF, like in specific factors
- When factors can be substituted, producers will choose based on relative wage and rental rate
- An isoquant line shows all combinations of inputs that create a specific output  
- Here you can see that anywhere on the red line is the same production, but with different inputs
- The blue line is just a higher level of production
- Inputs are perfectly substitutable, one unit of labour is equivalent to a certain amount of capital
- Earlier when making our PPF, we said inputs weren't substitutable
 
- With no substitution, our isoquants are right angles
- But we want isoquants with convex curves
- This is actually Leontief production

- Slope isn't constant, factors aren't perfectly substitutable
- Much harder to make something with all labour and no capital vs a mix of the two
- The slope of the isoquant is marginal rate of technical substitution
- Near the extremes, this slope of very steep/flat
- Represents the gains from moving away from extreme specialization
- The production function of cloth is C(LC, KC)
- Diminishing MPLC and MPKC in the production function
- Slope of the isoquant is equal to -MPLC/MPKC
- Full HO production function has 3 axes, KC, LC, and QC
- Therefore we can't really graph it conventionally
- This is why we use isoquants to keep QC fixed
- Therefore all three axes are still represented
- Think of the full HO production function as a three dimensional object that you can look at different sides of
- Here we are looking at the side of the cube where KC and LC are shown, but QC is hidden
- Cost of producing a certain amount of a good is w x LC + r x KC where w is the cost of labour per unit and r is the cost of capital per unit
- We can also use isocost lines, finding all combinations of labour and capital that collectively cost $1000
- By rearranging this equation we can use a set cost and find units of labour from units of capital or vice versa
- K = cost/r - w/r x L
- The slope of the isocost line is -w/r, the ratio of wage to rental rate
         

- Here, our isocost line is very steep, meaning labour is more expensive compared to capital
- By shifting our isocost to a point where we use less labour and more capital, we're still producing the same amount (still on the isoquant)
- However, we're on a lower isocost, meaning production is more efficient
- To find the most efficient production point, find the point where the slope of the isocost equals the slope of the isoquant
- **-MP****LC****/MP****KC** **= -w/r**

- When the slope of the isocost is steeper, use less labour and more capital
- The wage to rental rate ratio determines what mix of inputs we use in production

**Resources and Output**

- When output prices are held constant and the amount of a factor increases, then the production of the good using this factor increases, and the production of the other decreases
- PC/PF held constant, when the amount of labour increases there will be more cloth and less food produced in the economy

- Here we draw the isovalue line that maximizes production

- We increased labour here shown by QC on the x axis, so production of cloth increases and production of food decreases
- While overall production increased, the ratio of food to cloth decreased

- As you move up the slope of the RS curve, the price of cloth compared to food rises and so does the quantity

**Output in Trade**

- An economy with a high labour to capital ratio produces more cloth than food
- In our example home is relatively abundant in labour, so it has a relatively high supply of cloth
- Both countries can have the same technology and the same tastes
- Therefore everyone has the same relative demand for a given good
- Each country has a comparative advantage in the good that it has an abundance of the factor for
- At the same relative price, home will produce more cloth than foreign does
- Only one relative demand curve but two relative supply curves, one for each country  
- Here RS1 is foreign and RS2 is home
- Home has the advantage in cloth, and has a lower relative price of cloth
- Vice versa for foreign
- W is the world relative supply, it lies in between the two supply curves
- The two dots are autarky prices for home and foreign
- Home exports cloth, foreign exports food
- The Heckscher-Olin Theorem is that a country will export the good it has an abundance of the factor, and import the other good
- Owners of the abundant factor win in trade, and owners of the scarce factor lose
- It doesn't matter what industry they're in, they will lose out
- This persists even in the long run where factors are substitutable
