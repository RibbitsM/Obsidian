- International economics is the interactions between states involving money, investments, goods and services
- Includes movement of capital and goods, focus is on trade of goods and services
- Many countries rely heavily on international trade in comparison to countries like the US
- The purpose of trade is to leverage advantage
- Countries trading goods and services tends to generate mutual benefit
- Countries produce what they are best at producing, and import what they cannot produce
- Comparative advantage, even if a country doesn't have an absolute advantage, it can still benefit by focusing on the most productive option
- Countries can also make gains via international borrowing and lending and through migration
- Migration is essentially trade of labour, while borrowing and lending is a trade of current goods for future goods
- International exchange of assets like stocks and bonds can help diversify wealth and encourage stability
- International trade may be opposed by domestic groups
- Opening markets can harm certain corporations and industries locally
- For example, jobs without widely applicable skills like machine operators and fishermen
- Lesser skilled workers are often hurt the most
- Policy affects trade via tariffs, quotas, export subsidies, and other regulations like specifications
- Quotas can apply to imports or exports
- Product specifications can be changed so that foreign goods are "not up to specifications", barring foreign goods from the market
- There are 7 recurring themes in international economics
- The first is gains from trade, comparative advantage and so on

**Patterns of Trade**

- The second is the pattern of trade, which is the issue of who trades what and with whom, both the actors and goods are important
- The most accepted explanation is a model that looks at relative supplies of resources in relation to their use in the production of different goods

**Protectionism**

- The third is protectionism, or how much trade should be allowed
- States seek to balance the benefits of competition with the utility of having strong domestic industries
- Protectionist policies hide some industries from foreign competitors, ensuring their advantage
- After WWII, free trade became much more widely accepted leading to the WTO being formed and many free trade agreements being negotiated
- Recently, movements opposing globalization and free trade have gained traction, pushing public opinion in the opposite direction
- Economists use cost benefit analysis to determine whether to protect industries or not, but are not always followed
- Disagreement within nations affects trade more than disagreement between them
- Interest groups control policy, not the general will of the population

**Balance of Payments**

- The fourth is balance of payments, or trade surplus/deficits
- A surplus or deficit is never always good or bad, it varies situationally
- The US runs huge trade deficits which is considered a large problem
- South Korea ran a surplus in 1998, but their economy was terrible, the surplus was forced due to a financial crisis
- The same year China also ran a surplus, and benefitted greatly
- Related to FDI, international transactions and income accounting

**Exchange Rates**

- The fifth is exchange rates, comparisons between currency
- The existence of different currencies sets international economics apart from other fields of study
- Relative values can change drastically and quickly
- Exchange rates were historically fixed, but now are dependent on economic performance

**International Policy Coordination**

- The sixth is international policy coordination
- The economic policy of any country has effects beyond its borders
- Germany raised interest rates after reunifying, and caused a recession in Western Europe as a result
- Conflicts of interest between countries often prevents coordination on policy
- Lack of cooperation is a fundamental problem for international economists
- Trade policy is controlled by GATT, but macroeconomic coordination is still a growing field

**International Capital Market**

- The final theme is the international capital market
- The 2008 financial crisis showed how interconnected the global market really is
- A crisis in US securities affected the world economy
- As international trade grows, so does international capital exchange
- Global capital markets have special regulations not found in domestic markets, but also lack some restrictions found domestically
- Currency fluctuation causes unique risks, adding an additional factor to the value of investments
- Also, the lack of consequences for not paying national debts can lead to national default
- International trade analysis covers real, tangible exchanges
- A trade issue would be subsidies on lumber, a monetary issue is the disparity between currencies used to purchase the lumber
