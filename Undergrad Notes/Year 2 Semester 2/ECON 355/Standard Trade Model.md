- General model that includes Ricardian, specific factors, and Heckscher Olin models
- Two goods, food and cloth
- Each country has a smooth, curved, PPF
- Differences in countries can be due to labour services, skills, capital, land, and technology
- Countries PPFs determine their relative supply functions
- You derive the relative supply as you would in the Ricardian model
- You can also do this when marginal productivity of both factors is fixed
- The world relative supply function is determined by aggregating all the national relative supply functions
- This determines equilibrium under international trade
- Want to maximize production, find the highest isovalue line on the PPF
- The value of what countries consume must equal the value of what they produce, like in specific factors
- In autarky, production equals consumption
- In trade, it's more like a budget  
- The PPF is consumption possibilities in autarky, the isovalue line is consumption possibilities in trade
- The second graph is an indifference curve, where all combinations of cloth and food give the same amount of satisfaction to the consumer
- Exist in families like isovalue lines
- We want to find the furthest out indifference curve, highest level of consumption
- They are always downward sloping
- Become flatter when moving to the right due to opportunity cost
- Should be more of a gentle curve vs an L shape
- They can also be straight lines if substitution isn't an issue
- Meaning a consumer would be equally happy with all of one good or all of the other
- A curved indifference curve indicates that consumers prefer a mix of goods
- A right angled indifference curve indicates that goods are perfect complements
- People want to consume the goods in a fixed ratio
- When you put the PPF and the indifference curve together, you know where to produce
- Produce on the indifference curve furthest from the origin that is still tangent to the PPF
- The difference between the point where you produce and the point where you consume is the exports
- If the relative price of cloth goes up, countries that export cloth will benefit
- The isovalue line (PC/PF) becomes steeper, meaning a higher indifference curve can be reached
   

- Here the relative price of cloth increases, making the isovalue line steeper
- Production changes to favour cloth, moving consumption to a higher indifference curve  
- Here we see the same change plotted on the relative demand curve for cloth
- The relative price of cloth rises, and the relative quantity demanded for cloth decreases
- To put it another way, relative demand for food increases

**Terms of Trade**

- Terms of trade means the relationship between the price of the export good vs price of the import good
- Each country has its own terms of trade
- If home exports C and imports F, its terms of trade are PC/PF and foreign's is PF/PC
- Say we have a raise in the relative price of cloth, home's PPF would look like this

- Here, home's terms of trade improved, meaning its favoured good increased in price

**Economic Growth**

- Growth is almost always biased, meaning it specializes in one sector
- Related to the Heckscher-Ohlin model, where an increase in one factor creates uneven growth
- For balanced growth to occur, there must be proportional growth in all factors
- In this case, the slope of the PPF will remain the same at all points as it was previously
- If balanced growth occurs, the relative supply curve will remain the same
- Biased growth can even happen in the Ricardian model, if technology advances in one sector
- Rybczynski Theorem dictates that production of one good will be increased, and production of the other is decreased              
- Here is two different possibilities for biased growth, one favouring cloth and the other favouring food
- In the first example the ray (F/C) becomes flatter, and in the other it becomes steeper
- The slope at both points are the same, but the ray changes
- Therefore relative prices remain the same, but production changes
- This will cause a shift in the relative supply curve, shifting left when biased towards food and right when biased towards cloth   
   

- Growth may be shared between countries or limited to just one
- However, growth in one country will change the terms of trade, altering the other country
- Growth biased towards cloth will reduce relative price of cloth, lowering terms of trade for cloth exporters
- Remember, world relative supply is an average of both countries, so a change in one country affects the world relative supply
- When Home relative supply shifts to the right, world relative supply also shifts, but by less
- This lowers world relative price for cloth, worsening terms of trade for cloth exporters (Home) and improving terms of trade for food exporters (Foreign)
- Terms of trade is important because it expands consumption possibilities, one unit of your export is worth more units of your import
- Growth biased towards the food industry shifts world relative supply to the left, raising world relative price for cloth
- Export-biased growth is when a countries export sector grows, and import-biased growth is when a countries import sector grows
- Export-biased growth worsens terms of trade by reducing relative price of the exported good
- However, growth is never really considered a bad thing, even though terms of trade may be worse, your production still expanded
- Decrease in terms of trade balanced out by increase in production
- Import-biased growth benefits terms of trade and hurts welfare of foreign countries by increasing relative price of the exported good
- This is beneficial on both counts, and will always make the country better off

**Import Tariffs and Export Subsidies**

- Import tariffs are taxes on imports, where the government collects revenue on each unit of a good imported
- This artificially increases import cost to control imports
- Tariffs can be on specific goods and can either be a fixed amount of a percentage of the goods value
- EX. 10 dollar tariff on every unit or a 10% tariff on every unit
- Sales taxes are an example of an ad valorum tax
- Export subsidies are payments from the government to the producer per unit exported
- The government gives you money for exporting goods, to artificially lower export cost
- Subsidies and tariffs divide domestic prices of goods and world prices of goods
- A good is worth a different amount inside vs outside a country
- External relative prices are the rate at which goods are traded in the global market
- Internal relative prices are prices within the country
- Say home exports cloth and imports food, and imposes a food tariff
- This increases the relative price of food within the country, but world relative price of food is the same
- Domestic producers can charge more for food, since importing cheap food from the global market is no longer feasible due to tariffs
- This impacts relative supply of food and cloth in the country, with relative supply of cloth shifting left as more food is produced
- However, relative demand also shifts to the right at the same time, meaning relative quantity is untouched
- Relative price of cloth rises, improving terms of trade
- This is why countries implement tariffs and subsidies to improve terms of trade

- Here we have the original free trade price ratios compared to the price ratios after a tariff on cloth
- Exchange rate of cloth to food changes, terms of trade increase
- In the world market, cloth goes up in value, so consumption increases
- New consumption occurs on the green line, above initial cons
- When drawing indifference curves, we would consume where it touches the green line but has the slope of the internal price line
- In this example we're assuming that Home is large enough that it can influence the terms of trade
- Smaller countries can alter internal prices, but aren't influential enough to affect terms of trade

**Export Subsidies**

- Payments made to producers for each unit of a good they export
- Relative internal price of the good goes up, and therefore relative supply increases
- Relative demand for that good domestically decreases
   

- In this example of a cloth producing small country, welfare is reduced by implementing a cloth subsidy
- Distorts supply and demand without changing terms of trade  
- New consumption possibilities are less than originally
- Since global Pc/Pf is unchanged, the slope of the new line is same as the original
- An import tariff on a home country improves home welfare at the cost of the foreign country
- An export subsidy by home lowers home welfare and benefits the foreign country
- However, export subsidies will still benefit those involved in the export industry which is why we still sometimes see them despite not being objectively good
- Pc/Pf shows the exchange rate of cloth to food on the global market
- This ratio is adjusted by tariffs and subsidies
- For example, say a tariff is placed on food, making it more expensive to import food
- This encourages production of food leading to an decrease in Pc/Pf internally
- Relative supply of cloth decreases domestically and relative demand for cloth increases
- Assuming this is a large country, these shifts would be reflected on the global graph
