- If free trade is so beneficial for everyone, why is there widespread opposition to it
- Now we want to look at the distribution of gains from trade within an economy
- In the Ricardian model, Labour was freely mobile but this is not realistic
- In the Specific Factors model, some FaPs are fixed in an industry, meaning they cannot adapt easily
- Industries also demand different FaPs, some have more mobile needs and some have less mobile needs
- For our assumptions we have two goods, cloth and food
- There are three FaPs, Labour (L), Capital (K), and Land (T)
- There is still perfect competition between markets
- Cloth is produced using capital and labour but not land
- Food is produced using land and labour, but not capital
- Labour is mobile between sectors, land and capital are specific to their respective industries
- We also have a fixed labour force
- Our production function for cloth is QC = QC(K,LC) where K is the amount of capital in a country and LC is the number of workers in the cloth industry
- Capital is fixed at a certain number, Labour varies
- Our production function for food is QF = QF(T,LF) where T is the amount of available land in a country and LF is the number of workers in the food industry
- Land is fixed, Labour varies
- Short run model, cannot change capital or land
- When looking at our PPF, we want to think about how output changes when labour moves between industries
- All we need to know is the allocation of labour, we already know allocation of land and capital
    
- Since we are including capital, we now have diminishing MPL since as we add more workers, there are less machines available to spin cloth on
- If K increases to K', the production function is raised, increasing production possibilities
- Output increases at a decreasing rate, eventually even decreasing
- By looking at the slope of a certain point on the PF, we can find the MPL at that labour value
- Slope gets flatter as LC increases
 
- Here is the graph of the marginal product of labour which slopes down as labour increases
- We have a fixed amount of labour, but they can be allocated between cloth and food at any ratio
- By combining both production functions and the labour allocation, we derive our PPF

- Now that we have to deal with diminishing marginal productivity, our PPF is concave
- Specializing in either industry isn't ideal due to diminishing returns, so the greatest consumption possibility is somewhere around the middle
- At any given point on this PPF, the slope is the opportunity cost of producing cloth in units of food
- However, this opportunity cost is NOT constant unlike the Ricardian model
- When most workers are in the food industry, the opportunity cost is low since removing one worker won't cause you to lose a lot of food production and will greatly boost cloth
- When few workers are in the food industry the opportunity cost is high since removing one worker causes you to lose more food, and gain less cloth
- We don't really have unit labour requirements anymore, but you can still find it by dividing one by the MPL at any given point
- To free up one hour of labour, you must give up MPLF pounds of food which gives you MPLC yards of cloth
- Therefore the opportunity cost of cloth in terms of food is MPLC/MPLF

**Prices, Wages, and Labour Allocation**

- To determine how much labour is in each market, we look at supply and demand of labour
- In cloth, the wage is determined by MPLC x PC
- In food, the wage is determined by MPLF x PF
- Since the labour market is perfectly competitive, the workers are paid the equivalent of what they are producing
- If we know the price of cloth, we can find the wage at any point on the cloth production function
 
- This graph shows the demand for labour in the cloth industry
- At any given wage on the y axis, the company wants to hire workers up until the equilibrium
- If wage is higher, less workers will be hired and if the wage is lower, more workers will be hired
- The same goes for the food industry, if a worker isn't making cloth, they're making food
- In order to have both goods produced, the wages must be the same between both industries
- Therefore if the wages are the same then price will dictate where on the production function both goods are since w = P x MPL
- The width of the horizontal axis on either labour demand curve represents the total labour force
- EQ represents the division of labour between both industries
- The labour allocation curve always has a slope of -1 since removing 1 unit of labour from one industry adds 1 unit to the other
- Given PC/PF we can find the allocation of labour
- Wage = production and price = marginal ratios
- Therefore the slope of the PPF can be represented by -PC/PF
- Change in price ratio changes location of PPF, meaning labour allocation changed
- Slope = -Pbottom/Ptop and MPL(bottom)/MPL(top)
- Price does not equal MPL exactly, just the ratios
- If you have a question where MPL is calculated mathematically, there is likely no curve unless its a quadratic equation

**Equal-Proportional Increases**

- If prices change, production doesn't always change
- If prices rise proportionally, relative price is the same
- Proportional change alters nominal wage but nothing else
- If both production curves shift up the same amount, the labour allocation won't move
- Nominal wage rises, but real wage stays the same
- Since price and wage rise proportionally, w/PC is the same
- MPL stays the same, which determines real wage since wC= PC x MPLC and wC/PC = MPLC
- There are three main economic agents: Labour, Capital owners, and Land owners
- For capital owners, price of cloth/cost of capital is the rental rate per period of that capital
- For labourers, wage = PC x MPLC, but how to the capital owners earn money?
- For capital owners the equation is PC x MPKC (marginal product of capital)
- You can find MPKC by dividing the rental rate per period (r) by the price of cloth
- For workers, in this situation PC and PF both go up so nothing changes
- For capital owners, only PC goes up and r stays the same
- Nominal r changes, but real r is constant since the labour allocation is static
- The same can be said for land owners when looking at food
- In terms of real income, labourers, land owners, and capital owners all experience no change
- The PPF slope is also unchanged, since -PC/PF is the same

**Unequal Changes in Price**

- Say the price of cloth rises, but price of food remains constant
- The wage for all workers increases, but less than the price increase
- Since price increases, food workers move to the cloth industry, changing marginal ratios
- There is no change to capital, but there is higher employment
- Because of this, MPKC increases since you have more workers on each machine
- This creates an increase in real returns for capital owners
- For land owners, the amount of land remains the same but there are less workers
- Therefore MPTF decreases since you have less workers per plot of land
- Land owners experience a decrease in real return
- Capital owners are better off, land owners are worse off
- For labourers, there's been a reallocation from food to cloth but not all workers moved
- The nominal wage has increased for both industries, and real wage increased for food
- However, since cloth prices rose more than the wage, real wage decreases for cloth
- Since wage is just a measure of marginal product of labour, we can say that MPLF increased and MPLC decreased
- We can't really say if workers are better or worse off, it depends on what goods they value more
- Remember that the slope of the PPF is -PC/PF so the slope became steeper
- New highest isovalue line is in a different place
- We're still producing some food, unlike in the Ricardian model with pure specialization
- To find production, look at the area below the marginal product of labour

- The shaded in area here represents total revenue
- The red and blue area is the amount of wage paid to workers, and the red area is net profit
- All cloth profits are captured by the capital owners, so this represents the rent
- All revenue goes towards factors of production, some goes to labour and some goes to capital
- In terms of real measures, labour income equals real wage times employment
- Therefore, a raise in the price of cloth mainly benefits capital owners
- At L2 we can see how moving down the curve increases profits as MPLC decreases

**Income Distribution and Trade Politics**

- Free trade generally benefits the exported good and hurts the imported good
- Whatever good has the price advantage in autarky will benefit the most
- When free trade opens up, the slope of the isovalue line changes
- Therefore you might need to change production to get the most value
 
- Here, the relative price of cloth increases in trade, making the slope of the ppf steeper
- At the original production point, value isn't maximized so production shifts to a higher isovalue line
- Consumption possibilities are anywhere along the S2 line in trade
- Can also see this line as the country's budget constraint
- Possible to distribute income so everyone gains, but unlikely
- If the winners compensated the losers, they would still gain
- The goal is to achieve pareto improvement, where no parties are worse off
- However, opening a country to free trade is not a pareto improvement in this model
- It is a potential pareto improvement, meaning that with income redistribution a pareto improvement is possible
- In this situation, the winners could compensate for the loser's losses and still benefit
