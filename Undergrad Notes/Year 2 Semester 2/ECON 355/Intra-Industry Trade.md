**Imperfect Competition (Monopolistic Competition)**

- In imperfect competition, firms can influence prices but can only sell more by reducing price
- Firms have some degree of market power, but don't have a monopoly
- This occurs where there are a few large producers creating a similar but differentiated good
- Firms are partly price setters
- Also called an oligopoly
- Elements of monopoly and competition
- Takes prices charged by rivals as a given
- Firms set price above marginal cost, but price is equal to average cost
- Goods in imperfect competition are not perfect substitutes, there is differentiation
- Expected to sell more when sales and prices increase
- Expected to sell less when number of firms and prices decrease
- Represented by equation Q = S[(1/n)-b(P-Pbar)]
- Q is a firms sales
- S is total market size/sales
- N is number of firms
- B is a constant representing response of sales to price
- P is the firm price
- Pbar is the average price of competitors
- All else held constant, a change in price causes a linear response in sales
- Although all firms have different demands, they look similar
- All firms face the same demand and cost function
- All firms should charge same price and share market equally
- AC = (C/Q) = (F/Q) + C = n(F/S) + C
- C is cost of production
- With this equation C is the y intercept and F/S is slope
- We can so this since Q = S/n, we substitute it in
- Depends on size of market (S) and number of firms (n)
- Can calculate average cost as a function of the number of firms in the market (S held constant)
- Economies of scale means that costs diminish as production increases
- In equilibrium, we have a number of firms where P = AC
- In this situation profits are zero and no firms want to enter or leave the market
- From our production equation we can tell that when S increases, q increases
- When n increases, q decreases
- When P increases, q decreases
- When Pbar increases, q increases
- Remember, all firms are facing this cost function
- AC(n) = n(F/S) + c, so AC increases with number of firms
- Because of economies of scale, when the number of firms increases but overall production is the same, each company is producing less efficiently and AC increases
- Linear relationship between n and AC
- But what is the relationship between p and n?
- We know that MR is the change of area when we adjust p and q
- So MR = p + (dp/dq) where d represents delta, or change and -dp/dq is the slope
- Our expanded demand fn is q = (S/n) + b x S x Pbar - b x S x p
- However, the first half is essentially constant for single firms so we can ignore that
- B x S is the coefficient for p, so it represents dq/dp
- Q = p(dq/dp)
- Rearrange, and MR = p - (1/b x S) x q
- P = c + 1/bn              
- When price exceeds average cost, firms enter and when average cost exceeds price, firms leave
- At the equilibrium point, n remains the same
- Price function is independent of market size, but AC function is not

**Trade in Imperfect Competition**

- Countries differ in terms of market size, or S
- If home has a larger market size than foreign:
- Home will have a larger number of firms in autarky
- Home will have lower average prices in autarky
- Price function remains the same, but foreign AC curve is steeper due to the smaller market size
- When we move from autarky to free trade, the two markets effectively merge
- The resulting world price will be somewhere below the home price
- Since the market is becoming larger, it becomes more efficient and lowers overall price
- Nworld will be less than n + n* but will be greater than n and greater than n*
- Not perfect process, not all firms join the world market or some are consolidated
- The size of the market increases more than the number of firms, leading to the decrease in price
- Sworld = S + S* but Nworld < n + n*
- Product differentiation and economies of scale lead to trade that benefits both countries
- Consumers benefit since price decreases
- Competition tends to hurt the lowest performing firms
- Overall performance improves because the firms that drop out when moving to world market are usually low performing firms
- This is called intra-industry trade, where similar goods are exchanged both ways
- This increases variety and decreases price
- Only explained through imperfect competition
- 25-50% of trade is intra-industry, usually in capital intensive manufactured goods like pharmaceuticals, special machinery, and chemicals
- These kinds of goods benefit greatly from economies of scale
- The most capital intensive industries are the most traded

**Example**

- Say b = 1/30,000, F = 750,000,000, and MC is 5,000
- C(q) = 750,000,000 + 5000q
- For home, the demand fn is 900,000[1/n - 1/30,000(P-Pbar)]
- For foreign its 1,600,000[1/n - 1/30,000,000(P-Pbar)]
- S = 900,000 and S* = 1,600,000
- AC = 5000 + (750,000,000/900,000) x n
- AC = 5000 + 833.33(n)
- AC* = 5000 + 468.75(n)
- P = 5000 + 30,000/n
- In the world market, Sworld = 2,500,000
- Acworld = 500 + 300n
- Initially, n = 6 and n* = 8
- In the end, nworld = 10
- 5000 + 30,000/n = 5000 + 468.75(n)

**Issues**

- Demand function is established and is identical for all firms
- In autarky each firm produces q = s/naut and sells at paut
- When the market combines, quantity produced increases, and the price decreases
- When moving to free trade, total consumption at home decreases, but so does price
- Goes against basic principles of microeconomics
