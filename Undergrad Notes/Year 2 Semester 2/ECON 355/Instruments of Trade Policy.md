- Tariffs are taxes levied on an import good
- Can be specific (dollar amount), or ad valorem(percentage)
- We consider how tariffs function in a single market, for example wheat
 
- Autarky wheat prices are higher in Home vs Foreign
- Therefore Home will want to import cheap foreign wheat
- Wheat will be shipped from Foreign to Home up until the point where the price difference is eliminated
- At the autarky price, consumers don't want any more
- But if the price is lower than equilibrium, consumers want more than the producers are willing to supply
- Therefore, we can make up the difference with imports
- The same goes for foreign, if the price rises above equilibrium, people won't want to consume everything that's produced, and the excess is exported
- It's also possible for a country to be too small to affect the trade price
- This is also assuming no trade policy like tariffs, subsidies etc.

**Supply, Demand, and Trade**

- Import demand is derived from the difference between supply of the import and demand for the import
- Export supply is derived from the difference between the demand for the export and demand for the export
- The quantity in the third graph is not total wheat consumption, just amount of traded wheat
- Real wheat consumption is domestic plus trade consumption   
   

- Import demand is represented as QMD(P)
- Export supply is represented as QXS*(P)
- QMD(PAUT) = 0
- QXS*(P*AUT) = 0
- Vertical intercept is at autarky prices for both goods
- PW = QMD(PW) = QXS*(PW)
- If there's a tariff in place represented by t, PT - t = PT*
- But, QMD(PT) = QXS*(PT*)
- Price at home is t dollars higher than the price in foreign
- Assuming this is a specific tariff
- To find the effect of a tariff, find the price difference in foreign

**Tariffs in a Small Country**

- If a small country imposes a tariff on a good, they will have no effect on the world price
- This will only drive up prices in the home country
- New price is world price plus tariff
- The price is fully elastic, meaning horizontal  
- With a specific tariff, the equation for home price is the price at foreign plus whatever the tariff is
- Pt = Pt* + t
- With an ad valorem tariff, the equation is Pt*(1 + t)
- For both of these equations we assume that import demand equals export supply
- QMD(Pt) = QXS*(Pt*)

**Measuring Amount of Protection**

- The effective rate of protection is a measure of how much trade policy provides protection
- Doesn't measure the nominal rate of the policy, but rather then change in value resulting from changes caused by trade policy
- Effective rate of protection is different from the nominal value since tariffs can affect sectors that aren't targeted
- As an example, assume a small country producing cars
- They cost $8,000 and require $6,000 worth of factors
- Therefore, value added is $2000
- VW = 8000 - 6000 = 2000
- If there's a 25% tariff on imported cars so home firms can push the price up to $10,000 since that's the minimum price for imported cars that won't create a loss
- Therefore the new value added is $4000
- To find effective rate of protection, find Vt - Vw/Vw
- Here, the effective rate of protection is 100%, meaning it is greater than the tariff rate

**Effects of Trade Policy**

- Since tariffs raise the price of a good, it benefits producers and hurts consumers, and the government
- We use consumer and producer surplus alongside government revenue to measure the effect of a tariff
- Consumer surplus is the difference in the price they actually paid vs the maximum price they were willing to pay
- In our model, there is no price discrimination since we're not in a perfect monopoly
- We have a uniform price
- The consumer surplus is the area above the price, and below the price line
    
- At price P2, the consumer surplus is area A
- At P1, surplus is a + b
- Therefore, when the price rises, consumer surplus decreases
- For producer surplus, we look at industry marginal cost
- Producers will sell at a price up until the point where price equals marginal cost  
- Firms want to maximize profits, and profits are revenue minus total cost
- Marginal revenue must equal marginal cost
- This is perfect competition, not monopoly
- Firms are price takers, so they only set the amount of units they produce
- For a competitive firm, the marginal revenue is the market price
- Therefore, P = marginal cost as a function of quantity, P = mc(q)   
   

- Here, price drops when moving from autarky to free trade
- In this example, consumer surplus increases while producer surplus decreases
- The green section shows the increase in total surplus, which wasn't available in autarky
- CS is above price and under demand, PS is below price and above supply
- The same concept can be applied to changes in price caused by trade policy
- In terms of government profits, with a specific tariff they collect revenue equal to the price of the tariff times quantity of goods imported

- From a graph perspective, government revenue is the difference between home and foreign's prices
- The red shaded part is the consumer loss
- Blue part is producer surplus gain
- Green part is the government revenue
- The purely green part is a transfer of foreign surplus to home due to terms of trade
- The purely red parts are deadweight losses in home which are a product of price distortion
- If they are large enough, they can offset the purely green part which is beneficial
- Therefore, total benefit is foreign surplus minus deadweight losses

**Specific Import Tariff (cont.)**
 
- Since world price changes in response to actions taken by home (import demand is downward sloping), we know that home is a large country
- Increased production and decreased consumption at Pt compared to Pw at home
- Tariff raises price, producers make more and consumers consume less
- Change in consumer surplus is a - b - c - d
- Change in producer surplus is a
- Government revenue is c + e
- Net effect of all of these is net gain
- Producer surplus cancels out a
- Government revenue cancels out c
- Final equation is e - (b + d)
- This represents the amount of value transferred from foreign to home by changing the terms of trade
- E is the value transferred, and b and d are the deadweight losses from artificially adjusting prices
- More specifically, b is the production distortion and d is the consumption distortion
- Tariffs encourage inefficient production (overproduction) and consumption (underconsumption)
- The green section in the foreign graph is their loss of terms of trade, and red is the distortion losses
- When moving from autarky to free trade, everything above Pw in this triangle is gained by home, and everything below is gained by foreign
- The whole triangle represents change in world surplus
- When moving from free trade to an import tariff by home, the green section is captured by home
- The red section to the right is lost to world surplus, and the upper half of it represents distortionary losses
- When home creates an import tariff, it decreases the quantity traded between home and foreign
- It also depresses the price of the foreign export good (lowers foreign ToT and welfare)
- If foreign imposed an export tax of an amount equal to the home import tariff, the result would be the same decrease in quantity traded but foreign welfare would be unchanged
- In this case, the price in foreign would be unchanged, but home prices would be higher
- Instead of the home country keeping the taxes, now the foreign country gets to keep the taxes
- Therefore, foreign's terms of trade increase instead of home's

**Export Subsidies**

- Same as tariffs, can be specific or ad valorem
- Now, we're assuming home is the exporter and the home government provides the subsidy
- Find price from home export supply and foreign import demand  
- Deterioration in terms of trade for home and an improvement for foreign
- Home is worse off overall, assuming home is a large country
- In addition, there is distortionary losses in both production and consumption
- Encourages inefficient production and discourages efficient consumption

- Consumers lose a and b
- Producers gain a, b, and c
- Government loses b, c, d, e, f, g
- Foreign gains e, f,g, and b and d are deadweight losses
- With export subsidies, Ps = Ps* + s, where s is the amount of a specific subsidy

**Import Quota**

- Restriction on the quantity of goods that can be imported
- Can be applied evenly to all countries, or specific to each country
- Industries ask for quota licenses which allow them to impose quotas on their good
- Can be binding or non-binding, is binding if quota is less than import demand
- Binding import quota pushes up the price of the good in home
- Different from tariffs since the government does not receive revenue
- The revenue instead goes to quota license holders, called quota rents   
   

- This is the effect of a binding quota in the home market
- The price increased, indicating it is binding
- The amount of the good traded, Qt, decreases, causing an increase in home supply and decrease in home demand as a result of the price change
- Price raises from Pw to Pq
- Foreign price drops from Pw to Pq*
- Consumers at home lose a, b, c, and d
- Producers gain a
- Government gains nothing
- Quota rents are equal to c + g
- If domestic firms own the quota licenses, quota rents stay in home
- In this case, there is a terms of trade gain (g) and lose b and d
- If the licenses are granted to foreign firms, then the quota rents leave home
- Total surplus is a loss of b, c, and d

**Tariff Rate Quotas**

- First, a quota level is set, represented by q
- Then, two different tariffs are set, can be specific or ad valorem, one for units in the quota, and one for units outside the quota level  
- Consumer surplus decreases
- Producer surplus increases
- Govt revenue increases
- Quota rents are determined by the difference between Pq and (Pq + ti)

**Trade Policy in Monopoly**

- Until now, we assume that the import competing sector is perfect competition
- This means they are price takers, and produce where P = MC
- With trade and tariffs, they produce to world price or tariff price
- Monopolies are not price takers, they are price setters
- Only one firm, so it faces the market demand
- For perfectly competitive firms, their demand is flat since they are so small
- For monopoly firms, their demand is the same as market demand
- Marginal revenue is no longer consistent
- To sell an additional unit of a good, the monopoly must lower the price on all units
- This is assuming no price discrimination
- Marginal revenue is p + -slope x q
- Or, P = A - bq where the slope is b and A is the y intercept
- B = dp/dq
- Therefore, MR = A - 2bq
- This means that MR has the same vertical intercept as demand, but is twice as steep
- Firm maximizes profits where MR = MC

- Monopoly output is where MR = MC, but price is equal to demand
- Area above MC and below price is producer surplus, area above price and below demand is consumer surplus, and area between MC and demand is deadweight loss
- Difference between supply and demand at the world price is the quantity of imports where supply is represented by the MC curve
- Consumer behaviour is the same as in perfect competition
- This graph only shows variable costs and not fixed costs, the area under MC up until Qm is just the variable costs
- If average cost is above the demand line, this is not profitable and if it's below demand, it's profitable

**Monopoly in Free Trade**

- We assume that we're in a small country, and the monopoly is in an import competing sector
- Free trade limits the market power of the monopoly by creating an alternative via imports

- With the implementation of free trade, the demand curve of the monopoly is the squiggly green line
- Since it acts like a perfectly competitive firm, it's demand follows the free trade price and is downward sloping below that point
- Monopoly demand is perfectly elastic at Pw
- Still follows same principle, but now MR is the squiggly line and it produces at MC = new MR (Qf)
- Since MR has the same y intercept as D but twice the slope, since there is no slope the two lines are the same
- At output levels where P is below Pw, MR will slope down and separate from D
- The gap between monopoly production (Qf) and demand for the good (Qw) is the imports (i)
- Compared to Qm, production decreased due to trade, and so did the price
- If we implement a specific tariff, it just moves the horizontal line up by the value of the tariff
- This increases price and production, but decreases imports
- Provides protection to the monopoly, but not completely
- Say instead of an import tariff, instead we have an import quota
- The monopolist would still be affected, but their demand would be the market demand less the quota amount
- This is very different compared to the tariff example, since the firm no longer has to rely on the world price at quantities beyond the quota   
   
   

- The quota just shifts the demand of the monopolist left by the amount of the quota
- The difference between pre quota production and post quota production is the quota amount
- Whoever has the quota license can sell the quantity of the quota at monopoly prices
- Price is higher under quota compared to tariff
- However, distortion losses are higher under the quota since it allows more market power
- Marginal revenue is less than the price of the good, no price discrimination
- Demand function is q = a - b(p) where a is horizontal intercept and slope is 1/b
- Inverse demand is p = (a/b) - 1/b x q
- Marginal revenue is P - (Q/B)
- Total costs are F + c(Q) where F is fixed costs and c is a constant marginal cost
- Average cost would be F/Q + c
- This average cost curve is downwards sloping, not the expected U shape
- Average cost decreases as quantity produced increases since fixed cost becomes exponentially mitigated although c remains the same
- Monopolists earn profits when P > AC
- Quota rents are equal to Qbar times Pq - Pw
