The Disease

- New strain of the flu created
- Strain of H1N1, recently discovered
- Ran from 1918 to 1920
- Resurgence in 2009 (I got it)
- Extreme flu with pneumonic symptoms
- Most victims died of fluid filled lungs
- Death toll unknown, was often counted as either flu or pneumonia
- Not necessarily unique, many other pandemics
- H2N2, H3N2 both killed many people throughout the 19th century
- Novelty is the driving force behind pandemics
- When there is no previous knowledge and exposure about the virus, it spreads and kills quickly
- Infected about of a third of the entire human population
- Killed about 2.5 to 5% of the global population
- In some places more than a third of the population was infected, up to 90%
- Severity varied in local communities, some had much higher or lower death rates
- Much like covid, race played a big role in pandemic experience
- 24% of American First Nations living on reservations caught the flu
- African Americans actually had lower average infection rates than white people
- Some outliers were Japan
- Had a much higher than average infection rate, but lower than average death rate
- Could have been one of many factors, like misreporting of deaths
- Weird age trends, generally diseases are worse for youngest and oldest people
- Spanish flu disproportionately affected 20-29 year-olds
- Key factor in why Spanish flu had such a high death count
- Despite name, Spanish flu did not originate from Spain
- Likely source was around Kansas
- American troops take the flu to France and it spreads through Europe
- Spain was painted as the source of flu even though Britain and France were just as bad
- Likely because US reporters couldn't report on allied nations (Britain and France), and so they reported only on Spain
- Another theory is that the Spanish Flu originated in Northern China
- Chinese Labour Corp travel to Midwest US and give it to WW1 soldiers taking it to Europe
- Mutates once in France and becomes severe
- Not caused by troops coming home, but by US troops mobilizing
- Japanese historians report Spanish flu coming to Japan from sumo wrestlers touring in Taiwan
- Reported in April 1918, supports story that it originated in China

World War 1

- Until March 1918, WW1 would have been the first war where more people died of combat rather than dying of disease
- No correlation between involvement in combat and spread of disease
- Noncombat places like New Zealand were just as affected as Germany
- Demobilization was not a factor in the spread of the disease
- Does play a role by keeping people in close proximity
- Also encouraged a lot of mobility, soldiers being deployed often spread disease
- Civilians crammed in air raid shelters, soldiers crammed in military bases
- Doctors and nurses were more concerned with helping wounded soldiers
- Shortage of food and coal among civilians led to more vulnerability
- Little media coverage of the flu
- March 1918 was a key point in the war, took up most of the media's attention
- Medical knowledge was unprepared for a flu this fatal and contagious
- Some medical professionals overlooked the true severity of the disease

Science and Alternatives

- No vaccination, no prior experience
- Doctors didn't know how to avoid fluid filling lungs
- Many different home remedies brought up like violet leaf tea or goose grease poultices
- Huge boom in various remedies and preventative measures for Spanish flu
- Some inadvertently spread the infection, or even harmed customers
