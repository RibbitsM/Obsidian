Causes of Mexican Revolution

- Several key figures
- Porfirio Diaz was overthrown by Francisco Madero, Emiliano Zapata, and Pancho Villa
- Lazaro Cardenas implemented some revolutionary policies
- Economic policies of the Diaz regime led to unrest
- Mainly in rural regions
- Creates economic recession immediately before revolution
- El Porfiriato 1876-1911
- System of liberal economic policies
- Transformation of the Mexican economy to liberal principles
- In Mexico liberal economics were rarely accompanied by liberal social policy
- Huge boom in railroad construction creates export economy
- Land taken from indigenous population and given to big land owners
- Rural communities suffer but the elite and government benefit greatly
- Improved transportation leads to larger agriculture market
- Contrary to common theme, caused more hardship than good
- Dependency on foreign firms, did not reflect desires of working class
- Key group being targeted was Mexican peasants who owned land privately or communally
- Farmers were also being displaced and forced into new industries
- Created a cheap labour pool for factories
- Economic recession from 1907-1909
- Challenge to state authority
- Elites became more hostile to the government
- Urban unemployment skyrockets
- Government faces dissatisfaction from workers and elites, combined with unrest from farmers
- Diaz has waning support, calls election to assert authority
- Idea is to transfer power to a successor who shares his ideals
- Francisco Madero leads the anti-Diaz party
- Madero is jailed on election day, flees to the US
- Returns from the US to lead revolts in the north of Mexico
- Madero becomes president for the next two years
- Mexican revolution ran from 1910 to 1920
- Some reforms are made, but many disagree with the pace of reforms
- Disagreement leads to a civil war to overthrow Madero's government
- US intervenes in the conflict to protect state interests

Experience of Women and Men

- Millions of Mexicans took up arms to defend interests
- Soldiers lived miserable lives
- Resources scarce, little food, no support from government or organization
- All governments were unstable and underfunded
- Relied on support from female volunteers and foraged for food
- Peasants would shelter soldiers in their houses
- Civilians suffered greatly
- Death toll huge, 1-2 million died
- Food scarcity and inflation in the price of food
- Value of the peso dropped dramatically
- Millions of peasants still supported the war
- Both women and men were soldiers
- Called "camp followers" sometimes
- Some female commanders and colonels
- Paid a key role in shaping gender roles post-revolution
- Influenced policies like the 1917 constitution
- Pushed gender equality and women's suffrage

Outcomes

- Constitution of 1917 written after the civil war
- Secularization document, separated catholic church from state
- Also removed the church from education
- Promised free government education in it's place
- Two key documents
- Article 27: Land
- Entitles the state to expropriate property for the public good
- Allows government to give land back to the peasants
- Appealed to farmers hurt by the previous regime
- Article 123: Labour
- Important labour laws and protections
- 6 day work week, 8 hours a day
- Provided protection for pregnant mothers
- Demanded equal gender pay
- Allowed for workers to form unions and go on strike
- Actual implementation of these articles doesn't occur until later
- Lazaro Cardenas implements articles from 1934-1940
- Distributes 50 million acres of land to peasants
- Took 15 years for the policy to be implemented
- New political party established, Partido Revolucionario Institutional
- Redefines Mexican politics
- Represent the lasting impact of the revolution
- In 1933 Mexico expropriates all foreign oil and gas companies
- Huge education reform
- 3000 new schools built
- Enrolment and literacy raise all across the country

Conclusion

- Mexican Revolution was a reaction to Diaz's dictatorship
- Revolution set the foundation for modern Mexico
- Ideology of the 1910s and 20s shaped future politics
- Part of a wider struggle for land rights and representation in Latin America
