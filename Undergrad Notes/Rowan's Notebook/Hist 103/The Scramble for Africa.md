- Africa was a hotbed for competition between European forces from 1880 into the 1910s
- 1900-1914 was called high imperialism in Africa
- The goal of this scramble was to take control of the most possible unclaimed space in Africa
- Prior to the scramble there were only a few small coastal colonies in Africa
- With new technology the pace of colonization was ramped up significantly, starting with these small colonies
- New commodities were being discovered in Africa and were highly sought after
- New progress as well, advances in medical technology allowed Europeans to survive African diseases easily
- Over the course of 30 years, 90% of Africa was claimed by European powers
- As more countries joined, the fear of missing out on the rush gathered new powers
- Land was handed out to whoever could lay claim to it first
- Part of a larger global phenomenon happening in China, India, Burma, Afghanistan
- European powers were rushing to claim land with new technology worldwide

**Berlin Conference**

- 1884-1885, European powers, US, and Ottoman Empire meet
- Purpose was to establish the rules for how Africa could be colonized, what country owns what territory
- News of the conference spurred more frenzy for the colonization of Africa, increased hurry
- Metaphor of cutting a pie usually used, effectively they were dividing up the land of Africa between the countries at the conference
- Literal rush, the rules established stated that the first company with a trading and military presence owned it
- No African countries were involved in the conference, very Eurocentric view of the exploration process
- Lasted from 1884-1885
- The focus of the conference was redrawing the map of Africa and dividing it up into colonies
- Unheard of pace of colonialism, faster than any other previous era of expansion
- First time countries have been competing for colonial territory like this
- The conference was established to control this kind of unprecedented level of competition
- Prerequisite to claim land in Africa was only a little trade and a military presence
- Huge amounts of British soldiers sent to Africa to put down resistance
- Fights among indigenous people and British armies very common
- Existing infrastructure used as a springboard for expansion
- Claims were usually political only, borders were pretty flexible
- Borders drawn based on European trade routes rather than Indigenous populations
- Rival ethnic groups lumped into the same countries because of European mapmaking
- Mapmaking done from offices in Europe rather than by the officials in Africa

**Causes and Motivations**

- Main commodities were oil, rubber, gold, diamonds, palm oil, bauxite, cotton, ivory, and cocoa
- All were extracted en masse to Europe via new infrastructure
- Earlier the main focuses of expansion were Egypt and South Africa
- Until the 1830s, the common belief was that inner Africa was an inhospitable desert
- In the 1880s the resistance from indigenous groups increased
- The Europeans had a massive technological advantage but still struggled to deal with insurrections
- In the 1890s, the Ethiopian resistance (most significant resistance) begins
- The Abyssinian military was much more sophisticated than other African nations
- They were able to match the Italian military and establish their independence
- Another reason Africa wasn't explored more was disease
- 70% of soldiers died of malaria and sleeping sickness before the 1880s
- Advances in health technology allowed Europeans to overcome these "tropical diseases"
- Advances made during this time are still used and studied today
- An example of conflict in Africa was the Herero and Namaqua Genocide
- In 1903, 60 Germans were killed by the Herero people
- They killed them to send a message after the Germans repeatedly stole cattle from the Herero
- In response, over the next 3 years from 1904-1907, the Germans sent 20,000 soldiers to completely wipe out the Herero and Namaqua people
- This was considered "the first genocide of the 20th century"
- Over 50,000 people were killed
- Germany justified it as "quashing a rebellion"
- These techniques of genocide were seen in later German military efforts in the world wars
- Several officials in charge of the Herero Genocide were involved in the world wars
- Another example was the Congo Free State, a colony of the Belgian king
- The colony was essentially a slave labour regime focused on rubber production
- Any sign of laziness or rebellion was violently repressed, many workers were killed or mutilated
- Most officials in Europe condemned the colony, led to the book Heart of Darkness being written
- Workers were all captured from the indigenous population
- These practices led to a huge population decline
- Some estimates say 75% of the population died as a direct result of the regime
- Many indigenous workers also died of sleeping sickness carried from other parts of Africa by the Belgians
- Colonization disrupted Africa in other ways too
- Traditional agriculture customs were replaced by European farming techniques
- Local staple foods were replaced by cash crops
- Colonial cities displace indigenous people living in the areas and spread diseases to locals and the environment
- Slave trading moves people from different tribes into new areas, disrupts relations
- Increased poverty and marginalization
- War was very common, almost all people in Africa were directly affected by it

**Conclusion**

- Driven by economics, pursuit of materials, and competition
- Major expansion in Africa from 1880 to 1914
- Africans resisted initially and later on as rebels
