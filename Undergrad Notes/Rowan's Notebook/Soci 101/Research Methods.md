- Essential to social science research

Tuskegee Experiment

- Black men in the American south intentionally given syphilis
- Subjects unaware what was happening to them, lied to
- Subjects were not cured even after the cure was discovered
- Study lasted from 1932 all the way to 1972

Ethics Requirements

- Informed consent, voluntary participation, confidentiality, and no harm done are all required for an ethically sound experiment
- The point of an experiment should always be to further society and research
- Social science experiments often collect sensitive information
- Data collected could harm subjects reputations or expose them to crime

2 Types of Research

- Quantitative Research: analysis based on numbers (eg what number of people, what percent)
- Qualitative Research: analysis based on abstract concepts and meanings (eg how do people feel about x, what people prefer x)
- Best is a combination of both, maybe use the quantitative experiment as the base for the qualitative one

Experiments:

- Controlled environment where 1 variable is isolated and studied
- 1st step is to randomly assign subjects to control and exposure groups
- Has to be random, otherwise not a true experiment
- Every experiment needs a control group where they are not affected by the research and exist as context for the exposure groups
- Experiments are an example of quantitative research
- Upsides: reveals cause and effect, unlike other methods of research
- Downsides: Can be ethically problematic because of testing negative phenomena such as mental distress etc
- Controlled environment can lead to unrealistic situations that aren't similar enough to real life

Natural Experiments

- Naturally occurring control and exposure groups observed
- Subjects are not randomly assigned into these groups, difference from the true experiment
- Otherwise analysis is handled similarly to true experiments

Surveys

- Administering a predetermined set of close ended questions
- Analyzed quantitatively (open ended surveys are analyzed qualitatively)
- Upsides: fast and inexpensive
- Can be done through many different mediums
- Can gain lots of data from a wide variety of people
- Expensive/complex surveys can represent an entire population
- Can be used by other studies to answer future questions
- Can be easily confidential
- Downsides: generate low response rates (distrust of institutions)
- Wording/order can affect responses significantly
- Stigma reduces honest answers

Participant Observation

- Researcher participates and interacts with the social world they are studying
- "people watching" collecting info in the setting they want to observe
- Usually qualitative data (quantitative could be "how many times did x happen")
- Upsides: generates very detailed and realistic data
- Can observe behavior in a natural, specific setting
- Downsides: Very time consuming and expensive
- Very limited in sample size, about 100 absolute max
- Results may not be replicable
- Difficult to gain trust of subjects, never a truly natural setting

Qualitative Interviews

- Researchers ask open ended questions to get information on more complicated topics
- Can be used in combination with participant observation or separately
- Upsides: Can cover topics much more complex than other methods
- Can ask follow up questions unlike surveys
- Uses the wording of subject, not of the researcher
- Downsides: Not generalizable, usually specific to those interviewed
- Hard to replicate
- Takes specific skills to ask effective and truly open questions
- Small sample size

Historical/Content Analysis

- Uses existing sources as data
- Usually qualitative but can be quantitative
- Uses sources not initially intended as research material (tv shows, economic reports etc)
- Upsides: Can study phenomena not visible through other methods
- Can investigate patterns that emerge over time
- Can study things that occurred in the past
- Downsides: Can't control quality of the source data
- Data can be limited, and you can't collect more

Other Existing Data

- Any other kind of data analysis that's not historical
- Can pull from government, business, public, personal records
- Can take research data from other fields and reuse it for social sciences
- Can be qualitative or quantitative
- Upsides: Can answer questions impossible for social science experiments
- Downsides: Can't control data quality

Choosing a Method

- No best method
- Each method is a tool suited for different topics
- Some are more resource intensive than others
- Important to recall research goals when choosing your method

Research Steps

1. Determine research question
2. State hypothesis (not necessary, especially for qualitative studies)
3. Gather data
4. Analyze data
5. Develop conclusions
6. Before step 1, review all prior research
7. Have to make sure your question hasn't been researched before, if it has you at least have to improve on or update the original research

Variables

- Observable characteristics that can have more than one possible answer/value
- Determined by unit of analysis: what you're gathering data on
- Unit can be people, countries, corporations etc.

Ex1: peoples feelings on climate change  
Unit: individuals  
Variable: attitude towards climate change  
Ex2: environmental policies of nations  
Unit: nations  
Variable: policies on environment

- Independent Variable (X)
- Causes change in the dependent variable
- Dependent Variable (Y)
- Object of research, affected by the independent variable
- Researcher wants to study how men's attitudes about masculinity affect likelihood to go to the doctor
- Going to the doctor is dependent, attitudes on masculinity is independent

Hypothesis

- Statement on how variables are related
- EX: when men consider themselves more "masculine", they go to the doctor less

Operationalization

- How to measure your object of study
- How to measure abstract concepts such as satisfaction or displeasure
- Ex: using a 7 point scale for a survey question, or measuring number of complaints
- How a subject is operationalized is dependent on the question, method of research, and budget

Population vs Samples

- Population is the total of units you want to learn about
- Units can be companies, people, countries etc.
- Studying an entire population is a census
- Sample: smaller group that represents a whole population
- Can be chosen from a sampling frame (listing of units in that population)
- Ex: list of top 500 companies, phone book of New York

Types of Samples

- Random Samples: every member of the population must have a chance of being chosen
- For this, researchers must know every member of the population they aim to study for it to truly be random
- Allows researchers to generalize findings from a random representative sample
- All members of the population have a chance of being chosen, but not necessarily the same chance
- Non Random Samples: Not all members of the population have a chance of being sampled
- Not representative, can't generalize findings
- Usually used because of limitations, when budget is low or a sampling frame does not exist
- EX: homeless people or drug addicts
- Non random samples can also be specifically selected, this is usually how samples for interviews are done

Systematic Biases

- Different from individual bias, this is a pattern in your method of research that can lead to biased results
- Nonresponse Bias: Who does and who doesn't respond to a survey can cause a sample to be unrepresentative
- This can be due to different factors such as levels of enthusiasm, comfort of topic, method of data collection, characteristics of groups
- Can also be an issue with sampling frames, like university students not appearing on address lists
- Ex: in the US polling always seems to bias towards democrats, due to republicans being less likely to admit their beliefs and answer polls
- Bias can also come from non-random samples and self selected studies (like a social media ad)
- Small samples also create bias

Correlation

- Measure of how 2 variables are related, not causation, simply an observed relationship

Causation

- When one variable directly affects another variable
- Extremely difficult to establish causation
- First a correlation must be established
- Must determine which change occurred first (independent must change first)
- Finally you must rule out alternative and spurious relationships

Spurious Relationship

- Appears to be causation between two variables, but isn't
- Outside factor creates the correlation
- EX: space launches and sociology doctorates occur at the same time of year, look correlated on a graph

Validity and Reliability

- Reliability: A measure is consistent (questions asked the same way, and are understood the same way)
- Validity: A measure is accurate and appropriate to the research question

Selective or Inaccurate Observation

- Confirmation bias is a huge problem in research fields
- Confirmation bias is when you more readily accept information that reinforces your beliefs and you avoid information that challenges your beliefs
- Bias in research whether intentional or unintentional, harms research
- Confirmation bias cannot be removed, but being aware of it helps

Resistance to Change

- In a similar vein, people ignore evidence that heavily contradicts their worldview
- Ideally, all researchers are neutral, but in absence of that researchers clearly state their biases and conflicts of interest
- Previously established patterns are not disproven by exceptions to patterns
- No matter what, all social patterns have exceptions
- Non representative or non random samples do not reflect broader social patterns

Illogical Reasoning

- Individuals have a tendency to jump to conclusions without sufficient evidence
- No matter how much you believe in a theory, sufficient evidence is always required and alternatives must be considered

Misleading Data

- Simpson's Paradox: when the same data set can produce seemingly opposite conclusions based on how it's seen
- A hidden variable can hide the true conclusion, only seen when looking closer
- Graphs can also be misleading, usually by messing with the scale or time period
- Proper graphs should have consistent time frames and logical scales

Mis/Disinformation

- Misinformation: accidental spread of misleading or poorly made information
- Disinformation: intentionally spread false information made to mislead
- Often times disinformation is created and spread through misinformation
