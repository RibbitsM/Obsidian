- Deviance: attitudes behaviours and conditions that violate social norms
- Not a moral measure, just different from social norms
- Ideas of deviance are collective, not personal
- Some common things can be deviant
- Not all uncommon things are deviant
- Deviance differs across time, culture, context, etc.
- Social Control: how societies influence behavior
- Red hair is uncommon, but not deviant
- Pooping is common, but it is considered deviant to discuss
- Deviance is different across gender, women in the workplace often go to other floors or buildings to poop
- Menstruation is extremely common but considered deviant in some places (menstrual huts)
- Norms: Rules and expectations that guide behavior, shaped by values, often informal
- Folkways: informal norms, least serious (customs, etiquette)
- Mores: Norms considered very important, often formal
- Laws: codified norms (can be folkways, mores, taboos)
- Taboos: Norms that are almost never okay to defy (cannibalism)
- Smoking in a park: folkway, Drinking and driving: More

Conditions for Deviance

1. A norm or rule exists
2. Someone violates a rule or they are perceived as violating it
3. People judge this violation to be wrong
4. There is likelihood that people will react negatively
5. Violation can have a positive reaction, in this context there is no deviance
6. EX: activism

Social Change

- In the last year, many kinds of physical contact have become deviant because of covid tension
- Coughing in public has become much more deviant
- Going to work/school being sick is deviant now
- New norm, many schools/workplaces no longer emphasize still attending while sick

Stigma

- When a characteristic is considered undesirable and it incurs negative consequences
- Can be individual or collective
- Things that are stigmatized vs deviant changes across time, culture, context
- Very few things are universally stigmatized

Moral Panics

- Temporary and intense concern about an issue, usually associated with intense hostility towards a group
- EX: Red Scare in America, Jews in Nazi Germany, Satanic Panic in 1990s prairies
- Fear always out of proportion to threat
- Moral Entrepreneurs: People or groups who raise moral panics
- Another example was moral panic over teenage sexting via snapchat
- Wasn't very common, only 7%
- Classic example of moral panic, reaction was disproportionate to danger

Origins of Deviance

- Labelling Theory: Idea that people become deviants because they were labelled as such (self fulfilling prophecy)
- When you are treated like a deviant, more likely to be one
- Opportunity Theory: Idea that some people have more access to subcultures or resources are more likely to become deviant
- Theory of Differential Association: Idea that deviance is a learned behavior like any other
- Control Theory: Idea that ties to mainstream groups and culture make people less likely to be deviant, and weak ties make them more likely

Functionalist Frameworks

- Functionalists believe that deviants strengthen social cohesion
- Specifically negative reactions to deviance emphasizes what is right and wrong
- Immediate representation of what is and isn't expected
- Sometimes the response of society to deviance maintains social stability
- EX: introduction of vaccine passport increased vaccination rate
- Social change usually occurs when many people start accepting what was once considered deviant
- In Canada smoking weed and being lgbtq+ is much more acceptable than it once was

Strain Theory

- When there is a mismatch between the goals of individuals and the means they have to achieve them
- 5 responses: Conformity, Innovation, Ritualism, Retreatism, Rebellion   
   

Conformity Innovation
              
Ritualism Retreatism Rebellion
       
- Either reject or accept cultural goals, and either reject or accept institutionalised means
- Rebellion is rejection of both AND new goals and means
- Conformity is succumbing to culture and institution
- Ritualism is accepting an institution but not the goals (just going through motions)
- Innovation is achieving the goals via new means
- Retreatism is rejecting both through becoming a hermit or drug addict or similar route

Conflict Theory Responses

- Rules and norms are shaped mainly by those who are in power
- Dominant groups choose what is deviant, helps maintain power
- Ideology of society is shaped by dominant groups
- Dominant groups tend to label ideas and people that challenge them as "deviant"
- Establish hegemony through force and consent
- Consent can be seen through a minority group choosing to accept the dominant groups ideology, but elements of force are present too
- Force and consent must both be present for a hegemony to exist

Medicalization

- Process of problems previously considered nonmedical becoming medical
- Identification of new illnesses/disorder
- EX: erectile dysfunction and female sexual dysfunction have such broad definitions currently that it applies to most of the population
- Expands definition of deviance, narrowing who is normal and doesn't need "fixing"

Crime

- Many factors are part of leading someone to commit a crime
- Social networks, institutions, and media all aspects
- Crime is almost always a result of environment rather than personality
- Structural inequalities often lead to more crime
- Must use sociological imagination to understand crime
