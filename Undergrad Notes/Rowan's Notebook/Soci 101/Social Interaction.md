The looking-glass self and the generalized other 

- Looking-glass self: the way our perception of how others see us affect our sense of self 
- Generalized other: the values and norms of the larger culture that individuals use to guide their actions
- Our sense of self develops from our social experiences, interactions, and place in social structure 
- Reference group: a group individuals use as a comparison to guide their own beliefs, values, and behaviours, whether or not they are a part of that group

**Dramaturgy** 

- Erving Goffman’s theory of social interactions that uses the framework of theatre performances to describe how people behave and represent themselves 
    
    - The “audience” gives individuals a certain amount of leeway, forgiving small mistakes, but the interaction breaks down when the “performance” is no longer credible 
- Impression management: how people try to control how other people perceive them
    
    - Facial expressions, mannerism, personal appearance, what someone says and how they say it
- Face: the particular “image” someone presents of themselves, which depends on the situation and audience
    
    - “Saving face” or “facework”: how individuals make adjustments based on mistakes or “faux paus” they make 
    - Prop: object that one uses to maintain their "face", also called sign-vehicles (EX: police officers uniform)
    - Props can be almost anything, architecture of a classroom vs meeting room vs a concert
    - Help indicate what your role should be in a certain scenario

**Emotions**

- Feeling rules: a concept from Arlie Hochschild that refers to social norms that shape the emotions people experience and express in different situations 
- Emotional management: people work to change how they feel - or how they appear to feel - based on these norms
    
    - People produce or inhibit emotions based on the situation
- Emotional labour: how people manage their emotions in ways that are required by their occupation 

The social construction of reality  
   
**Thomas theorem:** "If men (people) define situations as real, they are real in their consequences." Most aspects of society are socially constructed, people have made their social surroundings.  
   
**Social Structure:** Set of social statuses, roles, groups, networks, and influences that organize and influence the way people live.  
   
==Social structures are beyond individual actions, but shaped by them==  
   
Social structures don’t determine individual actions, but do influence attitudes, behavior etc  
   
**Resources:** things people have that they can use to accomplish their goals

- Money, education, status, knowledge, etc

   
==Access to resources shapes== **life chances (Weber)**==: chances to get material goods, good experiences, living conditions, etc.==  
   
**Rules:** formal (laws, etc) and informal (social norms) expectations for behavior in a given situation.  
 

1. **Social Networks**
    
    1. Series of social relationships that link a person directly to other individuals and indirectly to even more people
    2. Exist all throughout society, can yield huge opportunities, or constrain access to resources
    3. Strength of weak ties: even weak ties in network structures can yield huge opportunities. Many people get jobs etc through weak ties.
    4. Norms, values, emotions also shaped by networks
    5. "It’s not what you know, it’s who you know"
    6. Examples: UBC and its internal organizations, family, friend group, neighbours, religious organizations
    7. Not necessarily mutual
2. **Social Statuses**
    
    1. ==Someone's socially determined role in society==, also prestige etc associated with that position
    2. Ascribed status: not chosen (involuntary) , age, racial identity, place individuals in social hierarchies, influence the resources they have access to
    3. Achieved status: something people need to try to get (voluntary). Usually chosen and worked towards.
3. **Roles**
    
    1. **Set of expectations of the behaviour of someone with a particular role**
    2. Role strain: one role requires too much effort (parenting etc)
    3. Role conflict: when multiple roles conflict with one another (working, being a student)
4. **Groups**
    
    1. ==Two or more people with similar values or expectations who interact with one another on a regular basis.== A group isn't transitory, there must be repeated interaction.
    2. Can act as external behaviour (peer pressure)
    3. ==Networks: made of many groups==
    4. Primary groups: members not interchangeable, lasting relationships. The deepest relationships we have in your lives. (family members, partners etc)
    5. Secondary groups: more impersonal, goal oriented, may be temporary, may have organized structures, rules, leaders etc (coworkers, this lecture, sports team)
    6. Has to be mutual
5. **Institutions**
    
    1. **The central domains of social life that guide behaviours and meet basic social needs** (education, family, religion, media, laws, government)
    2. Define, govern, constrain actions
    3. Governed by norms
    4. Can establish social order and perpetuate inequalities
    5. ==Exist beyond the individual buy can change over time==
    6. Institutions are most visible when they break down
    7. Trust in Canadian institutions is around 50%, religious institutions are the only ones that are deeply distrusted

   
**Socialization:** a process by which we learn to adhere to written and unwritten social rules.

- Learning culture, expectations associated with societal roles. ==Learn how to become people, to live in society==
- Gives an identity
- Occurs throughout life, but lots in childhood
- ==4 major agents of socialization (there are others):==
    
    - ==Family==
    - ==School==
    - ==Peers==
    - ==Media==

**Structure and agency:** everyone is socialized to behave a particular way, people's actions are constrained by their social position.  
   
**Agency:** Acting on one's own will within social constraints

**Crash Course Sociology: Socialization**

- Who do we learn about the social world from? Begins with families, source of primary socialization.
    
    - Race socialization: children learn racism or racial identity
    - Class socialization: learn about social position, class stuff
