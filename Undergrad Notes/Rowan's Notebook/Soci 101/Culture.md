- A collection of practices, rituals, values, norms, ideologies, beliefs, languages, and objects that are created by people
- Very difficult to define, not accurate definition
- Like institutions, above individuals but affected by them
- Constantly in a state of change
- Often "taken for granted", only notice when you experience a different culture for the first time
- Values: beliefs about morals, what is good or bad
- Norms: informal rules/expectations that shape behavior, based on norms usually
- Beliefs: ideas people believe to be true
- Ideology: A set of beliefs that shape your life and perspective
- Symbols: objects assigned meaning by a culture (can be material or immaterial)
- Rituals: Important routine activities done as a group (like weddings)
- Material Culture: physical things that represent a culture (Canadian coins)
- Symbolic Culture: immaterial aspects of culture like language, beliefs, etc. (words "loonie and toonie")
- Culture Wars: friction between cultures
- Few Canadians think that culture wars are a problem in our country
- Vs more polarized countries like the US and South Africa
- Culture wars can also happen between countries, just anywhere where cultures clash

Symbols, Values, and Norms

- Culture: combinations of things and ideas
- Ideas made of symbols, values, and norms
- Language also includes tone of voice, slang, etc.
- Sapir Whorf hypothesis: cultural lens created by language
- In Spanish and German the moon is different genders
- Spanish speakers associated the moon with feminine adjectives more often
- Values affect social structure
- Individualism vs Collectivism
- 3 types of norms
- Folkway: small unspoken rules with no real punishment, just generally weird (facing the wall in the elevator)
- Mores: More codified laws, tend to have consequences, not universal so sometimes might be allowed sometimes not (Taking your shirt off is bad at work, but not at the beach)
- Taboo: Behaviors that are never allowed, like cannibalism
- Murder is not taboo, because it's allowed in self defense, war etc.
- Norms are an invisible force, hard to see without looking in from the outside
- High Culture: cultural objects or concepts consumed by elite groups (gourmet food, fine art)
- Socioeconomic class: Groups who have the same amount of resources like income, education, occupation
- Consumer Culture: cultures where consumption of goods has a significant meaning and is tied to your identity
- Conspicuous Consumption: Clearly displaying consumption of excessively expensive items, or services like travel in order to gain prestige

Culture Industry

- Industries that focus on creating mass culture
- Big industries are music, tv, film, advertising, radio, podcasts
- Most cultural products are very uniform and standardized, diversity is generally limited
- Pseudo Individuality: Illusion of self definition through consumption of products
- In reality choices are curated by the culture industry

Corporate Consolidation: acquisition of smaller companies by larger ones leading to an industry where a few large companies control massive swaths of culture

- This results in a very homogenous cultural landscape

Culture and Inequality

- By a staggering margin, every aspect of western culture in dominated by white men
- Cultural Capital: cultural or non economic resources like knowledge, skills, mannerisms, tastes, appearances, hobbies, behaviours
- Informal, distinguishes groups of people by "tastes"
- Example of formal cultural capital would be education
- Types of cultural capital can be helpful based on the environment you are in or field
- Field: type of people who come from the same community, profession, class
- Habitus: An instinctual knowledge of how to navigate the world, feels like "common sense"
- Habitus is learned, not inherited but class, race, gender and many other aspects of status influence your habitus
- EX: in schools, higher class people are more likely to ask for grade changes
- Here, cultural capital is the knowledge of what to ask the teacher, what tone of voice to use, etc.

Subcultures and Countercultures

- Subcultures are groups within the dominant culture with distinguishing features from the rest of the culture
- Some minority religious groups, immigrant communities
- Countercultures reject the mainstream values of the dominant culture and replace them with their own values
- Hippies, terrorist organization
- Each persons cultural background is unique, can belong to more than one
- Low culture vs high culture
- Low culture is also called pop culture
- High culture appeals to a specific audience
- Groups with most power and influence become mainstream
- Melting pot idea is a misnomer, cultures are still dominant
- Christianity vs all other religions
- Ethnocentrism: judging other cultures from the lens of one
- Multiculturalism: advocates for all cultures to have equal standing
- Structural functionalist view cultures as necessary for cohesiveness in society
- Conflict theorists recognize the struggle between sub and mainstream cultures
- Counter cultures can become mainstream, like hippies in the 1960s vs now
- Inventions change culture like phones, tomatoes, etc.
- Cultural Diffusion: how cultures leak into each other and build new cultures
- As globalization increases, cultural lines become fuzzier

Internet Subcultures

- Social theorists never predicted the rise of the internet and it's subcultures
- The internet breeds subcultures like no other medium in history
- Social media also creates massive amounts of political polarization
- Hostility between political parties has increased drastically
- Dating apps are now a more common way to meet online
- This has been a huge change for LGBTQ people who use dating apps much more frequently than straight people

Globalization

- Exchange of cultural values between cultures worldwide
- Hybridity: new cultural practices and norms created by cultural exchange
- This concept is very noticeable in food and fashion
- EX: pizza, Italian dish made with ingredients from south America, popularized in north American culture
- Cultural relativism: assessing a culture by it's own standards
- Opposite of ethnocentrism (assessing culture negatively because it is different from your own)
- Multiculturalism: Diversity of cultures within one society (most effective with cultural relativism)
- Cultural Imperialism: when one culture imposes it's values on another
- US is one of the greatest offenders of cultural imperialism
- Western culture has spread worldwide, American companies are more widespread than any others
- Can be intentional or unintentional, sometimes just symptom of cultural growth
- Local cultures sometimes die out as a result
- Second-hand clothing markets are big in Africa, clothes come form thrift stores in North America
- However this kneecaps local clothing stores trying to compete
- Different versions of McDonalds worldwide, is a part of many cultures (Anya's spicy Singapore McDonalds fries)
