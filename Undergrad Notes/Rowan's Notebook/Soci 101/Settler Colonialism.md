Terminology

- Indian: legal identity of an indigenous person registered under the Indian Act
- Offensive term, should only be used in a legal context
- Native: a term referring to metis, first nations and Inuit collectively, mostly use "indigenous" instead
- Aboriginal: used in the Constitution Act of 1982 and includes metis, first nations and Inuit, legal term only
- Indigenous: Word used to refer to First Nations, Metis and Inuit collectively, most common or preferred term in Canada
- Settler Colonialism: colonialism where the goal is to acquire land where settlers can permanently inhabit it
- In contrast to classic colonialism which is where colonists aim to extract resources rather than settling
- Cultural Genocide: act of enslaving a people and attempting to stamp out their culture via forced assimilation
- Ideology of private property ownership forced on colonized areas
- Scope was enormous, almost 90% of the population wiped out, so many people died that the Earth's climate cooled slightly
- Dawes Act of 1887: act where the US Federal government divided collective tribal land into privately owned lots
- Tried to force communities into nuclear families
- Gave little land to each family, rest was divided between white families
- Indigenous land decreased from 138 million acres to 54 million
- Also sent indigenous children to boarding schools

Indian Act

- Canadian Federal Law
- Determines who is classified as "Indian"
- Controls bands and reserves
- Passed in 1876, remains today
- Royal Proclamation of 1763
- King George 3 verified the original occupancy, ownership and hunting rights of first nations people
- First step to signing future treaties between Britain and the first nations of Canada
- Bagot Commission (1842-1844): proposed the separation of children from parents for purposes of assimilation
- Others later suggested indigenous education would be focused on religion and agricultural training
- Gradual Enfranchisement Act of 1869: established elective band councils
- Determined who would be eligible to join a band
- Allowed gender based status restrictions and regulated alcohol consumption
- In 1876 Indian Act seized control over band councils, reserves, and determined who would be eligible for status and membership
- Primary purpose was to forcefully assimilate First Nations people into Canadian society
- Installed an Indian Agent on every reserve to ensure government policy was followed
- Ensured children were being sent to residential schools
- Assured complete control over indigenous people
- The apartheid system of South Africa modelled after this system
- Banned certain ceremonies until 1951
- Various axes of discrimination, limited legal power and property rights of indigenous people
- Only allowed to vote in federal elections in 1960
- Various acts of discrimination against indigenous women, marrying a non status man would strip them of their status
- This act was only removed in 1985

Ongoing Effects of Settler Colonialism

- Indian act is still a part of the Canadian legal system
- Indigenous women are still consistently murdered and go missing
- Indigenous suicide rates are 3x those of non indigenous people
- Mining projects continue to be approved on indigenous land
- In the US COVID death rates in indigenous people are very disproportionately high
- Due to lack of treaty rights (poor living conditions) and multi generational households

Two-Spirit

- Many indigenous people had understanding of gender beyond the European gender binary
- Colonists used the different understanding of gender as justification for genocide
- Term was developed in 1990 but existed much earlier
- Not all tribes had these third or fourth genders but most did
- Each tribe has own term for these genders, Two-Spirit is an umbrella term
- Term is a bridge between indigenous and European understanding of gender
- First established in Manitoba
- Solution to "The Problem of Naming"
- Each nation has a different interpretation of two-spirit
- Traditionally two-spirit people were high status and were given special roles
- The term Two-Spirit was an attempt from indigenous to label themselves
- Many tribes banned two spirit marriages
- In recent years these laws have been overturned

Indigenous Demography

- Entire indigenous population of Canada increased by 42.5% in 10 years
- First Nations increased by 39.3%
- Metis increased by 51.2%
- Inuit increased by 29.1%
- Low proportion of indigenous people in urban areas
- The more remote the community, the more indigenous people
- Southern part of Canada has much fewer indigenous people than the northern part
- 1 in 4 status First Nations are younger than 15
- 1 in 6 non indigenous people are younger than 15
- Indigenous population living off reserve increased dramatically
- Indigenous people have lower employment rate than non indigenous people
- Also have much lower average income
- First Nations living on reserve live in poverty more often than those off reserve
- Huge educational disparity between indigenous and non indigenous
- 45% of households on reserve are in need of repairs
- 2 main reasons for growth
- Natural growth, but also an increase in the amount of people identifying as indigenous
- Investigation into increased number of people falsely claiming indigenous ancestry
- Several politicians have falsely claimed indigenous ancestry
- Misunderstanding of the link between ancestry and race
- Cree is most prevalent indigenous language
- Many are endangered

Northern Indigenous People

- Nunavut is a territory of Canada
- Official language is Inuktut
- Inuktut is an umbrella term for a variety of indigenous languages
- Became an official territory of Canada in 1999
- Created to give Inuit people self-determination
- Higher growth rate than non-Inuit people
- Higher fertility rate
- Population is younger on average
- Forced assimilation present in the north too
- Labelled with "E-tags" or Eskimo tags
- Eskimo is an offensive term that should only be used historically
- Beginning in 1940, RCMP started assigning 4 digit numbers for all Inuit people
- Used to keep track of birth rate, death rate, marriages etc.
- First northern residential school OPENED in 1951
- Inuit only allowed to vote in 1950, but infrastructure wasn't in place until 1962
- The seal hunt is an important Inuit tradition
- Many attempts to ban the seal hunt have been made over the years
- Considered humane and legal by the Canadian government
- Inuit income relies heavily on the legality of selling seal products
