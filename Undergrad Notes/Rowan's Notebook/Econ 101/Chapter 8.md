- 2 ways to maximize profits

Long Run Cost Minimization

- Goal is to find the q of inputs that minimizes costs
- If one input is less expensive, use more
- MPk / Pk = MPn/Pn
- Same as maximizing utility
- Must equate marginal production
- Pk and Pn are given
- Determines how much K and N to get
- If capital is more productive than labour, switch into capital

Principle of Substitution

- As relative P of inputs change, firms shift into cheaper input
- If Pn rises, then MPk/MPn falls
- Hire more capital
- Reaches equilibrium because of LDMP

Long Run Cost Curves

- SRAC is minimum cost of production when one factor is fixed and prices are fixed
- LRAC is minimum cost of production when no factors are fixed and prices are fixed
- LRAC is a boundary of minimum costs, below the curve is impossible, above the curve is inefficient  
- Price of inputs is held constant, technology is constant
- Left side is decreasing costs (increasing returns to scale or economies of scale), middle is constant costs(constant returns to scale), right is increasing costs (decreasing returns to scale or diseconomies of scale)
- In SR you have diminishing returns, in LR you have decreasing returns to scale
- Means as production increases, average cost rises and MP falls
- Specialization of Labour is SR, Division of Labour is LR
- SR is switching jobs, while LR is assembly line
- Why does it look like this?
- Scale means in relation to size of your output
- IRTS means you double inputs, more than double outputs
- Has nothing to do with SR cost curve
- Many reasons why you can increase like this
- Indivisible Factors of Production: Cannot divide up a building, machine, manager. Say you buy a 100,000$ dollar building that barely produces anything, not very efficient. If you scale up your output and make more, you'll get more value out of that FAP, lower costs compared to output
- Division of Labour: When production is large, can divide up labour more efficiently
- Internal Economies: Marketing, Financing, Legal, Management, Research. All expensive departments that are worth more when output increases
- External Economies: Spending money on lowering pollution etc.
- Costs decrease when scale increases because of Economies of Scale
- Why would returns to scale decrease?
- Middle Management Mush: in large companies, more middlemen are involved, slowing down production and costing more
- Alienation of Workforce: As production scale increases, workers feel less motivated, less production for your costs
- These are called Diseconomies of Scale
- Economies of Scale affect most at lower production, Diseconomies of Scale take effect at higher production
- Minimum Efficient Scale is the minimum scale where a company is most efficient   
   

- Similar to capacity output on SR cost curves

Relation Between SRAC and LRAC

- When SRAC and LRAC are at tangency, production is the same
- This occurs when coincidentally, the fixed factor is fixed at the optimal point to maximize production
- Can't lower costs by changing any factor
- When SRAC is left of LRAC, production is lower
- The fixed factor is above the optimal point to maximize production
- In the LR, you can sell fixed input pull down costs further, but can't in SR
- SRAC and LRAC are out of sync
- When SRAC is right of LRAC, production is lower
- The fixed factor is below the optimal point to maximize production
- In the LR you can increase fixed input to pull down costs, but can't in SR
- LRAC is the "envelope" that contains a family of SRACs
- Each point on the LRAC represents a SRAC that is tangent, has the optimal level of the fixed factor  
- At the tangent, SRAC and LRAC have same combo of factors
- Go left or right, and combo of factors is inefficient (above the LRAC)
- However, at any other point on the LRAC there would be a SRAC that is tangent to it
- Say your fixed factor is office space, and variable factor is labour (secretaries)
- In the SR you can hire and fire secretaries, but can't change office space
- For each given size of office, there is an optimal number of secretaries to hire (min of SRAC)
- However, there is also an optimal level of production (how many letters you produce)
- Can only minimize that on LRAC
- If you have too many secretaries, can't increase office space to raise production, can only fire secretaries
- Can't reach optimal production unless on LRAC
- By copying and pasting a ton of SRACs next to eachother, you can see the outline of the LRAC
- Jacob Viner's Mistake: said LRAC is locus of the minimum points of all SRACS
- Wrong, it's the locus of the tangent points
- Tangent point is not necessarily the minimum point
- Tangent is when fixed factor is optimal to level of production
- Variable factor isn't taken into account

Very Long Run: Technological Change

- SR, capital fixed, price fixed, tech fixed, plotted on SRAC
- LR, capital varies, price fixed, tech fixed, plotted on LRAC (envelope of all SRACs)
- VLR, capital varies, price varies, tech varies, shifts of the LRAC
- VLR is about changing the costs of your inputs
- Technological Change: change in productive process
- Productivity: level of output per input
- More output for the same input = more productive
- Used to be exogenous, researchers would find tech individually and businesses would pick up on it
- In modern day is endogenous, firms hire researchers to find tech innovations
- To reduce costs in the LR you substitute away from costly inputs, in VLR you innovate less expensive inputs
- 3 Sources of Technological Change
- New Inputs: titanium or carbon fiber vs iron or steel
- New Techniques: typewriters vs computers
- New Outputs: maps vs GPSs
- Technology lowers the LRAC
- Firms choose to either substitute away (move on the LRAC) or innovate away (shift the entire curve down)

Isoquant Analysis

- 2 ways to maximize profits
- One is to equate marginals
- Other way is to find where isoquant line is tangent to isocost line
- Need to know 3 things
- TC (or budget), price of the factors, and scale of production (level of output)
- Isocost line is like budget line, all possible combinations of different factors given budget and cost of factors
- Conceptually same as the budget line        
- Pk x Qk + Pn x Qn = TC
- Basically the "budget" of a firm
- Slope is -Pk/Pn
- Slope is the ratio of the prices
- Iso means equal, called equal cost line
- Every point on the line has the same TC
- Shifts of the IC line happen when TC or Price of factors change
- If price of capital increases, rotate inward, increase TC and shift right
- Same as BL

Isoquant Line

- Shows all possible combinations of factors that yield the same output
- When moving along the line, TP is the same
- Has all the same characteristics of indifference curves
- Change in Qk x MPk + change in Qn x MPn is 0
- Slope of line is change in Qn/ change in Qk
- Or -MPk/MPn
- Whatever factor is on the horizontal goes in the numerator  
- Condition for profit maximization
- Also just cost minimization
- Different from ID curve analysis
- Start with IQ line
- Find the LOWEST isocost line tangent to the isoquant line
- Unlike ID curve analysis where you try to max utility
- Using your current output, find the lowest possible cost
- Start with IQ line and find the lowest IC line that is tangent  
- Here we see that with the same quantity produced, you can produce it at several isocost levels
- But we choose the lowest one because we want to minimize our costs
- When MPk/Pk = MPn/Pn, cost is minimized
- Isoquant line represents -MPk/MPn, Isocost represents -Pn/Pk
- When both are tangent, cost is minimized
- Identical conditions
