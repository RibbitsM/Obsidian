Market Structure and Firm Behaviour

- Market structure is the genetic features of a market
- Rather than hair/eye colour, height etc. we look at
- Number and size of firms
- Type of good (in terms of whether goods sold are similar or different)
- Whether entry/exit is easy or difficult
- High start-up costs can make things difficult
- These factors affect market power
- Many textbooks add pricing behaviour
- Can also be an effect of the first three
- Market Power: ability of a firm to increase price and keep it there
- Basically influence of price in a market
- Opposite of competition
- If you can maintain market power, there is little competition and vice versa
- Inverse relationship
- Perfectly Competitive Market Structure: there is no competitive behaviour
- Competitive Behaviour: some competition and market power
- Four Market Structures
- Perfect Competition: no market power
- Monopoly: absolute market power:
- Monopolistic Competition: some market power
- Oligopoly: substantial market power
- This chapter focuses on perfect competition
- For each market structure, cost side looks identical to chapter 8

Assumptions

- Assumptions affecting short run
- Assume that there are many, many firms
- All are selling identical goods (homogeneous)
- Because of number of firms, output of each firm is small relative to industry output
- A single firm is irrelevant to the industry as a whole
- One firm closing will not affect price
- Homogeneous goods are very rare, so as long as they are similar within reason it counts
- Examples can be gas, coffee, wheat
- Result of these assumptions is that firms must accept price
- The industry determines price of coffee, not the firm
- The firm is the price taker
- Causes market competition, no firm has market power
- Third assumption in the long run
- Assume that it is cheap to enter the industry
- BTE is very low
- Entry/exit is relatively cheap
- Leads to assumption that firms are producing at their minimum in the long run
- Assuming that firms are the price taker, the industry graph will look like this   
   

- Firm's demand curve is horizontal because they must accept the industry price
- If the firm wants to increase profits by raising price, they will lose customers
- If they firm wants to increase customers by lowering price, they will lose profits
- Demand curve is perfectly horizontal, and therefore perfectly elastic
- Firm can sell all they want, but at the market price

Revenue Curve

- TR = P x Q
- AR = TR/Q
- MR = change in TR/ change in Q
- Remember, no matter quantity sold the price is the same
- Value of AR and MR is P
- Demand is the price because it's a horizontal line
- Very easy
- This is SR only  
- If revenue is constant, how much should you sell?
- Sell up until the point that MR=MC
- First, you must be able to cover TVC and maybe TFC (in the SR)
- You can delay payments for TFC until LR
- But you must pay TVC
- TR must be greater than or equal to TVC
- Then, MB must be greater than or equal to MC   
   

- TR is a straight line
- Slope depends on price
- At the point marked on the graph, profit is maximized
- TR is furthest above the TC
- The slope of both curves are the same at this point
- MC and MR are the slope
- Therefore MC=MR   
   

- Here you can see that MC cuts MR from below at the point of profit maximization
- General methodology is to superimpose the cost curves on top of the demand curve
- First step is to find MC = MR (where scoop and line intersect)
- TR = P1 x Q1 (with Q1 being where MC = MR)
- TC = AC1 x Q1 (where Q1 cuts the AC curve)
- Economic Profit = TR - TC = (P1-AC1) x Q1 or the area of the rectangle formed

- Keep in mind this is a firm making an economic profit
- For a firm making normal profits, the AC cuts the MR line at the same point as MC  
- Here there is no yellow rectangle that represents economic profit
- For a firm not making a profit, the AC curve is above the MR line  
- Here the green rectangle represents the economic loss
- However, this does not lead to a firm shutdown as AVC is still being covered
- Since fixed costs can be delayed, no failure

- Here the firm is only breaking even and making zero accounting or economic profit
- Called the shut-down point

Short Run Supply

- Buyers and sellers both want to max happiness
- Buyers want to max TU and sellers want to max profit
- Effectively the same goal  
- MC curve is also the short run supply curve when above TVC
- Because maxed when MC=MR and MR is price
- At all three quantities profits are being maxed
- MC derived from TP curve
- Industry determines the price given to firms
- Demand is downward sloping
- Determines profits made by firms

Long Run Decisions

- If firms are making an economic profit, more firms join the industry
- Increases supply, which pushes down Pe
- Falls until firms are only making normal profits
- Industry output rises, firm output falls

- Prices are pushed down to the SRAC minimum
- In the long run, firms can adjust capital
- Allows firms to lower costs even further
- Creates more economic profit and more firms join
- Continues until P3 is reached, MES
- Firms cannot go any further
- In perfect competition, firms only make normal profits
- Because of easy entry, price always adjusts to meet costs
- Graph of an industry in perfect competition in the LR         
- All curves meet at MES
- Also notice that SRAC min is same as LRAC min here
- Meaning max efficiency
- 3 conditions for maximization
- P=MC (all firms max profits)
- P=SRAC min (firms making normal profits_
- P=LRAC min (all firms at MES)

Technological Change

- Assume the economy undergoes constant technological change
- New firms can enter with LOWER COSTS because of technology
- Older firms can still produce when new firms make profit as long as P isn't below AVC
- Three characteristics
- Older firms continue to exist at shut-down point
- Price eventually determined by new firms
- Old firms are rendered obsolete once they go below shut down point

Declining Industries

- When an industry in equilibrium faces declining demand
- Less demand for plastic and bottled water
- Tastes change over time
- Firms try to cut costs, can't afford to upgrade capital, make LR losses
- Government attempts to subsidize declining industries
- Only delays the inevitable
- Government should facilitate switch to healthy industries
