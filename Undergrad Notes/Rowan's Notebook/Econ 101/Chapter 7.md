- 3 forms of firms
- Sole Proprietorship: owner is the business
- Wong's Market: everything earned reported by Mr. Wong
- Hard to divide up the business (ex: selling quarter of the business) because business is the person
- Unlimited liability, get food poisoning from food and you can sue Mr. Wong for his house and car
- Managing is easy because entire business controlled by one person
- Taxes relatively easy, same as doing one person's taxes
- Partnership: 2 or more people carrying on business in common with a goal of profit
- 2 or more single proprietors
- Joint and severable liability
- Can be sued with unlimited liability individually but also can sue anyone in the partnership
- Business partners must be chosen very wisely
- Management more difficult, more people to make decisions
- Taxes mostly same as proprietorship
- Can't be split or sold like sole proprietorships
- Partnerships can easily break up
- Company: separate legal entity
- Limited liability, cannot sue the individual, only the company
- Function like fake people, almost like fronts for the business owners

Hybrids

- Can have limited partnerships, where one has unlimited liability and all others have limited liability
- LLP: limited liability partnerships, where all partners have limited liability
- Crown Corporations: company with one shareholder, that being the government
- NGO: companies that can't make a profit, or MNEs, multi national enterprises

Financing of Firms

- How do you put money into a firm?
- Either gift the firm money (equity financing) or lending the firm money (debt financing)
- Equity financing is usually buying a stock or share
- You give the company money in exchange for control of the company
- Debt financing is when you buy bonds
- "Buying debt" means loaning a company money
- Basically a piece of paper proving the company owes you money (plus interest)

Goals of Firms

- Firms seek to maximize profits
- Even if they don't we assume they want to
- All decisions are based on this core assumption
- We also assume the firm acts as one entity
- Every member of the firm acts to maximize profits

Corporate Social Responsibility

- Obligation of policy to be compatible with social goals and values
- Or firms are free to maximize profits subject to government regulation and consumer sovereignty
- Main debate between whether firms want to promote social values or are just held back by them
- Cause marketing: doing business while associating yourself with a cause
- EX: Telus ads about breast cancer research
- Signaling that corporation is "good" (halo effect) and encourages customers to buy for a cause
- Increases sales

Revenue, Costs, and Profits

- Objective is to find why when price goes up, so does Qs
- Producers want to maximize happiness (just like consumers)
- Does Marginal Benefit outweigh Marginal Cost?
- Firms maximize happiness by maximizing total profits
- Profits are revenue minus costs (TR - TC)
- Revenue is different for each market structure (Perfect Competition, Monopoly, Imperfect Competition)
- Assume that cost curves are same for each market structure
- This chapter develops cost curves

Production and Revenue

- Production: transformation of inputs into outputs
- Inputs same as factors of production or factors
- Outputs same as goods/services or total product (TP or Q)
- Inputs are the 5 Factors of Production
- Land, Labour, Capital, Technology, Entrepreneurship
- Production Function: Output = f (Inputs)
- Letters = f (Secretaries, Office Space)
- TR = P x Q
- Here Q means quantity of OUTPUTS

Costs

- Cost: value of the factor used up in production
- 2 kinds of costs
- Accounting Costs: explicit price of factors
- Cost of acquiring factors of production
- How much did this printing press cost? How much is the paper?
- Doesn't include intangible assets like goodwill or liabilities
- Not relevant to decision making
- Opportunity Costs: Value of the next best foregone alternative
- Main determiner of decision making in firms
- Also called Imputed, Implicit or Implied Costs
- OC can include intangible assets like labour of the owner
- Sunk Costs: asset that cannot be used alternatively
- Has zero OC
- Cannot be reused or salvaged
- EX: specialized computer program
- Sunk costs are omitted from OC

Profits

- Same 2 kinds of profit
- Accounting Profit: TR - Accounting Costs
- Often called net income
- Economic Profit: TR - TC (OC)
- Also just called profit, pure profit, or supra-normal profit
- Since OC is the only relevant cost, the word cost implies OC in economics
- Normal Profit:
- Normal profit is a cost
- Basically the cost of staying in business without making a profit
- The implied cost of taking a risk
- Also the return to Technology and Entrepreneurship
- OC of running your business vs quitting and putting that money in the bank
- Wages are cost of labour, rent is cost of land, normal profit is cost of technology and entrepreneurship
- Normal profit is also the average return across the industry
- In the long run, how much will the surviving firms make?
- If firms are making increased profit (above normal), signals more to enter industry
- If firms are making decreased profit (below normal), signals more to leave industry
- When a firm is making zero profit they are called allocatively efficient
- Firms can make accounting profits but zero pure profits

Profit Maximizing Output

- Profit = TR - TC
- Game plan: start with cost theory (7 and 8) and then apply revenue theory based on structure (9 10 and 11) and find maximization
- Which time horizon are we in?
- Not defined by actual length of time
- Defined by what kind of production function you have
- SR: time in which at least one of the factors is fixed
- Short run decisions are also called operating decisions
- LR: time in which all factors vary (except T)
- Long run decisions are also called planning decisions
- VLR: time in which all factors vary including T
- Very long run decisions are also called growth decisions

Production in the Short Run

- Short run, so one of the inputs is fixed
- In first stage, TP increases at an increasing rate
- This is because of specialization (Smith)
- After inflection point, no more benefit from specialization (saturation)
- Increases at a decreasing rate
- After a certain point congestion occurs, and productivity decreases at an increasing rate
 
- Average product hits it's maximum point in stage 2
- Marginal product hits it's maximum at the first inflection
- MP intersects when AP it's at it's max height
- AP is slope of ray from origin
- AP= TP/N
- N is the variable factor
- MP= change in TP/change in N
- MP is the slope of the tangent

Law of Diminishing Marginal Product

- ==As more of the variable factor is added, more of the fixed factor is used up==
- As the variable is added, additional output diminishes after a certain point

Costs in the Short Run

- 3 kinds of costs
- Total, average, and marginal costs
- Total costs can be fixed or variable
- Like cost of renting an office, no matter what your production cost is the same
- Variable cost would be cost of labour, increases as you hire more people
- Average costs are found from the total costs
- Average fixed costs are the average of total fixed costs, same for variable costs
- Marginal cost is change in total divided by change in quantity
- Also the slope of the tangent
- Marginal fixed costs are zero
- Marginal variables is change in TVC(total variable costs) divided by change in Q
- TC= TFC + TVC
- AC= AFC+ AVC
- MC=0+MVC
- Average and Marginal costs are found from total costs
- MC= MVC
- AFC decreases over time
- AFC is ray of fixed costs
- Since TFC is a straight line, AFC decreases linearly over time (rectangular hyperbola)
- To find TVC, take the total product curve
- Multiply the horizontal axis by whatever cost you are measuring (EX: cost of labour)
- Then, rotate it horizontally so cost is on the y axis
- Increases at a decreasing rate at first, then increases at an increasing rate        
- At the inflection point, AVC is minimized

- MVC decreases until a certain point (minimum slope), and then increases

- Total fixed costs just shift up the origin for total variable costs
- Total variable costs looks like an S shape, like TP curve
- 2 important inflection points, where slope is minimized and where ray is minimized
- MC is lowest when slope is minimized, AC is lowest when ray is minimized

Short Run Cost Curves

- TP curve is mother of all curves
- Creates AP and MP curves (children)

- Both have same inflection points
- Average intersects with marginal at max of average
- By rotating the horizontal axis of TP curve and multiplying it by price of variable to get TVC
- Add TFC to TVC to get TC
- From slope of tangent and ray of TC, you get MC and AC
 
- Both have same inflection points
- One where slope of tangent is minimized, one where ray is minimized

Shape of Cost Curves

- Cost curves are u shaped due to LDMR
- MC always cuts AC at AC minimum
- AC is generally cup shaped
- AVC is saucer shaped
- MC is spoon shaped
- Where AC is minimum is called capacity
- If producing at a point left of capacity, called working under capacity
- "Happy Face" of cost curves  
Shifts in Short Run Cost Curves

- Increase in P of variable inputs shifts both curves up
- Increase in P of fixed inputs only pushes ATC up
- Different level of happy face for each level of fixed input
- Functions similarly to indifference curves
- Increase in plant size may shift curves down
