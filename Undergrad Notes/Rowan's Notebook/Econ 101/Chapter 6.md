Marginal Utility Theory

- Utility: Satisfaction, happiness (preference/ranking of what makes you happy)
- A util is an ordinal measure of utility (only a ranking, 10 utils is more than 1 util but not necessarily 10 times more)
- Can also be indifferent to a choice
- Total Utility: satisfaction gained from consuming ALL units of the good
- Marginal Utility: additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit of a good

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

- As more is consumed, utility decreases after a certain point, ceteris paribus
- Usually the first few units increase in utility, and then decrease
- Additional units can even serve negative utility, decreasing total
- Reason is OC principle
- More likely to pay for first unit than last
- Behavioural assumption              
- In first section, utility raises increasingly (each increase is more)
- At inflection point, utility raises decreasingly (each increase is less)
- At second point, utility drops increasingly
- S-shaped curve
- First section fuelled by novelty
- In second phase novelty wears off
- By third phase, sick of the good
- Plotting slope of tangent gives marginal utility
- 2nd graph is a graph of the slope of 1st graph
- LDMU: TU increases at decreasing rate after certain point, CP
- MU: change in TU over change in Q, also slope of TU
- TU: sum of marginal utilities
- TU curve is S shaped

Total Utility Maximization

- 4 different proofs
- Proof 1: Marginal Principle
- Maximize total by equating marginals
- Same as marginal principle (individual acts when MB exceeds MC)
- Assume that consumers want to maximize happiness (people like being happy)
- Allocate income so last dollar spent on each good is equal
- MU per price on each good must be equal
- When MUx/Px = Muy/Py, it's maximized
- Must calculate per dollar to make it relative
- Assume no utility is gained from holding money unless money is a good
- Can use this principle on more than 2 goods

Example:

- Assume 2 goods, beer and pizza which both cost one dollar
- Also assume consumer is maxing happiness and gains no utils from holding money
- When Qb is 2 and Qp is 7, TU is 50
- MU of b is 3, MU of p is 1
- Swap 1 dollar to increase TU
- Qb is 3, Qp is 6
- TU is 52!
- Now MU of both is 2
- Any further switches will not affect or decrease TU
- Utility is maximized because MU of both is equal
- Proof 2: Opportunity Cost
- Consume a good until MB=MC
- Same concept as MUx/Px=MUy/Py
- Proof 3: Math
- This equation can be cross multiplied, so MUx/MUy =Px/Py
- If MUx/MUy= 3/1 and Px/Py is 2/1, then buy 2 less y and buy 1 more x
- TU goes up by 1 (3 utils - 2 utils)
- If MU was equal to P, doesn't work
- Proof 4: diagram        
- Here you can see that when the green lines shift, happiness is lost
- Space between the solid and dotted line represents utility
- This is because both sides are equal MU (dotted black line)

Utility Maximization Methodology

- Step 1: switch from less utility good into more utility
- Step 2: Adjust LDMU
- Repeat until MU is equal
- Continue, and utility decreases

Derivation of Demand from MU Analysis

- Find link between price and Qd of tofu
- Assume goods x and y are x: tofu and y: everything else (income)
- TU maximization is MUt/MUy = Pt/Py
- Say Pt increases
- MUy and Py are unchanging, so to balance MUt must rise to match
- To increase MUt, buy less of t, reducing Qt
- When P rises, Qd falls, just like chap 3 demand curve

Substitution and Income Effects

- Why do people want to buy more as P falls?
- Real Income: purchasing power, or how much of something you can buy
- If you have 20 dollars and coke is a dollar, Real Income is 20 cokes
- 20 dollars is your nominal income

Substitution Effect

- Change in demand due to change in relative price, holding real income constant
- Original purchasing power BUT new prices
- Say price of pocky's drops by half but so does your real income
- To maximize TU you must buy more?
- If P drops, then MU drops, and Q raises
- Compared to everything else, lower priced good is now cheaper
- So you must buy more of it
- Substitution effect is always negative as price falls
- Consumer will always substitute into cheaper good (assuming rationality)
- Just imagine a substitute good example, but second good is income

Income Effect

- Change in Qd due to change in real income holding new relative prices constant
- Say your income doubles and price is still half of what it was
- Or price halves, and suddenly you can buy twice as much as you could
- To maximize utility (assuming normal good) you want to buy more
- Because of change in price (drop), first you want to buy more to substitute into the cheaper good
- Second, with a higher real income you want to buy more with new income
- Combined this creates the Total Effect
- Both effects happen because of one variable, the price halving
- But Qd raises because of 2 different reasons
- Magnitude of total affect depends on proportion of income spent on good and magnitude of price change

Slope of the Demand Curve

- Elasticity used to determine difference between normal and inferior goods
- Normal Good: Positive income elasticity
- Income goes up, Qd goes up
- EX: Beer
- Inferior Good: Negative income elasticity
- As Income goes up, Qd goes down
- EX: KD
- Ordinary Good: Negative price elasticity
- Increase price, decrease Qd
- Negative sloped D curve
- EX: beer
- Giffen Good: Positive price elasticity (rare)
- Increase price, increase Qd
- Positive sloped D curve
- EX: potatoes in Ireland in 1850s

Example of Giffen Good

- Potatoes were a staple in 1800s in Ireland
- As price went up, people bought more
- As staple, large portion of total expenditure
- When price of potatoes increased, real income dropped
- To save money on meat (spent real income on meat), bought more potatoes to compensate
- In reality income elasticity was VERY negative
- When real income falls, Qd increases
- Here Qd increase from real income falling outweighed the price increase
- 2 requirements for a Giffen good
- Must be an inferior good
- Income effect must offset substitution effect              
- 1 is normal, 2 is inferior, 3 is Giffen
- In all 3, price decreases
- Substitution effect is always negative (black arrow)
- Income effect depends on elasticity
- Effect of substitution is always the same if price change is constant
- In 1 (normal), substitution and income both drive Qd up
- In 2 (inferior), substitution drives Qd up, but income drives it down but doesn't reverse it
- In 3 (Giffen), substitution drives Qd up, but income drives it down so much that the elasticity is reversed

Conspicuous Consumption Good

- Can be designer goods, snob appeal, or status symbol
- Upward sloping D curve
- Substitution affect is positive
- As relative price rises, consumer wants more
- Happiness is relative
- Can be because of passive consumption, buy what others have
- Demand is probably negatively sloped IF consumer could buy good without people knowing the price
- May be upward sloping for individual, but not for market
- For price elasticity, called ordinary or giffen goods
- For income elasticity, called normal or inferior goods

Consumer and Producer Surplus

- MUx = Px/Py x MUy
- Assume Y is money, 1 dollar is a dollar, and MU$ is constant
- Therefore, MUx = Px x MU$
- Utility given up is equal to utility gained at the margin
- Consumer surplus is value customer is willing to pay, minus what they actually pay
- Surplus is same as MB, so you can change price to MU$
- When consumer surplus is maxed (at Pe), you maximize TU

Producer Surplus

- Producer surplus is price they sell for vs how much it cost to produce
- When producer surplus is maxed (at Pe), you maximize TU
- When economic surplus is maxed, market is efficient

Indifference Curve Analysis

- Also called decision theory or choice theory
- Based on 3 factors or variables: price, income, and tastes
- Budget Line: shows all possible combinations of 2 goods, given the prices
- Handles the first 2 variables, price and income
- Find the max quantity of each good and plot a line between them
- maxQ is budget divided by price of good
- Points under the line are possible but not preferred
- Points above the line are impossible
- Functions like a PPC curve
- PxQx + PyQy = Y
- Expenditures = Income
- Slope = -Px/Py
- Rise over Run
- Called Marginal Rate of Transformation, represents OC of X
- Again, like PPC curve

Shifts

- Change in Real Income: Shift in BL (Budget Line)
- Positive relation, increase shifts right
- Change in Relative Price: Rotation of BL
- Negative relation, as P increases, BL rotates inwards
- A proportional change in both acts like an income increase

Preference Assumptions

- 3rd variable to account for is tastes
- First assumption is that consumers can rank their preferences
- Ordinal ranking, not cardinal
- Preferences are consistent over time
- Consumer is rational (complete, reflex, transitive, non-satiation)
- Complete: Consumer must say prefer X over Y, prefer Y over X, or is indifferent
- Reflexive: Can say a bundle is as least as good as an identical bundle
- Transitive: If X preferred over Y, and Y preferred over Z, then X preferred over Z
- Created to prevent circular logic
- Second assumption is that the subjective preferences are given
- Consumers act AS IF they maximize utility
- Observe PATTERNS of behaviour
- Consumer preferences are revealed through consumption
- Cannot make inter-personal comparisons (subjective), and can't make temporal comparisons either
- Based on psychology
- Third assumption is that indifference curves are well-behaved
- Behavioural assumptions used to make things simpler and realistic
- Non-Satiable: more is better (MIB), consumers will always choose the larger quantity between the 2
- Implies that ID curves have a negative slope
- Averages Preferred to Extremes: usually consumers seek an average of goods and don't specialize
- Leads to a convex shape in ID curves
- Strict Convexity: weighted average of two bundles is strictly preferred
- Gives ID curves rounded bottoms

Utility Functions and Indifference Curves

- TU= TU(X,Y)
- X is a bundle of goods
- Whatever choice offers more utility is the bundle chosen
- Indifference Curve: All combinations of X and Y that yield a given level of utility
- On the entirety of the curve, TU is constant
- Total happiness does not change
- Change in Y times MUx + change in x times MUy= 0
- Slope of ID Curve is -MUx / MUy
- Shifts only occur if a change in tastes occur

Four Characteristics

- Infinite number of curves increasing outwards
- Called map of ID curves, all have different TU
- All curves are downward sloping
- Cannot cross
- Convex to origin
- When on the same curve, utility lost equals utility gained
- Slope is -MUx/MUy
- Shifts only occur if tastes change, assume tastes don't

Total Utility Maximization

- Max TU subject to constraint of BL
- Get on the highest ID curve you can, depending on your BL
- How much of X and how much of Y to buy
- Find where ID curve is tangent to BL
- When does Marginal Rate of Substitution equal Marginal Rate of Transformation      
                  

Derivation of Demand from ID Curve Analysis

- As Px falls, BL rotates out to a higher ID curve, increasing quantity consumed
- As price falls, you want to consume more

Income and Substitution Effects

- Total effect increases BL (for ordinary goods)
- Substitution effect causes BL to rotate in place around the equilibrium, becomes flatter and parallel to the final BL
- Income effect causes BL to shift to final location
- Direction of shift depends on ordinary, inferior, or Giffen good
