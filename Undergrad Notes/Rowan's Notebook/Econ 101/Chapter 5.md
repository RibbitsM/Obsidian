Interaction Among Markets

- Markets do not exist in isolation
- New tech decreases costs in oil production, cost falls
- In labour market, increases jobs and raises wages
- Increase in wages raises costs of oil production again
- This is called a General Equilibrium Analysis
- A Partial Equilibrium Analysis looks at only one market
- In 101 we assume partial equilibrium, looking at one market only
- Also assuming it's one point in time'

Government Controlled Prices

- Disequilibrium Price
- Any price that is NOT the equilibrium price
- When the price is above Pe, Qd is what's actually exchanged
- When the price is below Pe, Qs is what's actually exchanged
- Smaller number rule, at disequilibrium, actual quantity exchanged is the smaller number

Price Floor

- Minimum price that price cannot go below
- Usually is either illegal to sell below, or government buys up excess supply
- If price floor is below the Pe, it usually has no effect
- If price floor is above the Pe, it is binding and creates excess supply
- Because the disequilibrium price is above Pe, Qd is actually exchanged causing a surplus
- In the labour market this excess is unemployment, in agriculture it is stockpiling
- The sellers who sell at a higher price win
- Consumers and sellers who can't sell excess lose

Price Ceiling

- Maximum price that price cannot rise above
- If price ceiling is above Pe, usually has no effect
- If price ceiling is below Pe, is binding and creates excess demand
- Since actual price is below Pe, Qs is what's actually exchanged, smaller number
- In the labour market this is a labour shortage, and in agriculture it's dumping, where sellers sell all the excess supply they have stockpiled
- Buyers who buy at the lower price win
- Sellers, and buyers who can't get as much as they want lose

Methods of Allocating Short Supply

- Government wants to prevent the market from "clearing"
- Government can create excess demand through several methods
- First come first serve, like with concert tickets
- Can let sellers choose who to sell to
- Ration goods
- Scalpers push up the price
- Golden rule is "Don't Mess With The Market"
- Market inherently wants to "clear"

Price Ceilings and Black Markets

- Black markets are just markets that sell at illegal prices
- Price ceiling is set below Pe (black markets don't occur when ceiling is above Pe)
- Illegal sellers buy all product at the market price to make a monopoly (below market price)
- See that consumers willing to buy more than market price
- Sell at black market price, above the Pe
- Profits are calculated as Qs minus zero times P2 minus P1 (black market price minus price ceiling price)
- Price ceilings create potential for black markets

Case Studies (Housing Market)

- Rent control is a price ceiling
- Initially supply is fixed, set number of houses and none are being built (inelastic)
- Lower price because of price ceiling causes an excess in demand
- Over time housing depreciates (poor conditions because lack of maintenance)
- Supply decreases, and long run supply curve is inelastic
- Excess demand increases even more, creates a housing shortage
- Alternatives to rent controls
- First come, first served: second years keep old apartments
- Seller's preferences: selling to friends and family only
- Rationing: social housing, give rooms to people who need them most
- Black Markets: key deposits are one way to bypass rent restrictions
- Recent adjustments for price ceilings include exemptions for new buildings, increasing rent to cover costs or inflation, or increase rent after an eviction
- In this situation, existing tenants win, and landlords and new tenants lose (less rent, and more homelessness)

Policy Alternatives

- Non market solutions include subsidized housing, public housing, and income assistance
- These are temporary solutions, OC of housing remains very high

Case Studies (Labour Market)

- Minimum wage is a price floor
- Price floors create excess supply of labour, or unemployment
- Existing workers win, but producers and those without jobs lose

Policy Alternatives

- Solutions include retraining, automation, mobility grants, guaranteed income
- All temporary solutions to problem that OC of labour is low

Market Efficiency

- Control prices have benefits for some, and drawbacks for others
- Minimum wage benefits existing workers and hurt employers and unemployed
- Rent control benefits renters but hurts landlords and homeless
- Goal is the betterment of society
- Calculated as a net calculation, did I hurt more than I helped

Demand (value) and Supply (cost) as "Benefits"

- Demand: Maximum price consumer is willing to pay
- Benefit of a good is subjective to each person and is given
- Cost to the buyer is the market price which is based on OC of alternative goods
- Benefit to the buyer is the difference between the enjoyment you get from the purchase minus the potential enjoyment (money) you give away
- This benefit is also called the consumer surplus
- The maximum price the consumer is willing to pay is when the price equals this amount of enjoyment or utility
- When MB (marginal benefit) is greater than MC (marginal cost or price), you want to buy
- You continue to buy until MB is equal to MC
- Therefore price is equal to marginal benefit, and the demand curve is also a benefit curve
- Combination of Incentive Principle and Marginal Principle
- Supply: minimum price the producer is willing to accept
- Benefit to the seller is the price, which is flexible
- Cost (cost of production) to the seller is given, and is decided by OC of alternative goods
- The minimum price to the seller is where the income (price) equals the cost (money spent on production)
- Incentive is price minus OC of production
- As price falls, incentive also falls until it reaches the marginal cost
- Producer will not sell at a price less than the OC of alternatives
- They would just produce the alternative instead

Conclusion

- Given Q, the price is either the max buyer is willing to pay, or the minimum the seller is willing to sell
- Goes counter to the traditional way to think of D and S
- What you give up equals what you get
- When both sides maximum happiness (most optimal situation, no OC), equilibrium is reached

Economic Surplus and Market Efficiency

- Also called cooperative surplus or total surplus
- It is essentially the net benefit to society as a WHOLE (so benefit minus cost)
- When the price is above the cost (demand curve is over supply), we have a surplus
- Consume up until the point where demand is equal to supply
- Surplus goes away after equilibrium point, no longer net benefit
- At equilibrium point, there is still surplus
- Government intervention removes some economic surplus (dwsl)
- Must be in a free market society (no minimum wage or rent control)

Price Controls and Efficiency

- If price floor is set, actual amount exchanged is less
- This creates a smaller economic surplus zone
- Creates a DWSL, deadweight social loss
- Name for economic surplus unused
- If price ceiling is set, actual amount exchanged moves to the right
- Creates smaller economic surplus zone
- Creates a DWSL
- If a quota is set (quantity ceiling)
- Reduces production, creates DWSL
- When government intervenes, Reduces economic surplus, and Redistributes it
