Definition of Demand

- Demand is measured by Qd
- Qd: the quantity that he household wants to purchase given the own price _ceteris paribus_
- Household: the buyer, the consumer, the purchaser
- The demand side
- Wants: the amount the buyer is willing to buy, not necessarily the amount purchased
- Dependent on Own Price: price of the given good
- Ceteris paribus: all other things held equal

 - All dependent on the customer
- In general there is an inverse relationship
- As price goes down, demand goes up
 
WHY?

- We assume buyers use the marginal principle
- All decisions made at the margin
- The key is the benefit and cost relationship
- Incentive is benefit minus cost (OC)
- Buyer determines the benefit to themselves
- Cost (OC) is the price of the good
- If cost is less than the benefit, more likely to buy
- Lower the cost, the more people will buy
- But as the price increases, the net benefit decreases
- Incentive comes in **consumer surplus**
- This is when you feel like you have gained a net utility or happiness from a transaction
- Benefit to the consumer is consumer specific but stays consistent
- Price fluctuates, changing the opportunity cost
- Ceteris paribus holds all the factors of consumer benefit constant
- Factors can be anything
- Ex: when you're buying Baja flash, how thirsty are you, how many people are you buying for, how many can you carry home
- These would all be constant, only price changes
- Formula: Qd is f(P; ceteris paribus variables)
- This creates your demand curve
- This formula works as an inverse as well
- Given P, find Qd or given Qd, find P
- P= g (Qd) = Inverse Demand Function
- Price is the regulator between demand and supply
- Price is plotted on the vertical axis
- At the margin, whatever you give up is what you get
- We assume customers always maximize happiness, so they will pay the exact price to gain an equal amount of satisfaction
- In econ 101, we don't use the actual shape of the curve (quadratic), we assume demand curves are linear
- Aggregate of all consumer's demand curves is the industry demand curve

CP Variables

- Income: budget of consumer, when income goes up people pay more (except for inferior goods)
- Tastes: consumer preferences
- Advertising: related to tastes, to affect consumer preferences
- Expectations: your predictions of how the price will fluctuate (if price will go up, buy more, if it will go down, buy less)
- Price of Related Goods (demand side): can be complementary goods or substitute goods
- Complementary Goods: goods bought together, like baseballs and bats or burgers and fries
- Substitute Goods: goods used interchangeably, like coke and pepsi
- If price goes up on a baseball, demand goes down
- The demand would also go down for its complementary good, the baseball bat
- If price goes up on coke, demand goes down
- Assuming pepsi stays the same price, demand would go up for it since it's a substitute for coke
- Complements are used jointly, substitutes are used alternatively

Shifts of the Demand Curve

- Change in price leads to a change in demand
- Curve doesn't change, you just move along the curve to match the new coordinates
- However, a change in demand causes the whole demand curve to shift
- Delta Q, movement along the curve, but Delta D, shift of the curve

How to Determine a Change in Demand

- Step 1: is it a change in the price? If yes, that’s a change in price, move along the curve, if no, a ceteris paribus variable changed.
- Step 2: Does the variable affect demand? If yes, (like income), the d curve is affected
- Step 3: Does it affect it inversely or directly? If it affects it directly, shift right. If it affects in inversely, shift left
- In the case of income, you are willing to buy more at the same price with your increased income, so the curve shifts to the right to accommodate  
**Supply**

- If the price is decreasing, does the company want to sell more or less?
- You would think the company would want to sell more to make the same profit, but the supply curve doesn't show this
- Supply: the quantity the firm wants to sell, given the own price, ceteris paribus
- Firm= Supply side
- Wants: the amount the firm WOULD LIKE to sell ideally, not actual amount sold
- Dependent on Own Price (price of the good)
- Ceteris Paribus: holding all things constant
- As price goes up, seller wants to sell more, as price goes up, seller wants to sell less

WHY?

- Seller also balances costs and benefits at the margin just like the buyer
- Benefit is the price, how much money they make (flexible)
- Cost is the OC, whatever inputs went into the goods
- OC comes from all the other goods the firm could've used these inputs for
- All about net benefit, just like buyer
- A firm always wants to use their inputs to make the most profitable possible product
- As price increases, profit increases
- Price minus cost equals net benefit to the firm
- The higher the net benefit, the more you want to sell
- When the benefit is less, you would want to sell less
- If it costs a firm more to make a can of coke than the price they sell at, why want to sell?
- Scarcity, choice and OC are the reasons for S and D
- 2 of the major principles, Incentive and Marginal Principle apply to these concepts!

Supply Curve

- Qs = f (P/ceteris paribus variables)
- Remember for 101 this is always linear, but in real life it curves more

Shifts of the Curve

- Change in price only moves along the existing curve
- A change in the ceteris paribus variable (like a supply increase) causes the curve to shift

CP Variables

- Input Prices : cost of manufacturing, going up causes an inverse relationship
- Technology: reduces input costs, causes a direct relationship
- Taxes are costs, cause an inverse relationship
- Expectations are same as demand, but related to how you think your costs will change
- Price of Related Goods (production side): can be complementary or alternative
- EX: you can easily produce oil and gas jointly, but producing wheat or oats requires the same equipment but can't be produced together
- When you produce less oil (because price is low), less gas will be produced
- When you produce less wheat (wheat is low), it is incentivized to produce more oats in its place (price is higher)
- All variables revolve around Costs
- Incentive is Price minus Costs
- Price causes movement, costs cause shifts
- Prices rising or costs falling cause more profit, more sales, costs rising or prices falling cause less profit, less sales
- How would an increase in technology affect supply?
- Is it a change in price? No, move on
- Is it a costs variable? Yes, it affects the supply curve
- Is it direct or inverse? Technology increases supply, so it affects it directly
- The supply curve shifts right

Four Steps to Success

- Step 1: Is it a change in the p (price)
- If no, it’s a shift of the curve so go to step 2, if yes, its movement along the line
- Step 2: Is the variable affecting D or S?
- Easy way to tell, if its costs, its supply, anything else is demand
- Step 3: Is it a direct or inverse effect?
- Step 4: which way does the curve shift?
- Direct: moves right
- Inverse: moves left

Equilibrium

- Supply and Demand do not need to be balanced
- However, the actual amount exchanged has to be equal
- Equilibrium Q: market clearing price, where both buyer and seller get what they want

|0.80|6|3|

- Here, the equilibrium price is 60 cents, where the supply and demand curves meet
- To find this on a graph, plot price and quantity exchanged, with both curves present
- The intersection of the two curves is the equilibrium price
- When price is higher than eQ, the producer has more than the consumer is willing to buy
- When price is below than eQ, the producer doesn't have enough supply to meet consumers demands
- Equilibrium Principle: once equilibrium is reached, it will remain at equilibrium over time
- Stability Principle: If you are not at equilibrium, it will be naturally reached
- If price is above Pe, inventories rise, signaling sellers to drop the price
- If price is below Pe, inventories fall, signaling sellers to raise the price
- Called Inventory Adjustment Model
