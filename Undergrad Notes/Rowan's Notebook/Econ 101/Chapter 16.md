Function of Government

- Without this, anarchy erupts
- Govt defines and enforces property rights
- Govt institutions are required in order to have a nation
- Smith stated that the second duty of the state was to protect each citizen from injustice and oppression from other citizens

Case for Markets

- What is proper mix of free and controlled market
- Formal defense of markets is allocative efficiency
- In absence of intervention, PC is allocatively efficient
- This is a Pareto Optimum, society is best off
- No DWSL
- If all markets were perfect competitions, economy would be ideal
- Problem is that this is unrealistic
- Most markets are not perfectly competitive
- Smith is wrong, self interest should not always be followed
- Informal defense of markets is coordination
- Markets automatically coordinate, no enforcement
- System of price matches demand and supply
- "invisible hand" signals supply decisions
- No need for central planning, don't mess with the market
- Market stimulates innovation and growth
- Innovation is profitable
- Some think that economic freedom will lead to political freedom
- Theory of creative destruction, old firms broken down by new technology

Case for Government Intervention

- When do markets fail?
- Big five reasons
- Monopoly, Externalities, Public Goods, Asymmetry of Info, Social reasons

Monopoly Power

- Monopoly power is inevitable (natural monopolies)
- Economies of scale/scope/networks, differentiated products, and innovation are all reasons
- Monopoly power creates inefficiency
- Policy seeks to balance negative effects of monopoly and positive effects of rivalry
- Needs G intervention to avoid

Externalities

- Non priced third party cost or benefit
- Not included in analysis
- Affects parties other than buyer and seller
- Can be production or consumption and positive or negative
- Production externality affects supply, consumption affects demand
- Positive pushes curve to the right, negative pushes curve to left
- Say you paint your house, other people feel better looking at painted house, positive cons externality
- Pollution is a negative production externality
- Unless policy to pay for pollution is in effect, then it is priced and not external
- Private costs are OC s to buyers and sellers
- External costs are OC s to third party
- Adding these together you get the social costs
- Network externalities are the effects of additional users
- Another person using a road is negative, degrades the road
- Another person joining a social media platform is positive, increases user base
- Pecuniary externalities are effect on potential buyers
- Technically not an externality
- Can be price of iphone rising which negatively effects people without iphones
- Pos Prod Ext is a beehive next to an orchard
- Neg Prod Ext is pollution
- Pos Cons Ext is getting vaccinated
- Neg Cons Ext is playing opera music at 2 am

Private and Social Costs

- Externalities create differences between private and social costs
- This creates allocative inefficiencies
- Even in perfect competition, externalities can make Pe inefficient  
- Here both sides are effected by negative externalities
- If supply side bears all of the externalities, socially optimal production is below actual production
- Very common unless govt intervenes
- Creates social overproduction
- Overuse of resources harms society
- Diseconomies create overproduction, economies create underproduction
- Called "tragedy of the commons"
- All farmers could graze in a common pasture for free
- Everyone used it, grass was consumed too fast and cows died
- Tragedy of the commons ^15b2b0
- Seen in overfishing, fossil fuels, etc
- In law, negative externalities are called nuisances
- Can sue for damages caused, imposing external cost
- Open Access Resources are vulnerable too
- Mostly occurs in fishing and roads
- Creates traffic congestion
- Carbon taxes are an example of govt imposing external costs on the buyer and seller
- Externalities are reciprocal
- Cannot reserve right to play loud music and reserve right to study in peace at the same time

Public Goods

- Goods can be rivalrous or non-rivalrous, excludable and non-excludable
- A rivalrous good is when consumption of a good diminishes consumption by another
- A excludable good is one that is consumed equally by all once produced
- Goods that are rivalrous and excludable work well in a market
- Chocolate bars, seats on a plane, legal advice
- Goods that are non-rivalrous (to capacity) and excludable don't
- These are usually places or media
- Art gallery, golf club (country club), netflix, roads
- These have zero marginal cost, so should be free
- But a price is still charged, therefore inefficient
- Goods that are non excludable and non rivalrous are called pure public goods
- Street lights, church bells, national defense, lighthouses
- Usually paid for in taxes, because you must force people to pay for these goods
- Otherwise free rider problem emerges
- Non excludable and rivalrous goods are common property goods
- Like the common pasture, fisheries, etc
- Susceptible to tragedy of the commons
- Usually govt deals with non-excludability
- Club goods are still inefficient
- Private goods do not need govt intervention
- MC of club goods is zero to produce another unit
- These are artificially scarce goods
- Produces less, sells for more
- With pure public goods, once they are created all can consume it for free
- Therefore noone wants to pay for it
- Government intervenes and uses taxes to pay for these goods
- Common property goods are susceptible to negative consumption externalities
- Govt must limit consumption to socially optimal levels
- In all these examples self interest does not serve the common good

Asymmetry of Information

- Means that buyer and seller have different amounts of information
- Adverse selection is when buyer and seller have different information before contract is made
- Because of this, selection of goods is tilted towards poor quality
- Problem of lemons
- Two types of used cars, plums and lemons
- Buyers value plums highly and don't value lemons at all
- Sellers value plums moderately and don't value lemons at all
- If buyer knows what cars are plums, economic surplus is made, benefit for both parties
- If buyer doesn't know if car is plum or lemon, values any given car less
- Seller knows that car is valuable and values it more than buyer
- No exchange, buyer won't buy
- Seller could also offer only lemons
- Could solve this by increasing buyers information, increasing incentives to be truthful, or increase average quality of goods
- When the buyer knows more than the seller, insurance problem
- Poor drivers are more likely to buy car insurance, smokers are more likely to buy health insurance
- Drives up premiums for good drivers and non smokers
- Buyers knows their risk, sellers don't
- Creates vicious cycle
- Can be solved with screening or policy by group
- Creates statistical discrimination, premiums are very high for young male drivers even if they are very safe drivers

Moral Hazard

- When a party is insulated from risk after a contract is made
- Therefore they behave adversely
- Usually created by misplaced incentives
- Homeowner with insurance has incentive to not make yard safe
- Insured driver has incentive to drive dangerously
- People with employment insurance have incentive to not work
- Big banks have incentives to take risks because they know they will be bailed out
- Solved by deductibles, co payments and co insurance
- Deductibles are amounts you still have to pay even if you're paid out
- Co payment is a flat fee payed up front even if covered
- Co insurance is an agreed percentage to be payed
- Co payments and co insurance are usually used for medical insurance
- Main difference between adverse selection and moral hazard is time
- Adverse selection is behaviour before contract made, moral hazard is behaviour after
- Principle agent problem is type of hazard where agent changes behaviour after being hired
- Real estate agents might act differently after being paid, they know more than the principle
- Employee might slack off after being hired even though they promised to work hard
- Basically motivation ends after payment occurs

Signalling

- Non free message from owner of the good
- Receiver must learn something about the sender
- A bachelors degree signifies proficiency in a subject
- Sheepskin effect, student one course short of a degree earns much less than someone with a degree
- However nobody really fails university, once you're in its fine
- Business people where more expensive clothing than professors, signifies success
- Warranties/guarantees signify quality
- Price sometimes signals quality or prestige of a good
- These do not equate marginals however, not allocatively efficient

Social Goals

- Income redistribution is equalling money between rich and poor
- Leaky bucket, lose money in process of redistribution
- Reduces efficiency of market
- Merit goods are goods provided universally because of their necessity
- Social obligations like military, jury duty and voting are required for social contract
- Need for economic growth like technology is a micro and macro issue
- These are all based on normative value judgements
- Normative issues are handled by the govt

Case Against Government Intervention

- Usually uses cost benefit analysis to determine whether to intervene
- This process is not perfect, how can you predict the future?
- Usually intervene by creating public goods, regulating, or redistributing
- Govt can induce or impose behaviour
- No matter what, govt changes incentives
- Intervention always incurs costs, but are they outweighed by advantages
- Can be direct costs like cost of building a new train line
- Indirect costs like increasing costs of production for other industries
- Firms can lobby govt for economic advantage

Government Failure

- Politicians have self interest
- Reasons to maximize votes, maximize authority, want to maximize consumer sovereignty
- No one looking out for public good
- What is the optimal level of intervention? No one knows
