Economic Theory  
**Positive vs Normative Statements**

- Positive: what IS
- An objective statement, has to be testable
- EX My professor has red shoes
- Normative: what SHOULD BE or what OUGHT TO BE
- Not testable, more subjective or values based
- EX My professor should care more about their students

**Why Do Economists Disagree?**

- Different definitions
- Different assumptions
- Different values or different normative views
- In the case of employment insurance, it depends what you want to emphasize most
- EX individual freedom vs equality
- Short term vs Long term
- Emphasizing either can be valuable, no testable option

**Building/Testing Theories**

- Individuals can act oddly
- But as a whole randoms cancel out (Law of Large Numbers)
- Group behavior is easier to predict than individual behavior
- When income rises, people tend to spend more
- Some people might not, but the group does
- AS IF TEST: individual acts as if they maximize happiness
- Basic assumption of economics
- A theory explains WHY a relationship exists only
- Must be testable by collecting evidence
- Theories can never be proven, only not disproven

**Components**

- Variables: item that can take on different values
- Endogenous Variables (determined inside the model)
- Exogenous Variables (determined outside, taken as a given)
- Assumptions: something we assume to be true
- 3 types: definitional, behavioral, conditions
- Definitional: self explanatory
- Behavioral: assumption of how people act, ex all people act in self interest
- Condition: If-then statement, If people are over 70 then they will retire
- Causation: when an independent variable causes change in a dependent variable
- Predictions/hypotheses: propositions logically inferred from assumptions
- Also used as if then statements

**Testing a Theory**

- Start with observation
- Make a hypothesis
- Prove or disprove your hypothesis via testing
- Remember you are trying to disprove your hypothesis always
- It can never be proven
- No lab tests in economics
- Theories are tested through historical data and statistics

Correlation: two variables move together EX sunspots and droughts  
Causation: if then, proven reason EX viruses cause pandemics

- CORRELATION DOES NOT EQUAL CAUSATION
- To test a correlation you must find a causation

**Index Numbers**

- Compare two time series with different start dates and find a common denominator
- Used for relative changes not absolute changes
- EX Consumer Price Index: weighted average of a basket of consumer goods
- We're comparing the CPI to the real price

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Year|Real Price|CPI|
|1|25|100|
|2|30|X|
|3|45|180|
 - Always set the base year to 1 for simplicity
- Use ratios to solve for X
- 25 to 30 is a 20% increase
- 100 plus 20% is 120, X is 120
- Index Number of period n is absolute value period N over absolute value base period

**Graphing Economic Data**

- Cross sectional Data: data set of multiple units plotted at the same time
- Time Series Data: data set of one unit over time
- Scatter Diagram: plotting 2 different variables on the same graph
