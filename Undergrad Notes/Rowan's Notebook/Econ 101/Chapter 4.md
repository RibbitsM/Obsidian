**Elasticity**

- How responsive is quantity to a change in price?
- What do the demand and supply curves look like?
- More responsive means more elastic
- 4 types: Price E of demand, price E of supply, income E of demand, cross E of demand
- Look at measurement and determinants for each

**Price Elasticity of Demand**

- Finding location AND inclination in one equation
- Location is price over quantity
- Inclination is one over the slope
- P over Q times one over the slope is price elasticity of demand
- Slope is delta q over delta p
- Final equation is relative change in quantity demanded divided by the relative change in price
- Also called price sensitivity
- When location changes but inclination doesn't, when the curve moves e falls
- When inclination changes but location doesn't, when the curve moves e falls
- When writing the slope use the absolute value
- Greater value means steeper, lesser value means flatter

Terminology

- What determines the responsiveness?
- A vertical line is completely inelastic (Qd doesn't change at all when a P is changed)
- A horizontal line is infinitely elastic (P doesn't change at all when Qd is changed)
- A steep curve is relatively inelastic ( P is insensitive to Qd)
- A flat curve is relatively elastic ( P is sensitive to Qd)
- A rectangular hyperbola is perfectly elastic (P will change in exact proportion to Qd)
- From least to most elastic: vertical line (0 elasticity), steep curve (<1, less elastic than rh), rh (perfectly elastic), flat curve (>1, more elastic than rh), horizontal line (infinitely elastic)

Factors Affecting PeD

- Mainly affected by the availability of substitutes of outputs
- Are there other goods like what I'm buying I could buy instead?
- If your good has close substitutes (coke vs pepsi) it will be elastic
- If price for coke goes up, the demand will drop quite a bit because people drink pepsi instead
- PeD is relative change in demand divided by the relative change in price
- With close substitutes, demand changes more than price
- Therefore PeD with close substitutes is >1, it's elastic
- With poor substitutes, like teslas, even if the price goes up, people will still buy it
- Demand doesn't change as much as price
- Here, the PeD is <1
- The wider the definition, the less elastic
- If all pop is more expensive, no good substitutes, low elasticity
- If only one brand is more expensive, many good subs, high elasticity
- Big ticket items are more elastic
- If the price of a low cost item increases by 10%, no big deal
- If the price of a high cost item increases by 10%, much less likely to buy
- The same increase affects demand of higher cost items much more
- Over time, elasticity increases
- When new innovations occur, new technology is inelastic because lack of options
- Over time competitors arise, and price becomes more elastic
- In the short run price increase only affects Qd slightly, but it affects it greatly in the long run
- All these factors are based on availability of substitutes
- Other factors can be money spent, joint demand, price range, nature of commodity, can demand be postponed

Elasticity and Total Revenue

- Total revenue is price times quantity
- 4 cans at 75 cents each is a TR of 3 dollars
- Elastic demand curve: flat, inelastic demand curve: steep
- (assuming location is the same)

- Size of box is total revenue
- Even though price went down, since the curve was flat, TR rose
- P1 x Q1 is less than P2 x Q2

- In a steep curve, dropping the price leads to a smaller TR
- As a general rule, dropping price is good for elastic goods and bad for inelastic goods
- Elasticity changes over a flat curve too
- When quantity is none, infinite elasticity, when price is 0, 0 elasticity
- When price is more than quantity, more elastic
- When price is less than quantity, less elastic
- When price and quantity are equal, perfectly elastic
- Left to right goes elastic to equal to inelastic
- As quantity increases, elasticity falls
- Using point A as the vertical intercept, B as your point, and C as the horizontal intercept
- AB/BC will give you your elasticity value
- Easy trick to find if elasticity is greater or less than 1

Total Revenue and Elasticity

- If price drops on elastic curve TR up
- If price drops on inelastic curve TR down
- As you move along the demand curve (price down, quantity up), elasticity decreases
- Combine the ideas
- TR moves in a curve from left to right

- Price falls constantly, shown by the blue line
- TR grows as price drops until the curve becomes inelastic
- TR maxes at the perfectly elastic point, and then falls
- Maximizing TR is not always the goal of business because it doesn't consider costs

Price Elasticity of Supply

- Supply is on the top, price is on the bottom
- PeS is relative change in quantity divided by relative change in price
- Same as the PeD equation but for supply side
- P over Q times 1 over the slope
- Responsiveness of Qs to a change in P
- Relative change vs percentage change
- Say price goes from 10 to 8 and Qs goes from 1 to 2
- All changes must be put in context
- Change is relative, 10 plus 1 is a more significant change than 1000 plus 1
- Change in Q was 2-1, so 1
- We put the change relative to the average
- So you take 1 and divide it by 2 + 1 (your change equation reversed) divided by 2 (the average)
- This leaves us with our relative change of 1 over 1.5
- Textbook uses relative change and percentage change to mean the same thing
- Relative change is price is 10-8 divided by the average
- So 2 divided by 10 plus 8 divided by 2 which is 9
- This gives us a relative change of 2 over 9
- This why relative change is important, because even though Qs moved by 1 and P moved by 2, the relative change of Qs was greater

Terminology

- A horizontal line is infinitely elastic
- A vertical line is completely inelastic
- A steeper line is mostly inelastic
- A flatter line will be more elastic
- So far it's the same as PeD
- However, in supply a perfectly elastic curve is a straight line through the origin
- No matter the inclination, if it's straight and through the origin, it's perfectly elastic

Determinants

- Availability of substitutes of INPUTS
- Focus on the perspective of someone in the company
- Flat: elastic, steep: inelastic
- Availability of alternate FAPs affect costs
- If FAPs are fungible (swapped out easily), curve is elastic
- If FAPs are not interchangeable (can't be swapped out easily), curve is inelastic
- If wheat price rises and oats are fungible (can divert FAPs to wheat easily), output increases dramatically
- If wheat price rises and cranberries are not interchangeable ( can't divert FAPs to wheat easily), output will not increase dramatically
- If costs increase slowly as Qs increases, elastic
- If costs increase rapidly as Qs increases, inelastic
- EX: if you can produce excess product or have ease of storage for cheap, more elastic
- If money is required to increase production, inelastic
- Elasticity increases over time as well
- Competing companies arise, and production technology advances
- Other determinants can be marginal cost, stock levels
- But ease of FAP substitution is the main driving force

Income Elasticity of Demand

- Demand goes on top, income goes at the bottom
- Relative change in Qd over relative change in income
- Responsiveness of Qd in response to change in income

Categories

- 3 categories, elasticity less than zero, less than one, and greater than one
- Normally as income goes up, you spend more
- Luxury goods (or normal luxury goods or superior goods) increase greatly with income (elastic)
- These are in the greater than one category
- EX: Air Travel, expensive universities, whisky
- Second group is necessity goods, increase moderately with income (inelastic)
- In the less than one category
- EX: Rail travel, moderately expensive universities, wine
- Last group is inferior goods (abnormal goods) decrease with income
- Goods that are replaced by expensive alternatives
- EX: Bus travel, cheap universities, beer
- 1 represents proportionate change
- Luxury greater than 1, necessity less than 1, inferior less than 0 (reverse)
- Can be put into 2 categories, normal and abnormal goods
- Normal goods are elastic (luxury) or inelastic (necessity)
- Abnormal goods have negative income elasticity         
         

- Income-Consumption line: relationship between Qd and Y, ceteris paribus

Determinants

- Characteristics of the good
- More necessary the good is, the lower the elasticity
- If income drops, luxury goods are cut out first
- Meanwhile necessities will pretty much always be bought at a similar rate
- Level of consumer's income
- As a general rule, luxury goods are preferred over necessities
- As income increases, necessities are replaced by luxury substitutes
- Inferior Goods
- Not necessarily a disparaging of the good, just a statement of it's niche
- A good way is imagining a 3 tiered tower, where as income goes up you rise up the tower
- The 2nd tier extends partially into the 1st and 3rd tiers
- The border of the 1st tier represents 0 for elasticity, the border of the 2nd tier represents 1  
Cross Elasticity of Demand

- Cross: price of the other good
- Responsiveness of Qd of good X to a change in P of good Y (PeXY)
- Relative change in Qd of X over relative change of price of Y
- Py over Qx times delta Qx over delta Py
- Can have any value between -infinity and infinity

Complements

- If Px goes up, Qy goes down
- When the price of baseballs goes up, Qd of baseballs goes down and so does Qd of bats
- Negative cross-elasticity

Substitutes

- If Px goes up, Qy goes up
- When the price of coke goes up, Qd of coke goes down, so Qd of pepsi goes up
- Positive cross elasticity
- Utility is that by measuring the cross elasticity of 2 goods you can determine whether something is a competitor (in the same market) or not
- Can also determine what is a complement, etc.
