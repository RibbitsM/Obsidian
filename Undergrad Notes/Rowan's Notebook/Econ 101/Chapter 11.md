Structure of the Canadian Economy

- Canadian economy exists on a scale between monopoly and perfect competition
- As you move right rivalry increases, as you move left market power increases
- Canada defines competition as a lack of market power
- Snippets are a small increases in price maintained for at least a year
- Rivalry does not exist at either extremes
- Canada is a large country with a small population
- Large country creates BTEs and high transportation costs
- Small population creates excess capacity, Canada tends to seek outside trade
- Monopolistic Competition: large number of small firms
- Oligopoly: small number of large firms
- Level of rivalry/market power is measured by the industrial concentration ratio
- What fraction of total market is controlled by a number of the largest firms
- CR4: what fraction of total sales in controlled by top 4 firms?
- 65% CR4 is the max legal threshold for market power in canada
- The higher the concentration ratio, the higher the market power
- In 2019 petroleum and beverages had the highest CR4, clothing had the lowest
- The problem with the CR4 is determining the relevant market
- Relevant market is determined by product sold and geographic region
- Ties competition to a specific number of firms, can be misleading
- A market with 4 equal sized firms dominating with a CR4 of 100 is more competitive than a market where one huge firm is followed by three small ones with a CR4 of 28
- CR is usually overstated in Canada because it ignores foreign competition

Imperfect Competition

- Rivalrous behaviour with some market power to set price within a range
- Can be divided into Monopolistic Competition and Oligopoly
- Oligopolies engage in strategic behaviour
- Assume there are several to many sellers
- Selling a differentiated product
- Entry and exit is not easy but not impossible
- Price can be set within a range

Behaviour

- Sellers differentiate their products
- Homogeneous good in same market, but different in consumer's eyes
- Noun plus adjective, firms try to distinguish their product from their competitors by selling it differently
- EX: toothpaste, shampoo, beer, spandex, cellphones
- Advertising and branding
- Sellers administer prices
- Set within a range with reference to S and D
- EX: gap jeans being 50$ vs 500$ naked jeans
- Prices are sticky, even if demand changes it is hard to change prices in response
- Exceptions are dynamic pricing for airlines and flash sales in department stores
- Firms engage in non-price competition
- Can include innovation, service quality, free upgrades, loyalty programs, branding, promotions
- Firms compete on basically any level related to sales that isn't price related
- Can also include things like warranties and guarantees
- Basically firms attempts to entice consumers
- Some argue that this is just a subtler method of price competition

Monopolistic Competition

- Assume many sellers
- Sell a differentiated good
- Significant BTEs
- Price setters within a range
- Sellers ignore the reactions of others, use non-strategic behaviour
- Branded products signify attempt to create market power
- Prices are sticky
- Monopolistic because of differentiation in the SR, downward sloping d curve
- Competition because entry and exit is possible in the LR
- In the SR, firms produce when MR=MC  
- Here the firm is making economic profit, causing other firms to enter the industry
- This causes a shift to the left until firm is only making normal profits

- Here in the LR, firm is only making normal profits and no new firms enter
- D for each firm falls

Excess Capacity Theorem

- Because of price setting, firms produce less and at higher prices
- Perfect competition and imperfect competition both only make normal profits
- The LR equilibrium is less than the MES
- Economists initially judged imperfect competition as being inefficient
- However, it seems that consumers benefit from more brands being present despite the inefficiencies

Oligopoly and Game Theory

- Assume there are several sellers but not many
- Products can be similar or differentiated
- Firms are interdependent, decisions are based on actions of other firms
- Engage in strategic behaviour
- Entry and exit is formidable (firm output affects industry supply)
- Firms are price setters within reason

Behaviour

- Goal of strategic behaviour is to increase market power
- Usually called Mergers and Acquisitions
- Goal is to absorb other firms to increase market power and decrease competition
- Rather difficult to gain the power to do this, hence higher BTEs
- Natural causes of these BTEs are high startup costs, and economies of scale and scope
- Economies of scale are cost savings related to increased production
- Economies of scope are cost savings related to producing two goods at once
- Artificial causes of BTEs is mainly just government intervention
- Key difference to note is strategic behaviour, different from other forms of competition

Game Theory

- A game is when two or more interdependent players make decisions while knowing the outcome
- Games can be simultaneous or sequential
- Every game contains three key elements
- First the players, who make the decisions
- There has to be choices, or strategies
- Finally there must be a payoff or reward
- Rock paper scissors is an example of a simultaneous game
- A Nash equilibrium (John Nash) is when a player cannot benefit by switching to any other strategy given the other players strategy
- A point where no player can play better (given the other players strategy)
- Therefore there are multiple equilibriums possible in a game
- When you find a best response, mark it with a star
- If two best responses are in play, you've reached a Nash Equilibrium
- Equilibrium because no player has motivation to move
- Self policing because no outside behavior is needed to enforce it, reached on its own
- A dominant strategy is a strategy that gives a higher payoff regardless of the other's strategy
- Always the best response
- A dominated strategy is a strategy that always yields a lower payoff than an alternative
- A strategy that should never be played

Prisoner's Dilemma Game

- Leads to an undesirable Nash Equilibrium
- Each player has a dominant strategy with a lower payoff then not playing the dominant strategy
- "Adam Smith needs revision"
- Argues that following self interest doesn't always benefit the group
- Can see this phenomenon all throughout society
- People standing at a concert, cartels, arms races, advertising
- Especially relevant in cartels, even though cheating is the dominant strategy, profit is only realized if members cooperate

Pareto Optimum

- When you cannot make someone better off without making someone else worse off
- The greatest good for the greatest number (utilitarianism)
- One concept of the best outcome
- Allocative efficiency is pareto efficiency
- Pareto Improvement/Efficiency: Make someone better off without making someone worse off
- Pareto Disimprovement is making someone worse off without affecting another
- Battle of the Sexes: both members have different interests but would rather be with eachother than alone
- Here there are 2 Nash Equilibria
- No dominant strategy
- Two possible Pareto Optimums

Sequential Game

- Games where you "take turns"
- Play happens responsively, you can see what your opponent did before you employ your strategy
- Plotted on decision trees rather than grids   
   

- All possible decisions in sequence with payoffs shown
- No matter what Alice chooses, left is bobs best decision
- Alice's best choice is to go right if bob went left, and to go right if bob went right
- Alice has a dominant strategy
- This is a NE AND a PO because it maximizes payoff for both and plays into both players best strategies

Ultimatum Game

- Here the first player has the power
- Establishes an ultimatum (take it or leave it)
- Say Bob gets a dollar
- He must divide it between him and his friend, if they reject the offer the dollar is gone
- Hypothetical optimal move is to offer the lowest amount possible
- Because any amount of money beats no money
- Player being offered can threaten to reject the offer if they don't get a certain amount of money
- Both players have equal power despite having different roles
- Depending on the credibility of the threat, different amounts will be offered
- The PO solution is to split 50/50
- Credible Threat/Promise: a promise which is in the promisor's interest to perform
- The Kidnapper Game is the concept of holding a hostage and deciding whether to free them or not
- Victim promises that if they are freed they won't reveal their identity
- Not credible promise
- Patti Hearst joined a bank robbery with her kidnappers, makes promise more credible

Problems

- All players must have full knowledge of payoffs
- Each payoff must have all relevant information
- Commitment Problem: player can't make a credible threat/promise
- Commitment Device: A method to create a credible threat/promise by changing incentives
- Players are motivated by self interest
- Often the player making the first move has the advantage
- All problems of sequential games with commitment devices

Commitment Problem

- All players are self interested
- Terrorists don't want to be caught, victim wants to live
- Without a credible promise, victim dies and both end up worse off (prisoners dilemma)
- Solution is to change the incentives, shift the balance
- Can alter material or psychological incentives
- Say victim guilts kidnappers with religious talk
- Other party must know incentives
- Is honesty the best policy?

Oligopoly in Practice

- Can be explicit or implicit
- Collusion is an agreement to cooperate and restrict Q to raise P
- Cartels are explicit, but create incentives to cheat
- Implicit collusion may or may not actually be collusive
- Conscious parallelism is where two firms are selling at the same price by accident
- Prices of gas being the same, or new technology
- Focal point pricing is like MSRP, where there is a recommended price that many use but some deviate from
- Even if firms agree on pricing they can still compete
- Can be competition for market share or competing to produce innovation

Barriers to Entry

- Same natural barriers
- Start up costs, economies of scope and scale
- Also govt legislation and predatory behavior by firms
- Contestable markets are those with free entry and exit
- Often firms don't try to charge monopoly prices for fear of being undercut
- Hit and run entry is undercutting a firm that is charging too much, making your profit, and leaving
- Tradeoff between monopoly power vs cost savings and innovation
