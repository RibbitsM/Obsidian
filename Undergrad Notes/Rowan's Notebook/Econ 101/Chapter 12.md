- Why do we prefer competitive markets to monopolies?
- Use thermometer and barometer
- Thermometer: economic efficiency
- Efficiency means minimizing costs
- Can minimize costs to individuals or to society
- When minimizing for individuals, called productive efficiency
- When minimizing for society, called allocative efficiency

Productive Efficiency

- Either minimizing inputs for given output or maximize output for given inputs
- These are represented by the LRAC and PPC respectively
- Think of it as the biggest bang for your buck
- Three concepts
- Firm Productive efficiency: firm produces given output at lowest cost
- Outputs held constant, so you're on the LRAC
- The LRAC shows the lowest cost of production
- Industry productive efficiency: industry produces given output at lowest cost
- Means every firm in the industry has the same MC curve
- Can't reallocate output to lower costs further
- Economy productive efficiency: economy produces all outputs at lowest costs
- Entire economy is on the PPC
- PPC shows maximum production of outputs given inputs
- Firm: LRAC
- Industry: MC
- Economy: PPC

Allocative Efficiency

- Theorized by Vilfredo Pareto
- Cannot reallocate resources to make someone better off without making someone worse off
- One possible idea of the best outcome
- Also called Pareto Optimum, from chapter 11
- Pareto improvement is when things are reallocated making someone better off without making someone worse off
- Minimizes cost to society, not just the individual
- The best society can do
- If MB=MC, then you are at allocative efficiency
- Marginal principle dictates that people will continue at the margin until MB = MC
- MB is equal to price
- MB signifies willingness to pay
- Price = Marginal cost means allocative efficiency
- When you are in perfect competition, P = MC
- Perfect competition creates a pareto optimum for society
- Second way to determine allocative efficiency is to see if economic surplus is maximized
- Total economic surplus is maxed when MB=MC
- This happens in perfect competition
- No DWSL unlike with monopolies
- Only works in free markets
- Determine allocative efficiency two ways
- See if P = MC, or look to see if economic surplus is maxed

Efficiency and the PPC￼

- Any point along the PPC curve is productively efficient
- The PPC curve by definition shows when outputs are maxed for given inputs
- Any point inside is productively inefficient
- Only one point is allocatively efficient, here it is B
- Don't figure out how to find this until higher level courses

Efficiency in PC and Monopoly

- In perfect competition, all firms produce at the LRAC min, so each is productively efficient
- Entire industry has the same MC that intersects at the MES since all produce at LRAC min
- Industry is productively efficient
- Perfectly competitive markets are allocatively efficient
- Since firms are price takers, price is always set at Pe by supply and demand
- This maximizes economic surplus
- As well, P= MC which also indicates allocative efficiency
- For an input greater or less than Qe, total surplus is less
- P= LRAC min = firms productively efficient
- MC same for all firms = industry productively efficient
- P= MC = economy allocatively efficient

Efficiency in Monopoly

- Monopolies are in fact productively efficient
- In the LR, monopolies minimize costs, but aren't necessarily at MES
- Since firm is the industry, don't need to look if MC is same for all firms
- All firms no matter what want to minimize costs
- Rare that firms are not productively efficient
- However, monopolies are not allocatively efficient
- To maximize profits, MR must equal MC
- But MR is steeper than AR in a monopoly so P is greater than MR
- Monopoly profits come from difference between P and MR, but to be allocatively efficient P should equal MR  
- In PC the yellow area is available and increases total surplus
- In M the yellow area is DWSL
- In M, producer surplus is greater than in PC but consumer surplus is much less
- In PC producer and consumer surplus are equal
- Difference is location of MR curve
- In PC the MR curve is the same as the demand curve
- Monopolies can produce less but charge more
- The space between Pe and Pm is turned from consumer surplus into producer surplus
- Price is above MC, someone's getting ripped off
- Sell less for more
- DWSL is present

Efficiencies Exemption

- Step 1 is assuming MC is constant
- Initially S curve is horizontal and D curve is downward sloping
- Determine Pc and Qc
- Step 2 is to monopolize the industry by adding MR
- Determine Pm and Qm
- Step 3 is to calculate DWSL
- However, creating a monopoly reduces costs
- MC line falls, increasing consumer surplus and reducing DWSL
- Possible that these cost savings offset effects of monopolization
- SLC: substantial lessening of competition

Market Failure

- Basis for economic regulation and competition policy
- Real issue is benefits of market power vs rivalry
- Superiority of perfect competition isn't absolute
- Efficiency is not always the goal
- Omits social costs and benefits
- Costs depend partly on market structure
- Monopolies might be necessary to enjoy economies of scale
- May also be necessary for innovation

Regulation to Promote Efficiency

- Natural monopolies are when economies of scale only permit one firm to produce at MES
- Usually demand is less than MES
- New firms entering the industry don't have access to economies of scale
- Monopoly power is unavoidable
- Can buy up monopoly and create a crown corp
- Can also regulate monopoly, subject the firm to restrictive laws
- In either case the government must dictate the price
- Three options
- Charge price equal to MC, equal to AC, or price that allows you to make "fair" profits
- Advantages and disadvantages of each  
- Here we can see three prices
- The monopoly price, MC price, and AC price
- When P=MC, firm is allocatively efficient
- However, it is producing at a deficit so it must be subsidized
- When P=AC, firms make zero profit, but aren't efficient
- This is the core conflict between AC pricing and MC pricing
- Rate of return pricing means making fair profits
- Say the government determines that 5% is a fair profit
- Firm is allowed to make 5% profit on it's capital per year
- Problems are determining what is a fair profit
- Also firm is guaranteed to make a profit so no incentive to minimize costs

Regulation of Oligopolies

- Historically government has protected oligopolies by controlling entry
- Done by creating crown corps or regulating
- Recently crown corps are being privatized and industries are becoming deregulated
- Reasons are to encourage innovation and discourage cross subsidization
- Regulations favoured producers rather than consumers
- Regulators and firms were becoming too interconnected
- Crown corps did not improve labour management
- Theory that international competition will discipline the market
- In the past 20 years Canada has been trying to reduce regulation by using competition policy

Canadian Competition Policy

- Created to strike a balance between market power and efficiency
- Most countries follow this approach of the "carrot" rather than the "stick"
- First examples of this is in criminal law in 1889
- Made it illegal to restrict trade, but this was really just union busting
- Needed proof beyond reasonable doubt
- Targeted conspiracy, mergers/monopolies, and unfair trade practices
- Reformed into the Competition Act in 1986
- Applies to every province
- Purpose was to maintain and encourage competition in Canada
- Competition investigates evidence of mergers, conspiracy etc
- Turned previous criminal offences into civil offences
- Conspiracy still goes to criminal court
- Cases brought before Competition Tribunal, a specialized court
- Detect if there is SLC, did the price raise significantly and stick?
- Should you look at the greater effect on the job market?
- Efficiency exception says that mergers are okay as long as efficiency balances out the effect of SLC
- Very challenging to balance
- Monopoly section renamed to "abuse of dominant position"
- Allows monopolies to exist as long as they don't abuse their power by engaging in predatory pricing, price squeezing, pre-emption, etc
- Cost savings for efficiency exemption MUST be unattainable without the merger
- Problem is effectively forecasting the effects of a merger
