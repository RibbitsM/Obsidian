Assumptions of Monopolies

- No competition in a monopoly
- One seller, the firm is the industry
- Firm is selling a unique good
- Entry and exit are impossible (long run concept)
- In a monopoly the firm has absolute market power
- Firm is the price setter

Demand Curve

- Demand curve of firm is demand curve of the industry
- Demand is downward sloping
- Monopolist sets the price that maximizes profits  
- Reverse of perfect competition
- Industry follows firm instead of firm following industry
- TR is maxed halfway down the d curve
- D curve is also your AR curve  
- MR is 0 at the apex of the TR curve
- On the left side elasticity is high, 1 in the middle, low on the right side
- For a monopoly, MR is always less than price
- When monopoly drops price, it applies to all previous units sold
- Therefore you lose revenue when dropping price because you lose it on previously sold units
- Monopolists never produce when elasticity is less than 1
- If extra revenue is negative, they won't sell
- MR falls twice as fast as AR
- Slope of MR is twice as steep as slope of AR
- Elasticity calculated by distance from point on D to the y axis divided by distance from point on D to the x axis

Short Run Profit Maximization

- Monopolies will not produce where MR is negative
- Avoid right side of the graph
- Same TR values on both sides, but left side has lower costs
- Same rules as other structures
- Monopoly will produce where it covers SR expenses (AVC)
- Monopoly will produce where MR = MC
- 3 key rules
- Stay on left side, cover SR expenses, and maximize total profits        
- This is a monopoly making economic profits
- Producing on left side, and TR is above TC
- AC x Q is TC, and P x Q is TR

- Here cost equal profit, meaning this firm is only making normal profits
- AR is tangent to AC
- P x Q = AC x Q
- AC = P
- Price is the same as average revenue   
   

- This is a firm experiencing economic loss
- Here average costs exceed the average revenue
- However, the firm can still cover AVC
- In the SR the firm can survive
- Remember, one of the conditions is to cover AVC and here that is met        
- Monopolies don't always have to maximize profit
- Here, even though Q2 is the profit maximization point, monopolies may produce between Q1 and Q3
- This area is where AR is above AC, meaning firm will make economic profit
- This could give the monopoly flexibility to do SCR or other incentives

Comparison of Perfect Competition and Monopolies

- Horizontal demand curve vs downward sloping demand curve
- P = MR vs P > MR (ripoff for consumers)
- Profit Maximization P= MR= MC vs P> MR= MC
- Perfect Competition is market efficient, monopoly creates DWSL
- Perfect competition is many similar firms, monopoly is only one firm
- In PC the marginal cost curve determines SRS curve, no supply curve in a monopoly
- PC only makes normal profits in the LR, Monopolies can make economic profits

Long Run

- Economic profits draw firms into the market
- Barriers to Entry (BTEs) prevent firms from entering
- BTEs enable economic profits in the LR
- Natural BTEs include high start-up costs and economies of scale
- Some economies have high start-up costs for reasons like branding, and establishing a dealer network
- In a natural monopoly, market demand conditions satisfy only one firm at MES
- On the LRAC there isn't enough demand to reach the MES, can only support one firm
- Mostly niche industries with a small consumer base
- Only the first firm takes advantage of economies of scale
- Economies of scope occur when you are producing more than one good
- Cost savings by producing more than one good with the same means, like oil and gas
- Network economies are when a products value increases the more people use it, like Rogers vs Speakout

Artificial Barriers

- Patents can create monopolies, used to motivate R & D
- Franchises can create monopolies by copyright protecting their products (like McDonald's has a monopoly on the big mac)
- Charters, you have to join the charter
- Licenses, similar to franchises
- Environmental regulations and red tape, restrict outside companies by slowing down production
- Government procurement policies, need to conform to local policies or unions
- Some barriers are created by firms
- Predatory pricing is a strategy where firms set prices below costs to scare away new firms
- Product differentiation

Effects

- In PC, there are no BTEs and entry causes LR to reach MES, creating normal profits only
- In monopolies, BTEs prevent entry allowing monopolies to make economic profits

Very Long Run

- Joseph Schumpeter proposed that technology would allow firms to bypass BTEs
- Creative Destruction: new ideas destroy old ideas creating economic growth
- Monopolies rarely last in the long run unless granted government protection

Cartels

- Voluntary association of producers who agree to act as a monopoly and maximize joint profit
- OPEC is an example of a cartel, 1979 oil embargo created a 300% price increase
- DeBeers would buy up all new diamond companies to create monopoly
- Also used product placement and convinced buyers to not resell
- Trade-off between restricting entry and maintaining technological progress
- Enforcement problems are present, firms have an incentive to cheat

Multi-Price Monopolies

- Producers must be able to charge different prices for reasons other than costs
- This is called price discriminations
- An example is quantity discounts, buying en masse costs less per unit
- Airlines charge different costs depending on how close to flight date you pay
- Segmentation of the market: can be different prices for same buyer or different prices for different buyers
- Hurdle pricing: early prices cost much more, geeks will pay
- Local vs International tuition

Conditions for Price Discrimination

- Why can buses charge different amounts for adults or children
- Firm must have monopoly power or market power to price discriminate
- Buyers must be willing to pay different prices
- There must be different price elasticities
- Segmentation, different customers value differently
- Buyers cannot resell by buying low and selling high
- Reselling captures the monopolies would-be profits
- This is called Arbitrage

Advantages of Price Discrimination
          
- Blue line represents the competitive price and quantity, where market power is 0
- Plotted by setting Q where S=D
- Consumer surplus is maximized (triangle above Pc)
- Black line represents single price monopoly, where Q is set where MR = MC
- Consumer surplus is reduced, creates a DWSL
- Red line represents multiple price monopoly
- Until Q2, they charge P2 and then charge Pm afterwards
- This converts the square above Pm into producer surplus
- Why not always do this and constantly change price?
- These are called perfect monopolies, where the firm charges the demand curve price for each quantity
- The MR line is now the price line because D=P=AR=MR
- Same as perfect competition but with a downward sloping demand curve
- The consumer surplus from PC is turned into producer surplus

Effects

- Creates higher profits and higher outputs
- Normative effects are debatable
- Depends on whether you value consumers or producers more
- In perfect price discrimination there is no DWSL
