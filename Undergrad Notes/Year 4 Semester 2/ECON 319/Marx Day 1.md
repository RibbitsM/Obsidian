- Coming into the late 19th Century, the origins of socialism mainly come from France, especially as a product of the French Revolution
- Auguste Blanqui, Louis Blanc, and Proudhon were all more revolutionary thinkers, while some non-revolutionaries contributed as well
- In fact, Proudhon contributed the phrase "all property is theft" to Capital
- Robert Owen was a famous English socialist who established the New Lanark textile mill
- There were others as well, mostly influenced by Ricardo or from the principles of Christianity
- The main tenets of socialism at the time are pretty common in government today
- Higher taxes for the wealthy, tax inheritances, reduce private property and give it to the government, shorten work week (which was 72 hours) and legalize unions
- Workers rights were paramount, and contrary to later forms of socialism, early socialists advocated for the decentralization of government power and diminution of the government
- Another plea that ended up being adopted into social democracy was universal suffrage for adults

**Marx**

- Born in Trier to a Jewish family, but converted to Christianity in the aftermath of the Napoleonic wars, and worked as a lawyer and later did a PhD on Philosophy
- He was heavily influenced by Hegel who he studied with extensively
- Became a journalist and moved to Paris where he was exposed to communism and met his benefactor Friedrich Engels
- The two wrote the Communist Manifesto in 1848
- During this time he wrote frequently about the human condition, human nature, and labour which developed into his ideas around alienation
- Emphasized that humanity was defined by its labour, we should live to work not work to live
- Wrote prolifically, including several critiques of Ricardo, Hegel, and more
- Arguably a lot of Marx's economic analysis came from a combination of Ricardo's economics and the idea of Hegelian dialectics
- By far his most influential idea was his ideas of history, and the process of thesis, antithesis and synthesis
- Marx modified these ideas by arguing that spirit force was not what drove history, but material conditions
- Argued that economic history was incredibly important, because by understanding the history of economics we could understand its future
- Marx disagreed with the common idea that capitalism came from Protestantism, but from the accumulation of power in the capitalist class during the 17th century
- Studied economics outside of Ricardo, and particularly despised Malthus for his dismal predictions
- Marx was particularly committed to two things, the labour theory of value and the abolition of private property (which he defined as the means of production)
- Das Kapital was first published in 1867, the same year Canada was founded, and the next two volumes were published later with significant edits by Engels

**Chapter 1**

- Marx starts by defining wealth in a material sense, as accumulation of commodities
- For Marx, commodities were objects with use value that satisfies some human need, very similar to Ricardo
- Defined the value of objects in terms of its use for humans, if no one wants something it has no value meaning that value is entirely subjective
- When someone labours to produce commodities, they are not only labouring for themselves but to create commodities that could be used by society
- This reflects a common theme of not using the individual as the unit of analysis, but the society as a whole
- The exchange value of an object comes from the use value, but these two things are not necessarily linked like in the water-gold paradox
- Borrowing from Aristotle, Marx identifies price as a ratio between dissimilar goods
- He argues that in order to equate two dissimilar commodities they must have some sort of common property, which he identifies as human labour
- Since commodities will be exchanged for certain amounts of other commodities, this ratio represents the exchange value which is separate from the use value
- For Marx, this exchange value is objective that is created by the labour embodied by that commodity which he quantifies in terms of time
- He does not attempt to measure utility in a similar way, saying that it is purely subjective
- To simplify this, Marx assumes that all human labour can be measured in some homogenous unit that lays the foundation for more advanced skilled labour
- He describes this as the average labour of a society or the socially necessary labour time which would be understood differently depending on the mechanization and skill level of the era
- For example, if advances in loom technology would reduce the labour required by 50%, then the value of one hour of labour would be cut in half
- If the exchange value is a function of the crystallized labour in a commodity, then if the labour time required to produce a good falls, then the value of that good also falls
- As mechanization increases, then labour becomes more productive and exchange value moves inverse to labour productivity
- This is partly problematic because not all machines work the same, for example a tool may not make labour more efficient but allow skilled labourers to do things they use to be unable to do
- Or similarly, a tool may make what was formerly a skill intensive task much easier

**Elementary Form of Value**

- One of the core questions Marx wants to answer is the question of how 20 yards of linen could be equal to one coat despite the two being fairly dissimilar
- In order to create a ratio of exchange between two goods, but the good being used as the measure of value is always placed there incidentally
- If wheat is your main unit of value, or iron, or coats, the same thing is happening
- Again, Aristotle already discovered this by observing that all prices are just ratios
- Because of this, Aristotle argued that money is just an abstraction to make the barter of goods easier as bartering and using money are effectively the same
- As a result, no price is ever a representation of the true value of a good
- Although different objects can never be of equal value in reality, it is good enough that people observe them to be equivalent
- There is always this discrepancy, whenever we buy something it will never be a perfect equivalent exchange, we just trick ourselves into doing it to satisfy our needs
- Marx developed this idea of ratios into the total form of value which is basically just a long list of the quantities of different goods that are perceived to be equivalent
- 20 yards of linen is one coat but also 5 grams of gold, 40 pounds of coffee, 10 pounds of tea, 5 pounds of iron, etc.
- To solve this issue, we just pick one commodity which in the 19th century was gold, and move it to one side of the equation to be our measure of value
- Marx is saying that we could just as easily be using tea as our currency, but we have arbitrarily chosen gold instead
- The total form of value will never be complete unless we somehow account for all possible commodities that you can buy
- But money is not just gold, there is a long history of how gold became money
- Before gold became the primary expression of value, the commodity of "money" did not exist

**Fetishism of Commodities**

- Marx looks at the existence of commodities and comments on the bizarreness of the entire thing
- For example, we take some wood, and turn it into a table
- As soon as that wood becomes a table, it is not just an object but a commodity which is immediately absorbed into the vast system of relative value connecting it to all other commodities
- There is something about turning that wood into a table that transforms it and imbues this social value onto it which Marx identifies as labour
- Somehow, all people can recognize that something has the character of human labour and thus is able to be considered as a commodity
- This has nothing to do with the physical properties of the table, there must be some sort of social aspect that allows a table to have a monetary value
- Commodities being fetishized like this must be a result of the labour that produces them
- As every commodity is the product of labour, then the history of that labour and who made it is imprinted in the final product
- For example, why do people buy fair trade goods or boycott certain companies?
- We have social relationships with our objects, but material relationships with their producers
- The social aspects of labour come from the creation of the commodity, but the value of that labour only emerges from the act of exchange
