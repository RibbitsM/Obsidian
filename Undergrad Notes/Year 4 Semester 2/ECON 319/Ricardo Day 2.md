- Land is owned by the aristocrat, and farmed by the farmer
- The farmer in turn hires workers and pays those workers their wages
- Capital is invested into the farm through buildings, machines, livestock, etc.
- This creates more profit, but does not increase the intrinsic value of the land
- While the rent on an improved farm may go up when we invest in capital, the increase is the money paid for that capital, not for the land
- When Ricardo talks about rent, he isn't using the usual definition of the word
- For Ricardo, rent is a payment for the original and indestructible powers of the soil
- These powers cannot be augmented by capital, and come inherently from nature like sunlight and wind
- Land gains its value from scarcity, initially no rent is paid for land since it is plentiful
- As the population grows, demand for food also increases and more land must be utilized
- Ricardo believes in Malthusian economics, and as such population constantly grows
- Rent comes not just from scarcity, but also from the relative quality of the land
- Land is also unequal in terms of the location, which can cause rent
- There is also the constant upward pressure of population growth
- Two assumptions, perfect or near-perfect competition and that there cannot be different prices for the same good in the same market
- While it isn't really an assumption, Ricardo also talks about a uniform rate of profit and uniform wages, as capitalists and workers will try to maximize their earnings by seeking cheap capital and high wages respectively

**Extensive and Intensive Rent**

- Extensive rent is mainly what Ricardo talks about, and relies on the assumption that the highest quality land will be used first
- Initially, when high quality land is abundant there is no rent
- However, once all high quality land has been used the lower quality land must be cultivated
- Now, there will be rent on the high quality land equal to the different in yield between the high quality and lower quality land
- This happens because the price has to rise to cover the cost of cultivation on the inferior land, and those producing on the high quality land get to pocket that rise in the form of rent
- Because of the law of one price, those with high quality land and those with low quality land must sell at the same price, which has to be high enough to cover the costs of cultivating low quality land
- This means that rent is nothing more than a product of inequality in production
- On the other hand, intensive rent is based on the law of diminishing returns
- This law was discovered in February 1815 independently by Malthus as well as two other economists, Edward West and Robert Torrens
- Ironically, another economist named Turgot published a similar idea in the 1760s but it was barely circulated
- This isn't the only example of something like this happening, Darwin discovered evolution at almost the same time as a man named Burnell
- Also, Mendel sent his essay on the Law of genetics to Darwin which would have completed the hole in his theory, but he never opened it
- It wasn't discovered until three other scientists found it years later
- Additional input of capital and labour increases output but at a diminishing rate
- In this situation, rent comes from the difference between investment and yield
- If you invest 200 units in capital and only yield 185 units, the rent is 15 units
- Ricardo's core idea is that land is not actually a factor of production
- Rent doesn't come from the intrinsic value of the land, but from the difference between outputs
- Those who profit from the gifts of nature add no value, as they only control land by coincidence
- Any profits that may seem to come from land actually just come from the difference between what you own and what others own
- These two theories of rent can coexist, both from the cultivation of new land and increases in investment in already cultivated land
- Earlier we talked about how Ricardo says the price of corn is based on the situation where no rent is paid, the land most recently cultivated
- This reinforces the Labour Theory of Value assuming capital is just embodied labour, since the price is set to cover only the costs of labour and capital, and the price of capital is the price of the labour required to produce it
- What Ricardo is saying here is actually very similar to Marxism, but he never advocated for any sort of change to the system of land ownership despite landlords providing no economic value
- His theory of price does not contain rent at all, only labour and capital
- "Corn is not high in price because a rent is paid, but rent is paid because corn is high in price"
- In modern economics, we believe that price is equal to marginal cost
- Ricardo doesn't explicitly say this, but he gets very close to the discovery
- Ricardo argues that rent can be lowered by creating increasing returns on existing land
- Also applies to intensive rent, if capital becomes more efficient and diminishing returns are avoided, there is less rent
- Since rent is unproductive, it cannot be the product of wealth in a country
- But, rent is common in wealthy countries since these countries tend to have extensive cultivation and capital investment

**Long-Term Tendencies**

- Generally speaking, when rents are rising profits are falling, and vice versa
- As population grows over time, rent will also increase because of increased scarcity of land and more intense cultivation is made necessary
- Ricardo talks extensively about the gifts of nature, and believes that they are made use of in the manufacture of any possible commodity
- From this perspective, if rents come from the gifts of nature than rent occurs from any possible venture
- In human terms, seeking rent requires getting exclusive access to the gifts of nature
- This can be done by owning land, getting a license to a mine, or even paying a person's salary
- If you can consider athletic talent to be a "gift of nature", how much of LeBron's contract is actually just rent?

- The same theory that Ricardo applied to agriculture also works for mining
- Price of minerals is determined by the cost of production at the margin
- One exception he mentions is when Spanish explorers discovered immense amounts of gold and silver in Latin America which lowered prices extensively
- However, this is an outlier because generally speaking gold is acquired from mines, and not from the corpses of native south americans
- Because gold mines are so rarely discovered, it is highly unlikely another event like this would occur
- As a result, gold is an excellent measure of value because it should almost always be determined by only the costs of labour and capital
- This makes it an excellent store of value, but also a good benchmark to measure other goods by for economic analysis
- Earlier, Ricardo talked about how there is no invariable measure of value, but he believes that gold is the closest thing you can get
- However, he did not account for the possibility of new discoveries of labour-saving capital in mines or additional mass discoveries of gold in North America and Australia
- But based on his analysis of gold, he thinks that it represents the average ratio of labour to capital
- If you look at every industry and calculate the ratio of labour to capital usage and then find the mean of all those ratios, you will get the ratio of labour to capital in gold production
- Therefore, the price of gold should not fluctuate if wages or profits change
