- The surplus profits created by capitalism come from the disparity between the value of labour power and the value created by labour power
- Recall that the value of labour comes from the need to sustain itself, keeping the labourer alive and healthy enough to raise a family
- The capitalists only have to pay the value of the labour in order to get the privilege of using the value-creation power of labour
- When a labourer is hired they are signing a contract, agreeing to receive payment equal to the value of labour power for this use
- Say a certain number of hours per day is necessary to sustain a labourer, whatever amount of hours spent creating value that exceed this value is surplus labour
- When labour is "creating value" it is not only transferring labour power into a commodity as use value, but also preserves the use value of the components required
- This labour must be of good quality, meaning workers are labouring continuously and effectively
- Marx also argued that all labour functions in the same way, skilled labour just concentrates more labour power into a shorter amount of time

**Chapter 8**

- Labour both adds and preserves embodied value, Marx calls this the two-fold nature of labour
- When means of production are used in this process, they also add value but unlike labour this also reduces their own use value by the same amount
- Since the use value of capital is created by the process of labour required to create it, this is also embodied labour
- This likely cannot be true in reality, but the idea of the process is what really matters
- The process of adding value through capital and labour is different, labour is the only true source of value in the world
- Just like in Ricardo, variable capital is the money used to pay the wages of labourers
- A labourer only needs to work 6 hours per day to match the value of his labour power, but the wage contract lasts for 12 hours per day
- This creates value in excess of the amount required to preserve the labourer and their family, and this excess value goes to the capitalist in exchange for a wage
- Since the only legitimate factor of production is labour, the surplus value comes entirely from the labour actively put into a commodity at a certain step, not the already existing embodied labour
- The ideal cost of a product is identical to the cost of producing it, but this is not true in a capitalist society
- Constant capital is the same as ricardo's fixed capital
- This includes raw materials, tools etc.
- There are no returns to constant capital
- Variable capital is what is used to pay for the wage bill, and is the only form of capital that creates returns
- It always produces value equivalent to what was expended, and usually more

**Chapter 9**

- Total capital (C) is equivalent to constant (c) and variable (v) capital
- As variable capital creates surplus value, the amount of variable and total capital increases
- If a worker is contracted to work 12 hours and could sustain themselves in only 6 hours, the ratio is 2/1 which will match the ratio of surplus value created
- This ratio is also called the degree of exploitation
- However, the rate of surplus value is less than the actual profit rate
- This is because the profit rate is the return to all capital, not just variable capital
- If we had a rate of surplus of 100% like in our example, the profit rate may only be 18%
- The amount of labour required for a worker to sustain themselves per day is called necessary labour, and depends on the average standard of living at the time
- Similar to how Ricardo talks about rent and land ownership, there is no form of merit associated with capitalists vs labourers, it's just a coincidence
- As a result, the surplus created when the working day exceeds the necessary labour time does not help society outside of the capitalist class
- This is a result of the labour laws existing to benefit the capitalists by setting work days at 12 hours
- In Marx's ideal world, each day everyone would labour only as much is necessary to preserve life, and no surplus or accumulation occurs
- Because necessary labour only creates enough to sustain a labourer, everything that it produces is also consumed
- Marx compares capital to vampires, draining life from labour and gaining vitality equivalent to the life extracted

**Chapter 10**

- Because the law of the length of work days determines the amount of surplus value created, capitalists and labourers conflict over this
- Capitalists want it to be as long as possible, and labourers want it to be reduced to only what is necessary

**Chapter 11**

- Capital reproduces through labour, and specifically through surplus labour
- Ideally, capital should be employed to augment labour, but the system of capitalism is about capital bending labour to its will
- This idea comes from Hegelian dialectics, which has the goal of identifying the contradictions in the world and explaining how they came to be
- Capital that is idle by night is a loss to the capitalists, and as a result they will try to capture the nighttime labour of the labourers
- Marx imagines surplus value as a function of the length of the working day
- In 1867, adult men worked 12-16 hours a day, 6 days a week
- Over time, this has been gradually reduced from 12, to 10, and finally to 8
- In Canada, the 40 hour work week did not appear until the 1960s and still many workers work longer hours than these
- Nassau Senior argued that all profits are made in the last hour of a day, and hence it is not possible to shorten a work day

**Chapter 12**

- In the same vein, if a capitalist can find a way to shorten the necessary labour time, then even if the working day stays the same length more surplus will still be created
- This can be done by increasing the intensity and productivity of labour
- Achieved through advances in machinery, division of labour, etc.
- Marx points out that this may encourage capitalists to shorten the work day as long as intensity increases as well which will allow more rest for labourers
- Surplus value will increase even though it appears that labourer's conditions are improving, meaning that the rate of exploitation has gone up

**Chapter 17**

- Under capitalism, spare time is created for capitalists by extracting the labour time of the labourers
- The accumulation of capital is the process of converting living labour into inert embodied labour

**Chapter 18**

- The rate of surplus value is s/v where s is hours of necessary labour, and v is hours of surplus labour
- The rate of surplus value is equivalent to the rate of exploitation
- If s and v are both 6, the rate is 100%

**Chapter 24**

- If instead of consuming the surplus value, the capitalist reinvests it, they are able to generate more and more capital indefinitely
- Of course this is another assumption that's probably not true
- Like anything else on Earth, capital needs to reproduce and it can only do so through labour
- He uses the example of the Bible where most important characters can trace their lineage through their fathers
- Instead of human reproduction, capital reproduces through investment and accumulation

**Chapter 25**

- Capital accumulates in step with labour, meaning constant population growth
- This is cumulative in nature, each increase allows a new, larger increase
- As wages increase, the population grows, and wages fall as a result
- Very similar to Ricardo, capitalism exists on cycles based on the fluctuations of the labour market
- Since the proletariat relies on the workers, they must coexist
- Countries in which capitalism has progressed the furthest have the cheapest goods
- Over time, investment causes shifts towards using fixed capital over variable capital
- Capital will always require workers, but this will happen at a diminishing rate
- This process creates what Marx calls the "Industrial Reserve Army" which consists of the masses of desperate unemployed or underemployed workers who compete with those already employed
- The mechanization of capitalism marginalizes workers but also makes them dependent
- Marx observes regular patterns in the economy where the cycles of boom and bust become more and more pronounced
- As competition between workers rises, then alienation also increases
- For Marx, a signature characteristic of capitalism is the increased misery of the worker which is created by this constant competition and exploitation
- He identified the tendency to mechanize, to increase surplus value, to monopolize, to increase unemployment, and to increase severity of cycles
- Elsewhere, he identifies tendencies for globalization, colonization/imperialism, war destruction, alienation, class struggle, and immiseration of every kind

**Conclusion**

- Capital involves very little discussion of the future, and mainly consists of exploring the past
- He did much to identify the current conditions of capitalism but wrote little about what to do about it
- He left the field of Marxian Economics which was later developed by thinkers like Rosa Luxemburg, Karl Kautsky, Lenin, and Michal Kalecki
- There are still Marxist economists today, but today he is viewed much differently
