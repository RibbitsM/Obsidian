- Marx discusses the phenomena of value as a "social hieroglyphic"
- A price does not necessarily reflect value directly, but it is a semantic label with the purpose of representing value, just as the word "table" represents a real object
- Through the process of socialization we tend to associate prices with values, Marx argues that the true value of a commodity is not its price but the labour embodied in it
- As money came to be the denominator of the general form of value, it became the money form of value and we began to understand value in terms of money
- This obscures the social nature of labour and the relationship between producers
- We're not seeing the world clearly as long as we are still viewing it through the lens of money
- Capitalism also reinforces the fetishization of commodities because mass production and alienation reinforces the social implications of commodities
- Marx mentions the primal economy of Robinson Crusoe and the simplistic structure of feudalism as systems where commodities were less fetishized
- The essential component is the divorce of commodities being produced from the need to labour to survive
- You still need to labour to survive, but capitalism adds extra steps
- Marx proposes a society where a community produces together, each takes what they need and each produces what they can
- Emphasizes the incongruity between appearance and reality, like how Copernicus discovered that the earth orbits the sun
- Marx also calls back to Ricardo, mentioning how he discovered that rent comes not from the powers of the soil, but the social process of land allocation

**Chapter 2**

- The only reason to sell a commodity is because it has no use value, only exchange value
- As the scale of markets grow, so does the total quantity of money
- Money is not just simply a hunk of precious metal, it has embodied social value
- We trust that our money will continue to be valuable, and we pledge to recognize the value of the money held by others
- Marx sees money as a commodity, and just as we rely on social relations to determine the value of money, the same is true for all other commodities

**Chapter 3**

- Commodities gain their use value when we purchase them, and at the same time they lose their exchange value when removed from the market
- This action is doubled, you are not just buying something, but enabling someone to make a sale
- Marx calls this a metamorphosis of commodities
- The act of selling or purchasing is effectively the transformation of commodities into money or money into commodities
- Money is the primary agent of exchange, as commodities may be consumed but money is always in circulation as the seller will eventually used the money from a sale to purchase something
- Capitalism didn't exist as a concept at the time of Ricardo, but had been fully defined by the time Marx comes around
- The process of circulation transcends local and personal bounds and groups together billions of people
- There is no equilibrium, a sale/purchase is one identical act of exchange between people on opposite sides of the system, producer and consumer

**Chapter 4**

- Marx identifies the origins of capitalism in 16th century Europe, specifically from the development of massive globe-spanning trade routes
- The ordinary exchange of commodities is replaced by a system where money is not just a unit of exchange, but also a store of wealth which serves as capital itself
- C-M-C is commodities sold for money which is exchanged for commodities, but in capitalism you perform an M-C-M operation
- You acquire money through a loan or inheritance, which is used to buy commodities which you can sell for profit
- The purpose of a merchant is to circulate commodities in order to create a surplus for themselves
- Each sale creates a degree of surplus value that the merchant will save in order to accrue more capital that can be used to buy further commodities and accelerate accruement of capital
- The question to be answered is where that surplus value comes from
- The answer is that capitalists can also exchange money for labourers, and their labour has the ability to create value
- Marx's primary criticism of capitalism is that it ignores the primary purpose of production which is to create goods for people to consume for their well being
- Instead, the goal of capitalism is the creation of more capital and the augmentation of exchange value
- He likens capitalists to the archetype of the miser, but while misers are capitalists gone mad, capitalists are rational misers
- For a capitalist to succeed, instead of pursuing the natural form of exchange where money is used to buy commodities, it is used to create more money
- The implication is that in order to do this, you must take unethical or underhanded actions

**Chapter 6**

- In capitalism, if you do not have commodities or money, all you have is your labour
- When money and commodities are exchanged, the appearance of equivalent exchange is created
- However, we are not accounting for the phenomena of labour power
- Labour power is the aggregate capabilities of a person which is used to created use values
- Like wheat and linen, labour power is a commodity that can be owned and sold
- We are always owners of our own labour, but depending on the contract signed we may donate our labour to others in exchange for something
- This works in the same way as the selling and purchasing of traditional commodities
- Even when an individual has no physical commodities to sell in a market, they still have their labour power to sell
- This phenomenon is of no interest to capitalists, as labour is simply another commodity
- Alienation emerges from the problem of labourers embodying their labour power into commodities and can never retrieve that labour
- Marx argues that the division between those owning capital and those lacking it is unnatural and a product of historical processes that eliminated alternate forms of social production
- Fun fact, Marx studied missionary reports of Canadian First Nations as an example of a potential vision of society with communal means of production
- The same argument is made here as in Ricardo, that the value of labour power comes from the need to reproduce and replace your own labour power
- What is required in order for an individual to reproduce depends on where in the world you live and at what time period

**Chapter 7**

- Capitalists will buy labour power in order to use it to produce goods
- Labour is also intertwined with nature, and the method through which man subjugates and controls nature
- He also argues that labour is something unique to humans
- Although spiders and bees also create complex structures, Marx argues that this is different because we do not create from instinct, but with intention and vision
- Human labour is required to transform the products of nature into something that has use value and exchange value
- Consumption exists as the interstitial state between production, as we need to sustain ourselves through food, rest, shelter, entertainment
- For Marx, production is the purpose of life, not consumption
- Once a labourer comes under the control of the capitalist, they lose control of their life
- The essence of the labourer is removed from the final product, which is homogenous and indistinct in terms of the labour power that created it
