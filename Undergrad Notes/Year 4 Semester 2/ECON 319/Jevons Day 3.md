- Utility theory of value is supposed to be universal, doesn't apply just to commodities
- Since anything that has a price must have utility, then all prices can be described using the utility theory of value
- This applies across time and space, meaning that value is independent of the past
- Jevons argues that once spent, labour has no influence on the future value of an object
- This is because utility looks to the future, the value of objects is determined by the utility they will have in the future
- The fallacy of sunk costs occurs because we let decisions about utility in the past determine our decisions in the future
- Jevons is making a radical shift in economics here, as most previous theorists tend to focus overwhelmingly on the origin of goods to determine their value
- He also posed his "Catena" which shows the process by which value is determined
- The cost of production determines the supply, supply determines the degree of utility, and the degree of utility determines value
- After avoiding the issue of the supply side, this is the first mention of a non-consumption related variable

**Chapter 5**

- This chapter covers Jevon's theory of labour, explained using the utility theory of value
- Labour is heterogenous, and is therefore not a good measure of value
- For Ricardo and Marx, all labour was reducible to a basic homogenous "labour unit"
- For Jevons, labour is any painful exertion undergone to create future good
- Labour ceases when the pain is equal to the pleasure gained
- On the graph where we measure utility, we can also measure the utility created by labour
- As we work, we begin in a state of pain, but there is a brief moment where it is pleasurable
- This moves us along the utility curve, generating significant utility at the beginning but less as time goes on
- When the amount of pain caused by working an additional amount of time is greater than the pleasure that comes from the utility created by that additional time, we stop
- From this, Jevons determines that the wage equals the final degree of labour productivity
- Similar to Ricardo's theory of rent,
- Prices and wages are determined simultaneously, at the moment of exchange
- In classical economics, wages are set before prices and determine the long term price
- Proposed that we should study the intensity of labour in scientific studies
- This is the origin of Taylorism, which emerged around this time

**Chapter 7**

- Capital is the sum of the commodities required for sustaining labourers, including the food, clothes, and shelter for those workers
- Tools and machines are included in this as well, but he doesn't mention this
- This is what Ricardo referred to as variable capital
- Jevons's definition of capital is something that allows us to expend labour in advance
- Similar to embodied labour, we can invest labour into a tool or some food ahead of time, and that can be used in the future to enable further labour
- Capital is also always depreciating, and must be repaired or replaced
- Some capital actually appreciates, but this is rare
- Because of this, capital is intimately connected to time
- As more capital is employed, the average interval of time between the first exertion of labour and the final product increases
- Pre-industrial production used little capital, and as such this interval was small
- The way that Marx thinks of labour as embodied, Jevons imagines capital
- Capital can be "fixed" into objects where it exists until it has depreciated completely
- Pretty much everyone is a capitalist by this definition, except we just consume all of our production ourselves
- We all own clothes and other objects, and replace and maintain these objects to continue living our lives
- This is a continuation of Adam Smith's assertion that Britain is a nation of shopkeepers
- If the return to labour is the wage, the return to capital is profit
- Then, if wage is determined by the marginal productivity of labour, then profit is the marginal productivity of capital
- Recalling Ricardo's theory of diminishing returns, this is that theory taken to its logical conclusion
- Since the price is determined at the margin, where no rent is paid, then the profit is determines by the price at the margin
- Profits tend to decline over time, but Jevons argues that this is because of capital accumulation
- Profit is determined in relation to the entire stock of capital

**Chapter 8**

- Given a certain population with needs and means of production, the way they employ their labour will maximize the utility of their production
- Population growth is ignored here, and also the profit-wage relationship
- The only thing that matters is maximizing utility
- Profits are determined before wages, because some workers have their wages determined by the profit rate
- Jevons's view is that the wage is the last thing to be determined, and because of that, all classes are sort of "in it together"
- Basically means that classes don't exist
- In conclusion, Jevons rejects the ideas presented by Ricardo and Mill
- Saying this to argue that economics must adopt the utility theory of value over the labour theory of value, and become a mathematical science rooted in calculus
- Marshall was sympathetic towards Ricardo, and argued that Jevons didn't give him enough credit
- Keynes was a great admirer of Jevons, as was Schumpeter

**Marginal Revolution**

- Jevons was part of the larger movement away from Classical economics, and towards the ideas of neoclassical economics where the previous views were still accepted but less emphasized
- Revolutions tend to be defined by large overturns of long-held assumptions
- Usually inspired by young thinkers, usually existing on the margins of their field
- In economics, we have the Marginal Revolution and the Keynesian Revolution
- Jevons was the main instigator in the English world, and was joined by Carl Menger in German, and Leon Walras in French
- The neoclassical school of economics is still dominant today
- Defined by the utility theory of value, analysis at the margin, and the heavy use of mathematics
- This usually means calculus, but statistics and probability theory also emerge
- Economics is seen as epistemologically equal to sciences like physics, with unchangeable laws
- Tends to focus heavily on the individual, and understanding the reasoning behind decisions
- This gets around issues such as class and the iron law of wages
- Some have argued that marginal analysis was present in Ricardo, and Jevons found that numerous other thinkers had discovered marginal analysis years before he did
- Always done by groups of three people working separately, often in different languages
- Nassau Senior, Dupuit, and Gossen did it in English, French, and German respectively
- Some argue that we should not treat the creators of marginal analysis together, but look at their influences separately
- Why did the revolution succeed, and why did it center around Jevons's ideas specifically?
