##### Philosophy of Science
- Defining science is often done by exploring the methods of science
- Knowledge is always subject to revision, as hypotheses can never be proven, only disproven
- Kuhn argues that all theories are "born refuted" because of inevitable flaws in their assumptions and methods
- Economists often [[Mill Day 1#^144920|compare economics to physics]] 
- Example of [[Jevons Day 1|Jevons]] and the lever
##### The Methodology of Positive Economics
- Friedman borrows the distinction between positive and normative economics from John Neville Keynes, [[Keynes Day 1|Keynes's father]] 
- Theories are essentially just sets of tautologies
- A tautology is a proposition that is always true
- Friedman focused mainly on the obsession with assumptions when it came to critiquing economic theories
- These theories are never fully confirmed, despite the mountain of evidence we have for the quantity theory of money, it is still debated
- Empirical evidence is used twice in the development of a theory
- First to create the hypothesis itself, second to test it
- These two stages happen simultaneously
- The assumptions of a hypothesis do not need to conform to reality as long as its implications and predictions do
- All science requires abstraction, and all assumptions are false to some extent
- Uses examples of perfect competition and the law of falling bodies
- Origin of the realist versus instrumentalist debate
- Realists claim that unobservable claims can correspond to the world, but instrumentalists say no
- Friedman emphasizes that theories treat assumptions "as if" they are true, not implying that they are true
- The goal of a scientific theory is not to perfectly replicate the real world, but to make predictions about it
