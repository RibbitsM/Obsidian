**General Remarks**

- Jevons was opposed to the labour theory of value, and rejected the Ricardo-Mill doctrine
- Believed strongly in the utility theory of value, and thought that the study of economics should focus on examining how utility is formed
- Borrowed ideas from Bentham about how pleasure and pain forms the decisions of individuals
- Uses the common quote drawn from Bentham about how mankind is governed by pleasure and pain
- Also wrote a book on utilitarianism himself, but mainly took just the concepts of utility for his works in economics
- This is because that Jevons thought that economic was aethical, meaning that it was value neutral
- Economics does not deal with moral considerations since it focuses on the most base human instincts
- Endorsed Mill's argument that economics is deductive, but also had his own theory that inductive logic is the opposite of deduction
- Generally in science there is a hypothesis to be tested, but this is more difficult to come by in economics where any verification is almost impossible

**Axioms**

- Jevons laid out a system of axioms that he believed govern human behaviour
- One example would be that every person will choose the greater apparent good
- The other were that human wants are more or less quickly satiated, and that prolonged labour becomes more and more painful
- Based on these axioms, he believed that one could deduce the laws of value formation as well as supply and demand
- Unlike Mill, Jevons does not include any caveats for these rules
- Jevons believed that since economists deal with quantities, then they should be reasoning mathematically
- Regardless of the use of symbols, economics is a fundamentally mathematical science
- Most importantly, he argued that economists should adopt calculus as all other sciences were currently using calculus
- Economics was currently an inexact science because verification was impossible
- Just because a topic is mathematical, does not mean that it is exact
- Points out that other branches of science at the time like the study of tides were also inexact
- It's not that he believed that these disciplines would never be exact, just that the necessary tools and date were not yet available

**Logical and Mathematical Sciences**

- Jevons was incredibly influential in the development of the logical sciences, and inspired Frege and Russell who argued that math was derived from logic
- Logic determines if something will happen or not, and mathematics simply adds a quantitative dimension to logic
- Economics is the perfect mathematical science, because it has an unheard of volume of quantitative data stored in the accounting books of the world to draw from
- Jevons saw this as an opportunity to perfect economics through data
- He compares economics to the study of temperature, we used to only understand this as hot and cold until we developed thermometers to quantitatively describe temperature
- This then led to the discovery of the underlying laws of temperature and the laws of thermodynamics
- Jevons sought to do this in economics, but with the quantitative measurement of utility
- However, we don't necessarily need some sort of instrument to measure "utils" because in economics we always focus on relative and marginal values
- We don't need to know the total pleasure received from an object, we just need to know the difference between determining the utility of the good vs the money spent to buy it
- The price is given, but each person has a metric by which they determine the utility they would gain or give up by paying it
- All the phenomena of a market can be boiled down to the human decisions to purchase and sell

**Chapter 2**

- Similar to gravity, we cannot directly observe utility but we can observe its effects
- Jevons saw the human will as a way to measure utility, which can be observed by looking at the effects on the market
- We can't read minds but we can infer what is happening in someone's mind by scrutinizing their actions
- Bentham came up with pleasure and pain, but also 5 more dimensions of utility
- Jevons takes pleasure and pain, but also duration and intensity
- He argues that the remaining three are best left to philosophy
- Feelings can be compared in terms of duration and intensity, regardless of what the actual emotion being experienced is
- Intensity of emotion is not fixed, it is constantly in flux, and this complicates the measurement of the duration of emotions
- To solve this, Jevons assumes that emotions are constant for brief periods, and increase and decrease by steps across these periods of time
- If a feeling is decreasing over time, we can graph this decrease to see the diminishment of utility
