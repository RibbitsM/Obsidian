**Chapter 3**

- For Jevons, the main problem of economics is to satisfy our wants to the greatest extent with the least possible effort, similar to Mill
- This is more or less what we use in economics today, it's an enduring assumption
- Utility is usually acquired or lost through objects, but this can include actions and services
- For Jevons, commodities are objects, substances, actions, or services that can provide utility or pleasure or prevent disutility or pain
- Utility is the quality held by an object that serves a human purpose, and is what defines an object as a commodity
- Jevons sought to make economics more realistic, and not make assumptions as occurs in science
- Instead of focusing on production, Jevons seeks to explain the contributions of consumption
- Utility is not intrinsic to objects, but is created through the interaction between the object and humanity
- The utility of an object is not linear, depending on the situation the utility of a commodity will change
- For example, someone dying of thirst would value a single glass of water at near infinite utility, while an ordinary person would not do so
- As someone drinks more and more water, they value it less and less
- What matters the most to economists is the utility of an additional unit at the point where a consumer stops consuming
- This is what we call the marginal utility of a good
- Similar to pleasure and pain, utility is determines by the quantity of a commodity and the intensity of the effect
- Jevons uses the same curve, with bars stepping down as consumption increases
- For him, economics is fundamentally about the individual, so if we can model how the average individual acts we will model the entire economy
- The final degree of utility is the degree of utility of the last addition to the commodity
- Jevons suggests that the law of diminishing marginal utility has a physiological basis
- Unbeknownst to him, psychologists in Germany were studying threshold sensations and basically studied the same thing
- Repeated stimuli had a diminishing effect over time

**Chapter 4**

- Jevons defines a market as access to information, telegraph technology had eliminated the need for a physical market ^e1da38
- A market would be theoretically perfect if all traders had perfect knowledge of supply and demand, which still hasn't been achieved
- Jevons also used the assumption of one price for one good
- Argues that the quantity of a good consumed is a function of the price, consumers will buy goods according to the price
- The market is dynamic, it is constantly in flux
- However, exchange is always measured at a static point
- When we observe an exchange, we assume all units of a good are exchanged at the same price
- The price is determined by the final degree of utility for a commodity when it is exchanged
- Price is equal to marginal benefit
- At the moment of an exchange, the effect of supply and demand on the price is insignificant
- Changes in price are caused by the overbalancing of those who will and will not sell at a certain price point
- Markets may briefly reach equilibrium, but this likely never actually happens
