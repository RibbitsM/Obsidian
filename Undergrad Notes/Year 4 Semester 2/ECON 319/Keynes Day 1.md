- Generally supported capitalism, but believed that it should be constrained
- Ideology built upon greed, but can be salvaged with extensive social programs
- Advised that the government should control the economy, was a great supporter of FDR and the New Deal project supporting infrastructure projects and social security
- In fact, his earlier writing may have even influenced these projects, though FDR would later meet with Keynes personally after he authored the _General Theory_
- Published _The End of Laissez Faire_ in 1926
- Government roles include reducing unemployment, controlling the interest rate and the money supply, and incentivizing investment
- Published his _General Theory_ in 1936, during the Great Depression
- Basically meant that the economic world was much more receptive to reform at the time

**Main Contributions**

- Advanced three main theses
- First, money matters, it is not a neutral element of economic growth
- In contrast to previous thinkers, it's not just seen as an measure of value but it has a tangible effect of the production and distribution of wealth
- Second, unemployment is difficult to correct, long term slumps in unemployment are possible because there is no guarantee that an economy will right itself
- Finally, the government needs to play a significant role in correcting the economy as the invisible hand cannot always correct itself sufficiently
- Keynes employed a number of technical terms, many of which were not original
- Talks about preference for liquidity, the three types of demand for money (precautionary, speculative, and transactional), sticky wages, the marginal efficiency of capital, and more

**Pre-Keynesian**

- The core idea of Keynes is that money matters, and for the majority of classical economics this is not true
- Since the writings of Copernicus in the 1520s, we've known that the price level is a function of the money supply
- If the supply increases we get inflation, otherwise we get deflation
- The quantity theory of money was determined by Irving Fisher in 1911, which includes the velocity of money and the volume of transactions into the equation
- The alternative view of money was the cash-balance approach, advanced mainly by Thomas Tooke
- Focuses on the purchasing power of money and the demand for money, also called liquidity
- Generally, your liquidity is determined by your income and the portion of your income held as cash
- People have reason to have liquid money, and this is central to Keynes
- The Fisher equation we talked about earlier distinguishes between the real and nominal interest rate
- The nominal rate is the real rate plus the change in price level
- Since we can't know the real interest rate, it is calculated by subtracting inflation from the nominal interest rate
- In practice, this is not as simple as you would think, especially when comparing real interest rates between countries
- Fisher described the interest rate as a monetary phenomenon, it's the price of loanable funds
- The supply of loanable money and the demand for loans determines the interest rate
- However, Keynes argued that it is also a function of the marginal product of capital
- Not just a measure of demand, but also an indication of productivity

**More Terms**

- The multiplier was developed alongside Richard Kahn, and represents the reciprocal of the Marginal Propensity to Save
- Distinguished between ex ante and ex post savings, meaning savings you plan to make, and the savings you actually carry out
- Often, there is a fairly significant gap between the two
- All actual savings are equal to investment, but not all planned savings
- The two factors that draw from income are savings and consumption, those are the only options

**Chapter One**

- Keynes defines the classical school of economics as anything between Ricardo and Marshall and Pigou
- However, he notably excludes Malthus and Jevons from his definition
- Generally disregarded the classical school, believed that their theories only applied to some cases
- Keynes believed that he was doing something original and radically different from what came before him, what we now call the Keynesian Revolution
- Argues against a unitary view of economics, believed that there were multiple different stable and unstable possibilities the economy could exist in
- The case that the classical school described does not apply to the society that we currently live in, and we should not adopt it's teachings because of this
- Also argued that unemployment could be involuntary, previously all thinkers believed that it was always voluntary
- Implicitly compares himself to Albert Einstein, who had written his theories of general and special relativity in 1905 and 1915
- Just as Einstein proved that the world was non-Euclidean, Keynes will disregard the notion that all employment is voluntary
- Assuming that this assumption is true, we must construct a new theory where this is possible
