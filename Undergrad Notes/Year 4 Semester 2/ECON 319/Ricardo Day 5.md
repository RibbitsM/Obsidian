**Chapter 30**

- Most other significant thinkers at the time held the opinion that prices increased and decreased according to the fluctuations of demand
- Ricardo accepts this, but believes that the true determiner of price in the long run is the cost of production
- Demand can change any amount, but the price will eventually settle to the natural price determined by the cost of production
- Humans do not actually produce anything themselves, they simply transform the gifts already provided to them by the earth
- These are the gifts of nature that Ricardo talks about earlier
- The principles of price also extend to wage and labour, which is determined by the cost of living
- If the cost of living rises, eventually wages will rise as well
- The exception to this rule is monopoly goods, prices in a monopoly will be determined only by supply and demand
- Generally speaking, for Ricardo any good that is luxury or otherwise uncommon is not produced under monopoly conditions

**Chapter 31**

- The existing view is that new machinery is always useful
- This saves labour, lowers the price which allows more consumption, more profit for the capitalist for a limited time
- Ricardo was writing at the time of the cotton gin and the mechanized loom, both very controversial technologies that created large surges in unemployment
- The common view was that this was temporary, and the capital that would have paid for their wages would be reinvested in another sector where those workers could be employed
- Ricardo posits that humans have satiable desires for food, but unlimited desire for any non-food commodities like clothing, ornamental goods, entertainment, etc.
- This means that there is always more demand, and this demand will prompt constant economic growth
- However, Ricardo argues that machinery is very damaging to labourers, and not just those who are immediately displaced
- Because the capitalist is indifferent to the number of workers they hire, portions of the population may become entirely redundant because of advances in machinery
- He uses the example of a manufacturer that produces both corn and cloth
- They have 20,000, 7000 in fixed capital and 13000 in variable capital and make a 10% profit
- Initially the product per year is 15,000, but after replacing labour with a machine their yearly product drops to 7500
- In the aftermath, variable capital is only 5500 and fixed capital is 14500, and although the gross product is only 7500, the capitalist will still be making 2000 profit, the same as before
- The capitalist is indifferent to this reduction in product, because they still make a profit
- Plus, machines have much more consistent performance than labourers and require less maintenance
- Ricardo was writing during the time of the Luddites, originating from the myth of Ned Ludd breaking into factories to smash mechanical looms
- He endorsed the Luddite movement, believing that workers were justified in opposing mechanization
- The only time all three classes would be better off would be if the mechanization was such a marked increase that both gross and net product were increased
- In this case, the savings would be sufficient to stimulate new investment and higher demand for workers
- Ricardo also makes a distinction between productive and unproductive expenditure of revenue
- Ideally, profits should be reinvested into the service sector since these movies would primarily go towards the wages of labourers
- When labour in manufacturing decreases, these workers will likely become servants
- Another observation he makes is that war increases the demand for labour since most of the labour force is recruited into the army
- Overall, Ricardo does not discourage the process of mechanization he is just pointing out the downsides
- The assumption in his analysis is that improved machinery is suddenly discovered and implemented everywhere, but this is rarely the case
- Also, this must be happening in sectors producing wage goods
- There is also the assumption that the funding for these machines comes from the same fund used to pay for the wages
- He also posited that America would not lean into mechanization because food is so plentiful there
- This was heavily based on Thomas Jefferson's vision of the US as an agrarian economy since they had so much arable land and working in agriculture was preferable to factory work
- In the long term, demand for labour will increase with demand for capital, but as mechanization increases, the demand for labour will not increase at the same rate
- There is always constant competition between machines and labourers, but mechanization is still preferable because if you don't mechanize, someone else will
- If a good is produced more cheaply abroad, there will be no demand at all for labour to produce that good because it will simply be imported
- In order to avoid the complete abandonment of labour, we must accept temporary falls in the amount of labour being employed

**Chapter 7**

- Going back to Ricardo's theory of trade, one key assumption is that goods can be represented in terms of abstract "labour units"
- These aren't necessarily wages or time, just the amount of labour required to produce a good
- Since Ricardo supports the labour theory of value, these units also represent the price
- If the total labour units in a country utilized can be reduced through trade, this surplus can be used for enjoyment
- To complicate this, Ricardo introduces money, wages, and exchange rates
- Trade will lead to higher wages and prices for the country with lower costs of production
- The monetary prices will mainly be determined by the access to precious metals in the respective countries
- However, currency will still be exchanged between countries which enables this model to approach the original barter system of trade
- Going back to Hume, he predicted that if a country imports more than it exports, inflation may occur
- As a result of trade, exchange rates across the world will adjust to approximate the flow of trade between countries
- Countries that are net exporters will tend to have lower value currencies compared to net importers
- As a result of comparative advantage and exchange rates, countries with few advantages can still profit from their most advantaged good and cheap currency
- Ricardo was not the only economist to propose comparative advantage, there were several other thinkers that though about this at the same time
- Another economist focused on trade was John Stuart Mill who tried further to incorporate foreign exchange rates
- Later on in 1933, the Heckshler-Olin model was developed to include all factors of production
- Ricardo's work was written in the context of the Napoleonic wars, one of the results of war was that english bank notes were no longer convertible to gold
- At the time, the bank of england was not a national central bank, there were several different private banks operating across the island
- This was one of the first examples of fiat money, and Ricardo strongly protested this
- Eventually, Britain's gold reserves increased once more and the gold standard returned
- Likely a result of Ricardo's obsession with gold as a store of value which he shared with other economists such as John Locke

**Conclusion**

- Overall, Ricardo has been criticized for his excessive abstraction and neglect to explain any of his assumptions
- However, most of these assumptions were based on earlier thinkers like Adam Smith, and he ended up setting the stage for the future of economics by pioneering the deductive method
- Schumpeter even coined the term "Ricardian vice" to refer to high levels of abstraction in economics, but as we have seen from most modern economists, the Ricardian method stuck
- An especially valuable contribution of Ricardo was the dismissal of historical accounts of economics, especially Adam Smith's historical diatribes
- Also, his model of trade was popular and simple enough that it is still taught as the introduction to international trade theory worldwide
- Ricardo also drew some criticism from his description of rent, but overall his definition of "return to the original and indestructible powers of the soil" was incredibly influential
- New discoveries have also come to light on the research done by Ricardo, especially in terms of how he described the economics of agriculture
- He was also a close friend of Jeremy Bentham and James Mill the famous utilitarians, which was reflected in his economic principles, especially in terms of trade and universal good
- While he did have some tendency to prioritize British interests, overall he was innovative in his more global ideals
- In terms of his politics, Ricardo was a Radical who served in Parliament for 5 years where he gave serval famous speeches on freedom of religion, establishing a capital gains tax, corn laws, and reducing/eliminating capital punishment
- Was also a good friend of Malthus who also wrote a book called Principles of Political Economy
- His primary contributions were the reinforcement of the labour theory of value, and by extension the theory of price based on the supply side
- He also talked about the three classes of labourers, capitalists, and landlords as well as some of the conflict between those classes
- Another important precedent set by Ricardo was his lack of discussion of money and the focus on commodities and their relative values
- Finally, he developed Malthus's ideas of population growth and contributed to the law of the falling long term rate of profit
