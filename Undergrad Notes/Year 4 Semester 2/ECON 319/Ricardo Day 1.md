- Ricardo is known for pioneering the axiomatic-deductive method which is the gold standard for economic analysis today
- Also used empirical studies to test and confirm his theories as well as implementing his ideas through his role as a member of parliament
- Born to a poor family in London, died with a fortune in excess of 20 million pounds adjusted for inflation that he made through his career as a stock broker
- His residence, Gatcombe Park, is now owned by the British royal family

**Chapter 1**

- Ricardo recognizes the difficulty of determining the absolute value of a good, and instead we can only determine the relative value of goods
- In fact, price is nothing more than a ratio of the relative value between currency and a good
- For anything to have a price, it first must have utility
- However, though all priced goods must have utility, the price isn't necessarily determined only by that utility
- He uses the example of water and gold, water has immense utility but is available at a very low price, while gold has little utility but a large price
- Ricardo also mentions the exception of rare goods like paintings and fine wines which are so scarce at such a high price that they are outliers with prices determined by demand, not the good itself
- He defines a commodity as a good that can be produced with little to no limits, what we would call an elastic supply
- Throughout this book we are primarily examining the supply side of the market

**Price Theory**

- Ricardo believes in the price theory, or exchange theory of value
- This sat in opposition to the labour theory of value which was pioneered by John Locke, David Hume, and Adam Smith and of course developed later by Karl Marx
- This theory claims that all goods are created through labour, and the value of a good can be objectively determined by the value of the labour necessary to produce that good
- Adam Smith believes that the opportunity cost of labouring to create something is the value of labour, the "toil and trouble" of making a good
- Subjectively speaking, people tend to think of cost in terms relative to their wage
- The value of a good is still a ratio, but the ratio of labour required
- The example he uses is if in a hunter-gatherer society it would take twice as much time to hunt and kill a beaver compared to a deer, then one beaver is worth two deer
- Ironically, though socialism didn't exist yet, Adam Smith did talk about how all labourers should be compensated in proportion to their labour
- He identifies that this does not happen, because of the ownership of capital
- Using this theory, we can predict that if the amount of labour needed to purchase a good falls, so will the price albeit not by the same magnitude
- Ricardo identified that a similar rule exists with wages, where when labour becomes more efficient then wages tend to rise
- However, wages are sticky, meaning that they rise and fall only incrementally over time
- Ricardo determined that the wage is not an accurate measure of value
- In order to embody the concept of labour in price we have to look at how much of a commodity is produced by labour, not how much workers are compensated for their labour
- Ricardo observed that when wages paid in wheat bushels increased while the price of wheat falls, nominal wages have increased, but real wages have fallen
- This proves a contradiction within Smith's model since his labour theory of value can't account for situations when nominal wage goes up but real wage goes down
- Ricardo looked at wage based on profession in the same way as Smith, through the "wage spectrum"
- There is a base wage for unskilled labour, and increases above the base wage can be accounted for by skill required, risk or investment involved, or the desirability of the work
- Ricardo is now using the same assumptions as most economists, perfect competition, uniform base wage, etc.

**Capital Input**

- Capital is required for almost all kinds of labour, but is also produced by labour
- Ricardo claims that because of this, capital can be measured in terms of embodied labour
- Labour time determines the value of a good, since if labour becomes more efficient then the good produced becomes less valuable
- Also identifies the inverse profit-wage relationship
- Assuming profit is return to capital and wage is return to labour, when profit goes up, wages go down and vice versa
- If capital did not exist and all goods could be created during the same production period, all prices would correspond directly to the quantity of labour put in
- Different parts of the economy use different K/L ratios
- Ricardo assumes that the capitalist has taken out a loan, and is paying their workers with this loan as well as paying operating costs and buying inventory
- This is called circulating capital, and is used up at a fixed interval
- Fixed capital is more permanent, and makes up the rest of capital expenditure
- Because of this, changes is wage affect different commodities differently depending on the capital to labour ratio
- Ricardo uses shoes as an example of a good requiring more circulating capital and beer as a good requiring more fixed capital
- If wages increase for all workers, shoes will rise in price more relative to the price of beer
- In opposition to Adam Smith, Ricardo claims that increasing wages does not cause inflation
- Another incredibly important economic law is the Quantity Theory of Money developed by Copernicus
- He determined that the price level in a state is a function of the money supply
- If the money supply in a state increases, so does the overall price level
- Because of this, for inflation to occur, more money has to enter the system
- Increasing wages doesn't do this because that money comes from the higher prices of labour intensive vs capital intensive goods
- Because of this, everything balances out and the overall money supply remains constant
- Also, because of the inverse profit-wage relationship since the profits in the beer industry are higher, capitalists will move into that sector
- As a result, supply of beer increases and price decreases, returning the system to equilibrium
- Since a rise in wages causes a fall in profits, capitalists will try and mechanize jobs further in response to replace workers
