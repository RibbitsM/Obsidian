**Chapter Two**

- This chapter is dedicated to the "postulates" of the classical economists
- Criticizes Ricardo for only focusing on distribution when trying to calculate national output
- The first postulate he talks about is that the wage is equal to the marginal productivity of labour
- Next, he says that the real wage is equal to the marginal disutility of labour
- Basically saying the supply of labour is based on the subjective desire for leisure
- Keynes does not assume perfect competition, the demand schedule is affected due to capital accumulation by cartels or large firms
- Also affected by competition between firms and trade unions, but also competition between trade unions
- Keynes centered his entire argument over involuntary unemployment, but no one really knows exactly what he meant by this
- He talks about three different definitions of unemployment, all very technical
- The first is what we call frictional unemployment
- These are people who are between jobs, whether because of seasonal demand, unplanned complications from weather, economic shocks, etc. or imperfect information in general
- For example, auto workers are paid high salaries because it is almost guaranteed they will be laid off for a period of time when demand for cars declines
- The second is voluntary unemployment
- This refers to people who are unable to work because they're on strike, or are disabled in some way, or are not legally allowed to work
- Can also refer to people who are just unwilling to work because they don't accept the system of exchanging labour for value
- These were the two types of unemployment advanced by Pigou, but Keynes added a third
- The third type is involuntary unemployment
- Refers to situations where if the real wage is lowered, even if some people are willing to work at that wage and there are firms willing to hire them, the two do not meet
- The reason for a market failure here is implied to be a lack of capital because money is not being invested, likely due to an excess liquidity of money
- Pigou identified four factors for increasing employment
- First, improve hiring mechanisms to decrease frictional unemployment
- Second, decrease the marginal disutility of labour
- Third, increase the marginal productivity of labour in the wage-goods sector
- Fourth, increase the price of luxury goods proportionate to wage-goods

**Wage Contracts**

- Keynes argued that the supply of labour is not solely determined by real wages
- Nominal wages are often sticky because time is needed for unions to negotiate wage contracts
- Money wages and real wages generally move in opposite directions
- Unions bargain for real wages, but they can't negotiate to lower their wages and in exchange, lower the price of goods
- Always comes in the form of nominal wage increases
- Remember that labour sectors matter because an increase in the wage of one sector usually means a decrease in the wage of another sector
- Keynes is overall supportive of unions, as they give stability to wages
- Not every change in price level causes a shift in the labour supply because unions keep the wages sticky through contract negotiations
- Workers will tend to not resist reduction in real wages, but will resist reductions in nominal wages
- Argues that workers are more reasonable than economists assume

**Postulates**

- Going back to the postulates, the first one states that the real wage is equal to the marginal disutility of existing employment
- The second is that involuntary unemployment doesn't exist
- Third is that Say's Law holds true, that supply creates its own demand
- Mill said that if we double the supply of commodities in all markets, the purchasing power of all producers double and everyone buys twice as much
- These three laws connect to one another and Keynes seeks to discredit all three

**Chapter Seven**

- Generally, savings are equal to investments
- Savings is understood as whatever money is not consumed
- However, there is no clear definition of investment
- Usually defined as the purchase of an asset, but all this does is cancel out prior investments
- For example, if someone buys a bond nothing new is created
- Keynes argues that we should focus on net investment
- This is the accumulation of capital, either fixed or liquid
- From here on, investment is used to mean net investment
- Ralph Hawtrey thought of investment as connected to unsold inventory
- The Austrian economists argued that capital was formed when the period of production was lengthened
- Keynes rejected both of these approaches, and chose to focus on the change in effective demand, determined by inventory and unused fixed capital
- Effective demand means demand from those who can afford the good
- This is not equal to income, because people do not spend all of their income
- Because of this, Say's Law is invalid, supply will not always create demand
- Just how each purchase is a sale, each deposition of savings is also an investment
- The bank gains the money, and you lose it
- Saving means acquiring an asset that represents an equal value
- In the aggregate, savings is equal to investment
- Argues that in the aggregate, demand effects supply and not the other way around
- These two values will never be equal

**Additional Material**

- Consumption is a function of income, as income increases so does consumption
- However, this occurs at a diminishing rate, called the marginal propensity to consume which is the inverse of the marginal propensity to save
- There are a number of factors that affect this propensity
- First is changes in real income, second is changes in the value of capital, third is preference for present versus future, fourth is changes in fiscal policy, and the last is expectations of future income
- There is also a number of subjective personality factors that will affect MPC and MPS, generally traits like generosity, avarice, foresight, enjoyment, etc.
- Defined the investment multiplier, calculated where k = 1/(1-MPC) where change in Y is k* delta I where Y is national income and I is national investment
- When aggregate investment increases, income increases by k times the increase in investment
- K is determined by the marginal propensity to consume across an entire economy
- Again, he is putting the majority of the weight on demand and consumption
- Actually devised by Keynes's student, Robert Kahn
- Keynes argued that the multiplier is usually slightly higher than 1
- When the MPC is low, people save more and the multiplier is small
- This keeps the economy stable, moderately increasing the output for each increase in investment
- Also talked about the marginal efficiency of capital which is just the marginal product of capital with a different name
- This value captures the expectations of future yields
- Very similar to the idea of the interest rate or profit rate
- As investment increases, marginal efficiency falls, as does the future yield
- This is because the supply of capital is increasing, which lowers the "price" or yield/interest rate
- Not all forms of capital are created equal
- Firms will envision schedules for the marginal efficiency of capital and demand for investment
- Based on these predictions, they will set output and hire employees
- Investment is the mechanism by which the future financially affects the present
- Again, money matters
- Holding money is not irrational, because it's not just a lubricant for the market
- It's an entity in itself that causes friction and change in the market
- Money is the drink that stimulates us to lift the cup, but some drops may fall in the process
- Opportunity cost of holding money is determined by the interest rate which is determined partly by the central bank and partly by the aggregate demand
- Aggregate supply is centrally controlled, and aggregate demand is collectively controlled
- There are three motivations for holding cash
- First is to simply have money on hand for purchasing
- Second is to hold money as a store of wealth to anticipate a downturn in the market
- This motive is precautionary, you're not trying to make money but avoid losing it
- Final is the speculative motive, which is when you hold money to profit from financial markets
- This generally happens because interest rates are very low and they will eventually rise
- Sell all your bonds, hold money in cash until they become cheaper again
