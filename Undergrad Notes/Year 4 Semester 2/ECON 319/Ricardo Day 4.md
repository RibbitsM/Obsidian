- Wages and rents will increase over time, this must be counterbalanced by increases in capital
- If wages continue to increase, profit will shrink to zero and there will be no more investment in capital
- Rent will also increase with wages, but the increase in rent will be larger than the increase in wages
- Recall from chapter 1 that increases in wages will not result in inflation
- Ricardo advocates for a free and fair labour market without barriers to labour or welfare for the poor
- This cannot be done all at once, it must be reduced slowly
- Ideally welfare will be handed out by the local government, not the national one
- His main argument is that those who are marginally employed have no motivation to work since they are better off subsisting on welfare
- Argues that as population grows and the frequency of prosperous periods drops, the number of the poor and the burden on the state will increase
- This is unlikely to occur soon, as capital innovation and investment will continue
- In the long term, profits and interest rates will converge since acquiring loans and other financial capital is required for capitalists to continue investment
- This was an idea created by David Hume, and Ricardo is just restating this

**Chapter 6**

- The inverse profit-wage relationship means that as wages increase, profits will decrease
- Ricardo illustrates this using the example of corn, and he argues that no matter what, the revenue of producing corn on the least suitable land is $720
- He illustrates this by showing that as the real price of corn increases, wages must be increased to compensate and the additional money paid in wages is equal to the rise in price
- Because wages and price increase proportionally, profits will never increase
- Producers on higher grade lands may reap a windfall, but overall the real wage of labourers will stay the same
- The only change is that rent will continue to increase as lower and lower grades of land are brought into cultivation
- If we take into consideration capital accumulation, there will be real raises in wages
- But without aggregation, we would have a scenario where wages and profits are caught in a deadlock and rent increases until innovation stagnates, creating the "stationary state"
- The rent is paid for by the consumers that are being faced with higher and higher prices
- However, farmers still want to keep rent low because this prevent wages from rising
- The prices may fluctuate in the short term due to changes in supply or demand, but the long term price will act consistent with these principles
- Some commodities may be more labour or capital intensive, but overall these factors will balance out
- Increases in wages may change relative prices between goods, but not raise prices overall
- Ricardo argues that nothing can affect profits except for wages, but this is questionable
- Says that profits are nothing but the difference between revenue and cost at the margin
- As usual, there can be temporary fluctuations in profit rates but these will be corrected over time
- As mentioned earlier, Ricardo believed that the profit rate was falling over time as worse land is cultivated
- This can be counteracted by the accumulation of capital, and the reason why we are seeing increases in profits is because of capital
- Despite this, there are diminishing returns to the accumulation of capital
- For Ricardo, this floor is at a 7% profit
- Presumably, this has something to do with the permanent productive nature of the land
- Because of the phenomenon of rent increasing when food is scarce, Ricardo argues that landlords are the only group that benefit from suffering

**Chapter 7**

- Ricardo argues that there is always gains to trade
- Also implements ideas of absolute and comparative advantage, you should always specialize in whatever you have the greatest comparative advantage in
- Using the labour theory of value, all goods should be measured in labour hours necessary to produce those goods
- There should be global free trade where all countries produce the good they have the most comparative advantage in and import all other goods
- This model leaves no room for tariffs or any other sorts of protection
- By doing this, the optimal amount of value would be created by every country
- Borrowing the idea of the invisible hand, encouraging all countries to maximize their national interest will create the maximum amount of good
- The point of this analysis is to encourage free trade
- Not only should goods flow freely, but so should capital
- However, this does not happen in reality because of security concerns and hesitancy to move
- Ricardo is assuming a barter system of trade, no money is involved
- Uses England and Portugal trading cloth and wine as an example
- Labour is the only unit of production, and different amounts of these commodities can be made using the same amount of labour
- Between countries, the productivity of labour on different goods differs
- Even if one country has an absolute advantage, it's still better to trade
