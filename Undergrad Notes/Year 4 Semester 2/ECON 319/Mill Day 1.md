- Child prodigy, was reading multi-volume works at age 6
- Arrested at age 21 for spreading information on birth control
- Was not allowed to attend major English universities because his father was atheist, studied in France at the University of Montpellier but never graduated
- Worked at the British East India Company for many years
- In love with Harriet Taylor, married her after her husband died and they wrote On Liberty together
- Allegedly she influenced him to become a socialist
- His philosophy mainly focused on strict empiricism, influenced by Bacon, Locke, and Hume
- Believed there is no knowledge _a priori_, all information comes from human interpretation and abstraction
- Also believed strongly in determinism, but not necessarily fatalism
- All events have a prior cause, and the purpose of science is to uncover these causes
- This is where his economic philosophy comes from, believes all humans have some common tendencies and this is what influences the economy

**Utilitarianism**

- Mill was one of the original proponents of utilitarianism, but was specifically an act utilitarian
- Actions are judged based on whether or not they create the greatest good for the greatest number
- Happiness is measured by non-pecuniary goods, and some goods are superior to others
- Claims that artistic endeavours are inherently better than play, and adhered to strict secularity but was never really hostile to religion
- Just wished for ethics to evolve regardless of religious affiliation
- Also a strong proponent of individual liberty, and was concerned over the tyranny of the majority
- This comes from the idea that social norms tend to dominate society and suppress individual expression
- Argued for economic reform, not for full equality but enough to address inequalities
- Socialist, but more in the social democrat way rather than the communist route
- Never advocated for the abolishment of private property, and believed that competition in the free market was often necessary for incentives
- Lived in a period where Britain was beginning to liberalize its trade policy, until tariffs were raised again in the 1870s
- He was also a member of parliament as a radical, same as Ricardo
- Sought to improve working conditions, particularly for male workers
- By 1867, pretty much any man was able to vote as long has he had a low to moderate income
- Worked to limit the workday, wrote about the subjection of women and advocated for universal education and population control
- Often seen as the final thinker in the classical school of political economy

**Core Principles**

- Agreed with most earlier theories, money is insignificant, free trade is good, economic growth is created by capital accumulation, labour theory of value, etc.
- Also agreed with Malthusian economics, and was anti-union despite claiming to be a socialist
- Taxation was his primary instrument of economic policy, advocated for 100% inheritance tax
- Assumes that people are exclusively motivated by wealth, and are all rational actors
- Argues that while people may have a variety of motives, generally achieving these desires requires at least some degree of wealth
- In general, people prefer to have more money as opposed to less so this is a safe assumption
- Said that all science is at least somewhat based on abstraction, and economics is still a legitimate science despite its assumptions
- While there may be actions where the main and is the acquisition of wealth, there are hardly any that are exclusively about obtaining wealth
- Not only do people want to accumulate the most wealth possible, but they want to do it using the least amount of labour and while fulfilling personal needs
- Defines political economy as a science, but also as part of the philosophical method
- Deduction and induction are both equally valid methods for Mill
- Economics is not really an experimental science since we can't do controlled experiments
- But, Mill argues that it is just as valid of a science as math or physics ^144920
- The goal is to uncover that _a priori_ knowledge, to make observations and derive conclusions about the underlying systems of the world based on those observations

**Disturbing Causes**

- There are layers of abstraction and confusion between the observer and the truth
- Mill calls these disturbing causes, just like how physics assumes a ball will accelerate uniformly down a hill but doesn't in reality because of friction, economics assumes people are purely profit motivated but in reality are sometimes charitable
- Mill is a determinist, so he believes that these disturbing causes will have their own explanations or theories, just like friction in physics
- All disturbing causes are just phenomenon that are yet to be theorized
- Whenever you find a law and there is an exception, find the law that explains that exception
- Sometimes, finding the solutions to these problems requires reaching outside the purview of economics ^d67dec
- Conjoining theory to practice will often create problems
- Philosophy may guide the practitioner, but putting theory into practice can also develop theory
- Made a distinction between positive and normative statements, developed further by Friedman
- Mill's ultimate goal was to develop a system of elementary laws to try and use them to discover the causes of a certain effect
