- Two forms of first person authority, epistemic and ethical
- Original trans theory focused on the idea of being "in the wrong body", has now moved towards incongruity between self-identity and how others see you
- Epistemic authority comes from the idea that people know their self-identity more than others
- Ethical authority comes from the idea that it is moral to respect the self identities of others
- In trans politics, the main rhetoric has come from ethical authority
- This is partly because it is more convincing, but also because epistemic authority has some issues, many trans people struggle with their identities for years
- On top of this, most people do not have a full grasp of their self-identity in general
- Feminists in general are anti-essentialist on gender roles, they are seen as unnecessary and harmful
- Trans feminists believe that respect for gender identity is essential
- The question is whether or not innate gender identity exists, and feminists and trans feminists seem to disagree on this point
- From a psychological standpoint, research seems to confirm the existence of innate gender identity

**Jenkins**

- Katharine Jenkins has likely the most popular theory of gender identity, called the norm-relevancy view
- The basic idea is that to have a gender identity means that the norms of some gender social position are relevant to you and your life
- Gender identities always correspond to social positions, so there is no suggestion that identity is innate, Jenkins is also an anti-essentialist
- In Jenkins example, feeling that a norm is relevant isn't the same as complying with that norm
- She uses the example of women shaving their leg hair
- If a woman feels a need to shave her leg hair and is relieved by doing so, she is norm compliant
- If a woman who does not shave her leg hair does it not just as personal preference but as rebellion, she is not complying with the norm but still feels that it is relevant
- Both of these experiences only exist because of gender norms around women's leg hair
- This theory has the problem of failing to account for the gender identities of trans women
- For example, if a trans woman transitions in her forties, she probably has a much better sense of how norms for men apply to her as opposed to norms for women

**Bettcher**

- Talia Bettcher wrote a response to Jenkins, focusing on the first person authority of trans people
- Bettcher identifies the importance of speech acts of self-identification
- Illocutionary force is a concept similar to first person authority used to guide the ethics of sharing information
- Bettcher refers to acts of self-identification as "avowals", and when someone makes this statement about themselves they are effectively authoring their identities
- Trying to dispute this kind of avowal is unethical because you are denying that persons right to author their own identity
- Whether or not we truly understand or know our identities, we still have the right to author them
- There may or may not be a universal experience of gender identity, but this solution hopes to at least establish a subjective experience

**Heaton**

- Gender identity is based on desire, the idea of wanting to occupy the social position of that gender
- Being trans is an expression of this desire, but it is not unique to trans people
- Gender dysphoria does provide some legitimacy to the problems of trans people, but also results in treating the experience of being transgender as a mental illness to an extent
- The other problem is that it creates a distinction between how cis and trans people experience gender
- Respecting the gender identity of trans people requires justification and explanation, while it is barely addressed at all for cis people
- Cis people may also experience gender dysphoria, taken straight from the textbook definition
- They show signs of distress when their bodies misalign with their gender identities
- Also may engage in risky behaviour in order to resolve this, including pursuing gender affirming care
- All of this is similar to the experience of trans people, but without the stigmatization
- Cosmetics, cosmetic surgery, some pharmaceutical companies, and underground surgeons all participate in this industry
- The processes may be similar, but the treatment and barriers to treatment are entirely different
