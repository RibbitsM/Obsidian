- Sex and gender are distinct concepts, sex is based on biological characteristics and gender is based on social characteristics
- Gender emerges from the social norm of heterosexuality
- This has lead to some thinkers to hypothesize that lesbians are not women because the concept of "women" is created from heterosexuality

**Gender Realism**

- If we accept Frye's claim that groups are targeted for oppression and women are oppressed, then women must be a group
- If women are a real group, then we can confidently say that feminism is about women and has the goal of supporting the interests of women
- Universalism of kinds states that a group of objects is a real kind if all of those objects share a non-accidental property
- An accidental property is a property grouping objects into a kind that is not an essential part of those objects and is probably temporary, coincidental, or easily changed
- For example, calling all cats a kind is more valid than calling all the objects on your desk a kind
- When it comes to gender realism and women, one view is the bioessentialist view
- Simply speaking, if you have XX chromosomes you are a women
- But this is an incomplete definition, there are chromosomal disorders to account for
- You could define women as a heterosexual role, which does capture the social aspect but still excludes too many people
- Hale thinks about this is a unique way, by applying cluster-kind realism
- This is realism without universalism, there is not just one thing that can make a person a woman
- Rather than having a kind united by one property, Hale thinks of many different properties that can mark someone as being a woman
- In cluster-kind realism, a group can constitute a kind as long as there is a cluster of properties that every member of the group possesses some of
- For Hale, these properties can be either social or biological depending on the situation
- For example, he points out that having a vagina isn't necessarily the most important biological characteristic next to not having a penis
- Women are also associated with social roles, you can lack the biological characteristics but still be considered a woman if you serve in the social role of a woman
- However, he does reject the heterosexual definition of woman despite noting the importance of the social roles of women
- Hale points out that lesbians are still treated like women despite being outside the heterosexual dynamic
- One problem with cluster-kind realism is that there must be hierarchy of properties within a cluster
- Different answers for this have found outside of gender, but Hale's solution was paradigms
- A paradigm is a prime example of a kind, a Socratic ideal version of what we would expect from that group
- This is one way to establish kinds, imagine the paradigm and then find its properties
- For gender this doesn't work so well, mainly because it reinforces gender stereotypes
- Social paradigms of gender are basically just representations of common gender stereotypes, which we don't want to do because we're trying to deconstruct these
- It also implies that some women are more like women than others
- Again, this is something we don't want to do because we are trying to avoid being exclusionary
- Furthermore, this implies that the interests of some women are more important than others
- If feminism is designed to support paradigmatic women, what do non-paradigmatic women do?
- In a cluster-kind, we can have multiple paradigms and they don't have to share the same properties
- But this doesn't totally solve the issue, it's something all cluster-kind realists have to consider

**Haslanger and Amelioration**

- When Hale talks about gender, he is taking a descriptive approach
- His explicit goal is to observe and describe the way gender is defined in every day life by ordinary people
- He critiques the heterosexual definition of gender because it is an inaccurate description
- However, this is assuming that what we want is an accurate description of gender, and what an accurate description even is
- Haslanger argues that this ignores the inherently political roots of gender definitions
- A good definition of gender is not an accurate one, but a useful one
- The question we should ask ourselves is how useful is our definition of gender for the current political work that feminism is doing
- This marks a distinction between a descriptive analysis of gender and an ameliorative one
- A descriptive analysis is successful when it accurately describes its subject
- An ameliorative analysis is successful when it furthers the goals of a specific political movement
- To help define what exactly is "helpful" we need to figure out what helping a movement means
- Remember, one of the reasons why philosophy is useful for feminism is through conceptual analysis

**Manifest/Operative Concepts and Amelioration**

- One way this works is by using philosophy to distinguish between the manifest and operative concepts of an object
- A manifest concept is how we think about something, and the operative is how it actually works
- For example, we think that sorting children into "gifted" or "remedial" in early education describes the intelligence of children, but it actually describes their socio-economic status
- Being put in one of these groups also predicts their future intelligence because of the kind and quality of education provided
- By understanding the relationship between manifest and operative, we can understand why children are labelled certain ways and why this results in certain outcomes
- Ideally, we want to synchronize manifest and operative concepts
- For example, when we think about the term "water", our manifest concept and the operative concept are likely very similar
- But when we talk about the term "marriage", it's usually thought of as a very personal thing
- But effectively, "marriage" is a demographic characteristic that gives you tax breaks and other special economic treatment
- Manifest and operative concepts do not clash with each other, they can coexist
- You may be invoking the manifest or operative concepts of the term whenever you use them, but for social critique we want to think about the operative concepts
- Amelioration is not intended to be false or misleading
- Rather, Haslanger is just suggesting viewing a concept through different views to answer specific questions
- Generally, we are trying to describe the operative concept of a term for an ameliorative analysis
- The manifest concept of a woman is someone who is biologically female and inherently suited to fill the social role of a woman
- The operative concept of a woman is someone who is seen as female, is socially disadvantaged, and is subordinate to men
- Marking someone as a woman is synonymous with marking them for oppression

**Intersectionality**

- Not all oppression is equal, as women are not the only oppressed groups
- Individuals can also be oppressed based on sexual identity, race, economic status, ability, and more
- This is where the idea of intersectionality comes in, that multiple identities can intersect and combine
- Within feminism, there has always been a level of division between white and black feminists
- Originally coined by the Combated River Collective, a black feminist lesbian group and popularized by academics like Kimberley Crenshaw
- At this point, feminism had been in progress for years but only now was being published and legitimized as an academic discipline
- The first individuals to get this opportunity were wealthy white women
- Resulted in a concept of feminism that not all women agreed on, black feminists felt like their ideas were not being represented by academic feminism
- Just as white women complain that men do not listen to them, black women complain that white women do not listen to them
- Historically, black feminists have allied more closely with black men than white women
- This is intersectionality in action, and shows the hierarchy of privilege between gender and race
- Despite being oppressed as women, white women are more respected than black men
- The experience of black women in feminism is being asked to ignore your race and engage as only a woman
- These two identities are not separable, and must be considered together
- When applying this idea to gender realism, this is a pretty big problem
- If social identities are not separable, then there is nothing that all women have in common
- There is no single set of properties we can identify that would mark all women

**Haslanger and Intersectionality**

- The 1970s feminist landscape was heavily reliant on the idea of gender realism
- Women all shared certain characteristics, and this marked them for oppression
- A few years later, intersectionality emerged and kind of shut down this idea
- Haslanger appears in the 2000s and tries to fix this, to find a way to acknowledge intersectionality while maintaining gender realism
- Her solution is to use the concept of social positions
- At its core, a social position is the social norms, roles, and institutions that a person is marked for
- Being marked for a certain position triggers different interactions and reactions to individuals while also justifying those interactions and reactions
- Social positions determine your social properties, for example a teacher has the authority to determine the grades of a student but a student cannot grade themself
- Certain social position can determine non-social properties, maybe the teacher position grants the physical property of being tired
- Extending this concept, Haslanger proposes gender positions, which are social norms someone is marked for based on their assumed sex
- One is not assigned to a female position because they are female, but because they are presumed to be female
- The social position of a women is not always the same, different properties will be assigned based on their different intersecting identities
- However, all of these properties share the function of oppression and subordination in the gender hierarchy
- The disadvantage of being a women is relational, no matter the gaps in oppression between the variety of identities women may hold we can always view them in relation to similar men
- Exposes you to Young's faces of oppression, almost always at least one
- To apply this idea to the need for an ameliorative definition, feminists just need to help those who are in social positions where they are oppressed on the basis of sex
- Anyone in that position, regardless of sex, counts as a woman for feminist purposes
- The core of feminism is to eliminate discrimination based on gender social positions by eliminating these categories and the hierarchies they create

**Butler**

- The expressivist view of gender identity states that gendered acts serve as expressions of an underlying gender identity
- Acts and identity do not always link up, it is always possible to act in a way that does not express who you really are
- This aligns pretty closely with the mainstream view, except the identity is often pre-assumed
- In the feminist view, gender does not express who we are, but once sexism is eliminated then all women will be free to express their identities freely
- However, all of these theories assume that a gender identity exists before it is expressed
- Butler challenges this, arguing that the body comes before identity
- When the goals of feminism are achieved, the female body will be able to express itself
- However, this is ignoring the fact that all bodies are social or cultural products
- A key component of Butler's theory is the idea of performance
- Instead of gender identity being expressed, Butler argues that it is performed, and is created and shaped by the process of performance
- Performances are a form of expression, which are knowingly false expressions of identity carried out with the goal of expressing a specific identity, often in an exaggerated way
- For Butler, gender only exists to the extent that it is performed
- Gender identity is not inherent to a person or to a body, but is created through their actions
- But this does not mean that all people define their own gender, because these actions are defined socially
- For example, the male body has certain properties, and one of those properties is having a penis
- However, depending on the functions of these properties, this body may or may not be defined as male
- When a body has a penis, the function is generally understood to be for penetration
- If a male body does not use the penis in this way, or is instead being penetrated, this signifies a loss of male status
- Social norms teach us that male bodies are supposed to be used in this way, and if you fail to do this then your status as a male is lesser
- What is considered "natural" or "unnatural" is determined by common social norms
- Since individuals do not have much control over broad social norms, then they do not have any significant control over how their actions are interpreted by society
- Bodies that act against social norms are considered "defective" or "unnatural"
- While heterosexuality is generally assumed, homosexuality always requires an explanation because it represents deviation from the norm
- This system also creates incentives to act in ways that align with these social norms, and in turn these actions solidify our understanding of our own identity
- Sense of self emerges from the repeated performance of socially defined actions using your body

**Critiques of Butler**

- This theory has been widely criticized by trans people for two main reasons
- The first reason is the problem that if gender identities are produced through actions guided by oppressive gender norms, then gender identities are also oppressive
- If you are a trans woman, this means you are seeking an oppressed social position while trans men are seeking a privileged social position
- There has been some exploration about what this means for nonbinary people, but not much
- The second reason is because Butler fails to acknowledge the problem of having a gender identity that does not match their gender social position
- The entire idea of performative identity directly clashes with the experience of gender dysphoria
- Trans people do not want to be seen as seeking a specific social position, and believe that gender identity exists separately of their actions
- We have so far been describing gender as mainly a system of oppression, but in reality it is more than that
- Rather than just an institution, it is also something to be experienced, something we live with
- Butler's theory marks a point where people began to push back against thinking of gender as an abstract structure rather than a lived experience

**Gender Identity and Trans Politics**

- Historically, the transgender experience has been defined by pathologization
- Initially it was described as Gender Identity Disorder, now called Gender Dysphoria
- These terms and concepts originated from the medical field as ways to "explain" the existence of trans people
- A core part of gender identity is the speech aspect, verbally identifying yourself as belonging to a certain gender category
- Gender identity can also take the form of sense of self, feeling like a certain gender
- The act of expressing gender through speech is meaningful because it expresses this inner subjective feeling of identity
- Trans feminists believe that trans people have first person authority over their identity
- This is a foundational point of trans feminism, that trans people have the right to self identify as a certain gender, and the right to express that identity
- The core of the trans political movement is the push to establish this right as a social norm
- One reason why we respect self identity is because they know best, this is epistemic self idenoty
- The other reason is ethical authority
