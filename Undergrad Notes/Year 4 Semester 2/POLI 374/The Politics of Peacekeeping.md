- The UN is mostly just a forum, the core of the UN is actually the Secretariat, most of the principal organs are controlled by the member states
- Within those member states, the great powers, especially the P5, have disproportionate influence
- Not just with their veto power within the UN system, but also their economic, cultural and military power
- The UN is not an independent actor, but rather an instrument of its member states
- For a peacekeeping mission to succeed or even exist, the political and operational support of at least some of the great powers is often required
- These powers also have vested interests all around the world, and will try to affect the peacekeeping process to support those interests
- A modern example of how power manifests itself in the UN system is the idea of the "penholders"
- Even within the P5, the US, UK, and France are often seen as the P3
- The way the penholder system works is that certain countries will support and focus on certain initiatives, and when those issues show up the penholder will lead the council
- For the vast majority of issues, the penholder is either the US, the UK, or France
- An ongoing issue in the security council is divisions among the P5, especially between the US, UK, and France on one side and Russia and China on the other
- A recent example of this would be the Kenyan-led mission in Haiti, which was not given support from the security council because China vetoed support since Haiti recognizes Taiwan
- Some more obvious examples are the total roadblocks put up by the US and Russia on any resolutions relating to the wars in Ukraine and Gaza
- Also, as part of their ongoing project to gain influence in Western Africa, Russia convinced the new Mali government to eject the MINUSMA peacekeeping force
- Another peacekeeping example was when the US wanted to raise support for a peacekeeping mission in Darfur
- While going around asking for support, the Nigerian president offered the Obama administration troop contributions on the condition they lobby the OECD to relieve their national debt
- These kinds of coercion, deals, and trades are extremely common in the UN, and even more common in the security council

**Politics of Peacemaking**

- Peacemaking is the ideal peak of the conflict curve, and is the process of bringing an armed conflict to an end by diplomatic means
- Securing a ceasefire, nevertheless a truce or peace agreement is often exceedingly difficult
- Common obstacles include refusal of parties to meet, irreconcilable demands, etc.
- The most common and effective method for facilitating peace agreements is the introduction of a third part mediator which can be a country, organization, or even an individual
- Ideally this would be a neutral party, or a the very least impartial
- A classic example is post-WWII Trieste
- At the end of WWII, Mussolini had been overthrown in Italy and on their Eastern border, Tito had just finished ejecting the Germans from Yugoslavia
- These two groups began to conflict on the Italy-Yugoslavia border, and the US sought a third party to mediate the conflict
- In this context, negotiations were to be done in secret as neither state would want to be seen talking to the other side
- The US Ambassador for Yugoslavia was brought in to be the mediator, which was a great choice given he ended up leaving his post for 8 months and noone in Belgrade noticed
- Finally, the decision was made to split the contested region into two zones, where one side preferred to live in Italy, and the other preferred to live in Yugoslavia
- Together, these two zones made up the Free State of Trieste which lasted for a couple years before each zone was absorbed by their respective preferred countries
- Another peace agreement that has unfortunately aged poorly was the Oslo Accords signed by the PLO and the Israeli government and mediated by Norway
- Of course most people remember that the accords were actually signed at the White House on Bill Clinton's request, resulting in the iconic photo
- Ideally, the UN wants to serve this purpose of the third-party mediator
- However, over time for all the reasons why the UN has become less effective in other areas, trust in this area has degraded significantly
- The UN is often seen as either paralyzed and useless, or not credible as an impartial party
- When the UN does want to appoint an envoy to mediate, they are either blocked access or the appointment is interfered with in other ways

**Politics of Contribution**

- In UN peacekeeping, a distinction is made between troop contributing countries and police contributing countries
- In 1990, the top TCCs were Canada, Finland, Austria, Norway, Ghana
- Fast forward 20 years, the amount of contributions had drastically increased, and now the top 5 were all developing countries
- Right now, the top 5 are Nepal, Bangladesh, Rwanda, and India, distantly followed by Indonesia
- Further down the list, China is rising as a contributing country
- Basically since the fall of Yugoslavia, western countries have almost ceased contributing troops
- Countries may contribute to bring an end to a conflict, but also to further their political influence in that country or even for monetary rewards
- For India, a significant motivator is India's desire to be seen as a great power
- Canada contributed to make a distinction from the US, but also to promote stability
- States like Senegal want to gain influence in Africa and support stability, and for some there are historical reasons like in Rwanda
- Peacekeeping can support foreign ministries, further military interests domestically, provide experience to supports
- Another strategy is "bluewashing", seeking to cover up less internationally inclined actions by supporting the UN elsewhere
- This last one is probably a significant motivator for China

**"Play and Pay"**

- A major consequence of this system is that historically countries that contribute troops are often not the countries funding the UN peacekeeping budget
- With the exception of China, the majority of those who contribute troops and take on the greatest risk in peacekeeping have little influence on peacekeeping outside their troops
- While Western countries are underrepresented in terms of troop contributions, they are overrepresented when it comes to financial contributions
- In the UN Charter, it explicitly states that countries that contribute troops to security council initiatives should participate in security council decisions regarding that mission
- However, this has rarely if ever happened in practice, leading to disillusionment from the TCCs
- Another weird dynamic is that the UN reimburses TCCs
- A country contributing troops gets $1428 USD per troop per month, but this does not go to the soldier, it goes to the government or in some cases the military
- It's a relatively small amount of money, but can be fairly significant for developing countries
- This furthers the divide between paying countries and contributing countries as TCCs want more compensation, but FCCs feel like they are wasting money
- Another thorn is the Contingent Owned Equipment System, which reimburses TCCs for maintenance required on equipment damaged during peacekeeping operations
- Arguing over troop reimbursement is one thing, but this causes even more conflict
- For some countries, this is an effective way to freely replace old and damaged equipment
- There is also the risk factor, while most missions are peaceful, casualties do happen
- The most recent examples were attacks by the M23 group on peacekeepers in the Congo, and Israel bombing UNIFIL installations in Lebanon

**Host Countries**

- Historically, most peacekeeping missions were Chapter 6 and as a result relationships between host countries and peacekeepers were pretty peaceful
- Now, more frequently peacekeepers are being deployed to regions under civil war where the host government is a belligerent
- Even worse, under Chapter 7 a mission may be carried out against the will of the host government
- For example, a peacekeeping mission mandated with ensuring delivery of humanitarian aid could be seen as the UN delivering aid to the enemies of the host government
- The MINUSMA mission in Mali is a prime example of this
- To summarize, the Mali government was overthrown in a Russia-backed coup and the new government immediately asked for the withdrawal of the UN
- In a similar situation, MONUSCO in the Congo was asked to leave after public opinion on the success of the mission soured
- Another potential factor was that the UN accused the government of committing human rights abuses which they did not appreciate
- It's also possible for this to go the other way, some governments have raised concerns about human rights abuses committed by UN peacekeepers
- One of the most high profile examples is the allegations of sexual abuse committed by MINUSCA peacekeepers in the Central African Republic

**Responsibility to Protect**

- This is one of the most debated concepts in international relations in the last 20 or so years
- Does the international community have the right or duty to intervene in the domestic politics of countries where serious human rights violations are being committed
- Comes from the conflicting concepts of the sovereignty of states and the responsibility of states to uphold human rights
- Really became a hot topic in the 1990s after devastating human rights violations in Somalia, Rwanda, and Bosnia
- Within the UN, political issues prevented any sort of progress on establishing the formal conditions where sovereignty can be violated
- Instead, a group of states met outside the UN and produced the R2P report
- This argued that sovereignty comes below the priority of protecting human beings from severe violations of their rights
- This norm was eventually normalized by a UNSC resolution, but almost immediately was met with opposition
- Main criticism that interventions were conducted for the interests of the intervening state, not for the benefit of the country being invaded
- UN peace operations got caught in the crossfire due to many having this motivation, since the protection of civilians is one of the main pillars of peacekeeping
- Effectively, this opposition went against the foundation of UN peacekeeping
- Modern opinions on R2P have shifted towards the ides of "responsibility while protecting"
- This norm tries to counter the problem of selfish interventions by putting the responsibility of ensuring interventions are benevolent at the forefront

**Institution Shopping**

- While the UN may be the largest international organization, it's not always the best suited
- When it comes to conflict management, it may be better handled by regional organizations
- For example, NATO took the lead in Bosnia as a more effective option to UN engagement
- But these missions usually want the wider consent that comes with UN authorization, so often these smaller peace operations still seek a UNSC resolution to authorize them
- The Security Council sets the mandate and provides permission, but the UN has no role in the actual execution
- This is basically the UN security council contracting out peacekeeping to a third party
- However, in most examples with like NATO and UNITAF, these "third parties" are generally controlled or led by P5 members
- A common critique of peace operations, especially UN peace operations, is that they are an extension of neo-colonialism
- While this has been used as a shield to prevent the UN from uncovering human rights abuses in some African countries, this is still a valid criticism
- Another developing issue is that more and more often UN forces are being sent into active conflict instead of the usual uneasy peace
- Because of this, more and more UN missions are mandated to use force and are employing violence to implement this mandate
- With the Trump administration coming into power, in the future the US is likely to pull out of most international agreements and projects
- At the same time, China is becoming more active but specifically where it has economic interests
