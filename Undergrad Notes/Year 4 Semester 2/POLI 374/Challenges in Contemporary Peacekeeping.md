**Strengths**

- The UN is globally recognized, and has devoted mechanisms for peacekeeping, force generation, and encouraging cooperation
- Despite the diminishment of its status, it is still generally seen as legitimate
- Also has extensive operational experience
- None of the challenges we will talk about negate these core strengths of the UN

**Limitations**

- Starting with the UN headquarters, it has a number of significant limitations
- Especially early on in the history of peace operations, the UNHQ suffered from a lack of staff and general logistics support
- In the 90s, there were only about 30 officers in the peacekeeping division
- There was also no 24/7 situation room, meaning that there were several incidents where UN peacekeepers on the ground needed support and business hours were over
- Intelligence capabilities were and still are very limited, mainly for optics reasons
- Besides the problems with 9 to 5 business hours, communications with field missions from the UNHQ were just generally spotty
- Most of these historical issues are gone, but some still persist
- Staffing limitations are still an issue meaning that planning processes still move slowly
- Analysis capabilities are also still limited due to the aversion to intelligence gathering
- Finally, the procurement process is extremely slow for basically any good that's required

**Finance**

- The UN currently has four different budget systems
- First is the regular UN budget, which covers most operations
- Then are the specialized agency budgets which pays for the operations of specific agencies like the World Food Programme which are determined separately from the regular budget
- Next is the voluntary program budgets which is funded entirely by voluntary state contributions, and last is the peacekeeping budget
- However, what we call the peacekeeping budget is actually made up out of several smaller budgets
- Each UN peacekeeping mission has it's own budget, which are separately determined and approved by the UNSC for each mission
- This means that there is no overall peacekeeping budget pool, and all missions have to be approved and funded on a case by case basis
- The sum of all mission budgets is what we call the peacekeeping budget
- This is the exact opposite of how budgets are supposed to work
- The regular and peacekeeping budgets are determined by something called assessed contributions
- Depending on a country's share of the global economy, each country contributes a different proportion of the overall budget
- This is capped at 22%, which is what the US contributes
- Roughly the same system is used for the peacekeeping budget, where the US is expected to pay 27% of the budget but only pays 25% because of a domestic law
- The peacekeeping budget last year was roughly $6.1 billion dollars, which is still 5 billion dollars short of the budget allocated to the NYPD

**Budget Process**

- The first step is the generation of a Concept of Operations, which is basically your budget proposal
- This is submitted to the Advisory Committee on Administrative and Budgetary Questions (ACABQ)
- Any proposals that pass through this committee will be sent to the Fifth Committee, which cannot change the mandate of a mission but can adjust the amounts allocated in the budget
- Finally, the budget must be passed by the General Assembly, which is usually ceremonial
- Main problems come from the fact that the budget contributions are often in arrears which can limit the amount of money actually available for budgets
- As you can see, the budget process is extremely slow and highly politicized
- Can take multiple revisions before a proposal leaves the Fifth Committee, if it makes it there in the first place
- The arrears mean that UN missions are often underfunded, meaning that they must cut corners to continue operating, or use emergency funds to compensate
- As of the 2023-2024 fiscal year, 35.9% of assessed contributions went unpaid
- The UN has some financial instruments it can utilize, mainly deferring payments to operations or otherwise distributing funding throughout the UN system to stretch out money
- Overall, the UN is entirely dependent on assessed contributions and has no alternative ways to fund its operations, including peacekeeping missions

**Force Generation**

- A key problem for UN force generation is a small pool of TCCs, and as a result these countries get tired of contributing very quickly
- This causes risks to compound over time, meaning countries are less likely to contribute
- Commitments are also inconsistent, some countries prefer to contribute to specific missions over others
- This leads to some missions getting a flood of contributions, and others getting hardly any
- Sometimes the issue comes with getting the necessary equipment and troops to the host country, ideally they will be reachable by sea
- If they are landlocked, getting large transport aircraft is very difficult and expensive
- The US and Russia are the primary owners of large transport aircraft, and usually don't contribute to peacekeeping missions
- The equipment itself is also usually pretty insufficient, and often extremely old
- When a military coup occurred in Bangladesh, a platoon of troops returning from a UN peacekeeping mission was ordered to suppress protests and rolled up in their UN white painted transport vehicles
- The UN has two support centers with devoted UN vehicle fleets, the main one is in Brindisi, and there is another regional one in Entebbe in Uganda
- Overall, UN equipment is often old, outdated, and damaged and in short supply

**Force Protection**

- Like any military venture, peacekeepers must protect themselves against groups that seek to impede their mission
- Trend of violence against peacekeepers has been on the rise, Bosnia and Baghdad being the beginning
- Recently, 14 peacekeepers were killed in the eastern DRC, one of the factors that led to the end of MONUSCO
- 4,409 total fatalities since 1948, 32 killed in deliberate attacks in 2022 and 11 in 2023
- Generally speaking, increasing force protection means using more advanced equipment, and adding more fortifications to bases
- This serves to keep peacekeepers safe on patrol, and in the base
- However, this also has the negative downside of making the presence of peacekeepers more hostile

**Host Government Relations**

- Another factor that has created obstacles to peacekeeping are the host countries themselves
- In recent years, government have been less willing to cooperate with peace missions
- This can range from limiting the movement of UN forces, to even obstructing the delivery of humanitarian aid
- Mainly occurs as a consequence of the host country being a party to the conflict in question
- The most extreme manifestation of this is the withdrawal of consent entirely, which we saw in both MINUSMA and MONUSCO

**Sexual Misconduct and Abuse**

- Recurring issue in peacekeeping, examples go as far back as early missions in the Congo, Cambodia, Haiti, and Timor-Leste
- Between 2005 and 2017, there were over 2000 allegations of sexual misconduct or abuse
- 758 of these came in 2023, 534 in 2022, and 265 in 2018
- Over half of the cases reported in 2023 were related to UN personnel, the rest came from personnel associated with organizations not under UN authority
- Most of the extreme cases of sexual trafficking came from either unaffiliated NGOs or private security firms that were present in a mission
- It is yet unclear whether or not this indicates an increased number of cases, or just a product of improvements in abuse reporting procedures
- In the vast majority of investigations and reports on this issue, the root cause is a lack of accountability, and a culture of impunity
- What is supposed to happen is that there is a built-in mechanism to the peacekeeping structure to report and punish this sort of thing
- Going back to the force generation process, TCCs must sign a Status of Mission or Forces Agreement between the UN and the TCC
- This allows the UN to have some control over these troops, but importantly does not give ultimate authority on these cases to the UN
- In the case where a personnel has an allegation with sufficient evidence, it will be reported to the member state and they have 10 days to choose to investigate the case
- The UN can also perform investigations on their own, but the process is very slow
- In both cases, the ultimate punishment depends on the contributing state
- As you can likely imagine, these member states are very hesitant to punish their own soldiers
- In most cases, the state will either not investigate, investigate but serve no punishment, or drag out the process long enough until the original incident is forgotten
- The UN has tried to remedy this through cultural projects, mainly the UN Zero Tolerance Policy
- This is basically just a strict set of ten rules that are widely distributed and heavily reinforced among all UN peace operations
- Makes the basic expectations for all UN peacekeepers extremely clear
- Another development is placing a Global Victim Rights Advocate in the Office of the Secretary General
- All current and future missions have at least one victim's rights advocate position as well, and the docked pay of all investigated personnel are contributed to a victim's trust fund
- TCCs have also attempted to do training of their own, for example Canada has mission-specific training for UN missions that includes info on local culture, religion, general practices, etc.
- What is happening now, is that militaries are incorporating sexual exploitation and abuse training into this cultural mission-specific training
- This is still a developing approach that varies widely between countries

**Gender Mainstreaming**

- Another prominent issue for the UN has been the representation of women
- As part of the overall UN liberal project with the WPS agenda, the UN wants to bring more women into the peace process from diplomacy to peacebuilding
- All of this is intimately connected to UNSC resolution 1325, which set the agenda on Women, Peace, and Security for the UN
- This was the trigger for a call for more female soldiers and police to be contributed to peace operations
- The main argument is that bringing more women is will benefit the peacemaking process, and improve the image of peace operations on the ground
- Having more women increases the recruiting base, allows closer connection with the women in local communities, some cultures do not allow interactions between women and men outside the family, etc.
- The current ratio as of 2016 is roughly 4% women, so they have a long way to go
- Some have argued that this is a harmful reinforcement of gender roles, but overall this approach has been pretty popular among UN agencies
- In 2015, the UNSC called for the number of women in peacekeeping operations to be doubled by 2028
- As of March 2022, 30% of civilian personnel were women, 7.9% of military, police, and corrections personnel were women
- 2022 targets for all categories of personnel have been met, except for military personnel
- The main problem with this is that there just aren't very many women in the military
- In fact, a number of major TCCs have no women in the military acting in combat roles

**Intelligence and Peace Operations**

- As we discussed earlier, intelligence is a highly sensitive issue at the UN
- Generally speaking, countries are uncomfortable with the UN having a spy agency
- However, the consensus is also that the UN would benefit greatly from having improved intelligence gathering capabilities, mainly in peacekeeping
- Even granting peace operations the most basic intelligence capabilities would allow troops to learn about the conflict situation and anticipate attacks against peacekeepers
- In 2017, the UN created the Peacekeeping Intelligence Policy, which was widely opposed
- First, there was the general concern over sovereignty, but there was also the concern that countries with intelligence capabilities would be disproportionately affected
- By default, Western countries had the most advanced intelligence capabilities, and there was a hesitance both by those countries to share their capabilities, and by countries receiving intelligence that this information may be incomplete or unreliable
- As a result, this policy was revised for 2019 and gutted almost completely
- The three goals of this new policy were to gain a general operational picture, get early warning on imminent threats, and identify peacekeeping risks and opportunities
- This has been generally accepted, but some concerns still exist in terms of implementation
- Mainly come in the form of concerns from host states and disagreements in the UNSC
- What this policy means is that member states are expected to share relevant actionable intelligence with the UN

**Cyberspace and Peace Operations**

- The main thing to note here is that the UN just needs to recognize the digital domain as important
- The idea of the "digital peacekeeper" can be using cyber operations to support a mission on the ground
- This means the physical equipment available to peacekeepers that allow them to collect and spread information
- Digital defence means protecting UN installations and equipment from cyber-attack
- The "Digital Blue Helmets" program was created in 2016 by the Office of Information and Communications Technologies
- Led to the Digital Transformation of UN Peacekeeping in 2021
- Mainly focuses on data-driven strategies and the quick adoption of new technologies into peacekeeping operations
- One relatively new concept that emerged is the concept of cyberpeacekeeping
- The idea is that you can apply the ideas and concepts of peacekeeping digitally, including cyber ceasefires and cyber peace agreements
- Peacekeepers would be supplied with relevant equipment to monitor and observe cyberwarfare, investigate violations, mediate parties to a conflict, etc.
