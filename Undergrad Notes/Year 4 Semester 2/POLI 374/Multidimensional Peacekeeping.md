- Post Cold-war era was defined by optimism in the UN system
- Up until the 1990s, we had only seen observer and traditional peacekeeping missions
- Complex or multidimensional peacekeeping missions use more force and operate in more dangerous environments
- Now that the east-west divide was weakened, the UNSC could operate how it was initially envisioned to work
- This period was exemplified by the Agenda for Peace authored by Boutros Boutros-Ghali in 1992
- This document emphasizes the potential for the UN to not just keep the peace, but also raise standards of living
- Also brings up the non-Westphalian approach by emphasizing the tension between a need for interconnection between states
- Sovereignty is no longer as exclusive as it once was
- Emphasis was no longer only on large scale international conflicts, but more about complex civil conflicts
- Common themes were humanitarian crises, failing states, and international crime
- More and more, the United Nations began to view civil wars as genuine threats to international peace and security
- This means that the UNSC is now authorized to intervene
- Global context was events like genocide and ethic cleansing in Rwanda and Serbia, humanitarian crises elsewhere in Africa, etc.
- UNSC was more and more willing to invoke chapter 7 to protect people, rise of the principle of humanitarian intervention

**The Second Generation**

- New generation of peacekeeping missions are more frequent, but also more intense
- The phrase "second generation" is pretty much obsolete now
- In 1988, the UN had only 5 missions but by 1994 that number had gone up to 17
- This also means increases in budget and personnel numbers
- Number and scale of peace operations is predominantly determined by political will, and the overall climate of global politics
- Increasingly, operations were deployed to areas with no existing ceasefire, or at least a partial peace agreement
- Sometimes called "less permissive" environments, often peacekeepers were dropped into areas in active conflict
- Often there was breakdown at the governmental level, or ongoing human rights abuses
- Because of this, consent becomes more difficult to obtain or there may be no consent at all
- Even if the central government agrees, they likely only control a portion of their territory
- Generally the strategy is to hold a country-wide peace conference with the goal of securing a ceasefire
- If this fails, the UN will usually deploy anyway, just wherever is most permissive
- This also implicitly erodes the principle of impartiality, as the UN will be siding with whichever groups provide consent
- Being in a more violent environment necessitates more forceful methods
- The tasks of these missions were usually more expansive, no longer just patrolling a border and observing
- This is when mandates began to expand further and further
- These can include supporting the government, providing aid to local populations, performing disarmament activities, etc.
- Bigger missions mean more expensive missions, and budgets begin to put strain on the UN system
- Of course, all of these changes put pressure on the original holy trinity
- All of this means that the UN is less likely to take on all of the tasks required of them by themselves
- This means contracting peace operations out to regional organizations or coalitions of the willing
- Marks the shift of peacekeeping operations no longer being the sole responsibility of the UN
- Although we no longer use the term "second generation", we still needed a new term for these missions
- Now we usually refer to these missions as complex peacekeeping operations, or multidimensional peacekeeping operations

**Haiti 1996**

- Multidimensional mission, but not nearly as forceful as others
- Soldiers stationed in bases set up specifically for the mission, with equipment provided by the TCCs
- Canadian forces brought their own vehicles, basically crappy jeeps
- Some armored vehicles, because at the time there were some armed gangs attacking the UN, but much less than currently
- Mission mainly focused on supporting local communities and patrolling regularly
- For example, donated supplies and other assistance to local schools
- Haiti was the poorest country in the western hemisphere at the time, and as such most of the mission focused on assistance and peacebuilding
- Success in the long term relies on the establishment of basic services and an economic foundation for success
- Otherwise, aid is basically just a temporary solution
- In fact, part of the mission involved reconstructing the ecology of Haiti as well as the societal problems

**Bosnia 1997**

- Again, there is a military base but the situation is much more tense
- The presence of the UN was intended to prevent another explosion of violence in the region
- Not a UN mission, NATO mission operating with UNSC permission
- Peacekeeping base included genuine fortifications, but still not operated like a military base expecting an imminent attack
- Ironically, the Canadian army was very useful because they had access to vehicles intended to operate in snowy regions
- Mine clearing vehicles were absolutely crucial, as Bosnia was one of the most mined countries in the world at the time
- Apart from the obvious, you still need whatever supplies are necessary to sustain a contingent of 800 Canadian soldiers
- There was also the possibility that local militias would try to obtain their old weapons and vehicles located at a local weapons depot
- As such, the peacekeepers were practicing anti-tank drills
- Because of the DDR agreement made in Bosnia, NATO could not move or destroy the stored weapons as they still were owned by Bosnia
- Still non-military tasks, there were new schools funded by Canadian aid money
- In the area there were entire fields that had not been cleared for mines, usually around villages that had been completely ethnically cleansed
- At the Canadian base, the gate was totally open but the American base had full barbed wire everywhere blocking the entrance
- Americans also brought M1 Abrams tanks as part of their operations, mostly just as a deterrence against anyone thinking about fighting
