**REVIEW BRAHIMI REPORT AND GLOBAL GOVERNANCE**
- Created August 17, 2000 by Lakhdar Brahimi
- First notable peace operations reform effort
- Five core recommendations
- Military should be able to defend itself and protect civilians simultaneously
- Security Council should consult closer with TCCs
- A mission should not be authorized unless it has the ability to achieve its mandate
- When planning operations, personnel should be recruited based on expertise and coordination should be increased
- More women should be included in peace operations
- To implement these changes, the Peacekeeping Doctrine was developed
- Also establish executive committee on peace and security information
- Timelines should be contracted to 30 for normal, and 90 for complex
- DDR programs need increased funding and coordination
- Coupled with the issue of global governance being insufficient for the tasks at hand
- After the Brahimi report in the post-cold war era, the next period of significant overhaul came in 2015
- Marked the first High Level Independent Panel on Peace Operations (HIPPO) Report
- Other main review document was the Advisory Group of Experts on the Review of the Peacebuilding Architecture (Peacebuilding Review)
- Also marked the 1325 Global Study, a review of the implementation of resolution 1325
- Each of these three documents called for reform to the different aspects of peace operations
- Together, they mainly focused on preventative, political solutions to conflict
- Advanced a more people-oriented approach focusing on community engagement and gendered approaches
- Called for more tailored approaches to each situation depending on the specific requirements
- This specific criticism came in response to the practice of building peacekeeping mandates by copy and pasting tasks from previous operations
- All of the relevant countries have their own preferred language, and the result is a mishmash of different priorities and language that has little to do with the actual task at hand
- Finally, these reports called for greater coherence and coordination between different aspects of the UN system

**HIPPO Report**

- Four essential recommendations, called "shifts" within the report
- The first was the primacy of politics, shifting away from the focus on mandates, force structures, new technologies to focus on political solutions
- Even the best mission will fail without the proper political support, peace operations live and die by diplomacy at the end of the day
- Second, UN missions should be responsive, and tailored to the specific context
- No longer should they be called peacekeeping operations, because not all situations require peacekeeping
- Instead, call them peace operations and apply them more broadly as the situation requires them
- Third was the need for stronger partnerships, the UN needs to coordinate more closely with regional organizations and NGOs
- Finally, peace operations should be field focused and people-centered
- Shift away from focusing only on the UN headquarters, but prioritize supporting peace operations in the field
- More specific recommendations include the creation of a UN vanguard force
- This would be a rapid deployment unit that would be perpetually available to the UN
- Basically the closest we've ever gotten to having a full on standing UN army
- Also called for the P5 to contribute more forces to peacekeeping operations
- In line with the recommendation for increased cooperation with regional organizations, proposed the use of the peacekeeping budget for non-UN missions
- Finally, in reference to the issue of disorganized mandates, called for specific mandates, but also constantly evolving mandates
- In too many cases, the security council will be too hesitant to adjust mandates in response to changing situations on the ground, see Rwanda

**Peacebuilding Review and 1325 Global Study**

- Peacebuilding review emphasized sustaining peace, and focusing on long-term approaches to peacebuilding
- Also emphasized national ownership, any peacebuilding initiatives should incorporate the input of local governments and collaborate to ensure we're not imposing top-down reforms
- 1325 global study mainly focused on incorporating women into peacebuilding operations
- Also discussed the need to incorporate a gendered lens into the work of the security council, and called for additional finance to all WPS commitments which had been previously underfunded

**Action for Peacekeeping**

- Came three years after the HIPPO report, mainly in response to no visible changes
- Political solutions were not forthcoming, mandates were still vague and lacking priorities, etc.
- Launched on March 28, 2018, and called for member states to cooperate to create a set of principles that all states could agree on
- Again, the goal was more targeted mandates, political solutions, but also safer operations and better equipped forces
- Overall, the same issues that have persisted since the early 2000s
- All states that signed onto the A4P process agreed to 8 shared commitments
- Politics, WPS, Protection, Safety and Security, Performance and Accountability, Peacebuilding and Sustaining Peace, Partnerships, and Conduct of Peacekeepers and Peacekeeping Operations
- Important things to note are that this initiative focused on streamlining the peace operations process
- This means faster security council decision making, budget approval, and TCC/PCC negotiations
- Also emphasized the need for increased cooperation on the ground
- The problem is that both of these reforms do similar things, but one is for headquarters and one is for field operations
- However, no efforts are made to bridge the gap between these two worlds
- After nothing happened for three years, they decided to bring in A4P+
- Basically just seven new reforms with the same issues stapled onto the original eight

**Digital Peacekeeping**

- In 2021, the digital transformation of UN peacekeeping was deployed
- Around the same time, the president of the security council declared that they would explore the possibility of new technologies as a force multiplier
- Basically, not a huge overhaul of peacekeeping but a focus on the possibility for data and digital technologies to improve existing peacekeeping strategies
- One thing that was particularly special in terms of UN operations, and to write this report they actually went out and surveyed field and headquarters personnel
- 83% of them said that key technologies were being unused or underused
- Especially in terms of responding to misinformation, almost half felt that they had no way to respond
- Created four main goals
- Drive Innovation, Maximize Potential of Technology, Understand Threats to Peacekeepers and Mandate Implementation, and Ensure Responsible Use
- This encompasses all aspects of peace operations, from peacebuilding, to intelligence, and even force protection in terms of protecting sensitive information
- Peacekeeping conference being held in Berlin in 2025, we'll have to see if the US participates or not
- In 2024, an independent study of peacekeeping operations suggested disaggregating the three or four kinds of peace operations we have and replacing them with 30 kinds
- Basically the idea is to assemble a mission from a set of 30 components depending on the situation
- Decreases the coherence of any given mission, but increases the flexibility

**Reform and Geopolitics**

- Main question is the future of the UNSC, kind of the core checkpoint of peace operations
- Russia, China, and the US are all likely to veto now
- Regional organizations are already more important, and are likely to adopt larger roles
- Another possibility is non-UN peacekeeping missions, separate from UN approved regional operations that are likely coalition-style
- The two key conflicts today are connected to P5 members, and as such are unlikely to get any UNSC-authorized solutions
- Narrower, smaller civilian missions may become more prevalent as the era of the huge multidimensional mission comes to a close

**Rapid Reaction**

- One of the main reasons to promote rapid reaction is the possibilities for agreements
- It is a lot easier to build confidence in a peacekeeping agreement if you know those forces will be on the ground within 6 months
- Not only does this enable swift military action, but can save lives by enabling humanitarian aid much sooner
- Especially useful in humanitarian intervention scenarios
- The Standby High Readiness Brigade was founded in 1996 by seven countries and eventually grew to 23, and consisted of 4-5,000 personnel
- Could be deployed within 15-30 days of mission approval, and was sustainable for up to 60 consecutive days
- The maximum time it could be deployed was 6 months
- Deployed 5 times, largest deployment was in UNMEE
- Disintegrated in 2009 due to lack of political will
- Never a UN organization, coalition of the willing that supported UN missions
- Designed to fill the gap between a UN mission being approved and deployed
- In 1995, Canada published the Rapid Reaction study for peacekeeping
- Idea was to create a reliable, cheap, and effective UN rapid reaction capability
- Would need early warning, effective decision making, available transportation, logistics support, finances, and well-trained personnel
- Two core recommendations
- First, establish a Rapidly Deployable Mission Headquarters
- Team of 30-50 people that could deploy anywhere within a week
- Would create a preliminary base for the full mission headquarters
- Also served as the base for a vanguard of 5000 personnel to be deployed in 3-5 weeks
- The result of this proposal was the Standby Arrangements System, which was a database of member states who were willing to contribute personnel or equipment in advance
- Constructed on the basis of low-level bilateral agreements between TCCs and the UN
- This system was replaced with the Peacekeeping Capability Readiness System in 2015
- Goal is to improve the efficiency of managing commitments and increasing collaboration between TCCs and UNHQ
- TCCs and PCCs pledge their intended contributions through a website called UN Force Link
- What you're submitting is a note containing the numbers and descriptions of all the battalions you are willing to pledge
- What the UN gets is a database of the equipment and personnel available to them at short notice and from which countries
- Of course, this list of pledges does not guarantee that the TCC will be able to give them to the UN
- However, it does make the process of force generation so much faster
- Based on the scenario, the UN can figure out what they need and who to ask right away
- No guarantee that they will actually get it, and also countries don't pledge everything that the UN needs as these are all completely voluntary
- There's also levels of pledges, which determines the readiness of the unit
- The highest level of pledge deploys within 60 days, and only 14 units have been pledged at this level
- On the bright side, this system is pretty robust it just needs more support

**Contracting Out**

- The main motivation behind contracting out peacekeeping comes from the idea that the UN is not the best organ for solving international conflict
- Regional organizations have more stake in the matter and generally are better positioned politically
- However, not all regions of the world have robust regional organizations that have peacekeeping mechanisms, it's really only Europe and Africa
- In the case where a mission is UN-authorized but regional-run, you'll likely have a UNSC mandate, but the mission will be run by the org, not by the SRSG
- Mission headquarters is operated by the organization that has its own force commander and national brigades and forces
- You may still have a UN mission headquarters hosting the political elements of the mission
- This contingent would be separate from the regional organization, and does not have a mandate it just acts independently or in cooperation to help fulfill the mandate through political means
- A coalition peacekeeping operation would have a similar structure, except the lead nation of the coalition supplies the headquarters and force commander
- National contingents report to the lead nation, and you may have a UN political wing
- For example, in Bosnia NATO provided support to UNPROFOR by imposing an arms embargo and a no fly zone until 1995
- The mission then became fully NATO-led, the largest UN-authorized regional peace operation in history
- ECOWAS deployed ECOMOG to Liberia in the 90s to support the UNOMIL mission in Liberia, and later deployed to Sierra Leone for similar reasons
- ECOWAS is often not considered a regional org considering it's dominated so heavily by Nigeria
- Another example is when the EU deployed to the Congo both in 2003 and 2006 to support MONUC
- African Union participates in a lot of peacekeeping
- A weird coalition one is Italy intervening in Albania
- What happened was the Albanian government collapsed in 1997 prompting an Italian-led restabilization mission into Albania to reconstruct the government
- Likely intended to prevent mass emigration into Italy
- Australia deployed a coalition force to support the independence of East Timor after Indonesian rebel groups continuously attacked local governments
- A couple other weird ones, Solomon Islands, Iraq, and Hebron
