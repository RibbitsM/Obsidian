- The principal organs of the UN all play a part in peacekeeping, but the Security Council, General Assembly, and the Secretariat are the most important
- ECOSOC and the ICJ do come into play occasionally, but only in niche scenarios
- Within the UN secretariat, the Department of Peace Operations and the Department of Political and Peacebuilding Affairs are particularly important
- These departments share the operations of most missions, but larger missions are primarily operated by the DPO
- Another important region is the Department of Operational Support which is mainly the logistics powerhouse of the UN system
- Most UN rights committees are also related to peacekeeping like the OHCHR

**Mission Planning Process**

- Once an issue has emerged that has drawn the attention of the Secretary General or has been brought to the security council by a UN member state, the planning process begins
- This can happen once a decision has been made by the UNSC, or beforehand
- The first step is something called a "strategic assessment", assuming no security council assessment has occurred yet
- This is carried out by an Integrated Task Force made up of whatever UN organs are involved
- Goal is to create a plan to quickly activate a mission if it ends up being approved by the SC
- The goal of the task force is to make an Integrated Assessment Plan which is guided by the UN Integrated Assessment and Planning Policy
- Some questions to answer are what will the scale of this mission be? What parts of the UN system will need to contribute? Should we send out a preliminary force to set the groundwork?
- Basically getting an early idea of what will be required to carry out the mission and how those requirements will be met
- Assuming the Security Council grants a mandate, the next step can be taken
- However, they first need to cover a list of considerations before authorizing a peacekeeping mission
- Of course this is a political process, so it's not as simple as just ticking off some boxes
- Now that we have a resolution and a mandate, the mission will need to be staffed
- Missions need a head, which is usually a representative of the Secretary General
- An Integrated Strategic Framework will be created, including an implementation plan and a UN Country team leading it
- This is when more detailed parameters will be established like the legal plan, more detailed financing plans, and a finalized mission plan with exact numbers of personnel
- These all fall under the umbrella of the Full Operating Capability of the mission
- Civilian sectors may be involved as well, especially for logistic support
- Scale of these missions can range from only 20 people to over 10,000
- Up to this point, about six to twelve months has elapsed from the initial outbreak of conflict

**Deployment**

- The command structure of a peacekeeping mission can be divided into strategic, operational, and tactical concerns
- The strategic level is the highest, and consists of the SC mandate, and the mission head from the secretariat
- The operational level is mostly the mission headquarters and leadership
- There are two coordinators, the humanitarian coordinator and the resident coordinator from the concerned country
- At the lowest level, there is a civilian and a military branch
- Civilian branch deals with the political aspect and provides financial, logistical, and non-military medical support
- Also where the human rights personnel and finance workers operate
- The civilian branch has no unifying commander, but the military branch all report to the force commander
- Also, if a peacekeeping mission has a significant policing contingent, they will have a military-style chain of command separate from the civilian division under the Police Commissioner
- Another key part of peacekeeping mission is the Contribution Agreement signed between the UN and the contributing countries
- National Contingents are not under the full control of the UN System, they are still under the control of whatever relevant command structure from their home country
- This means that if a contributing country wishes to do so, they can withdraw their forces at any time
- However, as long as they are part of the mission, those troops report to the Force Commander
- In the event of misconduct, the UN can investigate, but only a troops National Command can distribute punishment
- Also, if it's not a chapter 7 authorization mission, there will also be an agreement made between the UN and the host country
- The Rules of Engagement specify the circumstances where a unit or individual may use force
- This is generally ripped directly from the Security Council mandate
- For example, UN Emergency Force II can only open fire in a self-defence situation as a last resort, to defend UNEF buildings and vehicles from armed attack, or to support other UNEF troops that are under armed attack
- Those are the entirety of the ROE for UNEF II
- For a mission like peacekeeping in Bosnia, things get much more complicated
- Just one section from this document detailing what to do when under fire includes 5 different response options and refers to multiple other sections of the document
- Generally speaking, the one commonality in ROE is that self-defence is the primary and often only scenario when troops can open fire

**Mission Types**

- Traditional peacekeeping missions are usually like UNIFICYP, keeping a conflict that has yet to be resolved politically cold
- Multidimensional missions are much more complicated and are usually deployed in regions with humanitarian concerns
- Transitional authority missions are very unique, and basically take the form of a provisional government
- Special political missions
- 71 peacekeeping operations since 1948, and 11 current peacekeeping missions and 23 political missions
- Current missions are mainly in the middle east and Africa, include Cyprus, India/Pakistan, Lebanon, Abyei, etc.
- Currently around 60k personnel, with 51k being troops, 6k police, and the rest staff
- 121 contributing countries, and over 4k fatalities since 1948
- The exact numbers fluctuate from year to year, and follow the political climate
- In 2019, there were significantly more troops, over 89k which was peak numbers
- The budget of UN peacekeeping is theoretically 6.1 billion USD, but is conditional
- Generally, over the past 3 or 4 decades the number of peacekeeping missions has declined while political missions have increased
- Especially recently, few missions have been deployed mainly as a result of security council deadlock
- The UN is not the only organization with peacekeepers, there are more non-UN than UN missions
