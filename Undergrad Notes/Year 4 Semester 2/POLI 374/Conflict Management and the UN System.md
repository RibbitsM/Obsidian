- Peacekeeping is difficult to explain and understand outside the context of international politics
- Dependent on international law, organizations, norms, etc.
- Grounded mainly in the assumptions of the liberal international economic order
- Also contextual from mission to mission since peacekeeping operations are mainly dictated by the culture and situation in the country(s) where they are located
- Peacekeeping also exists in the context of war, and the assumption that war is the main problem of the international system
- SIPRI (Stockholm International Peace Research Institute) and PRIO (Peace Research Institute of Oslo) are both reliable data sources for armed conflicts
- In 2023 there were around 150,000 battle related deaths with the majority of those coming from the Ukraine and Gaza wars
- The goal of conflict management is to prevent and resolve armed conflict, and peacekeeping is the primary tool for doing so
- Every year the international crisis group posts their top 10 list of possible conflict zones for the next year
- For 2025 the top 5 are Syria, Sudan, Ukraine, Palestine, and Iran
- An important part of preventing armed conflict is identifying where it may erupt next, so tools like these are extremely useful
- Peacekeeping efforts are also influenced by the global security environment in general and the stability of the liberal international order that peacekeeping relies on
- As of the last decade or so, the security environment has been shifting towards a much less stable position for a wide variety of reasons

**Conflict Management Spectrum**

- 5 key terms used by the UN to describe various aspects of conflict management
- Preventative diplomacy, or conflict prevention are diplomatic efforts undertaken to prevent the escalations of tensions and conflicts
- Generally the first line of defence
- Peacemaking refers to diplomatic efforts with the goal of ceasing violence once a conflict has already started
- Also sometimes used to refer to armed force especially in American politics
- Peacekeeping is the deployment of armed or unarmed personnel with the goal of preserving peace and facilitating discussions

**Legal Framework**

- The UN Charter does not mention peacekeeping at all, the concept did not exist in 1945
- However, the legal framework for the peacekeeping come from chapters 6,7, and 8 of the Charter
- These chapters govern peace arrangements and the preservation of peace in the UN
- Most early operations were based on legal arguments of these sections, specifically chapter 6
- Chapter 6 refers to the pacific settlement of disputes, ad peacekeeping is seen as a way to handle this
- More comprehensive multidimensional missions began to take on the responsibilities of chapter 7, and the authority for the UN to delegate peacekeeping to other international orgs comes from chapter 8
- All of this relies on the UN Security Council and its ability to create legally binding resolutions
- Legal peace operations require an affirmative vote from the security council, meaning a 2/3rds majority with no P5 vetoes
- Every peace operation has a mandate handed down from the Security Council which determines the limitations of the mission and are renegotiated regularly
- Although the UNSC is the supreme authority regarding peacekeeping, the executive duties usually fall to the secretary general, who delegates authority to the organizing entities and heads of the peacekeeping mission
- Given that the UNSC is a political body, peacekeeping is subject to the whims of the UNSC members, and especially the P5
- Even when a mission is authorized, it has to take into consideration the political climate of the host state the mission is being deployed to
- The final key parts of the legal framework are IHL and international human rights law, which peacekeeping operations are also subject to and responsible for upholding
- There have been some efforts to reclaim the power over peacekeeping held by the Security Council, and there has been at least one example of the General Assembly creating a peacekeeping mandate

**Peacekeeping and War**

- Peace enforcement is often not peaceful, with the Gulf War and the Korean War being two major examples
- These are considered wars, but were intended as collective defense efforts to prevent further war
- It is extremely rare for the UN to outright authorize war, the most extreme authorization is "all necessary means"
- The US military prepared for the use of the military for non-war activities around the 1990s
- The Canadian military did a similar thing with the "three-block war" concept which includes stability operations like counterinsurgency and humanitarian operation with conflict
- Just like how peace operations can be war, military operations can be employed for peace reasons
- A common use for the military especially in the US and operations against failed or failing states is for "stability operations" to help prop up local populations post-conflict
- The primary military objective is to defeat an enemy, but peace objectives are protecting civilian populations and stabilizing communities
- Think of it like a yin and yang relationship, there are always elements of the military in peace operations and elements of peace operations in the military
- Of course, these two different kinds of forces have different ultimate goals, but can share methods

**Principles of Peacekeeping**

- Consent of the host country for peacekeepers to enter and remain in the country is a primary tenet of the peacekeeping process
- Peacekeeping missions must be impartial, meaning that it should not favour one side or another
- This does not mean that the mission is neutral, because it does have a set mandate it has to fulfill
- Peacekeeping missions should avoid the use of force unless necessary for purposes of self defense or when allowed by their mandate
- These are called the holy trinity of UN peace operations and are the core of peacekeeping ideology
- However, there are other relevant principles to consider
- Military personnel are a necessary part of peacekeeping operations, especially large ones
- Also, the UN does not have a standing army and instead relies on member state contributions to staff it's missions with military personnel
- This opens up the issue of what to do if no states want to volunteer
