- Pact signed by 15 state representatives in 1928, created in collaboration between the US and France
- Also called the Paris Pact, it was signed later by a total of 65 states out of roughly 70 recognized states
- Includes Germany, USSR, and Italy but not colonial countries in the Caribbean and Africa
- Supposed to establish the crime of aggression, but even in the Nuremberg trials the prosecutors didn't even try to charge anyone with it
- The purpose of the Kellogg-Briand Pact was to "outlaw war", and is largely ridiculed for its failure
- Similar to the Hathaway lecture, this misses the point because it ignores how important the ideas that it created were
- Prior to 1928, war was the generally accepted way to settle disputes between nations
- Sanctioning another country was illegal, but going to war with them was not
- There was a generally understood norm of neutrality, if you weren't a party to a war going on you had to stay neutral, and if not you were a legitimate target

**International Law and Grotius**

- Even in Grotius's ideals of international law, war was generally accepted as just
- Wars of aggression are not only tolerated, but rewarded in the sense that he believed it was an effective way for states to exercise and reinforce their rights
- Whatever is claimed in a war of aggression is rightfully owned by the conqueror
- The only limits he mentioned are that states should only go to war in self defense, and only if no peaceful alternative exists
- War is depicted similarly to going to court, you can go for almost any reason you can think of including collecting debts, and the winner rightfully takes the spoils
- Fun fact, Grotius's cousin seized a Portuguese ship in the Dutch East Indies and was able to successfully argue using international law that he was justified as a privateer
- Basically just legalizing thievery using principles like the law of the sea
- The obvious problem with Grotius's approach is that third party countries couldn't protest the decisions made by parties to a conflict
- Because of this, any state can claim self-defense or whatever justification they see fit and it can't be challenged
- Three core components of pre-Kellogg Briand international law
- The right to conquest means that states can pursue compensation by taking territory or goods by force
- The right to wage war extends to the right to threaten war
- Creates gunboat diplomacy, Commodore Perry opened the borders of Japan by threatening to shell the Tokyo harbour if they refused to trade with the US
- Even trade treaties signed under duress are still legally valid, and breaking these treaties often justifies warfare as well
- If war is a method of international policing, then everything committed in war is legal by extension
- Before Nuremberg, the concept of legal accountability in war didn't exist
- Finally, the principle of neutrality means that unless you explicitly declared war, you couldn't be involved in the war
- When declaring war, countries had to specifically establish why they were going to war, but this could range from self-defence to a family member being kidnapped

**Kellogg-Briand Pact**

- WWI changed this dramatically, and served as the tipping point for the rules surrounding war
- Two main articles to the pact
- Article one condemns the use of war for settling disputes and requires parties to avoid it in their relations with each other
- Article two requires that all disputes between signatories be settled by peaceful means only
- The wording of the pact attempts to eliminate war in any context
- No allowances made for wars of self-defence, but doesn't specifically prohibit the use of force for self-defence either
- Contrary to the UN Charter, which makes sure to establish this distinction
- There's also no specific language banning war over territorial disputes
- Imperial Japan and Nazi Germany both signed this pact, and we know what happened after that
- Japan's attack on Pearl Harbour was informed by American gunboat diplomacy, and Germany's invasion of the Sudetenland was justified by the presence of ethnic Germans
- The most immediate violation was Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931 which was widely condemned but never stopped
- Technically the pact was working, but only because it never specified what would happen if a signatory broke it
