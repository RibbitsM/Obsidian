**Melian Dialogue**

- To set the stage, Athens seeks to conquer Melos to expand their empire and also because of the bond between Melos and Sparta, making them enemies
- Today, generals are expected to follow the laws of armed combat, occasionally asking for clarification from lawyers
- Nothing of the sort existed at the time, the goal of a Hellenic general was the maximize their advantage in every situation and manipulate the outcome in favor of their polis
- The main unit of analysis was the polis, not the individual
- When Thucydides speaks of the Athenians and the Melians, obviously not every member of each polis is represented, but effectively the negotiators are each representing their entire state
- Negotiations occur between the elites on each side, standing in for their people
- The laws of war at the time basically consisted of principles that allowed the victors a blank check to do whatever they want with the people and properties they conquer
- The Athenians initiate the dialogue, and hold power over the discussion for the entire time
- When they refer to the facts and the Melian's understanding of those facts, they are referring to their interpretation of the facts
- The Athenians clearly believe that their military advantage is insurmountable and the only rational choice for the Melians is to capitulate
- The Athenian representatives do not attempt to justify their actions or make any moral appeals in the argument, simply to present the Melians with their ultimatum
- Basically say outright that justice is determined by power
- As a counter the Melians argue that it would not be in the Athenian's self-interest to treat them like this because one day they will be in the same situation
- Appeal to a principle that the defeated should be treated justly and fairly and have their arguments heard
- Basically an appeal to jus cogens or a peremptory law
- Recall that at this point, the Melians are seeking an out for themselves personally, not necessarily for their polis
- For the average Melian, the difference between independence and paying tribute would be minimal
- Once they realize they cannot convince the Athenians to let them go by reason, the Melians begin to threaten them with the influence of Sparta
- The Athenians are failing to convince the Melians that the threat they pose is insurmountable, and although they still set the terms of the dialogue they no longer control the outcome
- In another example of a "peremptory norm", the Melians argue that they have the favour of the gods, which are of course the same gods the Athenians claim to have the favour of
- The Athenians justify their conquest by appealing to a natural law that states that one should rule whatever one can
- Finally, they make one final appeal that it is in the Melian's self-interest to capitulate, but in the end they refuse
- In this appeal they use the term "reasonable", which is again judged by the Athenian's standards
