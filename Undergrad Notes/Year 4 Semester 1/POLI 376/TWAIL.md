**History**

- A key part of TWAIL is reinterpreting history
- Looking back and reexamining the history of international law as a method for European countries to acquire more territory and ensure their economic dominance
- Suggests there was an intentional move towards establishing global capitalism very early on in history and international trade and investment law is the result
- Criticizing the foundation of an entire discipline puts the whole into question
- Anghie argues the concept of sovereignty inherently advantages European/Western states
- International law was not created out of western intellectual movements, but rather as a result of imperial confrontation between the West and indigenous non-Western populations
- For example, 16th century Spanish scholars justified having title over Mesoamerican indigenous peoples based on a natural right to trade
- The Spanish have a natural right to enter foreign territory to trade, and if the indigenous population violates or denies this right that is grounds for war, and by extension conquest
- Basically delegitimizes the existence of people that don't conform to ideas of capitalism
- And this is not going away, this is how international investment law and human rights law works today, especially when it comes to universal secular principles of law
- States aren't equal, consider how law is established between equal states compared to unequal ones
- This is all about sovereignty, it was the concept of sovereignty that facilitated the exclusion of the third world from international law
- Sovereignty is often depicted as originating from Europe, specifically the treaty of Westphalia
- As a result of this, European states during the colonial era restricted the privilege of sovereignty to European states only
- Think about Christopher Columbus, he laid claim to islands in Central America for Spain solely on the basis they were not already controlled by other European powers
- Populated territory, and the legality of his claim lies solely on the fact that no one stopped him despite the fact that most indigenous peoples didn't know what he was doing
- This was not especially unusual, this is generally how law worked at the time
- Having the international law system weighted in favour of Western states has not gone away
- The IMF voting system, the P5 veto, and the entire concept of CIL are all ways this persists
- Self determination also didn't necessarily separate states from dependence on colonial powers
- This also connects to environmental law, preserving the environment requires abstaining from development
- If environmental law is focused on preserving Southern environments, this inherently leads into limiting Southern development

**Issues**

- Some argue that TWAIL is anachronistic, needs to be contextualized in specific historic context
- Criticizing classic international scholars by todays standards is ridiculous
- Also, despite the themes of TWAIL it is still mainly rooted in Western academic institutions and using Western theories and methods
- You can also argue that it fails as a academic theory by lapsing into pure advocacy and lacking a proper and consistent methodology
- The entire movement is about disregarding universal standards and questioning the origins of practices so maybe that criticism isn't that valid
- There is also the issue that the problems may go even deeper than just including third world voices, there may be issues with the way knowledge itself is being produced
- One of the main criticisms from TWAIL scholars is that projects like the human rights movement are bad because they attempt to impose a universal moral standard
- But in it's own way, TWAIL attempts to establish a better world according to another set of standards, falling into the same trap
