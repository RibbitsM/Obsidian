**Natural Law Theory**

- Associated with the belief that law is connected to morality
- Comes from the deontological approach, law is based on universal principles of morality that apply in every culture and context
- However, the source of the law varies whether it be religious, intellectual, evolutionary or others
- Positive law emerges from these "natural laws" so the laws we make are still subject to higher law
- Remember, natural law doesn't have to come from a divine source
- The big question here is that especially when it comes to divine sources of law, how do you know for sure that your interpretation of natural law is correct?
- Another important thing to consider is whether we interpret right or wrong depending on the outcome, or the principle?
- Plato was one of the first recorded thinkers to bring up this issue with his idea of "Forms"
- In his utopia ruled by the philosopher-kings, the world would be governed according to their interpretations of the Forms which were essential moral principles of the universe
- Aristotle had a similar conception, where law would be based on virtues and vices
- Aquinas took a Christian approach, basically reinterpreted Aristotle's model of law but with Christian values instead of Greek virtues
- Grotius went against this idea by proposing that humans could reason without having to conform to religious ideals
- First modern example of secular natural law
- This idea is heavily associated with social contract theory, which assumes that the social contract is based on universal rights to safety, happiness, and autonomy
- Some anthropologists like Margaret Mead have attempted to prove that some laws are universal across cultures like prohibition of incest and cannibalism, difference between justified and unjustified killing, and the right to own private property

**Forms of Natural Law**

- The 'traditional' form of natural can be attributed to Thomas Aquinas
- Argued that laws were only just if they conformed to the supreme authority of God
- If all laws conformed to these principles, all laws would promote the common good
- Some of the problems with natural law are that not everybody agrees what is best for the common good
- Common law has also frequently been employed by collectivist governments to disastrous results
- There is no guarantee that natural law will be motivated by justice and not the ideals of an individual with power imposed on society
- The modern version of natural law comes from the scholar Lon Fuller
- Fuller states that we can determine whether laws are just or unjust depending on their accordance to a set of core principles
- Argues that systems of law must fit a set of characteristics called the "inner morality of law"
- He lists eight pitfalls to avoid in order to establish a just legal system
- First, issues cannot be prosecuted on an ad hoc basis without existing laws
- Second, laws must be publicized, to be effective people have to know about a law and more important than that, they should be able to easily understand the law
- Third, legislation should be prospective and not retrospective
- Laws are supposed to guide the conduct of future citizens, so retrospective laws are pointless for this purpose
- After all, what is the purpose of following the law if you aren't certain your actions won't become illegal at some point in the future
- Fourth is similar to the second point, rules should be understandable and not unnecessarily complex
- Fifth, laws cannot contradict each other, making it impossible to follow the law
- Sixth, laws must allow the affected parties to comply
- Again, everyone must have the ability to follow the law
- Seventh, the legal system should be stable and continuous
- Governments should be constrained from making major changes to the law, if any new government was able to change the law as they see fit consistency would suffer
- Finally, laws must be enforced in order to have any weight
- Using these eight principles, Fuller argues that we can ignore rules that violate the inner morality of law
- This comes into play during the Nuremburg trials since natural law theory was employed to argue that Nazis can be charged even though their actions were legal under German law
- Because of their violations of natural law, Nazi decrees were unlawful and immoral
- For another example we can look at MLK Jr, he was quoted saying that one has the moral responsibility to obey just laws, but also the responsibility to disobey unjust laws
- Ultimately, Fuller's argument is that laws should promote the good of society which is what morality commands us to do
- However, what promotes the good of society is not necessarily moral depending on the interpretation

**Legal Positivism**

- Two main thinkers in this field, HLA Hart and John Austin
- John Austin wrote a piece on "law as command" to counter natural law in the 19th century, and HLA Hart later wrote a critique of Austin's work to further develop positivism
- According to Austin, laws are defined by the sovereign and are not based on moral concepts
- The law is the law for reasons of power alone
- This is harder to apply to international law since there is no real equivalent to the sovereign on the global stage
- Because of this, Austin does not recognize international law as law at all
- Legal obligation comes entirely from the threat of consequences from the state, and in the absence of enforcement law does not exist
- Hart gets around this issue by making a distinction between domestic law which has an enforcement mechanism, and international law which lacks some aspects of domestic law
- He argues that some laws exist to empower individuals like judges and legislators, but also ordinary people using the law to make and enter into contracts
- Looking at these examples, it's hard to argue that these laws can be interpreted as commands
- For Austin, all sovereigns rule by law but Hart argues that some governments are controlled by law
- The government is not necessarily above the law, they don't have complete agency
- The rules set out by the sovereign must be recognized by the people to be valid
- Under this definition, the international legal system is not really a legal system at all

**Other Legal Theories**

- Legal realism argues that society and law are inseparable, and should be studied together with an interdisciplinary approach
- Marxist law theory focuses on theories of distributive justice and the idea that law can be used to address the distribution of wealth and inequal class structures
- Feminist theories of law focus on how the law reinforces patriarchal conditions and gender roles
- Critical legal studies is a more open approach that focuses on the relationship between the law and economic exploitation
- And finally, critical race theory focuses on the influence of race and racism on legal systems
