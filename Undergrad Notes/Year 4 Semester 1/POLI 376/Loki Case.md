- Genocide is considered to be the denial of the right to live of a group of people, analogous to homicide which is the denial of the right to love of certain individuals
- Legally it has a very narrow definition, but is colloquially used to refer to large-scale killings
- The main difference between crimes against humanity and genocide is the existence of the Genocide Convention
- States have an obligation to prevent and punish all recognized acts of genocide, the same is not true for crimes against humanity
- International Commission of Inquiry on Darfur did not find sufficient evidence of genocide but argued that crimes against humanity can be worse in some cases
- Term was coined by Raphael Lemkin in 1944
- The Nuremberg Tribunal did not consider the crime of genocide as it was outside their jurisdiction and there were no laws relating to it at the time
- Retrospectively, it is blatantly obvious that genocide was committed in Nazi Germany but the issue is that you generally cannot apply laws like this
- Crimes against humanity laws exist to protect groups against discrimination, not elimination
- These acts must be widespread and systematic, but interestingly there is no equivalent requirement for genocide
- This is why Ukraine was able to charge Russia with genocide based on one instance of children being transferred to Russia
- To put it in legal terms, genocide relies on the existence of _mens rea_ to prosecute
- Genocide Convention does not mention social or political groups in their definition of genocide
- The ICTR attempted to define the four categories of groups mentioned in the Convention
- National groups are people perceived to be bonded legally by common citizenship, rights, and duties
- Racial groups are people grouped by shared hereditary traits that are identified with a specific geographical area
- Ethnic groups are people that share a common language/culture
- Religious groups are people of a certain denomination or mode of worship, or simply a group sharing common religious beliefs
- However these are not hard universally recognized definitions
- Groups are social constructs, and as such are inherently hard to concretely define
- The ICTY defined harm as anything creating a long-term disadvantage harming an individuals ability to lead a fulfilling life
- The component of deliberately inflicting on a group conditions calculated to bring about death is defined primarily by the ICC elements of crime
- Forced migration of people in general doesn't necessarily constitute a genocide
- As usual, there has to be clear intent of ethnic cleansing or genocide
- Sterilization and sexual mutilation is a more clear-cut indication of genocide, as it shows an intent to prevent births within a group from occurring
- Sexual violence is also considered a method of preventing births as in many patriarchal societies victims of sexual abuse may not be allowed to have children
- Genocide is a group action, but if a pattern of leadership is established it can just as easily be the project of one or a few individuals

**Mental Elements**

- In the latter case, does every individual involved need to be a driver of the project to be responsible, or is complicity enough?
- In the case of Bosnian Serb militants killing all "military age" Bosnian Muslim men in Srebrenica, the general was found not guilty despite the act being considered an act of genocide
- Despite his claims that the intent was to secure a military advantage, the tribunal decided there was no way his soldiers could not have known the consequences
- The act was there, but there was no evidence of active intent to commit genocide
- Motive is not a key part of genocide, the focus is mainly on intent
- It doesn't matter why they would do it, only whether they intended to do it or not
- While the text of the rule says "in whole or in part", precedent holds that this "in part" aspect is not always held up
- For example, the Hutu leaders in Rwanda never had clear intent to kill all Tutsis, just the Tutsis living in Rwanda
- The ICJ, ICTR, and ICC concurred that there must be intent to destroy a "substantial" part of a group, enough to impact the group as a whole
- "Destruction" in this definition is explicitly physical, this does not apply to things like social assimilation or restriction of cultural characteristics/practices
- There is limited support for cultural genocide in a general assembly resolution, but again this is not legally binding
- Loss of culture is certainly a result of genocide, but not a cause of it
