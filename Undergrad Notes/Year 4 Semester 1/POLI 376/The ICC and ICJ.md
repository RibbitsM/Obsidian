**ICJ**

- The ICJ deals with states and consenting non-state actors exclusively
- It is the principal judicial organ of the UN according to Article 92
- Composed of 15 judges that each serve 9-year terms and are elected by the UNGA and UNSC
- Of course these two units are not equal, the UNSC has significantly more sway
- All judges have to be from different countries and are selected based on qualifications depending on the legal system in their country
- Generally, judges are picked on a geographical distribution
- Usually 3 seats are from Africa, 2 are from Latin America/Caribbean, 3 from Asia, 2 from Eastern Europe, 5 from Western Europe and all other states
- This represents the idea that all states are equal and should be treated as such

**Jurisdiction**

- The ICJ has jurisdiction in two specific areas
- First is contentious jurisdiction, which is when states consent to the ICJ's authority to settle a legal dispute
- All states involved agree to submit the case to the ICJ and abide by the result
- Next is the ICJ advisory opinions
- These are not legally binding, but can be requested by the UNGA, UNSC, or other organizations like the ILO, WMF etc.
- There are no limitations on the subject of these opinions, they can cover any international legal question
- They are not binding in any form, but often form the basis of further legal action
- For example, an ICJ advisory opinion can classify actions as crimes against humanity which can be used as evidence in an ICC prosecution
- This is what is called a "legal characterization" while contentious jurisdiction results in a "legal determination" which actually imposes obligations
- In the UN Charter states are obligated to follow legal determinations under Article 94(1) and (2) under threat of referral to the UNSC
- Again the enforcement relies on the agreement of the UNSC to have any effect, but potential consequences can potentially include use of force

**Mitigating Bias**

- According to principles of court impartiality, laws should be interpreted based on evidence alone
- Even Lauterpacht who is considered to be one of the progenitors of the international legal system said that a lack of impartiality is the greatest challenge to international law
- A 1968 study showed that ICJ judges favoured their home country in the vast majority of cases and rarely ruled against national policies of their home country
- A newer study in 2015 showed that ICJ judges also tend to favour countries that are culturally similar to their own
- There seems to be little evidence of impartial voting
- Despite the exponentially increasing number of cases heard by the ICJ, this doesn't necessarily indicate increased trust in the court
- This could just be an escalation in the use of lawfare
- Informally, the P5 have seats on the ICJ
- The only exceptions were China until 1985, the UK since 2015, and Russia since 2023
- Despite the voting system, the P5 have been voted to receive a seat consistently through the entire lifetime of the ICJ
- Important to note that electing a justice requires absolute majority, but the P5 don't get any sort of special power in this vote
- Also, the ICJ has a recusal system to help maintain neutrality
- However, there is no requirement for judges to recuse themselves in case there is a conflict of interest
- In the Nicaragua case in 1984, the US judge did not recuse themself despite the US being a party in the case
- Throughout the history of the ICJ, recusals are quite rare

**ICJ Process**

- Even in the case of advisory opinions, problems can come up
- Often, the questions asked to the ICJ are loaded to begin with and come with many preestablished assumptions
- The first question ever asked to the ICJ was whether the Western Sahara was considered _terra nullius_ which was asked in 1975 due to the prompting of Morocco
- While this was a fair question, the court can only answer the question it was asked
- Therefore, if the question is not asked in good faith, the result will also be in bad faith
- The ICJ also needs jurisdiction even to make an advisory opinion
- One power the court does have is it can recommend "provisional measures" if a case is particularly urgent since ICJ cases often take years to conclude
- Evidence for provisional measures is not held to the same standard as usual
- The general process is first the ICJ determines whether it has jurisdiction, then it accepts the question asked, and finally it can issue provisional measures
- Serious evidence collection can only start after all this has happened
- The ICJ chooses who will collect the evidence, which can be basically any individual or organization that they find is appropriate
