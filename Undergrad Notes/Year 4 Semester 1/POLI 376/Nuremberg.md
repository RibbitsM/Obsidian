- Nuremberg serves as the foundation for basically all international criminal litigation
- Initial purpose was to prosecute wars of aggression, turned into crimes against humanity
- Led by four powers with differing legal opinions and systems
- Mainly led by the US, USSR, and UK with France playing a more minor role
- Primarily focused on the Holocaust
- 21 defendants charged with waging a war of aggression, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and conspiracy to commit the previous crimes
- A major problem with this is the charge of conspiracy, this is now an accepted part of common law but at the time it was not a German law or really a European law at all
- There were additional trials for physicians, industrialists, etc but the focus is on the Nazi officers
- Geneva Convention existed already at this time, but in a very different form
- The Allies did not have to do this, they could have legally just shot all the German officers, and Churchill actually suggested this at first
- Stalin agreed to a trial, but was under the impression that they would be killed no matter what
- But Jackson was able to convince the Soviets that there should be a fair and legal trial, and eventually they agreed
- Even if they are undoubtably guilty, there is still the question of legal responsibility
- The purpose of the law is that you cannot just kill people for being evil, there must be proof that they violated a law
- The Nazis violated international law, specifically customary and treaty law

**Criticism**

- Many Germans argued that the trial was collective punishment, even though most citizens had done nothing they were humiliated by having their leaders put on trial
- There is also the question of legal theory, many scholars questioned the IMT's legitimacy
- Crimes against humanity was not originally a charge, Hans Lauterpecht suggested it later on
- No treaty in existence at the time recognized or criminalized crimes against humanity
- However, it had been used before in 1915 to define the Armenian Genocide (it wasn't called that at the time)
- Jackson introduced the concept on July 29th, defining it as the killing of a very large number of people as part of a systematic program
- A problem with this is that the law was created after the acts were committed
- There can be no crime without a law
- Kellogg-Briand outlawed war, but did not provide for individual responsibility
- As stated earlier, the conspiracy charge also didn't exist when the acts were committed
- German law traditionally focused only in individual culpability, this idea of many individuals being collectively and equally responsible for a crime was foreign
- A generally understood legal principle is that people should be tried as individuals, not convicted based on the actions of others
- Some of the crimes thought up by the IMT could just as easily apply to them
- The bombings of Dresden and Hamburg could easily qualify as war crimes, and the Katyn massacre committed by the Russians could be crimes against humanity
- Also, the USSR violated the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, which is a war of aggression
- The law of aggression itself is not very clearly defined, and relies on subjective measures
- Nuremberg was political only, not a legal trial just a witch hunt
- Theories of natural law are not universal, Hobbes and Austin remain adamant that law is defined by a sovereign only, and as such would not apply here
- Going back to the Hart-Fuller debate, should the law be aligned with morals, or should morals be aligned with the law
- Basically, what makes a law legitimate: it's legal status or its moral validity

**Supporting the Trial**

- The Kellogg-Briand Pact did not provide for individual responsibility, but it definitely outlawed wars of aggression
- Germany signed it, so there is clear proof they violated law
- There's also the argument that no new laws were actually created here, just adapted from other conventions
- The Hague and Geneva Conventions were already in place and influenced the definition of war crimes
- Crimes against humanity is a little different, but the phrase was not new and there is language contained in the Hague Conventions that provide for matters outside defined law
- The Martens Clause says that in cases not covered by the Hague Convention, actors are still protected by the principles of international law
- These principles come from "the laws of humanity and the requirements of the public conscience"
- Suggests that even when specific laws do not apply, universal principles of humanity and morality apply
- The judges were intended to be impartial, and three defendants were acquitted on all counts and several more on some counts
- If the USSR or the UK had their way, this would have never happened
- Finally, the trial did the important work of publicizing the full atrocities of the Nazi regime and established a precedent going forward
- While the trial wasn't perfect, it's hard to argue that it was better than the alternatives
- It's good that they didn't just execute them, and they couldn't exactly let them go either
- All things considered, the Nuremberg trial probably gave the defendants the best chance they could have reasonably gotten
- More importantly, it sets a precedent of creating an alternative to vigilante justice

**Crimes Against Humanity and Genocide**

- The Genocide Convention and the 1949 Geneva Convention have not been signed by all states, but is generally recognized to be customary international law
- However, there is no standalone treaty that defines or punishes crimes against humanity
- Crimes against humanity are illegal, but the authority for this is spread out across the legal system
- There is movement happening right now to create a genocide convention equivalent for crimes against humanity
- The hope is that having a single specific treaty like this will increase the legitimacy of crimes against humanity and allow crimes of this kind to be recognized and punished more frequently
