**Definitions of Law**

- System of enforceable rules governing relations between members of a society and between those members and the society
- Problem with applying this to international law, where is the enforcement?
- Article 7 is sometimes invoked, but very rarely (requires consensus from UNSC)
- Generally the aspect of enforcement is what distinguishes the law from guidelines
- According to Hart, we can think of international law as a primitive form of law lacking enforcement
- There is rarely consensus on what is moral/ethical in a society, but laws are still generally based on ethical principles

**Definitions of Justice**

- What is the relationship between law and justice?
- Is justice an absolute concept or is it subjective?
- If so, how is it defined and who is doing the defining in each context?
- Apply this concept to international law, where does the idea of justice come from in the international legal system?
- The goal of the UN is not explicitly to uphold justice, but to preserve peace and security
- Justice is only one part of this goal
- Law and justice are distinct concepts, and theoretically everything that is legal should also be just
- But not only do laws change all the time, it's doubtful that any constant definition of justice exists
- Constant process of change to align law and justice
- Justice can be considered either as an end in itself (deontology), or a means to an end (utilitarianism)
- The Kantian or deontological approach views justice as universal and objective, and represents unalienable rights and responsibilities that must be upheld without exception
- With the other approach, justice is a path to another end like maximizing happiness (like in the case of utilitarianism)
- However, the end in this context could be anything you want
- These two ways of thinking lead to three distinct approaches to justice: corrective justice, retributive justice, and distributive justice

**Corrective Justice**

- Corrective justice is primarily connected with the deontological approach
- A person has a moral responsibility for the harm that is caused to another and that loss must be rectified or corrected usually in the form of compensation (wergilt)
- This is where the concept of "reparations" comes from
- Corrective justice is definitely present in international law, the Rome Statute allows the ICC to prescribe reparations for the victims of crimes within its jurisdiction
- For example, Poland sought further reparations from Germany in the past couple years for WWII, but this did not fall within the jurisdiction of the ICC since the crime occurred before the formation of the court
- But outside of the ICC, these principles are still applied
- When Serbia tried to join the UN, they had to first make reparations for their actions during WWII

**Retributive Justice**

- Classic antiquity law, straight from Hammurabi's Code
- Eye for an eye, proportionate justice, lex talionis, whatever you want to call it
- Not explicitly associated with instrumental or deontological justice, can be applied in either context
- Based entirely on the principle that the punishment should be directly proportional to the crime
- Surprisingly, this is not entirely absent from international law
- We can see retributive justice in the Geneva Convention, Protocol I, Article 51(5)(b) prohibits attacks that would cause excessive damage to civilians in relation to anticipated military advantage
- Not exactly the same thing, but the basic principle of proportionality is definitely still there
- This is one of the fiddliest bits in an incredibly fiddly document, many parts of this article are extremely subjective
- First of all, how can we define damage to civilian bodies and infrastructure, second how can we define damage being excessive compared to military advantage, and finally how can we expect commanders to predict any of these factors
- Generally, these definitions are never concretely established until the defendant is already in the courtroom
- There is no constitution or charter in the ICC, while it's authority is limited, it's effectively the highest legal authority in the world and its power is based almost entirely on consensus
- An unavoidable consequence of this is that any prosecution made is basically just the global community dogpiling on a specific individual, group, or state

**Distributive Justice**

- This flavour of justice is primarily concerned with the distribution of resources and rights
- What is each person entitled to, and are some people entitled to more or less than others?
- Also associated with the Pareto principle, can you make society better off by redistributing resources without making someone else worse off?
- A key modern thinker on distributive justice is John Rawls, who advocates for a very similar principle
- Basically, all resources should be distributed equally unless distributing them unequally satisfies the Pareto principle
- For an example of this in international law you can look to climate change law, where more lenience is given to developing countries in terms of emissions given that they were "late" to industrialization
- The idea is that allowing these countries to emit more right now is just and will reduce future emissions
