- Originated with Roman law, literally means "as you possess"
- This principle states that newly formed sovereign states should keep the borders of their prior administration
- Technically a law based on precedent which is unusual in international law, but has been widely applied to many different cases
- Uti Possidetis Juris was especially relevant during the wave of decolonization in the 19th and 20th centuries
- In Latin America, newly independent states declared their boundaries based on former Spanish colonial administration boundaries
- This way, all of South America was occupied by a state, leaving no "dead zones" to be claimed by other states
- A similar technique was applied to Africa, national boundaries were based on colonial administrations
- This caused a lot of issues post-independence because many groups were split up or forced to live together, administration borders were not based on tribal identities
- By the way, this argument of arbitrary colonial borders was used by Saddam Hussein to justify annexing Kuwait because historically speaking it should be part of Iraq
- This is an arbitrary way to draw borders, but it is a very easy and efficient way to draw borders
- It likely will not be the ideal outcome in many situations, but it ensures a system of borders that leaves no gaps and fairly clean lines
- Many other countries divided this way too
- India and Pakistan, Indonesia and East Timor, even Ukraine and Poland
- The Organization of African Unity endorsed this principle in 1964, arguing that it prevents border disputes
- Also has been used in multiple bilateral border disputes to resolve the situation
- Doesn't just apply to the colonial context, a similar strategy was employed in Yugoslavia and the USSR

**ICJ Rulings**

- Uti possidetis juris only exists in customary international law, it has never been formalized
- In a way, it's actually more effective as a norm than a treaty since it applies universally
- It would have more weight if it was in a treaty, but would have to go through a long signing and ratification process and would only apply to states that signed it
- Ratner calls it an "idiot rule"
- He defines this as a simple principle that gives an acceptable outcome in the majority of situations but is too simple to solve all situations
- This assumes that all borders are equally rational and no option is better than another but this is not true at all in reality
- It's extremely difficult to evaluate how important land is to people, which generally results in border disputes

**Mali and Burkina Faso**

- For an example, lets look at the ICJ judgement on the 1986 Burkina Faso/Mali dispute
- This is a judgement, not an opinion, which means that both of these countries agreed to the jurisdiction of the ICJ and agree to abide by the decision
- The ICJ argued that at the moment a state becomes independent, their borders are locked in and they cannot change them
- Principle of stability, ignores human rights principles to ensure that new states are able to more easily consolidate their independence
- If a new country is born into constant border conflict, it doesn't have much of a chance
- The ICJ specifically connects uti possidetis juris to decolonization multiple times in this ruling, drawing from precedent from Latin America and elsewhere in Africa
- However, connecting it to decolonization specifically and not borders indicates that it isn't a universal principle
- But at other parts of the ruling the court indicates that this is indeed a universal rule
- Depending on which specific quotes you look at, the message is quite different

**Dissolution of Yugoslavia**

- But for a non-colonial example, we can look at the Badinter Commission on the dissolution of Yugoslavia during the 1990s
- The opinion of the Commission was that Yugoslavia's internal boundaries should be used as the boundaries for the new states
- They argued that at this point, the principle of uti possidetis juris was a general principle of CIL and didn't just apply to decolonization
- As a fallback, they also allowed the principle of self-determination to be exercised within the new state boundaries which would be inviolable
- Also claims of statehood based on ethnic lines would be rejected if they threatened the integrity of previously recognized borders
- In 2010, the UNGA asked for the ICJ's opinion on whether Kosovo's 2008 declaration of independence violated international law
- The ruling was that international law had no prohibition on declarations of independence
- As such, based on the principle of uti possidetis juris, the internal administrative boundaries of former Serbia would determine the border of Kosovo
- While the question of whether Kosovo is a sovereign state or not is still in question, its borders are not
- As a result, its borders are provisionally respected, but Kosovo is not a UN member state

**Nagorno-Karabakh/Artsakh**

- Located in present day Azerbaijan, the Nagorno-Karabakh Oblast was considered an autonomous region since its declaration of independence in 1991
- Neighbouring Armenia claims the region (called Artsakh by Armenians) on historical grounds, as well as the region being majority Armenian
- In September 2023, Azerbaijan launched a major military offensive on the region
- Argument was that this was not a violation of the 2020 ceasefire agreement because of terrorist groups within Nagorno-Karabakh attacking Azerbaijan
- As a result, over 100,000 Armenians were ethnically cleansed and civilian infrastructure was destroyed
- As of January 1, 2024, the independent republic that was proclaimed in the region ceased to exist
- This relates to uti possidetis juris because Nagorno-Karabakh was a former USSR oblast and was considered autonomous within Azerbaijan by the USSR
- When the region attempted to establish its independence, it maintained these USSR borders
- However, prior to the breakup of the soviet union, Nagorno-Karabakh was within the borders of the Azerbaijan SSR
- Technically, according to uti possidetis juris the region should belong to Azerbaijan despite having an Armenian majority
- As a counter, Armenia appeals to the principle of self-determination
- Citing the UN Charter and the International Convention on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), Armenia argues that the people of Nagorno-Karabakh/Artsakh have the right to independence
- The importance of human rights supersedes uti possidetis juris, and as such the Armenian majority in Azerbaijan have the right to secede
- This does have some weight since Article 103 of the Charter states that the UN Charter is superior in any conflict with another treaty

**Pros and Cons**

- Main benefits result from the convenience of the rule as well as its finality
- It bypasses the issue of drawing borders when declaring independence
- Avoids fragmentation into further smaller states which will be less viable
- For example, if Quebec ever does actually secede from Canada, they'd likely not seek full independence because the province relies on the Canadian government fairly significantly
- Related to the first benefit, it provides a stable foundation for a new state
- Exists as kind of a constant in international relations that states can fall back on
- Finally, it has a pretty solid precedent as it's been used in Latin America and Africa as well as the breakup of the USSR and Yugoslavia
- As for the cons, the main issue is the arbitrariness of the boundaries
- First of all, this can exacerbate tensions between ethnic groups by creating minority groups
- It also clashes with the principle of self-determination, and can result in even further subdivision if the principle is applied post-independence
- Can also cause worse legal issues and humanitarian crises due to ethnic tensions
- Also eliminates the influence of the people on the region on their borders, not democratic
- Just because a people has a right to declare statehood does not mean they also get to declare the conditions for that statehood
- Overall, uti possidetis juris isn't a hard rule of international relations, it's more like a principle to use in the absence of a better solution via treaties or something similar
