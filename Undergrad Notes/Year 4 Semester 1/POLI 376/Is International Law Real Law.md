- International law is obviously law, it is considered a distinct category separate from other kinds of law
- However, the question is whether it can fit into the criteria we use domestically to identify law
- It's commonly understood that laws need to be enforced
- How can we reconcile this when the international system has no sovereign?
- Basically, can law exist on its own or does law require some sort of institution to reinforce its legitimacy

**Hart's Perspective**

- Hart sees international law as different from domestic law in the sense that it is underdeveloped
- There is no international legal system because there is no system of coercion or enforcement
- Doesn't necessarily mean it has to become like domestic law
- Makes the distinction between primary and secondary rules
- The primary rules tell actors what they can and cannot do, and secondary rules tell actors how to create/alter primary rules
- Since international law lacks secondary rules, the system is incomplete
- In addition to no enforcement, there's also no centralized law-making institution and no executive/judicial branch that applies the law
- All this relies on the perspective that law cannot exist without the state
- This ignores the fact that despite all this, we can see that international law actually does affect state behaviour
- Additionally, even if there are not comprehensive secondary rules, primary rules can still be changed in international law
- We can think of the international system as horizontal, not vertical
- Instead of rules coming from above and being enforced, they emerge out of interactions between actors
- Violators are punished through sanctions and outcasting instead of physical consequences
- Contrary to domestic law, the sources of international law are not exclusively institutions

**Sources of International Law**

- The four main sources of international law come from the Statute of the ICJ (1945), specifically article 38
- These sources are treaties, CIL, general principles of law recognized by civilized nations, and judicial decisions/academic publications (very conditionally)
- The category of treaties is further controlled by the 1980 Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT)
- Defines treaties as agreements between states in written form and governed by international law
- This means agreements between states and non-state actors don't count
- Article 53 specifies that treaties can be voided if they conflict with any peremptory norm (jus cogens)
- Also, any conflicts over jus cogens relating to treaty validation and interpretation should be referred to the ICJ
- As the primary source of international law alongside CIL, it's important to note that treaties are voluntary
- This aligns with the idea of voluntarist positivist law, international law is based on state consent
- There is also customary international law, likely the most nebulous but also most important source
- International custom is simply a general practice between states that is accepted as law
- _opinio juris_ is the belief that an action is taken because an actor is legally obliged to do so and is used to identify CIL
- Not all customs have _jus cogens_ status so apart from those cases it is unclear whether customs trump treaty law in most situations
- The general principles source is another very unclear area
- A couple main principles people frequently refer to are good faith, due process, impartiality, etc.
- These are not necessarily laws, more like guidelines as to how the law should be created and applied
- Finally, the last source is rarely invoked and mainly acts as a secondary source used to empower the first two (in accordance with the third)

**Other Sources**

- Outside the four main sources, the UN is also an important source of international law
- For example, UNSC resolutions are binding sources of international law
- The UN Charter also established the hierarchy of international law
- Outside of _jus cogens_, the UN Charter is the other "supreme" source of international law
- According to Article 103, obligations to the UN Charter supersede all other obligations to treaties or other agreements
- Overall, just because the international system lacks a sovereign does not mean its laws lack legitimacy
- There are many other sources to examine and argue for the importance of
- For example, the Nuremberg Charter uses ideas of natural law to impose legal obligations and process even in the absence of any authority
