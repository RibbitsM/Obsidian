- It is widely agreed that the main unit of analysis in international relations is the state
- The Treaty of Westphalia definitively established the concept of sovereignty, and the association of a state with a territory
- Territory is extremely important to the state, and to its population as well
- The principle of self-determination asserts the right of peoples to determine their political status and pursue development
- This can either take the form of internal determination or external determination
- In the first case, peoples receive autonomy within their existing territory in a larger state
- A great example of this is Quebec again, although the province is not completely independent it does have a higher degree of autonomy compared to the rest of Canada
- External determination requires remedial secession which we'll get to

**What is a "People"?**

- There is no universally accepted definition of a "people", but there is a general idea
- Despite its subjectivity, this term is used in several key documents including the ICCPR treaty and the UN Charter which were both used as support by Armenia
- What groups are allowed to define themselves as "a people" is highly political and naturally requires making some groups important and others less important
- The ICJ has indicated that the definition should take into consideration the identity and characteristics of a group as well as their historical ties to the territory in question
- Especially this historical ties section is extremely controversial
- For example, the ICJ recognized the Sahrawi people's right to self-determination in the Western Sahara case under principles of international law
- Since they were defined as a "people", the Sahrawi were allowed the right to decolonization primarily because of their status as indigenous groups to the Western Sahara
- The definition of what is an indigenous group does have somewhat of a definition, from the UN Declaration of the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007)
- This document is especially interesting, because alongside self-identification and historical ties to the territory pre-colonization, one requirement is that the group should be non-dominant
- Another important definition to consider here is the idea of a "minority"
- Again, there is no universal definition of the term but it is widely used
- Working definition is a group that is numerically less significant within a state that is ethnically, linguistically, or religiously distinct from the dominant group
- Minority groups should also have some sense of solidarity in preserving those unique characteristics
- ICCPR guarantees the rights of minority groups to self-determination

**Statehood**

- Just like uti possidetis juris, statehood started in Latin America
- The Montevideo Convention (1933) requires a defined territory, a permanent population, a government, and the capacity to conduct international relations for a region to be a state
- The last one is the most important and is the reason why recognition from other states is required
- While these are just legal criteria and is somewhat separated from the recognition process, they are still interconnected
- This plays into the two different theories of statehood that are most popular
- Constitutive theory argues that the most important factor is consent, a territory cannot be a state without recognition from other states
- Declaratory theory argues that all you need to do is meet the four criteria, which can be the same thing depending on how you interpret the fourth one
- Another question is how many states need to recognize a state for it to exist
- Is it a democracy? Do some states have more importance for recognition?
- Also, how do we know if a state recognizes another state
- Types of government are also important, the Montevideo Convention just requires a government but it doesn't specify any kind
- As for debates on the Convention, how can a state be a state if it doesn't have an international legal personality?
- A state can meet all four criteria and still be non-recognized
- When do we know when the criteria are met, and does it matter what had to happen to meet that criteria?
- The law is not the be all and end all, just because an entity is a state _de juris_ does not mean it's a state _de facto_

**Evolution of Statehood**

- The Montevideo Convention is almost a century old and a lot has changed since then
- At this point, the treaty has become CIL, but so has the idea of state recognition
- During the same period, many other things began to be considered CIL like the prohibition of slavery, genocide, and torture
- There has been a distinctive trend towards incorporating morality into international law, and this extends to declarations of statehood
- For a more recent example of recognition of states, we can examine the breakup of the USSR
- The European Commission stated in 1991 that they would only recognize states that conduct themselves on a "democratic basis" and have accepted international obligation as well as submitting in good faith to peaceful negotiations
- None of the existing states in Europe had to meet this criteria for recognition, so why should the new ones?
- This could also be considered intervention or imperialism by imposing existing state values on new states
- Also, there is no guarantee that the same standards will be imposed on all emerging European states

**Secession**

- A fairly common result of imposing human and minority rights and democracy on new states is secession
- The main motivation for the international community to allow secession is for the purposes of stability
- It is defined as the unilateral withdrawal of part of a state from its host, bringing its territory and population with it
- Taking the example of Kosovo, there is no international law banning independence but nothing authorizing it wither
- This is not internal sovereignty, it's external which means organizing as a state separate from its former host
- International law also permits the host to fight the secessionist state under article 2(4) because it's a violation of territorial integrity
- Human rights often works to emphasize claims for secession, which are generally motivated by the treatment of minority groups
- Often secessionist movements result from claims of oppression and persecution targeting a minority group within a state
- Remedial secession is not yet an element of CIL but it is conceivable that it could evolve into one
- The right to remedial secession only emerges in exceptional circumstances where a state is failing to respect the rights of a group or is actively severely mistreating that group
- Likely the most high profile case was the potential secession of Quebec in 1998
- The right to remedial secession was considered by the Supreme Court when evaluating Quebec's claim to secession
- The outcome was that this right only comes up in the most extreme circumstances, and the situation in Quebec did not meet this bar
- Recall that Quebec was already granted the right to internal self-determination and the Supreme Court ruled that they did not have sufficient reason to seek external self-determination
- The Court also mentioned three cases where this right would be valid
- External self-determination is justified when a people is oppressed, potentially by a military occupation, or when a group is denied access to government
- These are supposed to be examples where a group cannot seek internal self-determination

**Issues for Self-Determination**

- The borders of self-determination aren't always clear, it's possible that new boundaries could further restrict the rights of smaller groups
- The legal consequences may not always be clear
- Even using _uti possidetis juris_ to draw boundaries isn't always foolproof, any problem of self-determination requires political will to happen
- The strongest legal arguments can just be denied for political reasons
- To summarize, there has to be three conditions met for self-determination
- First, the group in question must prove they are eligible for the right of self-determination
- Second, the group must occupy a distinct territory, ideally separate from the rest of the state
- Finally, they must demonstrate that this group has been oppressed or denied the right to internal self-determination within the state
- Generally, states are encouraged to create inclusive, multicultural environments where states don't feel the need to seek self-determination
